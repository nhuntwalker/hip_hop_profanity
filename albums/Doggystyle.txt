<p><i><b>Doggystyle</b></i> is the debut album from <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> rapper <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, then known as <b>Snoop Doggy Dogg</b>, released by <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a> on November 23, 1993. The album was recorded soon following the release of <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>'s landmark debut album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i> (1992), to which Snoop Dogg contributed significantly. The style he developed for Dre's album was continued on <i>Doggystyle</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Critics have praised Snoop Dogg for the lyrical "realism" he delivers on the album and for his distinctive vocal flow.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Despite some mixed criticism of the album initially upon its release, <i>Doggystyle</i> has earned recognition from many music critics as one of the most significant albums of the 1990s, as well as one of the most important hip hop albums ever released.<sup id="cite_ref-AcclaimedMusic_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AcclaimedMusic-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Much like <i>The Chronic</i>, the distinctive sounds of <i>Doggystyle</i> helped introduce the hip hop style of <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a> to a mainstream audience, bringing forward <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast hip hop</a> as a dominant force in the early 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMG-TheChronic_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-TheChronic-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> As of 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA) has certified <i>Doggystyle</i> quadruple platinum in sales, and it serves as Snoop Dogg's highest-selling album.</p>
<p><i>Doggystyle</i> debuted at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, and sold 802,858 copies in its first week alone, which was the record for a debuting artist and the fastest-selling album ever until <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marshall_Mathers_LP" title="The Marshall Mathers LP">The Marshall Mathers LP</a></i> in 2000. <i>Doggystyle</i> is included in The Source magazine's list of the 100 Best Rap Albums, as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of Essential Recordings of the 90s.<sup id="cite_ref-AcclaimedMusic_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AcclaimedMusic-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> About.com placed the album in number 19 of the greatest hip hop/rap albums of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conception">Conception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Conception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Snoop Dogg came to attention of the music industry in 1992, through his vocal contributions on Dr. Dre's <i>The Chronic</i>. That album is considered to have "transformed the entire sound of West Coast rap" by its development of what later became known as the "<a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a>" sound.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-TheChronic_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-TheChronic-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Chronic</i> expanded <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a> with profanity, anti-authoritarian lyrics and multi-layered <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">samples</a> taken from 1970's <a href="/wiki/P-Funk" title="P-Funk">P-Funk</a> records.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-TheChronic_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-TheChronic-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Snoop Dogg contributed vocals to Dre's solo single, "<a href="/wiki/Deep_Cover_(song)" title="Deep Cover (song)">Deep Cover</a>", which led to a high degree of anticipation among hip hop for the release of his own solo album.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Snoop was also featured on the single <a href="/wiki/Nuthin%27_but_a_%22G%22_Thang" title="Nuthin' but a &quot;G&quot; Thang" class="mw-redirect">Nuthin' but a "G" Thang</a>, which reached number two on the Billboard Hot 100, giving him mainstream recognition.</p>
<p><i>Doggystyle</i> and <i>The Chronic</i> are associated with each other mainly because each prominently featured Snoop Dogg and because both contain G-funk style production from Dr. Dre. The two releases are linked by the high number of vocal contributions from Death Row Records artists, including <a href="/wiki/Tha_Dogg_Pound" title="Tha Dogg Pound">Tha Dogg Pound</a>, <a href="/wiki/RBX" title="RBX">RBX</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Lady_of_Rage" title="The Lady of Rage">The Lady of Rage</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-TheSource_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheSource-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> while both contain a high density of misogynistic lyrics and profanity in their lyrics.<sup id="cite_ref-Stylus-TheChronicVsDoggystyle_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stylus-TheChronicVsDoggystyle-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, the two albums are each viewed by critics as early "G-funk classics", and have been described as "joined at the hip". 'Doggystyle' also marked the debut of Death Row vocalist, Nanci Fletcher - the daughter of jazz legend Sam Fletcher.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stylus-TheChronicVsDoggystyle_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stylus-TheChronicVsDoggystyle-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">Gangsta rap</a> has been criticized for its extreme lyrics, which are often accused of glamorizing gang violence and black-on-black crime. The Gangsta rappers responded that they were simply describing the realities of life in places such as <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton, California</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-SOC_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-SOC-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Davemusic_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davemusic-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Describing <i>Doggystyle</i> in 1993, Snoop Dogg likewise points to the album's realism, and the extent to which it is based on his personal experience. He said, "I can't rap about something I don't know. You'll never hear me rapping about no bachelor's degree. It's only what I know and that's that street life. It's all everyday life, reality."<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes-PopMusic_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-PopMusic-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Explaining his intentions, Snoop Dogg claims he feels he is a role model to many young black men, and that his songs are designed to relate to their concerns. "For little kids growing up in the ghettos," he said, "it's easy to get into the wrong types of things, especially gangbanging and selling drugs. I've seen what that was like, and I don't glorify it, but I don't preach. I bring it to them rather than have them go find out about it for themselves."<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes-PopMusic_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-PopMusic-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> He further explained the "dream" that he would pursue after making the album: "I'm going to try to eliminate the gang violence. I'll be on a mission for peace. I know I have a lot of power. I know if I say, 'Don't kill', niggas won't kill".<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes-PopMusic_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-PopMusic-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Doggystyle</i> was recorded in early 1993 at <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Studios</a>. It was produced in a style similar to <i>The Chronic</i>; some critics called it a "carbon copy".<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Snoop Dogg collaborated with two music groups, <a href="/wiki/213_(group)" title="213 (group)">213</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tha_Dogg_Pound" title="Tha Dogg Pound">Tha Dogg Pound</a>. Daz Dillinger, of the latter group, accused Dr. Dre of taking sole recognition for the songs and alleged he and <a href="/wiki/Warren_G" title="Warren G">Warren G</a> were uncredited on many tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-DPG-Eulogy_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DPG-Eulogy-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Death Row Records co-founder <a href="/wiki/Marion_%22Suge%22_Knight" title="Marion &quot;Suge&quot; Knight" class="mw-redirect">Marion "Suge" Knight</a> stated in 2013 that, "Daz pretty much did the whole album", and that credit was signed over to Dr. Dre for a fee.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Snoop Dogg said Dr. Dre was capable of making beats without the help of collaborators and addressed the issues with Warren G and Daz, stating "They made beats, Dre produced that record". He discussed the track "Ain't No Fun", mentioning that Daz and Warren G brought Dr. Dre the beat but "Dre took that muthafucka to the next level!"<sup id="cite_ref-DubCNN-Interview_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DubCNN-Interview-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Bruce Williams, closely affiliated with Dr. Dre, discussed the recording process during Dre's time at Death Row Records, stating:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Dre’s going to be the first one in the studio and the last one to leave. He’ll start messing with a beat. As the beat starts pumping, the guys start filtering in. Everybody will get their little drink and smoke in. Soon enough the beat starts to make a presence. You’ll look around the room and every cat that was a rapper – from Kurupt to Daz to Snoop – will grab a pen. They would start writing while Dre is making a beat so by the time he’s finished with the beat, they are ready to hit the booth and start spittin'. To see those young cats – they were all hungry and wanted to make something dope. The atmosphere that was there, you couldn’t be wack.<sup id="cite_ref-HipHopDX_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HipHopDX-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Williams said the album was never finished and because of the demand for the record, the distributors insisted the album be completed, otherwise they would cancel the album's orders. This resulted in Dr. Dre mixing the album and inserting the skits within 48&#160;hours, which enabled the album to be released.<sup id="cite_ref-HipHopDX_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HipHopDX-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Rolling Stone</i> writer Jonathan Gold described how Dr. Dre produced a beat from scratch to complete instrumental: "Dre may find something he likes from an old drum break, loop it and gradually replace each part with a better tom-tom sound, a kick-drum sound he adores, until the beat bears the same relationship to the original that the Incredible Hulk does to Bill Bixby".<sup id="cite_ref-RS-DayOfTheDre_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RS-DayOfTheDre-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Gold also described how the track progressed with other musicians adding to the song, stating "A bass player wanders in, unpacks his instrument and pops a funky two-note bass line over the beat, then leaves to watch CNN, though his two notes keep looping into infinity. A smiling guy in a striped jersey plays a nasty one-fingered melody on an old <a href="/wiki/Minimoog" title="Minimoog">Minimoog</a> synthesizer that's been obsolete since 1982, and Dre scratches in a sort of surfadelic munching noise, and then from his well-stocked <a href="/wiki/Akai_MPC60" title="Akai MPC60">Akai MPC60</a> samples comes a shriek, a spare piano chord, an ejaculation from the first <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">Beastie's</a> <a href="/wiki/Licensed_to_Ill" title="Licensed to Ill">record</a> -- "Let me clear my throat"—and the many-layered groove is happening, bumping, breathing, almost loud enough to see."<sup id="cite_ref-RS-DayOfTheDre_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RS-DayOfTheDre-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While recording <i>Doggystyle</i> with Dr. Dre in August 1993, Snoop Dogg was arrested in connection with the death of Phillip Woldermarian, a member of a rival gang who was shot and killed in a gang fight. According to the charges, the rapper's bodyguard, McKinley Lee, shot Woldermarian as Snoop Dogg drove the vehicle; the rapper claimed it was self-defense, alleging the victim was stalking Snoop Dogg. He spent most of 1995 preparing the case which went to trial in late 1995. He was cleared of all charges in February 1996 when he began work on his second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Tha_Doggfather" title="Tha Doggfather">Tha Doggfather</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title_significance">Title significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Title significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album's title alludes to the <a href="/wiki/Doggy_style" title="Doggy style">doggy style</a> sex position and is a reference to the musician's name. The artwork, which was done by artist Joe Cool, represents the themes covered in the album and the style of implementation of those ideas. Some critics believe the artwork portrays a woman merely as a hole to be filled by the man, which they believe adheres to the narcissistic and sexist lyrical themes Snoop Dogg covers.<sup id="cite_ref-Rap-BlackRage_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rap-BlackRage-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In this interpretation, the cover art and lyrics convey what they refer to as the self-indulgent "gangsta" lifestyle, drugs, cars, sex, and money.<sup id="cite_ref-Rap-BlackRage_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rap-BlackRage-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The artwork uses several quotes from the 1982 <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(funk_musician)" title="George Clinton (funk musician)" class="mw-redirect">George Clinton</a> single "<a href="/wiki/Atomic_Dog" title="Atomic Dog">Atomic Dog</a>". The quotes come from the dogs at the top of the brick wall on the album cover, which say, "Why must I feel like dat?", "Why must I chase da cat?" and "Nuttin' but da dogg in me".<sup id="cite_ref-MTV-Lyrics_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV-Lyrics-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is interesting to note that The Rolling Stones used a woman with feet and hands tied with bruised black eyes as the promotional poster for their Black and Blue LP (1975). The Stones offensive and violent prone image towards women was embraced by most critics because they were seen as the "bad boys" of Rock and Roll.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dre's handling of the production was praised by critics. <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Erlewine" title="Stephen Erlewine" class="mw-redirect">Stephen Erlewine</a> stated: "Dre realized that it wasn't time to push the limits of G-funk, and instead decided to deepen it musically, creating easy-rolling productions that have more layers than they appear". He added that the beats were "laid-back funky, continuing to resonate after many listens".<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> writer <a href="/wiki/Tour%C3%A9" title="Touré">Touré</a> noted "<i>The Chronic</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s slow, heavy beats were a sonic representation of angry depression as accurate as <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Cobain" title="Kurt Cobain">Cobain</a>'s feedback blasts; <i>Doggystyle</i> is leaner, with its high-tempo <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Hayes" title="Isaac Hayes">Isaac Hayes</a>- and <a href="/wiki/Curtis_Mayfield" title="Curtis Mayfield">Curtis Mayfield</a>-derived tracks". He went on to say that "Most of Dre's hooks and nearly all his beats refuse to linger, as if the songs themselves are nervous, fearful of exposure, restless to get offscreen."<sup id="cite_ref-RollingStone_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> magazine's David Browne mentioned that "The mix of samples and live music on Dre's latest, <i>The Chronic</i>, gave it texture and depth, and he continues his knob-turning growth on <i>Doggystyle</i>, fluidly weaving together a gaggle of background singers and rappers, quirky samples, his trademark horror-flick keyboard lines".<sup id="cite_ref-EW_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EW-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The Source magazine columinst wrote: "Dre's brand of G-funk may be common now, but it is still painstakingly well-produced".<sup id="cite_ref-TheSource_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheSource-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">Lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Snoop Dogg's lyrics were generally praised by critics, although they caused some controversy. He was acclaimed for the realism in his rhymes and his harmonious flow.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Allmusic's Stephen Erlewine commended Snoop Dogg, saying: "he's one of hip-hop's greatest vocal stylists with this record" and he "takes his time, playing with the flow of his words, giving his rhymes a nearly melodic eloquence. Snoop is something special, with unpredictable turns of phrase, evocative imagery, and a distinctive, addictive flow".<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine's Christopher John Farley noted "Snoop's rapping isn't flashy, but it is catchy" and stated "His relaxed vocal style is a perfect match for Dre's bass-heavy producing. Snoop's voice is lithe enough to snake its way around the big beats".<sup id="cite_ref-Time_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The ideas put forward through the lyrics include Snoop Dogg's adolescent urges, as he freely talks of casual sex, smoking marijuana and gunning down rival gang members. <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine remarked that the notions "are often unnecessarily graphic; at some points they're downright obscene" and that "the album would have been stronger if such misgivings about the criminal life, as well as Snoop's touches of introspection, had been applied to some of the cruder songs".<sup id="cite_ref-Time_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The album also covered gun play, drug dealing and pimping. <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> said that the lyrical concepts were delivered in "crudest, rudest terms".<sup id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Some critics said Snoop Dogg was "obsessed with being a 'G', a gangster, a lawbreaker who smokes dope and kills with impunity" and that his lyrics depict the black-on-black crime in the inner-cities.<sup id="cite_ref-Rap-BlackRage_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rap-BlackRage-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The lyrics involve many derogatory terms against woman, with expressions such as "bitches" and "ho's" being used throughout, which illustrates the feeling of sexism and oppression within American society.<sup id="cite_ref-Rap-BlackRage_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rap-BlackRage-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In certain tracks Snoop Dogg and Tha Dogg Pound casually speak of group sex, illustrating the demeaning of women.<sup id="cite_ref-Journal_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Journal-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Snoop Dogg's lyrics depict drugs, alcohol, sex, and money as methods of escape from oppression, but they also show an underside of the "gangsta" lifestyle and the results of following this lifestyle.<sup id="cite_ref-Rap-BlackRage_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rap-BlackRage-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The lyrics' violent representations, including murder and aggressive behaviour, have also generated controversy. C. DeLores Tucker of the National Political Congress of Black Women named gangsta rap "a profane and obscene glorification of murder and rape", which can be attributed to <i>Doggystyle</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Journal_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Journal-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>"Who Am I (What's My Name)?" was the first single released from the album on November 30, 1993. It peaked at number eight on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Singles_%26_Tracks" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks</a> charts, and reached number one on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Singles" title="Hot Rap Singles" class="mw-redirect">Hot Rap Singles</a>. It is largely recognised as the album's 'leading single', and the RIAA certified it <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">Gold</a> on February 8, 1994. It was certified Platinum later the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA-Singles_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-Singles-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> It reached number&#160;20 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> in 1994 and re-entered the chart in 2004, reaching number&#160;100.<sup id="cite_ref-EveryHit_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EveryHit-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ChartsPlus_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChartsPlus-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In terms of critical reception and the volume of sales, it is Snoop's most successful single to date. "Gin and Juice" was the second single released on January 15, 1994. Like the previous single, it was a hit on multiple charts. It reached number eight on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, number&#160;13 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Singles_%26_Tracks" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks</a>, number one on <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Singles" title="Hot Rap Singles" class="mw-redirect">Hot Rap Singles</a>, and number&#160;39 on the UK Singles Chart.<sup id="cite_ref-EveryHit_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EveryHit-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Singles_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Singles-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The RIAA certified it Platinum on April 6, 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA-Singles_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-Singles-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The song was nominated at the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards_of_1995" title="Grammy Awards of 1995" class="mw-redirect">1995 Grammy Awards</a> for <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Best Rap Solo Performance" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Solo Performance</a>, but lost to <a href="/wiki/Queen_Latifah" title="Queen Latifah">Queen Latifah</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/U.N.I.T.Y." title="U.N.I.T.Y.">U.N.I.T.Y.</a>". "Doggy Dogg World" was released as a Europe-only single during June 1994. Even though the single was not officially released in the U.S., it received some radio airplay which resulted in position 19 on the Rhythmic Top&#160;40 chart.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Singles_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Singles-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> A music video was produced for the single, which gained American video TV-play and won the 1994 <a href="/wiki/MTV_Video_Music_Award_for_Best_Rap_Video" title="MTV Video Music Award for Best Rap Video">MTV Video Music Award for Best Rap Video</a>. It reached number&#160;32 on the UK Singles Chart.<sup id="cite_ref-EveryHit_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EveryHit-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"Lodi Dodi" and "Murder Was the Case" were not official singles, but they received radio airplay and charted in <a href="/wiki/Rhythmic_Top_40" title="Rhythmic Top 40" class="mw-redirect">Rhythmic Top 40</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Singles_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Singles-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> An 18 minute music video was shot for the two songs, with an accompanying <i><a href="/wiki/Murder_Was_the_Case" title="Murder Was the Case">Murder Was the Case</a></i> soundtrack.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The video won the 1995 Video of the Year award at <a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a> Hip-Hop Music Awards. "Gin and Juice" was nominated at the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards_of_1995" title="Grammy Awards of 1995" class="mw-redirect">1995 Grammy Awards</a> for <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Best Rap Solo Performance" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Solo Performance</a>. A bonus track, "Gz Up, Hoes Down", was included in the album's first pressing, but not in later versions because of sample clearance issues. Snoop Dogg could not gain the rights to use the beats because the record company was not willing to pay license fees for using the samples.<sup id="cite_ref-SnoopDogg_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SnoopDogg-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> "Gz Up, Hoes Down" was later released on the Death Row compilation "<a href="/wiki/15_Years_on_Death_Row" title="15 Years on Death Row">15 Years on Death Row</a>". "Tha Next Episode" was listed on the cover, but not included in any pressing. It is considered the original material used for the 2000 Dr. Dre single "<a href="/wiki/The_Next_Episode" title="The Next Episode">The Next Episode</a>" but bears no resemblance to the later song. It was 4&#160;minutes and 36&#160;seconds (4:36) long.<sup id="cite_ref-DubCNN_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DubCNN-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> "Tha Next Episode" was later released on the Dr. Dre mixtape <i>Pretox</i> under the name "Chronic Unreleased Studio Session", but only 1:10 long. "Doggystyle" featuring <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(funk_musician)" title="George Clinton (funk musician)" class="mw-redirect">George Clinton</a> was a 5:26 long outtake from the album sessions. It is a singing melody with vocals dominating the song and it extensively samples "Oh I" by Funkadelic from their album "Electric Spanking of War Babies". Jewell &amp; The Brides of Funkenstein are featured on the chorus.<sup id="cite_ref-DubCNN_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DubCNN-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The song was released on "Death Row: The Lost Sessions Vol. 1" amongst other songs recorded by Snoop Doggy Dogg during his tenure at Death Row.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album has sold five million copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT-Snoop_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-Snoop-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> It was certified four times <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> on May 31, 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA-Doggystyle_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-Doggystyle-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> It is Snoop Dogg's most successful album; his following albums were certified single or double platinum.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA-SnoopDogg_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-SnoopDogg-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Doggystyle</i> first appeared on music charts in 1993, peaking on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> and <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> at number one.<sup id="cite_ref-US200_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US200-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USR.26BAl_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USR.26BAl-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> It re-peaked at number one on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 in January 1994, when it was already certified three times platinum by the RIAA.<sup id="cite_ref-Variety_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Variety-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The record was mildly successful in Europe, reaching number&#160;18 in Sweden, number&#160;21 in Germany and number&#160;35 in Austria. It also peaked at number&#160;25 on the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_New_Zealand" title="Recording Industry Association of New Zealand" class="mw-redirect">Recording Industry Association of New Zealand</a> album chart.<sup id="cite_ref-AUT_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AUT-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GER_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GER-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NZ_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NZ-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SWE_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SWE-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> At the end of 1994, the album was number three on the <i>Billboard</i> Year-End Top Albums Chart and number one on the <i>Billboard</i> Year-End Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums Chart.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> It re-entered the charts in 2003, peaking on the Ireland Albums Top&#160;75 at number&#160;70.<sup id="cite_ref-IRL_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRL-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Doggystyle</i> was generally praised by critics. <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> writer <a href="/wiki/Tour%C3%A9" title="Touré">Touré</a> mentioned "<i>Doggystyle</i> is filled with verbal and vocal feats that meet its high expectations. It speeds through 55&#160;minutes of constant talk as if on a suicide hot line".<sup id="cite_ref-RollingStone_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> David Browne of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> noted "It is the most limber, low-rider gangsta album to date" and went on to say "<i>Doggystyle</i> is a grim, bleak-faced record. It's set in a dead-end, no-tomorrow world of cheap thrills".<sup id="cite_ref-EW_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EW-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Stephen Thomas Erlewine of Allmusic stated "<i>Doggystyle</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i> stand proudly together as the twin pinnacles of <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast G-funk hip-hop</a> of the early '90s"<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Stylus</i> magazine presented "The Chronic vs. Doggystyle" article, and stated a strong point of <i>Doggystyle</i> compared to Dre's album was its follow-up singles and that "some of the album tracks are more famous than the singles".<sup id="cite_ref-Stylus-TheChronicVsDoggystyle_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stylus-TheChronicVsDoggystyle-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_magazine" title="Vibe magazine" class="mw-redirect">Vibe</a></i> magazine expressed that "Snoop is no ordinary gangsta; that's impossible for an artist this playful. On his debut, with Dre riding shotgun anthems abound as often as gin-soaked debauchery".<sup id="cite_ref-Vibe_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibe-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a> magazine gave the album a 4/5 mic rating. It said Snoop Dogg emerged as a rapper who lived up to all the advance hype which came from his work on <i>The Chronic</i>, and discussed songs on the record, stating "If 'Murder Was The Case' is a stroke of near genius, then 'Lodi Dodi' is an example of total genius."<sup id="cite_ref-TheSource_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheSource-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>NME</i> magazine called the lead single "a pinnacle he conquered effortlessly" and went on to name the record a "benchmark album".<sup id="cite_ref-NME-DGITBS_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NME-DGITBS-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album also received some negative criticism. Erlewine of Allmusic mentioned the album did not "surprise or offer anything that wasn't already on The Chronic".<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Christopher John Farley noted Snoop Dogg had little examination over his emotions and feelings.<sup id="cite_ref-Time_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> David Browne spoke of "Ain't No Fun", stating it was an example of how "musically artful, yet lyrically repellent, this album can be" and went on to say "It's easy to be impressed one moment and appalled the next".<sup id="cite_ref-EW_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EW-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Renowned rock critic <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave the album a dud rating,<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> which signifies "a bad record whose details rarely merit further thought. At the upper level it may merely be overrated, disappointing, or dull. Down below it may be contemptible."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)" class="mw-redirect">Q</a>'</i>s <a href="/wiki/Danny_Kelly_(journalist)" title="Danny Kelly (journalist)">Danny Kelly</a> observed: "Snoop Dogg's record is more or less a 19-track homage to/gleeful rip off of <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(musician)" title="George Clinton (musician)">George Clinton</a>'s '<a href="/wiki/Atomic_Dog" title="Atomic Dog">Atomic Dog</a>'… It's inclined to become a touch unimaginative; a tad, let's be honest, dull… And the sleeve competes with <a href="/wiki/The_Waterboys" title="The Waterboys">The Waterboys</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Dream_Harder" title="Dream Harder">Dream Harder</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Billy_Joel" title="Billy Joel">Billy Joel</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/River_of_Dreams" title="River of Dreams">River of Dreams</a></i> as the worst attached to a recent release."<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the initial mixed criticism, critical perception of the album later improved, as <i>Doggystyle</i> has earned several accolades and rankings on critics' "best album" lists.<sup id="cite_ref-AcclaimedMusic_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AcclaimedMusic-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> A review of the album's reissue upped <i>Q'</i>s rating from three to four stars out of five. "A modern classic," observed reviewer Tom Doyle.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> The Source magazine later gave the album a classic five-mic rating.<sup id="cite_ref-Source_2002_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Source_2002-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hip-hop_music">Hip-hop music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hip-hop music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Doggystyle</i> is seen by many hip hop pundits as a "classic" and an "essential" album.<sup id="cite_ref-About_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> It is credited with defining <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast hip hop</a>; shifting the emphasis to more melodious, synth-driven, and funk-induced beats. About.com stated during the period the album was released, "Gangsta rap never sounded so sweet." The album is credited for further establishment of the slurred "lazy drawl" that sacrificed lyrical complexity for clarity and <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythmic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cadence_(music)" title="Cadence (music)">cadence</a> on <i>Doggystyle</i> and <i>The Chronic</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The album is considered as one of the first G-funk albums the style of which many rappers duplicated in later years.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hip-hop_culture">Hip-hop culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hip-hop culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has been suggested by some writers and publications that <i>Doggystyle</i> has considerably affected African-American culture. Some publications have held the rap genre responsible for social problems such as sexual violence and sexism, which has been blamed on Snoop Dogg and other rappers for calling their controversial lyrics "keeping it real."<sup id="cite_ref-PimpsUpHosDown_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PimpsUpHosDown-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> The problems of sexual violence and sexism are attributed to lyrics degrading women such as "bitches" and "ho's," which some believe have influenced black males.<sup id="cite_ref-Rap-BlackRage_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rap-BlackRage-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AACulture_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AACulture-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> Snoop Dogg and other hip hop artists, including <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Eazy_E" title="Eazy E" class="mw-redirect">Eazy E</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a> (due to their success) and <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a>, have been held accountable for developing the gangsta rap form; a genre which articulated the rage of the urban underclass and its sense of intense oppression and defiant rebellion,<sup id="cite_ref-Rap-BlackRage_16-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rap-BlackRage-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> which has been attained through the ability to communicate free of censorship, and has allowed hip hop culture to become a dominant style and ethos throughout the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Rap-BlackRage_16-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rap-BlackRage-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mariah_Carey" title="Mariah Carey">Mariah Carey</a> sampled the song "Ain't No Fun (If the Homies Can't Have None)" in her 1999 album <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_(Mariah_Carey_album)" title="Rainbow (Mariah Carey album)">Rainbow</a> for the remix of <a href="/wiki/Heartbreaker_(Mariah_Carey_song)" title="Heartbreaker (Mariah Carey song)">Heartbreaker</a> which featured <a href="/wiki/Missy_Elliott" title="Missy Elliott">Missy Elliott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Da_Brat" title="Da Brat">Da Brat</a>.</p>
<p>The writers of <i>Enculturation</i>, Steven Best and Douglas Kellner, have noted that Snoop Dogg and other rappers only condemn violence when it is directed against them, otherwise "they celebrate it, internalize it, and embrace it as an ethos and means of self expression," which some believe has an effect on the black-on-black crime.<sup id="cite_ref-Rap-BlackRage_16-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rap-BlackRage-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The release of music videos from <i>Doggystyle</i> and <i>The Chronic</i> has enabled the artists to add visual illustrations to their lyrics, which generally involve Dr. Dre and Snoop Dogg driving around <a href="/wiki/South_Central,_Los_Angeles" title="South Central, Los Angeles" class="mw-redirect">South Central, Los Angeles</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Lowrider" title="Lowrider">lowrider</a> (a vehicle with lowered suspension). This imagery of the "gangsta lifestyle" is thought to have influenced young black males into trying to live the same lifestyle and it is also noted by T. Denean, writer of <i>Pimps Up, Ho's Down: Hip Hop's Hold on Young Black Women</i>, that the videos highlight the representation of class, race and Black masculinity within contemporary urban America.<sup id="cite_ref-AmIBlackEnoughp80_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AmIBlackEnoughp80-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_work">Subsequent work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Subsequent work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Doggystyle</i> is generally considered Snoop Dogg's best record, in addition to being his highest charting and best-selling album.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-Snoop_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-Snoop-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-US200_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US200-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USR.26BAl_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USR.26BAl-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> It differs from following albums as his later work featured production from multiple individuals, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Neptunes" title="The Neptunes">The Neptunes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timbaland" title="Timbaland">Timbaland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daz_Dillinger" title="Daz Dillinger">Daz Dillinger</a>, with reduced input from Dr. Dre, which shows a shift from G-funk production.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Snoop Dogg's follow-up album, <i>Tha Doggfather</i> (1996), did not involve Dr. Dre, as he left Death Row Records. As a result, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Pooh" title="DJ Pooh">DJ Pooh</a> was the main beat-maker for the album. <i>Tha Doggfather</i> followed the methods of a G-funk record and initially sold well, but received mixed reviews and failed to produce a major hit single.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1998, Snoop Dogg left Death Row and joined <a href="/wiki/No_Limit_Records" title="No Limit Records">No Limit Records</a>, changing his stage name from Snoop Doggy Dogg to Snoop Dogg. During his tenure at the label, he continued several of the themes from <i>Doggystyle</i> with follow-ups to earlier songs, such as "Gin &amp; Juice II" (1998) and "<a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg_(What%27s_My_Name_Pt._2)" title="Snoop Dogg (What's My Name Pt. 2)">Snoop Dogg (What's My Name II)</a>" (2000).<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-R.26G_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-R.26G-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Subsequent studio albums such as <i><a href="/wiki/Paid_tha_Cost_to_Be_da_Boss" title="Paid tha Cost to Be da Boss">Paid tha Cost to Be da Boss</a></i> (2002) and <i><a href="/wiki/R%26G_(Rhythm_%26_Gangsta):_The_Masterpiece" title="R&amp;G (Rhythm &amp; Gangsta): The Masterpiece">R&amp;G (Rhythm &amp; Gangsta): The Masterpiece</a></i> (2004) exhibited a more mainstream, pop-oriented theme with new sounds, but remained "hardcore throughout" and featured "plenty of street and commercial appeal".<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-R.26G_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-R.26G-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> These releases included three hit singles, "<a href="/wiki/Beautiful_(Snoop_Dogg_song)" title="Beautiful (Snoop Dogg song)">Beautiful</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Drop_It_Like_It%27s_Hot" title="Drop It Like It's Hot">Drop It Like It's Hot</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Signs_(Snoop_Dogg_song)" title="Signs (Snoop Dogg song)">Signs</a>". Snoop Dogg was credited for returning to his G-funk roots in 2006, which was established with his eighth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Tha_Blue_Carpet_Treatment" title="Tha Blue Carpet Treatment">Tha Blue Carpet Treatment</a></i> (2006).<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was noted for being a "hard and very G-Funk record".<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>"G'z &amp; Hustlas contains uncredited appearances from <a href="/wiki/Bow_Wow_(rapper)" title="Bow Wow (rapper)">Lil' Bow Wow</a>.</li>
<li>"Gz Up, Hoes Down" was omitted on all but the original pressings, due to sample and clearance issues. On the original release's back cover, it is erroneously listed after "Pump Pump" instead of immediately before.</li>
<li>The original pressings of the album which contain "Gz Up, Hoes Down" list "W Balls" separately on the album's artwork. They also include an erroneous final listing for "Tha Next Episode", a track not included on the album, for a total of a total of sixteen tracks (out of nineteen featured). Later pressings of the album, which remove "Gz Up, Hoes Down", do not list either of these tracks on the album's artwork, although "W Balls" is featured.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cut_Tracks">Cut Tracks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doggystyle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cut Tracks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>"Gz Up, Hoez Down" (featuring Hug) - omitted on all but the original pressings, due to sample and clearance issues.</li>
<li>"Tha Next Episode" (featuring <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>) - listed on the tracklisting provided to retailers before the album's release, but does not feature on any pressings of the album. The instrumental was later used for <a href="/wiki/Warren_G" title="Warren G">Warren G</a>'s track "Runnin' Wit No Breaks" from his album, <i><a href="/wiki/Regulate...G_Funk_Era" title="Regulate...G Funk Era">Regulate...G Funk Era</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-djluicidal.com_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-djluicidal.com-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> The pair later recorded a track entitled "The Next Episode" for Dre's second studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/2001_(Dr._Dre_album)" title="2001 (Dr. Dre album)">2001</a></i>, which is completely different from the original.</li>
<li>"Doggystyle" (featuring <a href="/wiki/Jewell_(singer)" title="Jewell (singer)">Jewell</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(funk_musician)" title="George Clinton (funk musician)" class="mw-redirect">George Clinton</a>) - recorded during the album sessions, remaining unreleased until its inclusion on the compilation album <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Row:_The_Lost_Sessions_Vol._1" title="Death Row: The Lost Sessions Vol. 1">Death Row: The Lost Sessions Vol. 1</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-djluicidal.com_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-djluicidal.com-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>"The Root of All Evil (Outro)" (featuring <a href="/wiki/Teena_Marie" title="Teena Marie">Teena Marie</a>) - recorded during the album sessions, remaining unreleased until its inclusion on the compilation album <i>Death Row: The Lost Sessions Vol. 1</i>. The instrumental was later used for the remix of "<a href="/wiki/California_Love" title="California Love">California Love</a>", by <a href="/wiki/2Pac" title="2Pac" class="mw-redirect">2Pac</a> featuring <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a></li>
<li>"Every Single Day" (featuring <a href="/wiki/Kurupt" title="Kurupt">Kurupt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a>) - recorded during the album sessions, remaining unreleased until an alternate version was released on <i><a href="/wiki/Tha_Dogg_Pound" title="Tha Dogg Pound">Tha Dogg Pound</a></i> compilation album <i><a href="/wiki/2002_(Tha_Dogg_Pound_album)" title="2002 (Tha Dogg Pound album)">2002</a></i></li>
</ul>
