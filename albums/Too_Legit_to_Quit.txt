<p><i><b>Too Legit To Quit</b></i> is <a href="/wiki/MC_Hammer" title="MC Hammer">Hammer</a>'s fourth record and third official full-length studio album, released on October 29, 1991. While the album wasn't as successful as <i><a href="/wiki/Please_Hammer,_Don%27t_Hurt_%27Em" title="Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em">Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em</a></i>, it nevertheless proved successful, peaking in the Top 5 of the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a>, as well as going <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">triple platinum</a>.</p>
<p>A video for <a href="/wiki/2_Legit_2_Quit" title="2 Legit 2 Quit">the title song</a> was also produced, featuring many celebrity appearances. It was ranked one of the most <a href="/wiki/List_of_most_expensive_music_videos" title="List of most expensive music videos">expensive videos</a> ever produced.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Music videos were produced for all four tracks listed below with related articles. Each single released also <a href="/wiki/MC_Hammer_discography#Singles" title="MC Hammer discography">charted</a>.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Compilation_album" title="Compilation album">compilation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music videos</a> from this album were released on <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> (1992) and <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> (2002) called <i>M.C. Hammer: 2 Legit - The Videos</i> (102 minutes).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion_and_reception">Promotion and reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Too_Legit_to_Quit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Promotion and reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After dropping the "M.C." from his stage name, Hammer released this album (also produced by <a href="/wiki/Felton_Pilate" title="Felton Pilate">Felton Pilate</a>), answering his critics within certain songs from the album. Sales were strong (over five million copies)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> with the title track being the biggest hit. Another hit came soon after, with "<a href="/wiki/The_Addams_Family_Theme#.22Addams_Groove.22" title="The Addams Family Theme">Addams Groove</a>" (which appeared on both <i><a href="/wiki/The_Addams_Family_(film)" title="The Addams Family (film)">The Addams Family</a></i> <a href="/wiki/The_Addams_Family_(film)#Songs" title="The Addams Family (film)">motion picture soundtrack</a> and the vinyl and cassette versions of <i>2 Legit 2 Quit</i>), which reached No. 7 in the U.S. and No. 4 in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>. His video for the song appeared after the movie.</p>
<p>Hammer set out on a <a href="/wiki/Concert_tour#Concert_tour" title="Concert tour" class="mw-redirect">tour</a> for this album, but the stage show had become as lavish as his lifestyle; loaded with singers, dancers and backup musicians, the supporting concert tour was too expensive for the album's sales to finance, and it was canceled partway through.<sup id="cite_ref-zvents_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zvents-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1992, <a href="/wiki/Boyz_II_Men" title="Boyz II Men">Boyz II Men</a> joined Hammer's high-profile <i>2 Legit 2 Quit</i> tour as an <a href="/wiki/Opening_act" title="Opening act">opening act</a>. While traveling the country, their tour manager <a href="/wiki/Khalil_Roundtree" title="Khalil Roundtree" class="mw-redirect">Khalil Roundtree</a> was murdered in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, and the group's future performances of "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_So_Hard_to_Say_Goodbye_to_Yesterday" title="It's So Hard to Say Goodbye to Yesterday">It's So Hard to Say Goodbye to Yesterday</a>" were dedicated to him. As a result of this unfortunate experience, the song would help advance their success.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The song proved to be successful in the U.S., peaking at the Top 10 of the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a>, at No. 5. Despite the album's <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">multi-platinum certification</a>, the sales were one-third of <i>Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em</i>.</p>
<p>Prior to Hammer's next album, (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Funky_Headhunter" title="The Funky Headhunter">The Funky Headhunter</a></i>), rumors from critics and fans began claiming Hammer had quit the music/entertainment business and had suffered a financial downfall (since a couple of years had passed in between the two records), which Hammer denied at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The introduction of the extended-play music video for "<a href="/wiki/2_Legit_2_Quit" title="2 Legit 2 Quit">2 Legit 2 Quit</a>" addressed the fact that Hammer must have quit (with <a href="/wiki/Jim_Belushi" title="Jim Belushi">Jim Belushi</a> as a newscaster announcing it in a comical way) until Hammer shows up late to a concert dancing and rapping to the hit song.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_video">Music video</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Too_Legit_to_Quit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music video">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hand motions used within the "2 Legit 2 Quit" <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> became very popular as was the <a href="/wiki/Catchphrase" title="Catchphrase">catchphrase</a> itself.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At the end of the "2 Legit 2 Quit" video, after <a href="/wiki/James_Brown" title="James Brown">James Brown</a> enlists Hammer to obtain the glove of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>, a silver-white sequined glove is shown on the hand of a Jackson look-a-like doing the "2 Legit 2 Quit" <a href="/wiki/Hand_gesture" title="Hand gesture" class="mw-redirect">hand gesture</a>. It was a reference to Hammer wanting to challenge Jackson to a dance-off for rights to the famous glove, which is also referenced on the album.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>M.C. Hammer appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wendy_Williams_Show" title="The Wendy Williams Show">The Wendy Williams Show</a></i> (July 27, 2009) and went on to tell a story about a phone call he received from <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>, regarding the portion of the "2 Legit 2 Quit" video that included a fake Jackson, giving his approval and inclusion of it. He explained how Jackson had seen the video and liked it, and both expressed they were a fan of each other.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Hammer and Jackson would later appear, speak and perform at the funeral service for <a href="/wiki/James_Brown" title="James Brown">James Brown</a> in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The cassette tape version of this album is considerably longer, with the addition of four songs (two of which are remixes). After "Find Yourself a Friend" there follows "Rollin' On (Oaktown Style)" (5:50). After "Gaining Momentum" is "Burn It Up" (4:28).
