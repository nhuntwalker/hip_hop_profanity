<p><i><b>Ghetto D</b></i> is the sixth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American <a href="/wiki/Rapper" title="Rapper" class="mw-redirect">rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/Master_P" title="Master P">Master P</a>, released on September 2, 1997<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> on <a href="/wiki/No_Limit_Records" title="No Limit Records">No Limit Records</a>.In the first week it sold 260,000 copies in the US.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghetto_D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally slated to be titled as <i>Ghetto Dope</i>, the name was shortened to the current title before the release due to the drug reference in the aforementioned title. The album would be one of Master P's biggest albums.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghetto_D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original album cover, which depicted a crack addict sitting on a curb and smoking from a glass pipe, was recalled from store shelves. It was promptly replaced by the collage style cover.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chart_performance">Chart performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghetto_D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chart performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album became the biggest-selling of Master P's career, peaking at #1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a> and <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> charts. It was mainly on the strength of the three singles released; "I Miss My Homies" (US #11), "Make 'Em Say Uhh!" (US #7), and "Burbons and Lacs" (US #3) all became hit singles in the years 1997 and 1998. "Gangstas Need Love" samples <a href="/wiki/Diana_Ross" title="Diana Ross">Diana Ross</a>'s hit single "<a href="/wiki/Missing_You_(Diana_Ross_song)" title="Missing You (Diana Ross song)">Missing You</a>", while "I Miss My Homies" samples <a href="/wiki/The_O%27Jays" title="The O'Jays">The O'Jays</a>' song "Brandy" from the album <i><a href="/wiki/So_Full_of_Love" title="So Full of Love">So Full of Love</a></i>. "<a href="/wiki/Make_%27Em_Say_Uhh!" title="Make 'Em Say Uhh!">Make 'Em Say Uhh!</a>", is ranked at #36 on <a href="/wiki/Blender_(magazine)" title="Blender (magazine)">Blender</a>'s list of the "50 Worst Songs Ever", <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.blender.com/lists/61412/the50worstsongseverwatchlistenandcringe.html?p=3">[1]</a> and at #60 on the list of the 100 Worst Songs Ever by Matthew Wilkening of <a href="/wiki/AOL_Radio" title="AOL Radio">AOL Radio</a>. <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.aolradioblog.com/2010/09/11/100-worst-songs-ever-part-three-of-five/">[2]</a> In 2008, it ranked #94 on VH1's 100 Greatest Songs of Hip Hop. "Here We Go", featuring <a href="/wiki/Fiend_(rapper)" title="Fiend (rapper)">Fiend</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mystikal" title="Mystikal">Mystikal</a>, was a b-side, released on the "<a href="/wiki/I_Miss_My_Homies" title="I Miss My Homies">I Miss My Homies</a>" single. The album was certified 3x Platinum on August 4, 2006, with 3,185,221 copies sold, according to <a href="/wiki/SoundScan" title="SoundScan" class="mw-redirect">SoundScan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
