<p><i><b>To the Extreme</b></i> is the major label debut <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> of American rapper <a href="/wiki/Vanilla_Ice" title="Vanilla Ice">Robert "Vanilla Ice" Van Winkle</a>. Released in 1990, it is Van Winkle's best selling album. The album was initially released in 1989 by <a href="/wiki/Independent_music" title="Independent music">independent</a> <a href="/wiki/Record_label" title="Record label">record label</a> <a href="/wiki/Ichiban_Records" title="Ichiban Records">Ichiban Records</a> under the title <i>Hooked</i>. Van Winkle signed to <a href="/wiki/SBK_Records" title="SBK Records">SBK Records</a>, who reissued the album under its current title. The album contains Van Winkle's most successful singles, "<a href="/wiki/Ice_Ice_Baby" title="Ice Ice Baby">Ice Ice Baby</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Play_That_Funky_Music" title="Play That Funky Music">Play That Funky Music</a>". Although reviews of the album were mixed, <i>To the Extreme</i> spent 16 weeks at the top of the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard 200</i></a>, and sold 15 million copies worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=To_the_Extreme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1989, Robert Van Winkle, better known as Vanilla Ice, released an early version of <i>To the Extreme</i> under the title <i>Hooked</i> on <a href="/wiki/Ichiban_Records" title="Ichiban Records">Ichiban Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Price_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Play_That_Funky_Music" title="Play That Funky Music">Play That Funky Music</a>" was released as the album's first single, with "<a href="/wiki/Ice_Ice_Baby" title="Ice Ice Baby">Ice Ice Baby</a>" appearing as the <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">B-side</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Westfahl_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Westfahl-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wartofsky_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wartofsky-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/12-inch_single" title="12-inch single">12-inch single</a> featured the <a href="/wiki/Radio_edit" title="Radio edit">radio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Instrumental" title="Instrumental">instrumental</a> and <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a> versions of "Play That Funky Music" and the radio version and "Miami Drop" <a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remix</a> of "Ice Ice Baby".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> When a <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">disc jockey</a> played "Ice Ice Baby" instead of the single's A-side, the song gained more success than "Play That Funky Music".<sup id="cite_ref-Westfahl_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Westfahl-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> for "Ice Ice Baby" was produced for $8000.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The video was financed by Van Winkle's manager, Tommy Quon, and shot on the roof of a warehouse in <a href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas" title="Dallas, Texas" class="mw-redirect">Dallas, Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Heavy airplay of the video by <a href="/wiki/The_Box_(US_TV_channel)" title="The Box (US TV channel)" class="mw-redirect">The Box</a> while Van Winkle was still unknown increased public interest in the song.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1990, Van Winkle signed to <a href="/wiki/SBK_Records" title="SBK Records">SBK Records</a>, who reissued <i>Hooked</i> under the title <i>To the Extreme</i>. The reissue contained new artwork and music.<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> "Ice Ice Baby" was given its own single, released in 1990 by <a href="/wiki/SBK_Records" title="SBK Records">SBK Records</a> in the United States, and <a href="/wiki/EMI_Records" title="EMI Records">EMI Records</a> in the United Kingdom. The SBK single contained the "Miami Drop", instrumental and radio mixes of "Ice Ice Baby" and the album version of "It's A Party".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The EMI single contained the club and radio mixes of the song, and the shortened radio edit.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=To_the_Extreme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Van Winkle wrote "Ice Ice Baby" at the age of 16, basing its lyrics upon the <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a> area in which he was raised.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The lyrics describe a <a href="/wiki/Drive-by_shooting" title="Drive-by shooting">drive-by shooting</a> and Van Winkle's rhyming skills.<sup id="cite_ref-PerulloFenn_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PerulloFenn-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The chorus of "Ice Ice Baby" originates from the signature chant of the national <a href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African American</a> fraternity <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Phi_Alpha" title="Alpha Phi Alpha">Alpha Phi Alpha</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The song's hook <a href="/wiki/Sample_(music)" title="Sample (music)" class="mw-redirect">samples</a> the <a href="/wiki/Bass_guitar" title="Bass guitar">bassline</a> of the 1981 song "<a href="/wiki/Under_Pressure" title="Under Pressure">Under Pressure</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hess_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mercury" title="Freddie Mercury">Freddie Mercury</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a> did not receive credit or royalties for the sample.<sup id="cite_ref-Westfahl_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Westfahl-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In a 1990 interview, Van Winkle said the two melodies were slightly different because he had added an additional note. In later interviews, Van Winkle readily admitted he sampled the song and claimed his 1990 statement was a joke; others, however, suggested he had been serious.<sup id="cite_ref-Stillman_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stillman-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Van Winkle later paid Mercury and Bowie, who have since been given songwriting credit for the sample.<sup id="cite_ref-Stillman_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stillman-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The stylistic origins of "Rosta Man" are based upon <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a> <a href="/wiki/Deejay_(Jamaican)" title="Deejay (Jamaican)">toasting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>To the Extreme</i> became the fastest selling <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> album of all time,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> peaking at #1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Billboard_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The album spent 16 weeks at the top of the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_charts" title="Billboard charts">charts</a>, and seven million copies were shipped across the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>To the Extreme</i> was the best selling hip hop album up until that time.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> "Ice Ice Baby" has been credited for helping diversify hip hop by introducing it to a mainstream, white audience.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Reviews of <i>To the Extreme</i> were mixed. <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> reviewer Dom Lombardo gave the album a B, calling the album "so consistent in its borrowings that it could be a parody, if it weren't for its total absence of wit",<sup id="cite_ref-Udovitch_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Udovitch-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> but concluding that "if there's about a two-to-one ratio of winners [...] to clunkers, that's not the worst track record for a debut album."<sup id="cite_ref-Udovitch_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Udovitch-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Udovitch cited "Ice Ice Baby", "Play That Funky Music", "Dancin'" and "It's a Party" as the album's highlights.<sup id="cite_ref-Udovitch_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Udovitch-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave the album a C- rating, writing that Van Winkle's "suave sexism, fashionably male supremacist rather than dangerously obscene, is no worse than his suave beats".<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> reviewer Steve Huey wrote that "Ice's mic technique is actually stronger and more nimble than <a href="/wiki/MC_Hammer" title="MC Hammer">MC Hammer</a>'s, and he really tries earnestly to show off the skills he does have. Unfortunately, even if he can keep a mid-tempo pace, his flow is rhythmically stiff, and his voice has an odd timbre; plus, he never seems sure of the proper accent to adopt. He's able to overcome those flaws somewhat in isolated moments, but they become all too apparent over the course of an entire album."<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After audiences began to view Van Winkle as a novelty act, his popularity began to decline. Van Winkle would later regain some success, attracting a new audience outside of the mainstream audience that had formerly accepted him, and then rejected him.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
