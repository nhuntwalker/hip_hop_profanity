<p><i><b>Aquemini</b></i> is the third studio album by American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> duo <a href="/wiki/Outkast" title="Outkast">Outkast</a>, released on September 29, 1998 through <a href="/wiki/LaFace_Records" title="LaFace Records">LaFace Records</a>. The title is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of the two performers' <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">Zodiac</a> signs: <a href="/wiki/Aquarius_(astrology)" title="Aquarius (astrology)">Aquarius</a> (<a href="/wiki/Big_Boi" title="Big Boi">Big Boi</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Gemini_(astrology)" title="Gemini (astrology)">Gemini</a> (<a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_3000" title="André 3000">André 3000</a>), which is indicative of the album's recurring theme of the differing personalities of the two members. The group recorded the majority of the album in <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Brown" title="Bobby Brown">Bobby Brown</a>'s Bosstown Recording Studios in Atlanta, Georgia.</p>
<p>Released as the follow-up to the duo's 1996 album <i><a href="/wiki/ATLiens" title="ATLiens">ATLiens</a></i>, <i>Aquemini</i> expands on the previous record's outer space-inspired compositions by incorporating live instrumentation. The commercial success of <i>ATLiens</i> allowed for more creative freedom for the group, which led to the members self-producing the majority of the tracks. The band employed a large number of musicians for the album, who frequently entered and exited the studio throughout the recording process and had a major influence on the writing development of the songs on <i>Aquemini</i>. Lyrically, Outkast explores various themes including human nature, addiction, and interpersonal relationships.</p>
<p>The album was certified <a href="/wiki/Platinum_record" title="Platinum record" class="mw-redirect">platinum</a> in November 1998, only two months after its release, and was certified <a href="/wiki/Double_platinum" title="Double platinum" class="mw-redirect">double platinum</a> on July 2, 1999 by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>. <i>Aquemini</i> peaked at number two on both the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop</a> charts. Four of the album's tracks had already or would later become <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">singles</a>, although some were limited (promotional) releases and not available commercially. The record also received nearly universal acclaim from music critics, who praised the album's musicality and unique lyrical themes. It was ranked as number 500 in the book version of <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a>'s <a href="/wiki/500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">500 Greatest Albums of All Time</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aquemini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1994, the American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a> duo <a href="/wiki/Outkast" title="Outkast">OutKast</a> released their debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik" title="Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik">Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik</a></i>, which was recorded when members <a href="/wiki/Big_Boi" title="Big Boi">Big Boi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_3000" title="André 3000">André 3000</a> were eighteen years old. Bolstered by the success of the single "<a href="/wiki/Player%27s_Ball" title="Player's Ball">Player's Ball</a>", the record established OutKast as prominent figures in the Southern hip hop scene.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson3233_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson3233-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> After the album was certified platinum, LaFace Records gave OutKast more creative control and advanced money for their 1996 follow-up album <i>ATLiens</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> On <i>ATLiens</i>, André 3000 and Big Boi abandoned the "hard-partying playa characters" of their debut album in favor of more spacey, futuristic personas, and produced many of the songs on their own for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusicrev_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicrev-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sarig140_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig140-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Critics praised the group's maturing musical style on the record, which debuted at number two on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart and sold nearly 350,000 copies in its first two weeks of release.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The single "<a href="/wiki/Elevators_(Me_%26_You)" title="Elevators (Me &amp; You)">Elevators (Me &amp; You)</a>" reached number 12 and spent 20 weeks on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_100" title="Hot 100" class="mw-redirect"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-hot100_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hot100-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After the release of <i>ATLiens</i>, André 3000 entered a relationship with R&amp;B singer <a href="/wiki/Erykah_Badu" title="Erykah Badu">Erykah Badu</a>, who was part of a burgeoning movement in the urban music scene described as "more bohemian than ghetto".<sup id="cite_ref-Sarig171_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarig171-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> André 3000 adopted a more flamboyant style of dress during performances in promotion of <i>ATLiens</i> that included large glasses, blond wigs, and marching band uniforms. Badu gave birth to his first child in November 1997, which presented new artistic inspiration for the rapper.<sup id="cite_ref-Sarig171_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarig171-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aquemini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hitsville_USA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Hitsville_USA.jpg/280px-Hitsville_USA.jpg" width="280" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Hitsville_USA.jpg/420px-Hitsville_USA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Hitsville_USA.jpg/560px-Hitsville_USA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1152" data-file-height="864" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hitsville_USA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.24wmf6/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Producer Neal H. Pogue compared the recording of <i>Aquemini</i>, with its wide variety of live musicians, to that of 1960s <a href="/wiki/Motown_Records" title="Motown Records" class="mw-redirect">Motown Records</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hitsville_U.S.A." title="Hitsville U.S.A.">former headquarters</a> pictured).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Due to Outkast's newfound commercial success and higher budget for the album, the group enjoyed a more relaxed schedule and "could really just live" at the studio.<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The duo and studio musicians "camped out" in the studio for weeks straight, with Big Boi noting, "It usually takes us two to three years to make a record because we take our time; we ain't giving you that fast food, trying to meet a deadline. It ain't done until it's done sonically."<sup id="cite_ref-remembers_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-remembers-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> For <i>Aquemini</i>, the duo utilized live instrumentation and improvisation, bringing a baby grand <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> into the studio and hiring musicians who played "everything from stoner <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a> to <a href="/wiki/Prog_rock" title="Prog rock" class="mw-redirect">prog rock</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Westhoff110_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Westhoff110-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Producer Neal H. Pogue recalled, "That was the beauty of making all those records - having musicians come in and out. It was almost like a <a href="/wiki/Motown_Records" title="Motown Records" class="mw-redirect">Motown</a>, that's what we had. Or like a <a href="/wiki/Stax_Records" title="Stax Records">Stax Records</a> thing. That's what I loved about it. It brought back that whole feeling of making records. It was organic."<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Much of the music on <i>Aquemini</i> was formulated during jam sessions, in which one musician would begin with creating a chord pattern and the rest would incorporate their instruments following that sequence. After the musical aspects of a song were developed, André 3000 and Big Boi would then create lyrics that they felt would fit with the tone of the song.<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>For the record, Big Boi undertook the responsibility of crafting the songs' <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hooks</a>, while André 3000 involved himself with the album's production.<sup id="cite_ref-remembers_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-remembers-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> André 3000 and producer <a href="/wiki/Mr._DJ" title="Mr. DJ">Mr. DJ</a> learned about beat creation through observing the members of <a href="/wiki/Organized_Noize" title="Organized Noize">Organized Noize</a> at work, with Mr. DJ observing that despite André 3000's normally frugal lifestyle, his enthusiasm for production led him to splurge on costly recording equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> At one point, André 3000 attempted singing and modifying his voice with pitch-correction equipment, but Big Boi warned him that this would alienate the group's urban audience.<sup id="cite_ref-Westhoff111_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Westhoff111-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Another point of contention was the order of the tracklisting; Big Boi wanted to begin the album with "Y'all Scared" while André 3000 and the other producers and musicians wanted the first track to be "Return of the G", preferring that the record start with a song with just the duo as opposed to one with multiple collaborators. Big Boi missed his flight to attend the album mastering session and by the time he arrived, the others had already settled on a cohesive tracklist for the record. After a long discussion that was sometimes fiery, Big Boi eventually agreed to opening the album with "Return of the G".<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> While recording <i>Aquemini</i>, André 3000 drew influence from reggae music and listened to <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley" title="Bob Marley">Bob Marley</a> constantly during the sessions.<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The album features the song "West Savannah", which had been recorded during the <i>Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik</i> sessions, and was included on <i>Aquemini</i> to give listeners a "bonus" that had sentimental value for the duo, as well as to pay homage to Big Boi's family living in <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_lyrics">Music and lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aquemini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music and lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width: 25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div style="margin:0px 2em;">
<p>OutKast wore their musical connections easily, and sometimes literally. The references to 70s funk and soul weren't just in the deep, colour-saturated funk underpinning the whole album&#160;... It's also a very musical album. Sure, there are samples, but it's mostly real musicians playing guitars and horns, or in the case of the charmingly named South Central Chamber Orchestra, strings and woodwind.</p>
</div>
<div style="position: absolute; left: 0px; top: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">“</div>
<div style="position: absolute; right: 0px; bottom: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">”</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: left;">Emma Warren of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, on the record's musical style.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
<p>Big Boi described the music on the album as "very experimental" noting that the creative freedom afforded to the duo allowed them to "really [dive]" into the production.<sup id="cite_ref-remembers_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-remembers-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The album features live instrumentation that includes horns, guitar, piano, and harmonica.<sup id="cite_ref-chictrib_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chictrib-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a>'s Steve Huey detected the "ethereal futurism" of <i>ATLiens</i> in the music on <i>Aquemini</i>, but also noted that "more often Aquemini plants its feet on the ground for a surprisingly down-home flavor".<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> He also observed a "Southern earthiness and simultaneous spirituality" in Organized Noize's production work on the record.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Lyrically, much of <i>Aquemini</i> features introspection about the desolation of the <a href="/wiki/Human_condition" title="Human condition">human condition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LA_Times_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LA_Times-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Overarching themes addressed on the record include <a href="/wiki/Drug_addiction" title="Drug addiction" class="mw-redirect">drug addiction</a>, precarious <a href="/wiki/Interpersonal_relationship" title="Interpersonal relationship">relationships</a>, and freedom from self-inflicted struggles.<sup id="cite_ref-chictrib_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chictrib-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> On the record, the group often shifts between <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>-inspired topics and the harsh realities of urban life.<sup id="cite_ref-yaleherald_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yaleherald-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Saul Austerlitz of the <i><a href="/wiki/Yale_Herald" title="Yale Herald" class="mw-redirect">Yale Herald</a></i> notes the central theme of the group's "ability to move between two worlds--the G-funk glamorized by the <a href="/wiki/Notorious_B.I.G." title="Notorious B.I.G." class="mw-redirect">Notorious B.I.G.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a> and the group's own spacier, more poetic excursions" in the album.<sup id="cite_ref-yaleherald_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yaleherald-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Other subjects include excessive reliance on technology and the Atlanta club scene.<sup id="cite_ref-remembers_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-remembers-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Another theme is the duality of the two members and their differing personalities, with Big Boi as "the player" and Andre 3000 as "the poet", according to the album's cover.<sup id="cite_ref-yaleherald_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yaleherald-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Big Boi generally covers the more conventional hip-hop topics such as his childhood in the South and attractive women, while Andre 3000 discusses more unorthodox themes.<sup id="cite_ref-citypages_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-citypages-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In contrast to much of hip hop music in the late 1990s, OutKast did not tone down the regional qualities, like the <a href="/wiki/Harmonica" title="Harmonica">harmonica</a> break on "Rosa Parks" and distinctive <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> slang and diction throughout.The duo experimented with several delivery styles on the record, utilizing "relaxed, hyper, distorted, speedy and conversational presentations."<sup id="cite_ref-LA_Times_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LA_Times-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Aquemini</i> begins with an introductory track entitled "Hold On, Be Strong," which was written by session guitarist Donny Mathis and was originally a full song with verses, but the group preferred to only use the hook.<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> André 3000 played a <a href="/wiki/Kalimba" title="Kalimba" class="mw-redirect">kalimba</a> on the song after purchasing the instrument at a flea market, drawing inspiration from <a href="/wiki/Earth,_Wind_%26_Fire" title="Earth, Wind &amp; Fire">Earth, Wind &amp; Fire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> "Return of the G" addresses concerns from fans who felt that the group's style had changed too drastically since the release of <i>Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik</i>, as well as those who make poor decisions in order to keep their street credibility.<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SlantReview_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SlantReview-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> When discussing the lyrical content of the song, André 3000 explained, "I was young and wilder and some of my fashion choices people didn't accept at the time. I started getting flak from some people, so they were like, 'Either he's gay or on drugs'&#160;... 'Return of the Gangsta' was trying to give them a sense of, 'Hey, I'm still a regular person.'"<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> "Rosa Parks" contains <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a>-influenced guitar work and folksy harmonies that "announce OutKast's distinctive style of Southern boogie. The groove goes into overdrive during a clapping, foot-stomping breakdown funkified by a fierce harmonica as the kick drum pounds incessantly."<sup id="cite_ref-RSReview_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSReview-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The next track, "Skew It On the Bar-B" features rapper <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a> and discusses the disappointment of the group's debut album not achieving the coveted "five-mic" rating from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i>: "I gotta hit the Source / I need my other half mic / because that <i>Southernplayalisticadillacmusik</i> was a classic right?".<sup id="cite_ref-Sarig174_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarig174-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> "Skew It On the Bar-B" is followed by the title track, which has been compared to the music of soul singer <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Hayes" title="Isaac Hayes">Isaac Hayes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSReview_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSReview-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Pogue experimented with <a href="/wiki/Delay_(audio_effect)" title="Delay (audio effect)">delays</a> and echos in his production to make the song "dimensional, like you could actually put your hands through the song."<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> "Synthesizer" contains elements of <a href="/wiki/Electrofunk" title="Electrofunk" class="mw-redirect">electrofunk</a> and features <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a> musician <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(musician)" title="George Clinton (musician)">George Clinton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSReview_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSReview-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Emma Warren of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> called the track "raw and woozy", dubbing it the "weirdest moment of the record".<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> On "West Savannah", Big Boi discusses his Southern roots, and references individuals who grew up outside of the South who fail to recognize different regional Southern identities by stating "You might call us country, but we's only Southern".<sup id="cite_ref-Miller3839_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller3839-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tracks_8-16">Tracks 8-16</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aquemini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tracks 8-16">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Da Art of Storytellin' (Part 1)" tells the story of a self-destructive childhood friend named Sasha Thumper who dies of a <a href="/wiki/Drug_overdose" title="Drug overdose">drug overdose</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hess462_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess462-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Commenting on the song's lyrical content, author Mickey Hess remarks that André 3000 "manages to walk the fine line between emotionalism and masculinity by articulating this highly emotional narrative with an almost emotionless tone."<sup id="cite_ref-Hess462_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess462-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The next track, "Da Art of Storytellin' (Part 2)", is an apocalyptic song that represents the group's vision of "the last song recorded in the world", with André 3000 commenting, "I do remember thinking, 'What if it was the end of the world and we had to get to the Dungeon on some <a href="/wiki/X-Men" title="X-Men">X-Men</a> superhero shit. I think I was vibing on some end-of-the-world, last-recorded-song shit'".<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Following is "Mamacita" which features Dungeon Crew rapper Madasa in a meditation on male-female relationships, inspired by an ex-girlfriend of André 3000 who began pursuing women after their breakup.<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> "SpottieOttieDopalicious" relates a story of ill-fated romance, with André 3000 describing the infatuation during an encounter with a woman at a club and Big Boi noting the hopelessness of the relationship as the song progresses.<sup id="cite_ref-Wang133_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang133-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"Y'all Scared" is a collaboration with three members of <a href="/wiki/Goodie_Mob" title="Goodie Mob">Goodie Mob</a> and contains the chant "If you scared, say you scared" accompanied by prominent organ and guitar work.<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> "Nathaniel" is an <a href="/wiki/A_capella" title="A capella" class="mw-redirect">a capella</a> rap by the group's close friend who at the time as an inmate in a Georgia prison, and the song is a recording of an actual collect call during his time in jail. The track an introduction to "Liberation".<sup id="cite_ref-Makingof_9-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Makingof-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The eight minute-long "Liberation" combines a variety of musical styles, including <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, blues, and <a href="/wiki/World_music" title="World music">world music</a>. The song is notable for not including rapped vocals and instead features vocal alternations between singing and spoken word styles.<sup id="cite_ref-Sarig172_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarig172-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Lyrically, the track utilizes images of slavery to symbolize artistic freedom and not being concerned with the opinions of the public and record labels.<sup id="cite_ref-Sarig172_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarig172-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The album closes with "Chonkyfire", which features a fuzzy guitar riff and snippets from Outkast's speech at the 1995 Source Awards in which the group emphasized the legitimacy of the Southern hip-hop scene.<sup id="cite_ref-Sarig173174_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarig173174-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aquemini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The group originally planned to create a film in conjunction with <i>Aquemini</i>, completing a script three months before the release of the record. Outkast met with <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> for the project, who, despite liking the idea for the film, hoped to instead buy the project and cast <a href="/wiki/Missy_Elliott" title="Missy Elliott">Missy Elliott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a> instead, feeling the latter two had more "star power."<sup id="cite_ref-Kasting_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kasting-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Although the duo recalls being "heartbroken" at the time, André 3000 and Big Boi continued to work on ideas for a collaborative film, eventually resulting in the 2006 musical <i><a href="/wiki/Idlewild_(film)" title="Idlewild (film)">Idlewild</a></i> starring the group.<sup id="cite_ref-Kasting_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kasting-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> David Browne of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> viewed the album art as an homage to <a href="/wiki/Blaxploitation" title="Blaxploitation">blaxploitation</a> films of the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-shrinking_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shrinking-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The record's title is a combination of the two members' zodiac signs, with André 3000 being a <a href="/wiki/Gemini_(astrology)" title="Gemini (astrology)">Gemini</a> and Big Boi being an <a href="/wiki/Aquarius_(astrology)" title="Aquarius (astrology)">Aquarius</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sarig174_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarig174-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Aquemini</i> debuted behind <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Vol._2..._Hard_Knock_Life" title="Vol. 2... Hard Knock Life">Vol. 2... Hard Knock Life</a></i> at number two on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 200, the same opening position of both <i>ATLiens</i> and the group's next release, <i>Stankonia</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-assault_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-assault-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-beating_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beating-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The record sold 227,000 copies in its first week of release, ahead of <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="A Tribe Called Quest">A Tribe Called Quest</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Love_Movement" title="The Love Movement">The Love Movement</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Lauryn_Hill" title="Lauryn Hill">Lauryn Hill</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Miseducation_Of_Lauryn_Hill" title="The Miseducation Of Lauryn Hill" class="mw-redirect">The Miseducation Of Lauryn Hill</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-assault_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-assault-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> In Norway and Germany, the album peaked at numbers 39 and 66, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Hung_Medien_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hung_Medien-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, civil rights activist <a href="/wiki/Rosa_Parks" title="Rosa Parks">Rosa Parks</a> filed a lawsuit against Outkast for the song "Rosa Parks", claiming that the song used her name without her permission, constituting false advertising and an infringement on her right to publicity, in addition to <a href="/wiki/Defamation" title="Defamation">defamation</a> of character.<sup id="cite_ref-supremecourt_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-supremecourt-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> The lawsuit was dismissed on first amendment grounds that year by a federal judge, but the appeals court partially reinstated the suit in 2003, stating that the group must have an artistic reason for titling the song with her name.<sup id="cite_ref-supremecourt_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-supremecourt-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> The case was settled in 2005 with Outkast and LaFace records agreeing to create educational programs to "enlighten today's youth about the significant role Rosa Parks played in making America a better place for all races."<sup id="cite_ref-settlement_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-settlement-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Aquemini</i> received general acclaim from music critics upon its release. <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a>'s Steve Huey called it "a stroke of brilliance". He praised the record for avoiding the "hardcore clichés" and summed up his review by saying that <i>Aquemini</i> is "a virtuosic masterpiece, and a landmark hip-hop album of the late '90s".<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> also commented positively on the album, stating that the record "evolved G-funk with denser instrumental crosstalk."<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> writer Soren Baker complimented OutKast's "intelligent hip-hop" and commented that "musically, the collection supplies some of the lushest tracks ever included on a hip-hop record", noting that the music will "stimulate the mind, touch the soul and pack the dance floor."<sup id="cite_ref-LA_Times_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LA_Times-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)" class="mw-redirect">Q</a></i> named it the group's "third best offering" and called it "breathtaking in its ambition makes most rappers seem drab and doltish in comparison."<sup id="cite_ref-Q_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Q-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Sia Michel of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i> called it "an impassioned state-of-hip-hop address".<sup id="cite_ref-Village_Voice_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Village_Voice-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> In a brief review for "<a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a>," Cheo Tyehimba qualified <i>Aquemini</i> as the hip-hop album of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-EW_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EW-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Fernando Jr. of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> wrote "OutKast prove that you don't have to sell out to sell records. Sporting plenty of live chops and soulful harmonies, <i>Aquemini's</i> fresh, original feel defies rap's coastal clichés."<sup id="cite_ref-RS_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RS-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Steve Jones of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> commented that the duo's "molasses-smooth raps speak to the stark realities of urban streets. And with the hard-driving, Southern-fried grooves provided by live studio musicians, these songs not only make you dance, they make you sweat".<sup id="cite_ref-USA_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USA-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Tony Green of <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> said that although they are not as spiritual as Goodie Mob, Outkast's "streetcorner signifying" offers listeners more than simple musical pleasure and that they "have crafted some of the most seductive and dramadelic textures."<sup id="cite_ref-Spin_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spin-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Giving it a <a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)#The_Source.27s_Five-Mic_albums" title="The Source (magazine)">five out of five "mic"-rating</a>, Charlie Braxton of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i> praised the duo's "submersion into the baptismal waters of the African American musical continuum" and "their superb use of the urban narrative." Braxton called <i>Aquemini</i> "a brilliant record" and commented that it "possesses an uncanny blend of sonic beauty, poignant lyricism and spirituality that compels without commanding".<sup id="cite_ref-Source_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Source-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In a retrospective review, Tim Stelloh from <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a> described the album as "loud, unpretentious, eclectic kick in the ass". He praised it for being "full of both fear and curiosity, and those emotions were channeled through its production."<sup id="cite_ref-Popmatters_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Popmatters-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Dave Hughes, writing in <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a>, stated: "Ten years on, Aquemini is the single strongest aspect of one of the art form's deepest benches. Snappier and more experimental than the pair's early work, and focused enough to feel comfortable in a sprawl, it's the moment when OutKast came fully into itself."<sup id="cite_ref-Slant_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slant-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aquemini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Aquemini</i> has been included in several publications' best album lists. In 2003, <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> ranked it number 500 on its list of <a href="/wiki/500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">500 Greatest Albums of All Time</a>. The staff explained the inclusion by stating: "OutKast unleashed an explosive hip-hop that deployed live musicians, social commentary and a heavy dose of deep funk".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was placed at number 11 on the list of the "100 Best Albums of the Nineties" made by the same magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-Rolling_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolling-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Paste_(magazine)" title="Paste (magazine)">Paste</a></i> named the album "the best Atlanta hip-hop album of all time".<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> ranked the record at #50 on their "Top 100 Albums of the 1990s", describing it as "smooth and well-conceived".<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> included it on three of their lists. They ranked it number 35 on the "90 Greatest Albums of the '90s" and number three on the "Top 20 Albums of '98".<sup id="cite_ref-Q_36-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Q-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Spin90s_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spin90s-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> The magazine also ranked it 76th on their 2010 list of "The 125 Best Albums of the Past 25 Years".<sup id="cite_ref-Spin25_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spin25-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2004, <i><a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus</a></i> ranked it 185th on their "Top 101-200 Favourite Albums Ever" list.<sup id="cite_ref-Stylus101-200_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stylus101-200-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2013, <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> named it the 20th greatest album since 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-Vibe93_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibe93-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Ego_trip_(magazine)" title="Ego trip (magazine)">ego trip</a></i> ranked it the second greatest hip hop album from 1980 to 98,<sup id="cite_ref-Acclaimed_Music_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Acclaimed_Music-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> while <i><a href="/wiki/Hip_Hop_Connection" title="Hip Hop Connection">Hip-Hop Connection</a></i> ranked it the 11th greatest rap album from 1995 to 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-HHC100_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HHC100-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/New_Nation" title="New Nation">New Nation</a></i> named it the 80th best album by black artists.<sup id="cite_ref-Acclaimed_Music_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Acclaimed_Music-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album was included in <i><a href="/wiki/Blender_(magazine)" title="Blender (magazine)">Blender</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s "500 CDs You Must Own Before You Die" (2003),<sup id="cite_ref-Acclaimed_Music_52-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Acclaimed_Music-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> Tom Moon's <i>1000 Recordings to Hear Before You Die</i> (2008),<sup id="cite_ref-Acclaimed_Music_52-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Acclaimed_Music-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Continuum_Books" title="Continuum Books" class="mw-redirect">Continuum Books</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/33%E2%85%93" title="33⅓">33⅓</a>: A Series of Books about Critically Acclaimed Albums</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-3313_Aquemini_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3313_Aquemini-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Q_magazine" title="Q magazine">Q</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s "The Ultimate Music Collection" (2005)<sup id="cite_ref-Ultimate_Q_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ultimate_Q-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> The lead single "<a href="/wiki/Rosa_Parks_(song)" title="Rosa Parks (song)">Rosa Parks</a>" was nominated in the category <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Performance_by_a_Duo_or_Group" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group">Grammy Award for Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1999_Grammy_Awards" title="1999 Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">1999 Grammy Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's twelfth track "SpottieOttieDopaliscious" was ranked at number 16 on Pitchfork Media's list of the top 200 tracks of the 90s.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aquemini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width: 30%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #ffeeaa;">
<div style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div style="margin:0px 2em;">
<p><i>Aquemini</i> is a hip-hop classic that pushes the limits of what we normally consider hip-hop, yet is decidedly true to its roots.</p>
</div>
<div style="position: absolute; left: 0px; top: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">“</div>
<div style="position: absolute; right: 0px; bottom: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">”</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: right;">— <a href="/wiki/AllHipHop" title="AllHipHop">AllHipHop</a>, on the album's legacy<sup id="cite_ref-AllHipHop_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllHipHop-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
<p>Critics hailed the recording as OutKast's most fully realized up to that time and one of the best of the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> Steve Huey wrote: "<i>Aquemini</i> fulfills all its ambitions, covering more than enough territory to qualify it as a virtuosic masterpiece, and a landmark hip-hop album of the late '90s".<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Ebony_(magazine)" title="Ebony (magazine)">Ebony</a></i> observed that <i>Aquemini</i> is "perhaps Outkast's best effort" and "a huge commercial and artistic success".<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> Matt Wink concluded that Outkast with this album "carved their place in the game and grabbed the world's attention. No two people with a similar background could be more different and no two artists could have made this masterpiece."<sup id="cite_ref-Popstache_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Popstache-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> labeled the album "OutKast's third brilliant slice of hip-hop".<sup id="cite_ref-LA_Times_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LA_Times-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> wrote that "Atlanta's reputation as hip-hop's most avant-garde area code — the Long Island of the Nineties — was cemented" with this effort.<sup id="cite_ref-Rolling_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolling-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> In a column for <i><a href="/wiki/Jazz_Times" title="Jazz Times" class="mw-redirect">Jazz Times</a></i>, Tony Green wrote that "Outkast's <i>Aquemini</i> dispels any notion that hip-hop is out of sonic ideas. If anything, it shows that the genre's appetite for new sounds is as ravenous as ever."<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> According to Emma Warren from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, this album is "a high point of 90s hip-hop" and a prove that "the old push and pull between the east and west coast of American hip-hop was over".<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Tim Stelloh of <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a> felt that "<i>Aquemini</i> far surpassed OutKast's previous release <i>A-tliens</i>  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>], and made the group one of those rare commercial anomalies—kind of like Nirvana, Rage Against the Machine, or Public Enemy".<sup id="cite_ref-Popmatters_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Popmatters-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Several reviewers of <a href="/wiki/Kendrick_Lamar" title="Kendrick Lamar">Kendrick Lamar</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Good_Kid,_M.A.A.D_City" title="Good Kid, M.A.A.D City">Good Kid, M.A.A.D City</a></i> (2012) perceived influence from <i>Aquemini</i> in the record's production and aesthetic.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
