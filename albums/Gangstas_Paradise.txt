<p><i><b>Gangsta's Paradise</b></i> is the second studio album by rapper <a href="/wiki/Coolio" title="Coolio">Coolio</a>, released on November 21, 1995. The album is Coolio's best-selling album, with over three million copies sold in the United States.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Album_information">Album information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta%27s_Paradise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Album information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album contained three hits: "<a href="/wiki/Gangsta%27s_Paradise_(song)" title="Gangsta's Paradise (song)">Gangsta's Paradise</a>", which reached number 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>; "<a href="/wiki/1,_2,_3,_4_(Sumpin%27_New)" title="1, 2, 3, 4 (Sumpin' New)">1, 2, 3, 4 (Sumpin' New)</a>", which reached number 5, and "Too Hot", which peaked at number 24.</p>
<p>The album was nominated for a 1997 Grammy for Best Rap Album. The song "Gangsta's Paradise" won a 1996 Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance. "Sumpin' New" was nominated for a 1997 <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance">Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance</a>.</p>

<p><i>Spin</i> gave the album 8 out of 10, and said, "Today's rappers aren't interested in sharing any knowledge with outsiders. Coolio is the great exception... the most stylistically broad mainstream rap album you'll probably hear all year. Even upon first listen, it sounds like a collection of hits."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> ranked the album #4 on EW's Top 10 Albums Of 1995, and said, "The level-headed street philosopher, preaching respect for black women, fatherly responsibility, and safe sex... continuing in the great tradition of Sly Stone and Stevie Wonder... Coolio and his crew make the ghetto seem not just a place of desolation, but of hope."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i> gave the album 4 Mics out of 5, and said, "The crazy braided one brings skilled and insightful lyrics that reach and relate to all levels of the Black experience... sets standards that corny lyrics and catchy loops can't compete with... Few artists can straddle the line between commercial and underground hip-hop."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Musician</i> said, "Coolio ties his music to a message that's as deep as the groove."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Rap Pages</i> gave the album 7 out of 10, and said, "Maybe because he rapped for a decade with no props, got yanked on record deals... this cool-ass homie from Compton has stayed grounded... Coolio rejoices in the only life he knows."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> gave the album 7 out of 10, and said, "Instead of playing the role of the Uzi-toting big-shot, he adopts a more reflective view of urban life in Los Angeles... In doing so, Coolio takes a deliberate step forward from last year's light-hearted debut."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Samples_and_interpolations">Samples and interpolations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gangsta%27s_Paradise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Samples and interpolations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>"Gangsta's Paradise"is a cover of "Pastime Paradise", performed by <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a>.</li>
<li>"Too Hot" contains an interpolation of "Too Hot", originally performed by <a href="/wiki/Kool_%26_The_Gang" title="Kool &amp; The Gang" class="mw-redirect">Kool &amp; The Gang</a>.</li>
<li>"Cruisin'" contains an interpolation of "Cruisin'", originally performed by <a href="/wiki/Smokey_Robinson_%26_the_Miracles" title="Smokey Robinson &amp; the Miracles" class="mw-redirect">Smokey Robinson &amp; the Miracles</a>.</li>
<li>"Sumpin' New" contains samples of "Thighs High (Grip Your Hips More)", performed by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Browne_(trumpeter)" title="Tom Browne (trumpeter)">Tom Browne</a>, and "Wikka Wrap", performed by The Evasions.</li>
<li>"Smilin'" contains an interpolation of "You Caught Me Smiling", originally performed by <a href="/wiki/Sly_%26_The_Family_Stone" title="Sly &amp; The Family Stone" class="mw-redirect">Sly &amp; The Family Stone</a>.</li>
<li>"Kinda High, Kinda Drunk" contains interpolations of "Saturday Night" and "The Boyz in Da Hood"</li>
<li>"For My Sistas" contains an interpolation of "Make Me Say It Again Girl", originally performed by <a href="/wiki/The_Isley_Brothers" title="The Isley Brothers">The Isley Brothers</a>.</li>
<li>"A Thing Goin' On" contains an interpolation of "<a href="/wiki/Me_and_Mrs._Jones" title="Me and Mrs. Jones">Me and Mrs. Jones</a>".</li>
<li>"The Revolution" contains an interpolation of "Magic Night".</li>
<li>"Get Up, Get Down" contains an interpolation of "<a href="/wiki/Chameleon_(composition)" title="Chameleon (composition)">Chameleon</a>", originally performed by <a href="/wiki/Herbie_Hancock" title="Herbie Hancock">Herbie Hancock</a>.</li>
<li>The song "Gangsta's Paradise" is used in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Dangerous_Minds" title="Dangerous Minds">Dangerous Minds</a></i> (1995).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The first line of "Gangsta's Paradise" is taken from <a href="/wiki/Psalm_23" title="Psalm 23">Psalm 23</a>.</li>
</ul>
