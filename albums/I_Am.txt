<p><i><b>I Am…</b></i> is the third <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American rapper <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, released April 6, 1999 on <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>. It debuted #1 on the charts, selling over 470,000 copies in its first week. <i>I Am…</i> would later be certified 3x platinum in the United States. Upon its release, <i>I Am...</i> received generally mixed to positive reviews from <a href="/wiki/Music_criticism" title="Music criticism">music critics</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_Am..._(Nas_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This album was originally to have been a <a href="/wiki/Double_album" title="Double album">double album</a> titled <i>I Am...The Autobiography</i>, however most of the original sessions were bootlegged forcing Nas to discard many songs and adjust the release to one disc. <i>I Am...</i>became one of the first major label releases to be widely leaked using <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a> technology.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Some of the leaked songs were later released on the compilation LP <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Tapes" title="The Lost Tapes">The Lost Tapes</a></i> in 2002. The concept of this album as can be seen by tracks such as "Fetus" from <i>The Lost Tapes</i> was to be an autobiography of sorts for Nas. Although several bootleg versions have appeared on the internet over the years, an official version of the intended double album has never leaked and it remains unclear whether or not it was ever completed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_Am..._(Nas_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The two singles from <i>I Am...</i> were "<a href="/wiki/Hate_Me_Now" title="Hate Me Now">Hate Me Now</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Nas_Is_Like" title="Nas Is Like">Nas Is Like</a>". "Hate Me Now" features <a href="/wiki/Puff_Daddy" title="Puff Daddy" class="mw-redirect">Puff Daddy</a> and is produced by D-Moet, Pretty Boy and The Trackmasters. It was a <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> hit, and had a controversial music video directed by <a href="/wiki/Hype_Williams" title="Hype Williams">Hype Williams</a>. The song had a version of the <a href="/wiki/O_Fortuna" title="O Fortuna">O Fortuna</a> from <a href="/wiki/Carl_Orff" title="Carl Orff">Carl Orff</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Carmina_Burana_(Orff)" title="Carmina Burana (Orff)">Carmina Burana</a>." "Nas Is Like" is one of two tracks produced by <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a> who scratches vocal samples from Nas' "<a href="/wiki/It_Ain%27t_Hard_To_Tell" title="It Ain't Hard To Tell" class="mw-redirect">It Ain't Hard To Tell</a>" into the chorus. The "Nas Is Like" music video was directed by Nick Quested and is still very popular in underground circles and continued a long list of popular Nas/DJ Premier collaborations.</p>
<p>The album also contained the song "We Will Survive", a tribute to <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> The song criticized his peers, most notably <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, who "claimed to be New York's king" following B.I.G.'s death, the record has been cited as potentially encouraging the <a href="/wiki/Nas_vs._Jay-Z_feud" title="Nas vs. Jay-Z feud" class="mw-redirect">Nas vs. Jay-Z feud</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cover_art">Cover art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_Am..._(Nas_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cover art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Famed photographer Danny Hastings has shot iconic cover images for Big Pun’s <i>Capital Punishment</i> and Raekwon’s <i>Only Built 4 Cuban Linx</i>, but his album art shoot for <i>I Am...</i> almost ended in disaster. In order to make the iconic mask featured on the cover, Hastings and his crew put a clay mold on his face and poked air holes so he could breathe, but he almost suffocated after clay got lodged in his nose.</p>
<p>“The funny part was that the first attempt, Nas was getting asphyxiated. We almost killed Nas," Hastings told MTV.com. “We cleaned him up, and he was like, 'Let's do it again!' […] Nas was a true sport."</p>
<p>He also explained the meaning behind the cover, and how it built off of previous album art for <i>Illmatic</i> and <i>It Was Written</i>. "The first one, you have him being a boy, very young. The second was a little bit older. And the third one, he was a king," he continued. "He already conquered the world. He was on top of the world. He was doing a lot of big things. We came with the concept of making a King Tut sarcophagus piece."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Music" title="Yahoo! Music">Yahoo! Music</a>'s Billy Johnson, Jr. criticized the album's production as "somber" and described its songs as "thought-provoking, though average quality".<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Jeff Stark of <i><a href="/wiki/Salon.com" title="Salon.com" class="mw-redirect">Salon</a></i> noted "distinct identities" for each song and wrote that it does not sound "coherent", but "as if it belongs to a scattershot demographic of subway riders".<sup id="cite_ref-Stark_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stark-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Franklin Soults of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i> viewed that its music attempts to meet "halfway" with consumer demographics, noting that Nas' "most salient talent is finding and exploiting the middle ground".<sup id="cite_ref-Soults_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soults-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In his consumer guide for <i>The Village Voice</i>, critic <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave <i>I Am...</i> a B- rating and named it "dud of the month",<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> indicating "a bad record whose details rarely merit further thought".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Christgau criticized Nas' "ethos" and stated, "The question is how convincing he is, and only two themes ring true: the bad ones, revenge and money. His idea of narrative detail is to drop brand names like <a href="/wiki/Bret_Easton_Ellis" title="Bret Easton Ellis">Bret Easton Ellis</a>; his idea of morality is everybody dies".<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Craig_Seymour" title="Craig Seymour">Craig Seymour</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> attributed its thematic inconsistency to the replacement of tracks that <a href="/wiki/Internet_leak" title="Internet leak">leaked</a> to the Internet prior to the album's release, concluding that "Anyone with a good Web connection might wonder what a profound personal opus <span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span><i>I Am</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span> could have been".<sup id="cite_ref-Seymour_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seymour-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Miles_Marshall_Lewis" title="Miles Marshall Lewis">Miles Marshall Lewis</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/LA_Weekly" title="LA Weekly">LA Weekly</a></i> viewed that Nas "tightrope[s] the line between order and chaos, gangsterism and enlightenment" on the album, which he found to be "not a horrendous album. But Nas has now established a pattern of declining album quality, and that makes <i>I Am...</i> Nas' worst album".<sup id="cite_ref-Lewis_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> writer Rebecca Little gave the album two-a-and-half out of four stars and stated, "if you get past the torrent of [profanity] and a few terms referring to women as dogs and garden tools, [...] <span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span><i>I Am</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span> is a notable effort", adding that "The finer moments lie in the rapper's trademark ability to spin a compelling tale about ghetto life".<sup id="cite_ref-Little_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Little-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Kris Ex of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> gave it three-and-a-half out of five stars and stated, "Nas is still a diamond in the rough — perhaps the rawest lyrical talent of his day but lacking the guidance and vision to create a complete album [...] But what <i>I Am...</i> lacks in content, it makes up for in lyrical acumen; the album doesn't deliver the introspection its title implies, but it compensates for it in storytelling and craftsmanship. <i>I Am...</i> offers tantalizing hints of promise tethered by a need for pop acceptance".<sup id="cite_ref-Ex_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ex-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Christopher_John_Farley" title="Christopher John Farley">Christopher John Farley</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> complimented Nas' lyrics and themes and the album's musical approach, noting "grander, more aggressive, more cinematic" songs.<sup id="cite_ref-Farley_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farley-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Tom Sinclair compared the album to "a bona fide <a href="/wiki/Rap_opera" title="Rap opera">hip-hopera</a>", noting string and keyboard-laden songs and "universal themes".<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> writer Soren Baker commended Nas for "adroitly balancing hard-core subject matter with production that should easily find its way onto urban radio".<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Steve Jones of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> gave it four out of four stars and complimented Nas' "dense and deft rhymes" and "nimble, cinematic descriptions", writing that the album "nestles nicely between the underground grittiness of 1994's <i><a href="/wiki/Illmatic" title="Illmatic">Illmatic</a></i> and the high gloss of 1996's <i><a href="/wiki/It_Was_Written" title="It Was Written">It Was Written</a></i>".<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In a retrospective review, <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> editor M.F. DiBella gave the album three out of five stars and noted "blandness" in its production, writing that "Musically, <i>I Am</i> is somewhat unimaginative by Nas' stratospheric standards. [...] some of these tracks lack the sonic depth to do justice to the prophecies of the pharaoh, Nas".<sup id="cite_ref-DiBella_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DiBella-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> However, DeBella added that Nas "still shines as the old soul storyteller and crime rhyme chronicler" on some tracks and cited "Nas Is Like" and "N.Y. State of Mind, Pt. 2" as highlights, adding that they "are nothing short of <i>Illmatic</i> perfection".<sup id="cite_ref-DiBella_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DiBella-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Steve Juon of RapReviews gave <i>I Am...</i> an eight-and-a-half out of 10 rating and viewed it as an improvement over <i>It Was Written</i>, praising Nas' "power of description" and "much improved" lyrics.<sup id="cite_ref-Juon_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Juon-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stone_Album_Guide" title="The Rolling Stone Album Guide">The New Rolling Stone Album Guide</a></i> (2004), music journalist Chris Ryan gave the album two-and-a-half out of five stars and wrote that it has "[its] share of solid material, but ultimately fail[s] in the face of Nas' inability to navigate the divide between the street reporting that made him a legend and the commercial hits that made him a star".<sup id="cite_ref-RSguide_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSguide-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
