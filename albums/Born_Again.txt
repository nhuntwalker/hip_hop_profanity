<p><i><b>Born Again</b></i> is a posthumous album by <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>. It was released on December 7, 1999. Unlike the first album released after his death (<i><a href="/wiki/Life_After_Death" title="Life After Death">Life After Death</a></i>), this album was created with no input from Biggie himself. It is composed primarily of unfinished material and unreleased verses recorded mainly during his <i><a href="/wiki/Ready_to_Die" title="Ready to Die">Ready to Die</a></i> era from 1993 to 1994, with new production and guest rappers. The album debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200 chart with 485,000 albums sold in the first week, and was later certified Triple-platinum. Along with the usual guest roster of <a href="/wiki/Sean_Combs" title="Sean Combs">P. Diddy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lil%27_Kim" title="Lil' Kim">Lil' Kim</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Junior_M.A.F.I.A." title="Junior M.A.F.I.A.">Junior M.A.F.I.A.</a>, the album also featured appearances from <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missy_Elliott" title="Missy Elliott">Missy Elliott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Method_Man" title="Method Man">Method Man</a>, <a href="/wiki/Redman_(rapper)" title="Redman (rapper)">Redman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beanie_Sigel" title="Beanie Sigel">Beanie Sigel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Boys" title="Hot Boys">Hot Boys</a>. Before his death, Biggie had intended his third album to be a three disc collection. However, there was not enough finished material to piece together such a massive project. This is the final album of Biggie to contain mainly original material. However, in 2005, a remix album was released called <i><a href="/wiki/Duets:_The_Final_Chapter" title="Duets: The Final Chapter">Duets: The Final Chapter</a></i>.</p>

<p>The album received moderately poor reviews from critics. Upon its release, <i>Rolling Stone</i> wrote that the "album won't damage his legacy. But <i>Born Again</i> won't improve that legacy much, either."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In a retrospective review of his output in 2004, the magazine wrote "The posthumous <i>Born Again</i> proved Biggie was still dead, but his place in the MCs Hall of Fame remains untouchable."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a>, who gave the release a "dud" rating, later wrote, "Remember that posthumous outtakes CD Bad Boy attributed to Biggie? No? Good then—it was foul, not just ill shit but stupid ill shit."<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<dt>Notes</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>"Dead Wrong" was an unreleased song from a demo tape back in 1994. The first verse of the original version was used in this version.</li>
<li>Biggie's verse from "Hope You Niggas Sleep" comes from the second verse of the original version of "Dead Wrong".</li>
<li>Biggie's verse in "Dangerous MC's" comes from an unreleased song called "The Ugliest" which was originally going to be on <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>' 1996 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Coming" title="The Coming">The Coming</a></i>. Because he had subliminal disses aimed towards <a href="/wiki/2Pac" title="2Pac" class="mw-redirect">2Pac</a> the track was not included on the album to avoid beef between 2Pac and Busta Rhymes. The original is produced by <a href="/wiki/J._Dilla" title="J. Dilla" class="mw-redirect">J. Dilla</a>.</li>
<li>The Notorious B.I.G.'s part in "Biggie" was from "Let's Get It On" from the album <i>Eddie F. and the Untouchables</i> on the title track "Let's Get It On" featuring 2Pac, <a href="/wiki/Heavy_D" title="Heavy D">Heavy D</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grand_Puba" title="Grand Puba">Grand Puba</a>. The compilation featured the only known in-studio collaboration of two up-and-coming hip-hop MC's at the time, 2Pac and The Notorious B.I.G..</li>
<li>"Niggas" is a remix of an original unreleased version from 1993.</li>
<li>Biggie's verses in "Big Booty Hoes" comes from the song "Bust a Nut" by <a href="/wiki/Uncle_Luke" title="Uncle Luke">Uncle Luke</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Cutlass" title="Frankie Cutlass">Frankie Cutlass</a>.</li>
<li>"Come On" is a remix of the original unreleased song from 1993 produced by <a href="/wiki/Lord_Finesse" title="Lord Finesse">Lord Finesse</a>.</li>
<li>Biggie's part in "Rap Phenomenon" comes from the song "Keep Your Hands High" by <a href="/wiki/Tracey_Lee" title="Tracey Lee">Tracey Lee</a>.</li>
<li>"Tonight" has Biggie's first verse from "Long Kiss Goodnight", from <i><a href="/wiki/Life_After_Death" title="Life After Death">Life After Death</a></i>.</li>
<li>"If I Should Die Before I Wake" has Biggie's verse from a song called "Stop The Breaks" which features Raekwon, Killa Sin, O.C. and KRS-One. It was recorded without portion of lyrics, so verse is censored too.</li>
<li>"Who Shot Ya?" is a B-Side to his 1995 single "<a href="/wiki/Big_Poppa" title="Big Poppa">Big Poppa</a>".</li>
<li>"I Really Want To Show You" has Biggie's verses from "Everyday Struggle", from <i><a href="/wiki/Ready_to_Die" title="Ready to Die">Ready to Die</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
