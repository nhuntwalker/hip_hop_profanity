<p><i><b>Raising Hell</b></i> is the third album by <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Run%E2%80%93D.M.C." title="Run–D.M.C.">Run–D.M.C.</a>. The breakthrough album trumped standing perceptions of commercial viability for hip-hop groups, achieving triple-<a href="/wiki/Platinum_record" title="Platinum record" class="mw-redirect">platinum</a> status and receiving critical attention from quarters that had previously ignored hip hop, dismissing it as a <a href="/wiki/Fad" title="Fad">fad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raising_Hell_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Raising Hell</i> features the well-known cover "<a href="/wiki/Walk_This_Way" title="Walk This Way">Walk This Way</a>" featuring <a href="/wiki/Steven_Tyler" title="Steven Tyler">Steven Tyler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Perry_(musician)" title="Joe Perry (musician)">Joe Perry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aerosmith" title="Aerosmith">Aerosmith</a>. While the song was not the group's first fusion of <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock</a> and hip hop (the group's earlier single "<a href="/wiki/Rock_Box" title="Rock Box">Rock Box</a>" was), it was the first such fusion to make a significant impact on the charts, becoming the first rap song to crack the top 5 of The <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a>. <i>Raising Hell</i> peaked at No.&#160;1 on <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Music_Charts" title="Billboard Music Charts" class="mw-redirect">Billboard</a>'s <a href="/wiki/R%26B" title="R&amp;B" class="mw-redirect">R&amp;B</a>/Hip Hop Album chart as the first hip hop/rap album to do so, and at No.&#160;4 on the Billboard 200.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>It ranked fourth on <a href="/wiki/Chris_Rock" title="Chris Rock">Chris Rock</a>'s list of the Top 25 Hip-Hop Albums of all time, and the comedian called it "the first great rap album ever".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, the album appeared in The Source's 100 Best Rap Albums.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Q_magazine" title="Q magazine"><i>Q</i></a> magazine (12/99, p.&#160;162) - 5 stars out of 5 - "...the apex of pre-<a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(group)" title="Public Enemy (group)">Public Enemy</a>, beatbox-based hip hop, a monument of massive, crisp beats plus the genre-bending 'Walk This Way'."</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)"><i>Vibe</i></a> (12/99, p.&#160;162) - Included in Vibe's 100 Essential Albums of the 20th Century.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Uncut_(magazine)" title="Uncut (magazine)"><i>Uncut</i></a> (11/03, p.&#160;130) - 4 stars out of 5 - "[An album] that forced the music biz to take rap seriously."</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> (12/11/03, p.&#160;126) - "[T]he pioneering trio took hip-hop into the upper reaches of the pop charts, introducing mainstream to a new urban thunder: <a href="/wiki/Rap_rock" title="Rap rock">rap rock</a>." (It is the earliest hip-hop album to get a 5-star review from <i>Rolling Stone</i>).</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a></i> - 5 stars out of 5 - "...the music was fully realized and thoroughly invigorating, rocking harder and better than any of its rock or rap peers in 1986..."</p>
<p>In 2003, the album was ranked number 123 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of <a href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, the album was chosen by <i><a href="/wiki/TIME_(magazine)" title="TIME (magazine)" class="mw-redirect">TIME</a></i> as one of the 100 greatest albums.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Magazine" title="Time Magazine" class="mw-redirect">Time</a></i> named it No.&#160;41 of the 100 best albums of the past fifty years and stated that the album was "rap's first masterpiece".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a> listed the album at No.&#160;65 on its list of "Best Albums of the 1980s".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1987, <a href="/wiki/Soul_Train_Music_Award_for_Best_Rap_-_Single" title="Soul Train Music Award for Best Rap - Single" class="mw-redirect">Soul Train Music Award for Best Rap - Single</a> for "Walk This Way" (jointly awarded to both Run-D.M.C. and Aerosmith)</p>
<p>Public Enemy's Chuck D considers <i>Raising Hell</i> to be the greatest hip-hop album of all-time, and the reason he chose to sign with Def Jam Records.</p>
<p>Peter Piper was performed by <a href="/wiki/Justin_Timberlake" title="Justin Timberlake">Justin Timberlake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Fallon" title="Jimmy Fallon">Jimmy Fallon</a> on <a href="/wiki/Late_Night_with_Jimmy_Fallon" title="Late Night with Jimmy Fallon">Late Night with Jimmy Fallon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vinyl_sleeves_and_labeling">Vinyl sleeves and labeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raising_Hell_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vinyl sleeves and labeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>There are at least two <a href="/wiki/Color_scheme" title="Color scheme">color schemes</a> for this <a href="/wiki/LP_album" title="LP album" class="mw-redirect">LP</a>. One is a purple front photo with lime green writing and a red back photo with teal writing. Another is a green front photo with rose letters and a teal back photo and orange letters.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>There are some pressings that are mislabeled on both sides as side 2, though the grooves in the <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">vinyl</a> are etched correctly.</li>
</ul>
