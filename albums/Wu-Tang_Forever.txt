<p><i><b>Wu-Tang Forever</b></i> is the second studio album of American hip-hop group <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, released June 3, 1997 on <a href="/wiki/Loud_Records" title="Loud Records">Loud</a>/<a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA Records</a> in the United States. Pressed as a double album, it was released after a long run of successful solo projects from various members of the group, and serves as the follow-up to their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Wu-Tang_(36_Chambers)" title="Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)">Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)</a></i>. <i>Forever</i> features several guest appearances from Wu-Tang affiliates <a href="/wiki/Cappadonna" title="Cappadonna">Cappadonna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Streetlife_(rapper)" title="Streetlife (rapper)">Streetlife</a>, <a href="/wiki/4th_Disciple" title="4th Disciple">4th Disciple</a>, <a href="/wiki/True_Master" title="True Master">True Master</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tekitha" title="Tekitha">Tekitha</a>.</p>
<p>Despite limited radio/TV airplay, and a lead single that famously clocked at nearly six minutes with no chorus, <i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> debuted at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> with 612,000 copies sold in its first week.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was certified 4x platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA) on October 15, 1997 (each disc in the double album counted as separate unit for certification purpose),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and has sold over 2 million copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-yahoo_sales_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo_sales-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It is the group's highest selling album to date. Upon its release, <i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> received favorable reviews from most music critics, while it also earned the group a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> nomination for Best Rap Album at the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">40th Grammy Awards in 1998</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_lyrics">Music and lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wu-Tang_Forever&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Music and lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wu-Tang_Forever&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the group's previous album is known for its minimalistic production style, producer <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA" class="mw-redirect">RZA</a> had been expanding the musical backdrop of each solo Wu-Tang album since then. <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx..." title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...</a></i>, in particular, was praised for its cinematic feel. RZA earned accolades for his new dense style of production; incorporating strings, heavy synthesizers, and the kung-fu samples of old. The production of the record also pioneered RZA's technique of chopping up and speeding up <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a> samples to where it is unusually high-pitched. This style of production would be influential on later producers such as <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> marked the first group album in-which RZA assigned some of the album's production to Wu-Tang protégés <a href="/wiki/True_Master" title="True Master">True Master</a> and <a href="/wiki/4th_Disciple" title="4th Disciple">4th Disciple</a>, as well as Wu-Tang member <a href="/wiki/Inspectah_Deck" title="Inspectah Deck">Inspectah Deck</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrical_themes">Lyrical themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wu-Tang_Forever&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lyrical themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The group's lyrics differed in many ways from those of <i>36 Chambers</i>, with many verses written in <a href="/wiki/Stream_of_consciousness_writing" title="Stream of consciousness writing" class="mw-redirect">stream-of-consciousness</a> style, while being influenced by the teachings of the <a href="/wiki/Five_Percent_Nation" title="Five Percent Nation" class="mw-redirect">Five Percent Nation</a>. The group showed some mature depth as well, speaking on the pitfalls of life's vices ("A Better Tomorrow") and the harsh realities of the inner city life. "Impossible," for instance, even touches on the less glamorized realities of the same violence that the group often paraded.</p>
<p>The Wu-Tang Clan took advantage of the double-disc format, allowing each of the nine members with a significant number of appearances, including four solo tracks. Several of the members have been recognized for particularly strong performances:</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Inspectah_Deck" title="Inspectah Deck">Inspectah Deck</a> raised his stock in the public eye with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Hip-Hop Quotable for his performance on "Triumph".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> This verse is also considered by many to be one of the greatest hip-hop verses of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite being one of the last group members to release a solo album, his contributions throughout <i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> led to being a highly sought after guest collaborator; appearing in the near future on tracks for <a href="/wiki/Gang_Starr" title="Gang Starr">Gang Starr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rock" title="Pete Rock">Pete Rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Big_Pun" title="Big Pun">Big Pun</a>, among others.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ghostface_Killah" title="Ghostface Killah">Ghostface Killah</a> continued his rise to fame with his verse in "Impossible," which was hailed by RZA in the <i><a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Manual" title="Wu-Tang Manual" class="mw-redirect">Wu-Tang Manual</a></i> as "the greatest Wu-Tang verse ever written," and was also featured in <i>The Source'</i>s Hip-Hop Quotable. "Cash Still Rules/Scary Hours" has also been noted as one of his memorable verses on the album, for his verse cut-off, first popularizing the feel that he could "go on forever". Ghostface Killah would follow up his work on <i>Forever</i> with <i><a href="/wiki/Supreme_Clientele" title="Supreme Clientele">Supreme Clientele</a></i>, which is generally regarded as a classic in the Hip Hop Community.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Upon its release, <i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> received positive reviews from music critics, who praised RZA's production work and the group members' lyricism. Matt Diehl from <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> gave the album an A rating, and commented "<i>Forever</i> continues the group's artistic grand slam. Like their forebears in <a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(group)" title="Public Enemy (group)">Public Enemy</a>, Wu-Tang are musical revolutionaries, unafraid to bring the noise along with their trunk of funk. The RZA allows a few outside producers behind the board this time, but it's his gritty samples and numbing beats that get the party moving."<sup id="cite_ref-EntertainmentWeekly_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EntertainmentWeekly-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Sash Frere-Jones from <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> magazine gave the album a 7 out of 10 rating, and wrote "This album is for hip-hop junkies, rhyme followers who want to hear their favorite sword-swallowers drop unusually good styles over unusually good beats."<sup id="cite_ref-Spin_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spin-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Comparing some of the album's production to that of Wu-Tang member GZA's <i><a href="/wiki/Liquid_Swords" title="Liquid Swords">Liquid Swords</a></i> (also produced by RZA), Neil Strauss from <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> wrote a favorable review of the album and stated "<i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> is a smooth, clean set of 25 songs and two speeches, with only a few throwaways on the second CD. The Wu-Tang Clan offers something for every kind of rap fan. More important, after a four-year wait, on <i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> the Clan retains its mantle as rap's standard bearers."<sup id="cite_ref-TNYTimes_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TNYTimes-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Melody_Maker" title="Melody Maker">Melody Maker</a></i> gave <i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> a favorable review as well, stating "It had to be this big. It didn't have to be this good ... Every single track is a detonation of every single pop rule you thought sacrosanct .... <i>Forever</i> is one of the greatest hip hop LPs of all time."<sup id="cite_ref-CDUniverse_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDUniverse-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> from <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> gave the album 4 out of 5 stars, and stated:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Where contemporaries like <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">2Pac</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> issued double-discs cluttered with filler, <i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> is purposeful and surprisingly lean, illustrating the immense depth of producer RZA and the entire nine-piece crew... The result is an intoxicating display of musical and lyrical virtuosity, one that reveals how bereft of imagination the Wu-Tang's contemporaries are.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="templatequotecite">—Stephen Thomas Erlewine</div>
</blockquote>
<p>Describing the album's lyrics as "hauntingly descriptive tales of ghetto hustlers and victims," <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> rated the album 3½ out of 5 stars, and stated "The whole of <i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> crackles with a shootout-at-midnight electricity that more than justifies the double-disc indulgence, while the back-and-forth wordfire of Method Man, Raekwon, Ghostface Killah, etc. confirms the Clan's singular zing at the mic, and their ghetto-wise might as storytellers."<sup id="cite_ref-RollingStone_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Cheo Coker from <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> gave the album 3½ out of 4 stars and commented "The Clan's beats push the limit between cutting-edge hip-hop and industrial feedback, with jugular-clutching rhymes following their own melodic dictates and insular messages running the gamut from ancient maxims of the art of war to spiritual knowledge, wisdom and understanding from the Islamic Five Percent Nation. Clocking in at two hours, this two-disc, 27-track collection starts in earnest with the brilliant "Reunited," in which producer RZA introduces menacing fiddles to his graveyard hoedown. Behind the rhymes lie exciting string movements that give the song an eerie edge that electric guitars just can't project."<sup id="cite_ref-LATimes_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Edna Gunderson from <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> also gave the album 3½ out of 4 stars, and wrote "Hip-hop's most anticipated album crackles with the nine-member clan's unique hard-core rhymes and beats. On this two-disc, 112-minute set, the whole is definitely greater than the sum of its parts. The RZA avoids overproduction, using the beats to propel the lyrics, and keeps the music free of clichéd R&amp;B loops.<sup id="cite_ref-USAToday_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wu-Tang_Forever&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> was ranked as one of the best albums of the year by several notable publications, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Melody_Maker" title="Melody Maker">Melody Maker</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-accolades_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-accolades-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Popular <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> magazine <i><a href="/wiki/HUMO" title="HUMO">HUMO</a></i>, and popular <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Spex_(magazine)" title="Spex (magazine)">Spex</a></i> both ranked it number six on their albums of the year lists.<sup id="cite_ref-accolades_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-accolades-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1999, <i><a href="/wiki/Ego_trip_(magazine)" title="Ego trip (magazine)">Ego Trip</a></i> ranked <i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> number three on their Hip Hop's 25 Greatest Albums by Year 1980-98 list.<sup id="cite_ref-accolades_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-accolades-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In their March 2005 issue, <i><a href="/wiki/Hip_Hop_Connection" title="Hip Hop Connection">Hip Hop Connection</a></i> ranked the album number 57 on their 100 Greatest Rap Albums 1995-2005 list.<sup id="cite_ref-accolades_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-accolades-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Also in 2005, <i>Blow Up</i> magazine from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> included <i>Wu-Tang Forever</i> in their 600 Essential Albums list.<sup id="cite_ref-accolades_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-accolades-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
