<p><i><b>Chicken-n-Beer</b></i> is the fourth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American <a href="/wiki/Rapper" title="Rapper" class="mw-redirect">rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>, released on October 7, 2003 through <a href="/wiki/Disturbing_tha_Peace" title="Disturbing tha Peace">Disturbing tha Peace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Recordings" title="Def Jam Recordings">Def Jam Recordings</a>. The album's production took place from 2002 to early 2003, and was handled by several record producers, including <a href="/wiki/DJ_Nasty_%26_LVM" title="DJ Nasty &amp; LVM">DJ Nasty &amp; LVM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mo_B._Dick" title="Mo B. Dick">Mo B. Dick</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Paul" title="DJ Paul">DJ Paul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juicy_J" title="Juicy J">Juicy J</a> and Ludacris himself. A <a href="/wiki/Southern_hip_hop" title="Southern hip hop">Southern hip hop</a> album, <i>Chicken-n-Beer</i> is musically similar to Ludacris' previous work, with his use of a fast, highly versatile <a href="/wiki/Rapping#Flow" title="Rapping">flow</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Misogyny" title="Misogyny">misogynistic</a> themes in his lyrics often prominent throughout, although some of the songs on the album take a more <a href="/wiki/Political_hip_hop" title="Political hip hop">political</a> overtone.</p>
<p>The album debuted and peaked at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, selling 429,000 copies in its first week; unlike Ludacris' previous albums, the album achieved success outside the United States, charting in several European territories, including Germany and the United Kingdom. Upon its release, <i>Chicken-n-Beer</i> received positive reviews from most music critics, many of whom praised Ludacris' performance, although some criticized the adult humour in the lyrics as excessive. Four singles were released from the album, two of which – "<a href="/wiki/Stand_Up_(Ludacris_song)" title="Stand Up (Ludacris song)">Stand Up</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Splash_Waterfalls" title="Splash Waterfalls">Splash Waterfalls</a>" – became Ludacris' first to peak in the top ten of the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chicken-n-Beer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics_and_themes">Lyrics and themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chicken-n-Beer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lyrics and themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ludacris frequently employs a fast paced <a href="/wiki/Rapping#Flow" title="Rapping">flow</a> on <i>Chicken-n-Beer</i>, whilst often suffusing the words he raps with humorous punchlines and innuendos.<sup id="cite_ref-AMGreview_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMGreview-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBCMusicrev_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCMusicrev-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Writing for <a href="/wiki/BBC_Music" title="BBC Music" class="mw-redirect">BBC Music</a>, Lewis Dene noted Ludacris to typically incorporate "lightning-quick phrasing, cutting wit and reference points a plenty" into his lyrics.<sup id="cite_ref-BBCMusicrev_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCMusicrev-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the songs on <i>Chicken-n-Beer</i> are of a predominantly sexual nature,<sup id="cite_ref-USAToday_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and were compared by Nathan Rabin of <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i> to the material recorded by the likes of the hip hop group <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AVClubreview_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AVClubreview-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Stand_Up_(Ludacris_song)" title="Stand Up (Ludacris song)">Stand Up</a>" was described as a "steamy sex rap",<sup id="cite_ref-AMGreview_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMGreview-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and "Hoes in My Room", a collaboration with fellow rapper <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, details a fictional encounter with a collection of <a href="/wiki/Groupie" title="Groupie">groupies</a> following a live performance. Rabin further observed that Ludacris addresses such topics with "irreverent glee", despite describing these themes "well-worn" and clichéd.<sup id="cite_ref-AVClubreview_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AVClubreview-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Other songs take on a noticeably <a href="/wiki/Political_hip_hop" title="Political hip hop">political</a> tone.<sup id="cite_ref-RCrev_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCrev-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Ludacris was inspired to write such material after political commentator <a href="/wiki/Bill_O%27Reilly_(political_commentator)" title="Bill O'Reilly (political commentator)">Bill O'Reilly</a> specifically criticized Ludacris for the content of his lyrics and noted him as a bad influence.<sup id="cite_ref-AVClubreview_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AVClubreview-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Blow_It_Out" title="Blow It Out">Blow It Out</a>" and "Screwed Up" contain direct references to O'Reilly and his comments, as well as observations on the difficulties of fame.<sup id="cite_ref-RCrev_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCrev-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chicken-n-Beer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chicken-n-Beer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album debuted at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, becoming Ludacris' first album to top the chart: the overall first-week sales of 429,000 copies were an improvement on Ludacris' previous album <i>Word of Mouf</i>, which sold 282,000 copies in its first week in the United States in debuting at number three on the <i>Billboard</i> 200.<sup id="cite_ref-USFW_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USFW-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> In its second week, <i>Chicken-n-Beer</i> fell to number two in selling 194,000 copies, representing an overall sales decrease of 55% and bringing overall sales of the album to 623,000.<sup id="cite_ref-USSW_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USSW-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The album spent five weeks inside the top ten of the chart, selling 936,000 copies in that time:<sup id="cite_ref-USTensales_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USTensales-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> it went on to spend a total of 45 weeks on the chart.<sup id="cite_ref-US200_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US200-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> It also peaked at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart, although it remained on the chart for a longer length of time than on the <i>Billboard</i> 200, lasting a total of 51 weeks before exiting the chart.<sup id="cite_ref-USR.26BAl_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USR.26BAl-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> On June 18, 2004, the album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">two times platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> for shipments of 2,000,000 copies within the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-United_StatesLudacrisChicken_.26_BeeralbumCertRef_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-United_StatesLudacrisChicken_.26_BeeralbumCertRef-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike Ludacris' previous releases, <i>Chicken-n-Beer</i> achieved reasonable commercial success outside the United States. It became Ludacris' first album to chart in Australia, where it debuted and peaked at number 98, then exited the chart after a single week.<sup id="cite_ref-AUSAl_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AUSAl-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The album peaked at number five on the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Albums_Chart" title="Canadian Albums Chart">Canadian Albums Chart</a>: although it only spent a single week on the chart, no other Ludacris album has achieved a higher position on the chart,<sup id="cite_ref-CANAl_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CANAl-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> and was certified platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Recording_Industry_Association" title="Canadian Recording Industry Association" class="mw-redirect">Canadian Recording Industry Association</a> on January 8, 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-CanadaLudacrisChicken-N-BeeralbumCertRef_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaLudacrisChicken-N-BeeralbumCertRef-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> In Ireland, the album debuted and peaked at number 71 on the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Albums_Chart" title="Irish Albums Chart">Irish Albums Chart</a>, before exiting the chart the following week: to date, it remains Ludacris' only album to appear on the chart.<sup id="cite_ref-IREcharts_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IREcharts-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In the United Kingdom, <i>Chicken-n-Beer</i> achieved a peak position of number 44 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a>, spending a total of four weeks on the chart – the longest length of time any Ludacris album has lasted on the chart.<sup id="cite_ref-UKAl_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UKAl-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> It also became Ludacris' most commercially successful album in Germany, appearing at number 87 on the <a href="/wiki/Media_Control_Charts" title="Media Control Charts">German Albums Chart</a> in the only week in which it charted.<sup id="cite_ref-GERAl_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GERAl-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Upon its release, <i>Chicken-n-Beer</i> received generally positive reviews from music critics.<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album received an <a href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean" class="mw-redirect">average</a> score of 75, based on 10 reviews, which indicates "generally favorable reviews".<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> David Jeffries of <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> noted Ludacris' "lightning-quick phrasing and cutting wit" and "rollicking, all-in-good-fun persona": he went on to commend the eccentric and upbeat nature of Ludacris' music, calling him "one of the few who's actually celebrating something – and having a great time doing it".<sup id="cite_ref-AMGreview_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMGreview-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i>, Neil Drumming praised Ludacris' rapping skills, including his "explosive enunciation", "witty wordplay" and "punchline potency", although he reacted negatively towards the album's production, feeling it to contain "campy sampling and cartoonish composition".<sup id="cite_ref-EWreview_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EWreview-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Rabin" title="Nathan Rabin">Nathan Rabin</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i> complimented Ludacris' "impeccable comic timing" and his ability to "wring the most out of every line", although he also noted the album to be Ludacris' most "emotional, autobiographical major-label release to date".<sup id="cite_ref-AVClubreview_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AVClubreview-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Although he dismissed much of the album's production as "duds", <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> writer Toure praised Ludacris' musical style as appealing to genres outside hip-hop music, calling him "cocksure, witty and just hard enough to be taken seriously" yet "playful enough to be pop":<sup id="cite_ref-Toure_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toure-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> he further noted Ludacris to be one of the most technically skilled rappers of the time, stating:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Ludacris is one of the most liquid MCs in the game today. He varies his flows with such dexterity and has so much musicality in his tones that his mouth truly seems like an instrument. Ludacris is an outsize character – he's Mr. Fantastic from the Fantastic Four, perhaps a Southern <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>, a guy who started as an Atlanta radio DJ but whose third album... finds him part of the hip-hop establishment.<sup id="cite_ref-Toure_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toure-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="templatequotecite">—Toure</div>
</blockquote>
<p>However, some felt the adult humour displayed on many the songs was excessive, and detracted from Ludacris' technical abilities. Jeffries called Ludacris' personality "cartoonish":<sup id="cite_ref-AMGreview_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMGreview-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Drumming noted "too many cuts are simply one-note jokes stretched out to three or four minutes", and criticized the album's female-orientated material as "cringeworthy, misogynistic snorefests", but admitted Ludacris to be "a funny guy" and called many of the featured insults and boasts "gut-busting... for days".<sup id="cite_ref-EWreview_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EWreview-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> In contrast, Rabin described Ludacris as "fun incarnate, the joyous embodiment of rap's commitment to hedonism at all costs" and felt that few rappers covered sexually-orientated topics as well as Ludacris, "writing few of his peers mine the well-worn topics of freaky sex and drugged-out debauchery with such irreverent glee".<sup id="cite_ref-AVClubreview_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AVClubreview-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
