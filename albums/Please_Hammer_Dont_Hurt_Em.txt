<p><i><b>Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em</b></i> is the third album (and second major-label release) by <a href="/wiki/MC_Hammer" title="MC Hammer">MC Hammer</a>, released between January 1, 1990<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> - February 12, 1990<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a>. The album was produced, recorded, and <a href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(recorded_music)" title="Audio mixing (recorded music)">mixed</a> by <a href="/wiki/Felton_Pilate" title="Felton Pilate">Felton Pilate</a> and James Earley.</p>
<p>The album ranked Number One for 21 weeks on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a>, due primarily to the success of the <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">single</a>, "<a href="/wiki/U_Can%27t_Touch_This" title="U Can't Touch This">U Can't Touch This</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The song has been and continues to be used in many movies and television shows to date, and appears on <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack" title="Soundtrack">soundtrack</a>/<a href="/wiki/Compilation_albums" title="Compilation albums" class="mw-redirect">compilation albums</a> as well. Likewise, the album saw longevity on the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart at number one because it peaked there for 28 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Hammer being good friends with <a href="/wiki/Arsenio_Hall" title="Arsenio Hall">Arsenio Hall</a>, was invited to first perform "U Can't Touch This" prior to its release, on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Arsenio_Hall_Show" title="The Arsenio Hall Show">The Arsenio Hall Show</a></i> in late 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> He also performed "Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em", a song that didn't make it on this album, but did appear in his <a href="/wiki/Please_Hammer,_Don%27t_Hurt_%27Em#Film" title="Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em">movie by the same name</a>.</p>
<p>The album singles released all proved to be successful on radio and video television, with "U Can't Touch This", "Pray", "Have You Seen Her", "Here Comes the Hammer" and "Yo!! Sweetness" (<a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK</a> only) all charting. The album raised <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rap music</a> to a new level of popularity. It was the first hip-hop album <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">certified</a> <a href="/wiki/Diamond_album" title="Diamond album" class="mw-redirect">diamond</a> by the <a href="/wiki/RIAA" title="RIAA" class="mw-redirect">RIAA</a> for sales of over ten million.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It remains one of the genre's all-time best-selling albums.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> To date, the album has sold as many as 18 million units.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Album_details">Album details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Please_Hammer,_Don%27t_Hurt_%27Em&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Album details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Notorious for <a href="/wiki/Diss_track" title="Diss track">dissing</a> rappers in his previous recordings, Hammer appropriately titled his third album (and second major-label release) <i>Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-rapreviews_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rapreviews-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> which was released January 1, 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> It included the successful single "<a href="/wiki/U_Can%27t_Touch_This" title="U Can't Touch This">U Can't Touch This</a>" (which <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampled</a> <a href="/wiki/Rick_James" title="Rick James">Rick James</a>' 1981 "<a href="/wiki/Super_Freak" title="Super Freak">Super Freak</a>"). It was produced, recorded, and <a href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(recorded_music)" title="Audio mixing (recorded music)">mixed</a> by <a href="/wiki/Felton_Pilate" title="Felton Pilate">Felton Pilate</a> and James Earley on a <a href="/wiki/Customised_buses" title="Customised buses">modified tour bus</a> (while on tour) in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite heavy airplay and a #27 chart debut, "U Can't Touch This" stopped at #8 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> chart because it was released only as a twelve-inch single.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, the album was a #1 success for 21 weeks, due primarily to this single, the first time ever for a rap recording on the <a href="/wiki/Record_chart" title="Record chart">pop charts</a>. The song has been and continues to be used in many movies and television shows to date, and appears on <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack" title="Soundtrack">soundtrack</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compilation_albums" title="Compilation albums" class="mw-redirect">compilation albums</a> as well.</p>
<p>Follow-up successes included "<a href="/wiki/Have_You_Seen_Her" title="Have You Seen Her">Have You Seen Her</a>" (a <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Chi-Lites" title="The Chi-Lites">the Chi-Lites</a>) and "<a href="/wiki/Pray_(MC_Hammer_song)" title="Pray (MC Hammer song)">Pray</a>" (a beat sampled from <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince's</a> "<a href="/wiki/When_Doves_Cry" title="When Doves Cry">When Doves Cry</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Faith_No_More" title="Faith No More">Faith No More</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/We_Care_a_Lot" title="We Care a Lot">We Care a Lot</a>"),<sup id="cite_ref-RS_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RS-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> which was his biggest hit in the US, peaking at #2. "Pray" was also a major <a href="/wiki/UK" title="UK" class="mw-redirect">UK</a> success, peaking at #8. The album was notable for <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampling</a> other high-profile artists and gave some of these artists a new fanbase. "Dancin' Machine" sampled <a href="/wiki/The_Jackson_5" title="The Jackson 5">The Jackson 5</a>, "Help the Children" (also the name of an <a href="/wiki/Outreach" title="Outreach">outreach</a> <a href="/wiki/Foundation_(nonprofit_organization)" title="Foundation (nonprofit organization)" class="mw-redirect">foundation</a> Hammer started)<sup id="cite_ref-macysinc_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-macysinc-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> interpolates <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Gaye" title="Marvin Gaye">Marvin Gaye</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Mercy_Mercy_Me_(The_Ecology)" title="Mercy Mercy Me (The Ecology)">Mercy Mercy Me (The Ecology)</a>", and "She's Soft and Wet" also sampled Prince's "<a href="/wiki/Soft_and_Wet" title="Soft and Wet">Soft and Wet</a>". All of these songs proved to be successful on radio and video television, with "U Can't Touch This," "Pray" (most successful), "Have You Seen Her," "Here Comes the Hammer," and "Yo!! Sweetness" (<a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK</a> only) all charting.</p>
<p>During 1990, Hammer toured extensively in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> which included a sold-out concert at the <a href="/wiki/National_Exhibition_Centre" title="National Exhibition Centre">National Exhibition Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>. With the sponsorship of <a href="/wiki/PepsiCo" title="PepsiCo">PepsiCo</a> International, <a href="/wiki/Pepsi" title="Pepsi">Pepsi-Cola</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Christopher_A._Sinclair" title="Christopher A. Sinclair">Christopher A. Sinclair</a> went on tour with him during 1991. By June 1991, the album sold 14.5 million copies worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> It would go on to become the first hip-hop album to earn <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">diamond status</a>, selling more than 18 million units to date.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The album increased the popularity of hip-hop music. It remains the genre's all-time best-selling album.<sup id="cite_ref-sing365_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sing365-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a> of hit singles, the album cost just $10,000 to produce.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The video for "Here Comes the Hammer" proved to be the most expensive video on this album, Hammer's second most expensive behind "<a href="/wiki/Too_Legit_to_Quit_(song)" title="Too Legit to Quit (song)" class="mw-redirect">Too Legit to Quit</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album's <a href="/wiki/Liner_notes" title="Liner notes">liner notes</a> contains a "Special dedication to the victims of the <a href="/wiki/1989_Loma_Prieta_earthquake" title="1989 Loma Prieta earthquake">California Earthquake of 1989</a> and the victims of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hugo" title="Hurricane Hugo">Hurricane Hugo</a>".</p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Critical_thinking" title="Critical thinking">critical</a> <a href="/wiki/Backlash_(sociology)" title="Backlash (sociology)">backlash</a> began over the repetitive nature of his lyrics, his clean-cut image, and his perceived over-reliance on using <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hooks</a> from other artists for the basis of his singles—<a href="/wiki/Criticism" title="Criticism">criticisms</a> that were also directed at his contemporary, <a href="/wiki/Vanilla_Ice" title="Vanilla Ice">Vanilla Ice</a>. He was mocked in <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music videos</a> by <a href="/wiki/3rd_Bass" title="3rd Bass">3rd Bass</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_D.O.C." title="The D.O.C.">The D.O.C.</a>, DJ Debranz, and <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>. <a href="/wiki/Oakland" title="Oakland" class="mw-redirect">Oakland</a> hip-hop group <a href="/wiki/Digital_Underground" title="Digital Underground">Digital Underground</a> mocked him in the CD insert of its <i><a href="/wiki/Sex_Packets" title="Sex Packets">Sex Packets</a></i> album when placing his picture in with the other members and referring to him as an unknown <a href="/wiki/Homelessness" title="Homelessness">derelict</a>. <a href="/wiki/LL_Cool_J" title="LL Cool J">LL Cool J</a> mocked him in "To tha Break of Dawn," a track on his <i><a href="/wiki/Mama_Said_Knock_You_Out" title="Mama Said Knock You Out">Mama Said Knock You Out</a></i> album, calling Hammer an "<a href="/wiki/Amateur" title="Amateur">amateur</a>, swinging a Hammer from a <a href="/wiki/Bodybag" title="Bodybag" class="mw-redirect">bodybag</a> [his pants]," and saying, "My old gym teacher ain't supposed to rap." (LL Cool J would later compliment and commend Hammer's abilities/talents on <a href="/wiki/VH-1" title="VH-1" class="mw-redirect">VH-1</a>'s <i>100 Greatest Songs of Hip Hop</i>, which aired in 2008.)</p>
<p>However, <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a> came to Hammer's defense on his 1991 album <i><a href="/wiki/O.G._Original_Gangster" title="O.G. Original Gangster">O.G. Original Gangster</a></i>: "A special shout-out to my man M.C. Hammer: a lot of people dis you, man, but they just jealous." Ice-T later explained that he had nothing against people who were <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">pop-rap</a> from the start, as Hammer had been, but only against <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Ceremonies" title="Master of Ceremonies" class="mw-redirect">emcees</a> who switch from being <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_hip_hop" title="Hardcore hip hop">hardcore</a> or "dirty" to being pop-rap so they can sell more records.</p>
<p><i>Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em</i> was also criticized for its <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampling</a> of other musicians' songs.<sup id="cite_ref-RS_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RS-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The album sampled high-profile artists and gave some of these artists a new fanbase as a result. "U Can't Touch This" sampled "<a href="/wiki/Super_Freak" title="Super Freak">Super Freak</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Rick_James" title="Rick James">Rick James</a>; "<a href="/wiki/Dancing_Machine" title="Dancing Machine">Dancin' Machine</a>" sampled the <a href="/wiki/Jackson_5" title="Jackson 5" class="mw-redirect">Jackson 5</a>; "<a href="/wiki/Have_You_Seen_Her" title="Have You Seen Her">Have You Seen Her</a>" is a semi-cover of <a href="/wiki/The_Chi-Lites" title="The Chi-Lites">The Chi-Lites</a> song; "Help the Children" interpolates <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Gaye" title="Marvin Gaye">Marvin Gaye</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Mercy_Mercy_Me_(The_Ecology)" title="Mercy Mercy Me (The Ecology)">Mercy Mercy Me (The Ecology)</a>"; "<a href="/wiki/Pray_(MC_Hammer_song)" title="Pray (MC Hammer song)">Pray</a>" and "She's Soft and Wet" sample the <a href="/wiki/Prince_(artist)" title="Prince (artist)" class="mw-redirect">Prince</a> songs "<a href="/wiki/When_Doves_Cry" title="When Doves Cry">When Doves Cry</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Soft_and_Wet" title="Soft and Wet">Soft and Wet</a>" respectively.</p>
<p>Despite the criticisms, MC Hammer's career continued to be highly successful including tours in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. Soon after, MC Hammer <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a> dolls, lunchboxes, and other merchandise were marketed. He was also given his own <a href="/wiki/Saturday_morning_cartoon" title="Saturday morning cartoon" class="mw-redirect">Saturday morning cartoon</a>, called <i><a href="/wiki/Hammerman" title="Hammerman">Hammerman</a></i>, which he hosted and <a href="/wiki/Voice_acting" title="Voice acting">voiced</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AWL_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AWL-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_in_pop_culture">Impact in pop culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Please_Hammer,_Don%27t_Hurt_%27Em&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact in pop culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album title was often used as a chant by the crowd during live performances. ("Please, Hammer, don't hurt 'em..."!) Additionally, "Hammer Time" (from the track "U Can't Touch This") became a major <a href="/wiki/Pop_culture" title="Pop culture" class="mw-redirect">pop culture</a> phrase and used in many television shows and movies, eventually becoming Hammer's <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a> and the title of his own <a href="/wiki/Reality_television" title="Reality television">reality show</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Hammertime" title="Hammertime">Hammertime</a></i>. "Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em" (featuring vocals by <a href="/wiki/Teddy_Riley" title="Teddy Riley">Teddy Riley</a>) was a track used in Hammer's <a href="/wiki/Please_Hammer,_Don%27t_Hurt_%27Em#Film" title="Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em">film by the same name</a>. However, the song was never released as a single.</p>
<p>The song "U Can't Touch This" has been used in many shows and movies, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fresh_Prince_of_Bel-Air" title="The Fresh Prince of Bel-Air">The Fresh Prince of Bel-Air</a></i> (1990), <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Shots!" title="Hot Shots!">Hot Shots!</a></i> (1990), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Super" title="The Super">The Super</a></i> (1991), <i><a href="/wiki/Doogie_Howser,_M.D." title="Doogie Howser, M.D.">Doogie Howser, M.D.</a></i> (1992), <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Be_a_Menace_to_South_Central_While_Drinking_Your_Juice_in_the_Hood" title="Don't Be a Menace to South Central While Drinking Your Juice in the Hood">Don't Be a Menace to South Central While Drinking Your Juice in the Hood</a></i> (1996), <i><a href="/wiki/Charlie%27s_Angels:_Full_Throttle" title="Charlie's Angels: Full Throttle">Charlie's Angels: Full Throttle</a></i> (2003), <i><a href="/wiki/Into_the_Wild_(film)" title="Into the Wild (film)">Into the Wild</a></i> (2007), <i><a href="/wiki/Tropic_Thunder" title="Tropic Thunder">Tropic Thunder</a></i> (2008), <i><a href="/wiki/Dancing_with_the_Stars" title="Dancing with the Stars">Dancing with the Stars</a></i> (2009), <i><a href="/wiki/Glee_(TV_series)" title="Glee (TV series)">Glee</a></i> (2010) and many more.</p>
<p>Additionally, during this time, "This Is What We Do" was a 1990 track released by Hammer (featuring <a href="/wiki/B_Angie_B" title="B Angie B">B Angie B</a>) for the <i><a href="/wiki/Teenage_Mutant_Ninja_Turtles_(1990_film)" title="Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles (1990 film)">Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles</a></i> film and <a href="/wiki/Teenage_Mutant_Ninja_Turtles_(soundtrack)" title="Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles (soundtrack)" class="mw-redirect">soundtrack</a>. Other songs from this album featured in shows and/or film were "<a href="/wiki/Pray_(MC_Hammer_song)" title="Pray (MC Hammer song)">Pray</a>" (<i><a href="/wiki/License_to_Wed" title="License to Wed">License to Wed</a></i>) and "Let's Go Deeper" (<i><a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills,_90210" title="Beverly Hills, 90210">Beverly Hills, 90210</a></i>), among others.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Please_Hammer,_Don%27t_Hurt_%27Em&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rick_James" title="Rick James">Rick James</a> sued Hammer for <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">infringement of copyright</a> on the song "<a href="/wiki/U_Can%27t_Touch_This" title="U Can't Touch This">U Can't Touch This</a>", but the suit was settled out of court when Hammer agreed to credit James as co-composer, effectively cutting James in on the millions of dollars the record was earning. Hammer was also sued by a former producer, <a href="/wiki/Felton_Pilate" title="Felton Pilate">Felton Pilate</a> (who is also a member of the successful R&amp;B band, <a href="/wiki/Con_Funk_Shun" title="Con Funk Shun">Con Funk Shun</a>) and by several of his former backers, and faced charges that performance troupe members endured an abusive, militaristic atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, Hammer also admitted in <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(law)" title="Deposition (law)">depositions</a> and court documents to getting the idea for the song "Here Comes The Hammer" from a <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_Christian_music" title="Contemporary Christian music">Christian recording artist</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas" title="Dallas, Texas" class="mw-redirect">Dallas, Texas</a> named Kevin Christian. Christian had filed a US$16 million <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">lawsuit</a> against Hammer for <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a> for his song entitled "Oh-Oh, You Got The Shing". This fact compounded with witness testimony from both Hammer's and Christian's entourages and other evidence including photos brought about a settlement with <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a> in 1994. The terms of the <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">settlement</a> remain sealed. Hammer settled with Christian the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Please_Hammer,_Don%27t_Hurt_%27Em&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em</i> album was accompanied by a film, called <i>Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em: The Movie</i> (1990),<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> which included footage used for many of <a href="/wiki/MC_Hammer" title="MC Hammer">M.C. Hammer</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music videos</a> from the album.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Long_Form_Music_Video#Recipients" title="Grammy Award for Best Long Form Music Video" class="mw-redirect">long form music video movie</a> is about a rapper, played by Hammer himself, returning to his hometown and defeating a <a href="/wiki/Drug_lord" title="Drug lord">drug lord</a> who is using kids to <a href="/wiki/Illegal_drug_trade" title="Illegal drug trade">traffic</a> his product. Along with his dance tunes being featured, he also plays a <a href="/wiki/Preacher" title="Preacher">preacher</a> character.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cast">Cast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Please_Hammer,_Don%27t_Hurt_%27Em&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film starred M.C. Hammer as himself and as "<a href="/wiki/MC_Hammer#Christian_beliefs_and_pastoral_ministry" title="MC Hammer">Reverend Pressure</a>". It also featured Juice Sneeed, Keyon White, Joe Mack and Davina H'Ollier. Michele Jennings appeared as one of the dancers.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Please_Hammer,_Don%27t_Hurt_%27Em&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A story, featuring music from <i>Please Hammer, Don't Hurt 'Em</i>, about Hammer returning to his home of <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland, California</a> to confront a <a href="/wiki/Illegal_drug_trade" title="Illegal drug trade">drug pusher</a> who is victimizing his old neighborhood.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_awards">Music awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Please_Hammer,_Don%27t_Hurt_%27Em&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Music awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The movie won Hammer, <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Wainwright" title="Rupert Wainwright">Rupert Wainwright</a> (director) and John Oetjen (video producer) a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Long_Form_Music_Video" title="Grammy Award for Best Long Form Music Video" class="mw-redirect">Best Music Video, Long Form</a> at the <a href="/wiki/33rd_Grammy_Awards#Music_video" title="33rd Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">33rd Grammy Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Besides Hammer, music talent included Ho Frat Hoo! (<a href="/wiki/1991_MTV_Video_Music_Awards" title="1991 MTV Video Music Awards">1991 MTV Video Music Awards</a> <a href="/wiki/1991_MTV_Video_Music_Awards#Best_Choreography_in_a_Video" title="1991 MTV Video Music Awards">Best Choreography in a Video</a> winner for "<a href="/wiki/Pray_(MC_Hammer_song)" title="Pray (MC Hammer song)">Pray (Jam the Hammer Mix)</a>" along with Hammer), Torture, <a href="/wiki/Special_Generation_(band)" title="Special Generation (band)">Special Generation</a> and One Cause One Effect.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Releases">Releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Please_Hammer,_Don%27t_Hurt_%27Em&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Originally released on <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> (on July 1, 1991), the movie can now be found on <a href="/wiki/Youtube" title="Youtube" class="mw-redirect">Youtube</a> or purchased online through services such as <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_LLC" title="Blockbuster LLC">Blockbuster.com</a>, along with <i><b>The Making of Please Hammer Don't Hurt 'Em</b></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Additional VHS releases (each approximately 60 minutes of Hammer's music videos) during this time were: <i>Hammer Time</i> (1990) and <i>Here Comes the Hammer</i> (1991).<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> All projects were <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a> Productions.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
