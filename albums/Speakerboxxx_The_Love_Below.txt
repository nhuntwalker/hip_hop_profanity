<p><i><b>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</b></i> is the fifth studio album by American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> duo <a href="/wiki/OutKast" title="OutKast" class="mw-redirect">OutKast</a>, released September 23, 2003 on <a href="/wiki/LaFace_Records" title="LaFace Records">LaFace Records</a>. Issued as a double album, its playtime of more than two hours is distributed over solo albums from each of the group's members. <i>Speakerboxxx</i> is the solo project of <a href="/wiki/Big_Boi" title="Big Boi">Big Boi</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Southern_hip_hop" title="Southern hip hop">Southern hip hop</a> album with a <a href="/wiki/P-Funk" title="P-Funk">P-Funk</a> influence, while <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_3000" title="Andr√© 3000">Andr√© 3000</a>'s <i>The Love Below</i> features <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_music" title="Psychedelic music">psychedelic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electro_(music)" title="Electro (music)">electro</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> styles.<sup id="cite_ref-Southall_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Southall-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i> received acclaim from most music critics, earning praise for the consistency of Big Boi's <i>Speakerboxxx</i> and the eclectic musical style of Andr√© 3000's <i>The Love Below</i>. The album was supported with the hit singles "<a href="/wiki/Hey_Ya!" title="Hey Ya!">Hey Ya!</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/The_Way_You_Move" title="The Way You Move">The Way You Move</a>", which both reached number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>. It is the second hip-hop album to win a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Album_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Album of the Year">Album of the Year</a>. In 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> ranked <i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i> number 44 on its list of the top 100 greatest albums of the decade,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> while <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> ranked the album number one on its list of the ten best albums of the decade.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i> has been <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">certified</a> <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">diamond</a> and 11 times <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> for shipping more than 11 million units (in this case, 5.5 million double album sets, which are double-counted by the RIAA).<sup id="cite_ref-riaa.com_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-riaa.com-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_recording">Background and recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speakerboxxx/The_Love_Below&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the release of OutKast's fourth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Stankonia" title="Stankonia">Stankonia</a></i> (2000), Andr√© 3000 felt urged to do something different from his previous projects and moved to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> to pursue an acting career. He was relatively unsuccessful, obtaining a minor role in <i><a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Homicide" title="Hollywood Homicide">Hollywood Homicide</a></i> (2003) and a one-episode appearance in the drama series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shield" title="The Shield">The Shield</a></i>. He quickly returned to music and began recording a solo album that was far different to the material he had recorded as part of OutKast. The output was a blend of <a href="/wiki/Pop_(music)" title="Pop (music)" class="mw-redirect">pop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a> with live instruments and singing instead of <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapping</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> When writing songs he used a <a href="/wiki/Cassette_recorder" title="Cassette recorder" class="mw-redirect">micro cassette recorder</a> in order to "record melodic ideas and lyrics, then build the melody around the lyrics".<sup id="cite_ref-XXL_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The recording of <i>The Love Below</i> began at Andr√© 3000's Los Angeles home, which was unconventional for the time, but he was enabled to do so by the recent release of <a href="/wiki/Pro_Tools" title="Pro Tools">Pro Tools</a> software.<sup id="cite_ref-XXL_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> As a frequent collector of music equipment, Andr√© 3000 had a lot of equipment at his disposal, including a drum machine, keyboards and various synthesizers.<sup id="cite_ref-SOS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOS-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> He also enjoyed the atmosphere of recording at home instead of a studio, saying to <i><a href="/wiki/XXL_(magazine)" title="XXL (magazine)">XXL</a></i>, "it didn't start in the studio because if you have a bunch of people around, they‚Äôre coming from the party and I'm in there singing falsetto ... those vibes didn't match." His initial sessions were hampered by his inexperience with Pro Tools and, unaware how to edit his recordings, he opted to record songs such as "Pink &amp; Blue" in their entirety.<sup id="cite_ref-XXL_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Other software used included an Avalon VT737 SP and AD2055 EQ and AD2044 compressors for his vocals.<sup id="cite_ref-SOS_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOS-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> After creating five songs, he informed Big Boi of the solo project he had been working on.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Big Boi had already recorded some songs when Andr√© 3000 had contacted him, but after their conversation he decided his next project would be <i>Speakerboxxx</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Describing his approach in the studio, Big Boi later commented to <i>XXL</i>, "the idea was just to keep it funky, keep it jamming, it‚Äôs always bass-heavy. And lyricism, it's all about lyrics, taking pride in your pen and your pad." His favorite song to record was "Unhappy". He spent several days working on "Unhappy"'s hook before driving to his mother's home and playing the song in her driveway, to which she responded enthusiastically.<sup id="cite_ref-XXL_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> At some point in the recording, the project moved to OutKast's Stankonia Studios in downtown <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, which had been used to record OutKast's previous release and namesake. John Frye, the studio manager and an engineer, would later recognise that much of the media attention surrounding the album's recording was concerned with Andr√© 3000 and Big Boi's working relationship and why they had chosen to record separately. He concedes that both enjoyed working solo and were doing so more frequently, but they continued to share and critique each other's music.<sup id="cite_ref-SOS_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOS-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>John Frye also describes how the format of the projects changed rapidly. Initially intended as two separate solo releases, they decided to merge their work and create a soundtrack album as Andr√© 3000 had initially intended. The duo then began preparing to work on a motion picture, but reconsidered and compromised by interpolating background noise into songs, such as the slamming of car doors and footsteps.<sup id="cite_ref-XXL_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SOS_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOS-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> They eventually settled on releasing a <a href="/wiki/Double_album" title="Double album">double album</a>. Frye noted the end of the recording sessions as particularly stressful for Andr√© 3000, who he described as drained from working at four studios simulatenously. In total, an estimated 120 songs were recorded for <i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-SOS_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOS-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speakerboxxx/The_Love_Below&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below'</i> is a two-disc set that features thirty-nine tracks, including several <a href="/wiki/Interlude" title="Interlude">interludes</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Postlude" title="Postlude">postlude</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-discogs_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discogs-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> with the intention of each disc delivering each member's individual perspective and sound.<sup id="cite_ref-XXL_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Love Below</i> is substantially longer than Big Boi's <i>Speakerboxxx</i>, clocking in at almost 78 minutes, compared to 56 minutes for <i>Speakerboxxx</i>. Featured guests on <i>Speakerboxxx</i> include <a href="/wiki/Sleepy_Brown" title="Sleepy Brown">Sleepy Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazze_Pha" title="Jazze Pha">Jazze Pha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cee-Lo_Green" title="Cee-Lo Green" class="mw-redirect">Cee-Lo Green</a>, <a href="/wiki/Killer_Mike" title="Killer Mike">Killer Mike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goodie_Mob" title="Goodie Mob">Goodie Mob</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lil%27_Jon" title="Lil' Jon" class="mw-redirect">Lil' Jon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>. Guests on <i>The Love Below</i> include <a href="/wiki/Rosario_Dawson" title="Rosario Dawson">Rosario Dawson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norah_Jones" title="Norah Jones">Norah Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kelis" title="Kelis">Kelis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fonzworth_Bentley" title="Fonzworth Bentley">Fonzworth Bentley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-discogs_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discogs-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Speakerboxxx</i> is built on <a href="/wiki/Southern_hip_hop" title="Southern hip hop">Southern hip hop</a><sup id="cite_ref-sarig_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Speakerboxxx</i> demonstrated more social awareness than its counterpart, with themes of family, philosophy, religion, politics and "a wider emotional terrain ... from melancholy to outrage to expression."<sup id="cite_ref-sarig_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In contrast, <i>The Love Below</i> was identified as far more musically experimental. Its sound was described as jazz and funk with comparisons to the work of <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The disc's abounding theme is <a href="/wiki/Love" title="Love">love</a>, examining the emotions one experiences when falling in love and loving oneself.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Roni Sarig suggests that Andr√© 3000's split with <a href="/wiki/Neo_soul" title="Neo soul">neo soul</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Erykah_Badu" title="Erykah Badu">Erykah Badu</a> had influenced much of the lyrical content on the album, which he sees as concerned with the search for true love.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i> received acclaim from contemporary music critics.<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album received an <a href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean" class="mw-redirect">average</a> score of 91, based on 25 reviews, which indicates "universal acclaim".<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> writer Stephen Thomas Erlewine called both discs "visionary, imaginative listens, providing some of the best music of 2003, regardless of genre".<sup id="cite_ref-Erlewine_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Erlewine-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Will Hermes commented that the album's "ambition flies so far beyond that of anyone doing rap right now (or pop, or rock, or R&amp;B)".<sup id="cite_ref-Hermes_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermes-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Kris Ex of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> called it "a cohesive statement" and praised both discs' musical quality.<sup id="cite_ref-Ex_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ex-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In his review for <i><a href="/wiki/Blender_(magazine)" title="Blender (magazine)">Blender</a></i>, Ex gave the album five out of five stars and commented that it "holds an explosion of creativity that couldn‚Äôt have been contained in just one LP".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Dorian Lynskey described both discs as "sublime ... hip-hop's <i><a href="/wiki/Sign_o%27_the_Times_(album)" title="Sign o' the Times (album)" class="mw-redirect">Sign o' the Times</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beatles_(album)" title="The Beatles (album)">The White Album</a></i>: a career-defining masterpiece of breathtaking ambition".<sup id="cite_ref-Lynskey_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lynskey-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Andy Gill of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> asserted that the album "sets a new benchmark not just for hip hop, but for pop in general".<sup id="cite_ref-Gill_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gill-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a>'s Nick Southall called it "a series of spectacular moments and memorable events".<sup id="cite_ref-Southall_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Southall-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Greg_Tate" title="Greg Tate">Greg Tate</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i> drew comparisons to <a href="/wiki/P-Funk" title="P-Funk">P-Funk</a> on <i>Speakerboxxx</i> and to <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a> on <i>The Love Below</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tate_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tate-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> In his consumer guide for <i>The Village Voice</i>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave the album an A- rating,<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> indicating "the kind of garden-variety good record that is the great luxury of musical micromarketing and overproduction."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> He viewed that the album could have been "the classic P-Funk rip it ain't quite" had <i>Speakerboxxx</i> alone been issued with "Roses", "Spread", "Hey Ya!", and "an oddity of [Andr√© 3000's] choosing", but nonetheless commended its "commercial ebullience, creative confidence, and wretched excess, blessed excess, impressive excess".<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In a mixed review, <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> writer Jon Caramanica was ambivalent towards Andr√© 3000 expressing his "right to be peculiar in a hip-hop context".<sup id="cite_ref-Caramanica_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caramanica-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Sal Cinquemani of <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a> felt that the album's indulgence was a weakness, but commended OutKast's ambition and stated, "<i>The Love Below</i> is more consistent than <i>Speakerboxxx</i>. Still, as was probably intended, the double-album is greater than the sum of its parts, and this kind of expertly crafted pop and deftly executed funk rarely happen at the same time‚Äînot since <i>Stankonia</i>, at least."<sup id="cite_ref-Cinquemani_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cinquemani-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> By contrast, Brent DiCrescenzo found <i>Speakerboxxx</i> to be superior to <i>The Love Below</i> and wrote that the former "manages to maintain consistent brilliance and emotional complexity throughout".<sup id="cite_ref-DiCrescenzo_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DiCrescenzo-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> John Mulvey of <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> described its two discs as "two Technicolor explosions of creativity that people will be exploring, analysing and partying to for years".<sup id="cite_ref-Mulvey_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mulvey-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Cynthia Fuchs of <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a> described it as "imperfect and ambitious, sometimes startling and always smart".<sup id="cite_ref-Fuchs_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fuchs-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> In a retrospective review, <i>Rolling Stone</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Roni Sarig gave the album four-and-a-half out of five stars and stated, "[F]or sheer breadth, ambition, and musical vision, there's little doubt <i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i> is a classic."<sup id="cite_ref-Hoard_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoard-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speakerboxxx/The_Love_Below&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i> was voted as the best album of the year in <a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a> <a href="/wiki/Pazz_%26_Jop" title="Pazz &amp; Jop">Pazz &amp; Jop</a> critics poll. The album was nominated for six <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards" title="Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">Grammy Awards</a>, winning three (<a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Album_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Album of the Year">Album of the Year</a>, Best Urban/Alternative Performance for "Hey Ya!", and <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Best Rap Album</a>). OutKast's other nominations were for Producer of the Year, and Best Short-Form Music Video and Record of the Year, both for "Hey Ya!". <i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i> was the second hip hop album to receive the Grammy for Album of the Year (following <i><a href="/wiki/The_Miseducation_of_Lauryn_Hill" title="The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill">The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</a></i> in 1999). In Australia, "<a href="/wiki/Hey_Ya!" title="Hey Ya!">Hey Ya!</a>" was voted number two on the 2003 <a href="/wiki/Triple_J_Hottest_100" title="Triple J Hottest 100">Triple J Hottest 100</a>, the country's biggest alternative music poll of its type. The jazz periodical <a href="/wiki/Down_Beat" title="Down Beat">Down Beat</a> chose it as the best "beyond" album. In 2012 <a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a> named the album one of the classic albums of the last decade.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2013, <a href="/wiki/New_Musical_Express" title="New Musical Express" class="mw-redirect">NME</a> ranked <i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i> as #183 on their list of the 500 greatest albums of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speakerboxxx/The_Love_Below&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After having had three number two-albums on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, OutKast enjoyed their first chart-topping album with <i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i>. The album debuted at number one during the week of October 11, 2003, selling more than 510,000 copies in its first week. It became the second-biggest debut for a double album during the <a href="/wiki/Soundscan" title="Soundscan" class="mw-redirect">Soundscan</a>-era (beginning in 1991). The album sold 235,000 copies in its second week, holding its position atop the Billboard chart. <i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i> spent the next three weeks in the top 5 before returning to the top spot for one more week. Sales remained strong, and the album would spend another four weeks at #1 between January and February 2004. In all, <i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i> amassed a total of seven weeks at #1, 24 weeks in the Top 10, and 56 weeks on the Billboard 200. <i>Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</i> has been <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">certified</a> <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">diamond</a> and 11 times <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> for shipping more than 11 million units (in this case, 5.5 million double album sets, which are double-counted by the RIAA).<sup id="cite_ref-riaa.com_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-riaa.com-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The single "<a href="/wiki/Hey_Ya!" title="Hey Ya!">Hey Ya!</a>" went to number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> in the United States, topping the charts there for 9 weeks. It was the act's second #1 single, following 2001's "<a href="/wiki/Ms._Jackson" title="Ms. Jackson">Ms. Jackson</a>". "Hey Ya!" also topped the singles charts in Canada and Australia, and charted in 28 countries around the world. "Hey Ya!" was also the first platinum download on iTunes. Follow-up single "The Way You Move" knocked "Hey Ya!" off the top of the charts in the U.S. in February 2004, just the seventh time a recording act replaced itself at number one. "The Way You Move" topped the singles chart for one week. The third single released from the album was "Roses" from <i>The Love Below,</i> which reached #5. The fourth and fifth singles released, "Prototype" (<i>The Love Below</i>) and "GhettoMusick" (<i>Speakerboxxx</i>), did not chart.</p>

<dt>Note</dt>
<dd>In 2003, the album was reissued, with "The Love Below" having a revised track listing. A 21 second skit was placed before "My Favorite Things", entitled "The Letter". To make room, the radio excerpt from "A Life in the Day of Benjamin Andr√©" was excised, shortening the track to 4:50. This revised album now serves as the version for sale on MP3.</dd>
