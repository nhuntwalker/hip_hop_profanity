<p><i><b>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</b></i> is the debut <a href="/wiki/Solo_album" title="Solo album">solo album</a> by American recording artist <a href="/wiki/Lauryn_Hill" title="Lauryn Hill">Lauryn Hill</a>, released on August 25, 1998, by <a href="/wiki/Ruffhouse_Records" title="Ruffhouse Records">Ruffhouse Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>. After touring with former group the Fugees, Hill met <a href="/wiki/Rohan_Marley" title="Rohan Marley">Rohan Marley</a>, and became pregnant with his child. The pregnancy, along with several other circumstances, would inspire her to make a solo record which would eventually become <i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i>. Recording sessions for the album took place from late 1997 to June 1998, and were held primarily at <a href="/wiki/Tuff_Gong_Studios" title="Tuff Gong Studios" class="mw-redirect">Tuff Gong Studios</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston, Jamaica</a>.</p>
<p><i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Neo_soul" title="Neo soul">neo soul</a> album that incorporates styles such as <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a>. Its lyrics touch upon Hill's pregnancy at the time and the turmoil in her former group the <a href="/wiki/Fugees" title="Fugees">Fugees</a>, along with themes of love and God. The album's title was inspired by the film and autobiographical novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Education_of_Sonny_Carson" title="The Education of Sonny Carson">The Education of Sonny Carson</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Carter_G._Woodson" title="Carter G. Woodson">Carter G. Woodson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mis-Education_of_the_Negro" title="The Mis-Education of the Negro">The Mis-Education of the Negro</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-inside3_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inside3-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Upon its release, <i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i> received rave reviews from music critics who frequently complimented it for Hill's honest presentation of a female's view on life and love. The album debuted at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 422,624 copies in its first week and eventually being certified eight-times-platinum in the U.S. The album's success propelled Hill to superstardom and helped bring hip hop and neo soul to the forefront of popular music. <i>The Miseducation</i> has been ranked in numerous best-album lists by critics. At the <a href="/wiki/41st_Grammy_Awards" title="41st Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">41st Grammy Awards</a>, <i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i> earned her five <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Awards</a>, including the award for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Album_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Album of the Year">Album of the Year</a>.</p>
<p>The album produced three <a href="/wiki/Hit_single" title="Hit single">hit singles</a>—"<a href="/wiki/Doo_Wop_(That_Thing)" title="Doo Wop (That Thing)">Doo Wop (That Thing)</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Ex-Factor" title="Ex-Factor">Ex-Factor</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Everything_Is_Everything_(Lauryn_Hill_song)" title="Everything Is Everything (Lauryn Hill song)">Everything Is Everything</a>". The album's lead single "<a href="/wiki/Doo_Wop_(That_Thing)" title="Doo Wop (That Thing)">Doo Wop (That Thing)</a>" peaked at number one in the US, with the latter two singles peaking with in the top forty of the country. The album was later promoted with an international supporting tour by Hill in 1999.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Miseducation_of_Lauryn_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While on <i>The Score</i> Tour with her former group the <a href="/wiki/Fugees" title="Fugees">Fugees</a>, Hill met <a href="/wiki/Rohan_Marley" title="Rohan Marley">Rohan Marley</a>, son of reggae musician <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley" title="Bob Marley">Bob Marley</a>. The two gradually formed a close relationship, and while on this tour, Hill became pregnant with his child.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> This pregnancy, along with several other circumstances, would inspire her to make a solo record which would eventually become <i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i>. In late 1996, fellow Fugee member <a href="/wiki/Wyclef_Jean" title="Wyclef Jean">Wyclef Jean</a> began writing and recording his debut solo album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Carnival" title="The Carnival">The Carnival</a></i>, which Hill would lend a hand with production, as well as guest verses and vocals. After the album was completed, Hill took time off from touring and recording <sup id="cite_ref-Nickson132_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson132-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> due to her pregnancy and cases of <a href="/wiki/Writer%27s_block" title="Writer's block">writer's block</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This pregnancy, however, would later revive Hill's artistic flow, as she would recall in an interview several years later: "When some women are pregnant, their hair and their nails grow, but for me it was my mind and ability to create. I had the desire to write in a capacity that I hadn't done in a while. I don't know if it's a hormonal or emotional thing.[...] I was very in touch with my feelings at the time."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Hill also went on to say: "Every time I got hurt, every time I was disappointed, every time I learned, I just wrote a song."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While in this creative zone, Hill wrote over 30 songs in her <a href="/wiki/Attic" title="Attic">attic</a> studio in <a href="/wiki/South_Orange" title="South Orange" class="mw-redirect">South Orange</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson132_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson132-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Many of these songs heavily drew upon the turbulence in the Fugees, as well as a past love experience that ended sour. In regards to this, Hill stated: "I spent so many years working at a relationship that didn't work, that I was like; I'm gonna write these songs and pour my heart into them."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In the summer of 1997, as Hill was due to give birth to her first child, she was requested to write a song for gospel musician <a href="/wiki/CeCe_Winans" title="CeCe Winans">CeCe Winans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson132_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson132-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Several months later, she went to Detroit to work with soul legend <a href="/wiki/Aretha_Franklin" title="Aretha Franklin">Aretha Franklin</a>, writing and producing the song "<a href="/wiki/A_Rose_Is_Still_a_Rose_(song)" title="A Rose Is Still a Rose (song)">A Rose is Still a Rose</a>." This song would turn out to be Franklin's up-coming single for her <a href="/wiki/A_Rose_Is_Still_a_Rose" title="A Rose Is Still a Rose">album of the same name</a>, and Aretha would later have Hill direct the song's music video.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Shortly after this, Hill did writing work for <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Houston" title="Whitney Houston">Whitney Houston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Having now written songs for musical acts ranging from hip hop to gospel, to R&amp;B, Hill brought all of these influences and experiences to bear upon an album of her own.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Miseducation_of_Lauryn_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:168px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Julian_Marley_(Cascais_2010)_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Julian_Marley_%28Cascais_2010%29_1.jpg/166px-Julian_Marley_%28Cascais_2010%29_1.jpg" width="166" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Julian_Marley_%28Cascais_2010%29_1.jpg/249px-Julian_Marley_%28Cascais_2010%29_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Julian_Marley_%28Cascais_2010%29_1.jpg/333px-Julian_Marley_%28Cascais_2010%29_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1860" data-file-height="2460" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Julian_Marley_(Cascais_2010)_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.24wmf6/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Julian_Marley" title="Julian Marley">Julian Marley</a> was one of several members of <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley" title="Bob Marley">Bob Marley</a>'s family to be present at recording sessions for <i>The Miseducation</i>.</div>

<p>Recording for the album began in late 1997 at Chung King Studios in New York,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and ended in June 1998 at Tuff Gong Studios in Jamaica.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> In an interview, Hill described the first day of recording, stating: "The first day in the studio I ordered every instrument I ever fell in love with: <a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">harps</a>, <a href="/wiki/String_instrument" title="String instrument">strings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timpani" title="Timpani">timpani drums</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">organs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinets</a>. It was my idea to record it so the human element stayed in. I didn't want it to be too technically perfect."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Initially, Wyclef Jean did not support Hill recording a solo album, but eventually offered his production help, which she did not accept.<sup id="cite_ref-mystery_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mystery-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Aside from doing work at Chung King Studios, Hill also recorded at Perfect Pair Studios in New Jersey, as well as Sony Studios,<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson149_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson149-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> with some songs having different elements recorded at different studios.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson149_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson149-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The bulk of the album, however, was recorded at Tuff Gong Studios in Kingston, Jamaica, the studio built by reggae legend <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley" title="Bob Marley">Bob Marley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson150_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson150-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Regarding this shift in environment, Hill stated: "When I started recording in New York and New Jersey, lots of people were talking to me about going different routes. I could feel people up in my face, and I was picking up on bad vibes. I wanted a place where there was good vibes, where I was among family, and it was Tuff Gong."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Many members of the Marley family were present in the studio during the recording sessions, among them <a href="/wiki/Julian_Marley" title="Julian Marley">Julian Marley</a>, who added guitar elements to "Forgive Them Father."<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson150_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson150-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview, recording engineer Gordon Williams recalled the recording of "Lost Ones", stating: "It was our first morning in Jamaica and I saw all of these kids gathered around Lauryn, screaming and dancing. Lauryn was in the living room next to the studio with about fifteen Marley grandchildren around her, the children of <a href="/wiki/Ziggy_Marley" title="Ziggy Marley">Ziggy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Marley_(musician)" title="Stephen Marley (musician)">Stephen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Julian_Marley" title="Julian Marley">Julian</a>, and she starts singing this rap verse, and all the kids start repeating the last word of each line, chiming in very spontaneously because they were so into the song."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a> considered bringing in an outside producer for the album and had early talks with <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA" class="mw-redirect">RZA</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>. However, Hill was adamant about writing, arranging, and producing the album herself: "It would have been more difficult to articulate to other people. Hey, it's my album. Who can tell my story better than me?"<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> She recalled <a href="/wiki/Ruffhouse_Records" title="Ruffhouse Records">Ruffhouse Records</a> executive Chris Swartz ensuring her <a href="/wiki/Artistic_freedom" title="Artistic freedom">artistic freedom</a> while recording the album: "I had total control of the album. Chris Swartz at Ruffhouse, my label, said, 'Listen, you've never done anything stupid thus far, so let me let you do your thing.'"<sup id="cite_ref-Furman.3B_Leah.2C_Elina._1999._P:140_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Furman.3B_Leah.2C_Elina._1999._P:140-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_lyrics">Music and lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Miseducation_of_Lauryn_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music and lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Neo_soul" title="Neo soul">neo soul</a> album,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i> incorporates musical styles such as <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Some songs are based in <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_soul" title="Hip hop soul">hip hop soul</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Price_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> "When It Hurts So Bad" is musically old <a href="/wiki/Roots_reggae" title="Roots reggae">roots reggae</a> mixed with soul. While mostly in English, "Forgive Them Father" and "Lost Ones" both feature singing in <a href="/wiki/Patois" title="Patois">patois</a>, which is the common dialect in Jamaica. Although heavily R&amp;B, the song "Superstar" contains an <a href="/wiki/Interpolation_(music)" title="Interpolation (music)">interpolation</a> of the song "<a href="/wiki/Light_My_Fire" title="Light My Fire">Light My Fire</a>" by the classic rock band <a href="/wiki/The_Doors" title="The Doors">The Doors</a>. In an interview, Hill said that she "didn't want to come out with a <a href="/wiki/Fugees" title="Fugees">Refugee All-Stars</a> type of sound", but create "something that was uniquely and very clearly a Lauryn Hill album."<sup id="cite_ref-Furman.3B_Leah.2C_Elina._1999._P:140_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Furman.3B_Leah.2C_Elina._1999._P:140-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> She also said that she did not intend for the album's sound to be commercially appealing: "There's too much pressure to have hits these days. Artists are watching <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> instead of exploring themselves. Look at someone like <a href="/wiki/Aretha_Franklin" title="Aretha Franklin">Aretha</a>, she didn't hit with her first album, but she was able to grow up and find herself. I wanted to make honest music. I don't like things to be too perfect, or too polished. People may criticize me for that, but I grew up listening to <a href="/wiki/Al_Green" title="Al Green">Al Green</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sam_Cooke" title="Sam Cooke">Sam Cooke</a>. When they hit a high note, you actually felt it."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The majority of <i>The Miseducation</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s lyrics were written in Hill's attic during her first pregnancy, with much of the content dealing with motherhood, the Fugees, reminiscence, love, heartbreak, and God.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson132_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson132-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Commenting on the album's gospel content, Hill stated "Gospel music is music inspired by the gospels. In a huge respect, a lot of this music turned out to be just that. During this album, I turned to the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> and wrote songs that I drew comfort from."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Several of the album's songs, such as "Lost Ones," "Superstar," "Ex-Factor" and "Forgive Them Father" were widely speculated as direct attacks at Fugee members Wyclef and Pras.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-inside1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inside1-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> "Ex-Factor" was originally intended for a different artist, however, Hill decided to keep it after it was completed, due to its personal content.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Although a large portion of the album's love songs would turn out to be bitter from Hill's previous relationship, "Nothing Even Matters,"<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> a duet performed by Hill and <a href="/wiki/D%27Angelo" title="D'Angelo">D'Angelo</a>, showcased a brighter, more intimate perspective on the subject. The song was inspired by Hill's relationship with Rohan Marley. Speaking about "Nothing Even Matters"' lyrics, Hill remarked: "I wanted to make a love song, á la <a href="/wiki/Roberta_Flack_%26_Donny_Hathaway" title="Roberta Flack &amp; Donny Hathaway">Roberta Flack &amp; Donny Hathaway</a>, and give people a humanistic approach to love again without all the physicality and overt sexuality."<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"To Zion," among the more introspective tracks on the album, spoke about how Hill's family comes before her career<sup id="cite_ref-J._Bush_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-J._Bush-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> and her decision to have her first child, even though many at the time encouraged her to <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abort</a> this pregnancy, as to not conflict with her blossoming career.<sup id="cite_ref-inside2_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inside2-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> In an interview she discussed the song's origin and significance, commenting "Names wouldn't come when I was ready to have him. The only name that came to me was Zion. I was like, 'is Zion too much of a weight to carry?' But this little boy, man. I would say he personally delivered me from my emotional and spiritual drought. He just replenished my newness. When he was born, I felt like I was born again."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> She further stated: "I wanted it to be a revolutionary song about a spiritual movement, and also about my spiritual change, going from one place to another because of my son."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout <i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i>, several interludes of a teacher speaking to what is implied to be a classroom of children are played. The "teacher" was played by <a href="/wiki/Ras_Baraka" title="Ras Baraka">Ras Baraka</a> (poet, educator and politician) speaking to a group of kids in the living room of Hill's New Jersey home.<sup id="cite_ref-inside3_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inside3-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Hill requested that Baraka speak to the children about the concept of love, to which he improvised in the lecture.<sup id="cite_ref-inside3_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inside3-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a>'s Paul Schrodt remarked on the title's reference to Carter G. Woodson's <i>The Mis-Education of the Negro</i>: "[Hill] adopts Woodson's thesis and makes it part of her own artistic process. Like the songs themselves, the intro/outro classroom scenes suggest a larger community working to redefine itself."<sup id="cite_ref-P.Schrodt_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P.Schrodt-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i> received rave reviews from contemporary music critics,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> and was the most acclaimed album of 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-Boucher_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boucher-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> Reviewers frequently praised it for Hill's honest presentation of a female's view on life and love.<sup id="cite_ref-Boucher_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boucher-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> Eric Weisbard of <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> called Hill a "genre-bender" whose confident singing and rapping is balanced by vulnerable themes and sentiment.<sup id="cite_ref-Spin_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spin-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ann_Powers" title="Ann Powers">Ann Powers</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> found it "miraculous" and "exceptional" for Hill to use "her faith, based more in experience and feeling than in doctrine," to "connect the sacred to the secular in music that touches the essence of soul."<sup id="cite_ref-A._Powers_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A._Powers-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a>'s John Bush was impressed by how Hill produced most of the album, "not as a crossover record, but as a collection of overtly personal and political statements", while demonstrating "performing talents, vocal range, and songwriting smarts".<sup id="cite_ref-J._Bush_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-J._Bush-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Browne" title="David Browne">David Browne</a>, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i>, called it "an album of often-astonishing power, strength, and feeling", and praised Hill for "easily flowing from singing to rapping, evoking the past while forging a future of her own".<sup id="cite_ref-Browne_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dream_Hampton" title="Dream Hampton" class="mw-redirect">Dream Hampton</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, said that the album is "majestic" in "the seamlessness with which [Hill] travels her realm within any given song."<sup id="cite_ref-Hampton_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hampton-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Greg_Kot" title="Greg Kot">Greg Kot</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> felt that, despite its "between-songs interludes and skits", the album is a "vocal tour de force" with arrangements that "bristle with great ideas".<sup id="cite_ref-Kot_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kot-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a>'s Neil Lieberman said that, "striking lyrics" and "beautiful melodies" notwithstanding, the album has some flaws, including a diffuse running time, "tiresome" ballads, and Hill's occasional "sweet tooth for cheesy '70s tunes".<sup id="cite_ref-Lieberman_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lieberman-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> John Mulvey of <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> felt that the album is "essential" even with "churlish" quibbles of certain elements, including redundant skits and Hill's "propensity" for histrionics and declarations of "how brilliant God is".<sup id="cite_ref-NME_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NME-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> In his consumer guide for <i>The Village Voice</i>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave the album a three-star honorable mention,<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> indicating "an enjoyable effort consumers attuned to its overriding aesthetic or individual vision may well treasure."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> Christgau cited "Lost Ones" and "Superstar" as highlights and quipped, "<a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/politically_correct" class="extiw" title="wikt:politically correct">PC</a> record of the year—songs soft, singing ordinary, rapping skilled, rhymes up and down, skits <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/de_trop" class="extiw" title="wikt:de trop">de trop</a>, production subtle and terrific".<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Miseducation_of_Lauryn_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/41st_Grammy_Awards" title="41st Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">41st Grammy Awards</a> in 1999, Hill was nominated ten times, making her the first woman to ever be nominated ten times in one year. She won five Grammys, including <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_New_Artist" title="Grammy Award for Best New Artist">Best New Artist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_R%26B_Song" title="Grammy Award for Best R&amp;B Song">Best R&amp;B Song</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Female_R%26B_Vocal_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Female R&amp;B Vocal Performance">Best Female R&amp;B Vocal Performance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_R%26B_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best R&amp;B Album">Best R&amp;B Album</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Album_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Album of the Year">Album of the Year</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Grammys_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grammys-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> making <i>The Miseducation</i> the first hip hop oriented album to ever receive that award. Hill set a new record in the industry, as she also became the first woman to win five Grammys in one night. <i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i> also earned her several other awards, including several nominations at the thirteenth NAACP Image Awards for Outstanding Female Artist, Outstanding Album, Outstanding Music Video (for "A Rose Is Still A Rose"), and finally, competing against her own self, for Outstanding Song, nominated for both "Doo Wop" and "A Rose Is Still A Rose."<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At the Annual <i>Billboard</i> Music Awards, <i>The Miseducation</i> won for R&amp;B Album of the Year, while at the 20th <i>Billboard</i> Music Awards, "Doo Wop" won Best R&amp;B/Urban New Artist Clip.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> On January 11, 1999, at the 26th Annual American Music Awards, Hill won the award for Best New Soul/R&amp;B artist.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson185_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson185-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> She also won a <a href="/wiki/Soul_Train" title="Soul Train">Soul Train</a> award, and a nomination for Best International Female Solo Artist at the <a href="/wiki/Brit_Awards" title="Brit Awards">Brit Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson190_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson190-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Miseducation_of_Lauryn_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i> debuted at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart in the week ending August 30, 1998,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> on sales of 422,624 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-pqasb.pqarchiver.com_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pqasb.pqarchiver.com-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's debut broke the record for first-week sales by a female artist.<sup id="cite_ref-pqasb.pqarchiver.com_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pqasb.pqarchiver.com-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> It topped the <i>Billboard</i> 200 for a second consecutive week, during which it sold 265,000 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> In less than a month, the album had sold one million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-Boucher_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boucher-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> On September 29, 1998, it was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">gold</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), for shipments of 500,000 copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> The album spent 81 weeks on the <i>Billboard</i> 200,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> and topped the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Year-End" title="Billboard Year-End"><i>Billboard</i> Year-End</a> Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums chart.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i> was promoted with three <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">singles</a>—"<a href="/wiki/Doo_Wop_(That_Thing)" title="Doo Wop (That Thing)">Doo Wop (That Thing)</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Ex-Factor" title="Ex-Factor">Ex-Factor</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Everything_Is_Everything_(Lauryn_Hill_song)" title="Everything Is Everything (Lauryn Hill song)">Everything Is Everything</a>"—all of which became <a href="/wiki/Hit_single" title="Hit single">hits</a> and produced popular music videos.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> "Doo Wop" charted at number 1 and number 3 in the United States and the United Kingdom, respectively, and "Ex-Factor" reached number 22 and number 4, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Larkin_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Larkin-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's sales increased after Hill's appearance at the Grammy Awards, as it sold 234,000 more copies in the week of March 3, 1999,<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> and 200,000 copies the following week.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> The album had sold 10 million copies worldwide, including nearly 700,000 in Canada, by August.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> On December 17, 2001, it was certified 8x platinum by the RIAA.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> In April 2002, Columbia said that the album had sold 12 million copies worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/1,000_Recordings_to_Hear_Before_You_Die" title="1,000 Recordings to Hear Before You Die">1,000 Recordings to Hear Before You Die</a></i> (2008), <i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i> has sold 18 million copies worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> By 2013, it had sold more than eight million copies in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tour">Tour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Miseducation_of_Lauryn_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Initially, there was no immediate tour planned due to the album not needing the promotion, and also, Hill was pregnant yet again, and the child was due in September 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson185_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson185-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> Her first live performances of the songs were at <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Music_Awards" title="Billboard Music Awards" class="mw-redirect"><i>Billboard</i> Music Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> In January 1999, Hill recruited a band and began rehearsals for what would become <i>The Miseducation</i> Tour.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> As soon as the tour was announced, tickets immediately sold out.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson185_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson185-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Miseducation Tour</i> began at <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Budokan" title="Nippon Budokan">Budokan</a> in Tokyo on January 21, 1999. Hill performed there again the following night, and played at two other Tokyo venues in the following week.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson185_56-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson185-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> One week later, she flew to London for her performance at the <a href="/wiki/Brixton_Academy" title="Brixton Academy">Brixton Academy</a> on February 8, 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson185_56-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson185-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> With 20 U.S. dates total,<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson188-189_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson188-189-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> the American part of the tour, which featured <a href="/wiki/Outkast" title="Outkast">Outkast</a> as the opening act, started on February 18 in Detroit, and ended on April 1, 1999, at Hill's hometown, Newark, New Jersey.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson188-189_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson188-189-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> After the U.S. dates, she flew to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, where the tour was finished.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson190_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson190-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Miseducation_of_Lauryn_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the large success of the album, Hill became a national media icon, as magazines ranging from <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> to <i><a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)">Esquire</a></i> to <i><a href="/wiki/Teen_People" title="Teen People" class="mw-redirect">Teen People</a></i> vied to place her on their front covers. In a February 8, 1999, <i>Time</i> cover-story, Hill was credited for helping fully assimilate hip-hop into mainstream music, making her the first hip hop artist to ever appear on the magazine's front cover.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2003, <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine ranked <i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i> number 313 on their list of <a href="/wiki/500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> Jon Caramanica, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stone_Album_Guide" title="The Rolling Stone Album Guide">The Rolling Stone Album Guide</a></i> (2004), called it "as earnest, unpretentious, and pleasantly sloppy an album as any woman of the hip-hop generation has ever made", and said that, by appealing to a wide spectrum of listeners with hip hop filtered through a "womanist lens", the album propelled Hill to superstardom "of epic proportions" and "the focal point at hip-hop's <a href="/wiki/Crossover_(music)" title="Crossover (music)">crossover</a> into the mainstream."<sup id="cite_ref-RSguide_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSguide-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Music journalist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Shapiro_(journalist)" title="Peter Shapiro (journalist)">Peter Shapiro</a> cited it as "the ultimate cross-over album of the hip-hop era."<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Along with <a href="/wiki/Erykah_Badu" title="Erykah Badu">Erykah Badu</a>'s 1997 debut <i><a href="/wiki/Baduizm" title="Baduizm">Baduizm</a></i>, <i>The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill</i> was also an important release in the neo soul music scene.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> It brought neo soul to the forefront of popular music,<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> and became the genre's most critically acclaimed and popular album.<sup id="cite_ref-Price_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> According to the <i>Encyclopedia of African American Music</i> (2010), "the record is filled with live musicians and layered harmonies, and therefore it is a trendsetting record that connects modern hip hop, R &amp; B, and classic soul music together, creating groundwork for what followed it in the neo soul genre."<sup id="cite_ref-Price_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuit">Lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Miseducation_of_Lauryn_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Lawsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though <i>The Miseducation</i> was largely a collaborative work between Hill and a group of musicians known as New Ark (Vada Nobles, Rasheem Pugh, Tejumold and Johari Newton), there was "label pressure to do the <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a> thing," wherein all tracks would be credited as "written and produced by" the artist with little outside help.<sup id="cite_ref-mystery_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mystery-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-inside8_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inside8-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> While recording the album, when Hill was asked about providing contracts or documentation to the musicians, she replied: "We all love each other. This ain't about documents. This is blessed."<sup id="cite_ref-mystery_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mystery-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, New Ark filed a 50-page lawsuit against Hill, her management, also her record label, stating that Hill "used their songs and production skills, but failed to properly credit them for the work."<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> The musicians claimed to be the primary songwriters on two tracks, and major contributors on several others,<sup id="cite_ref-The_Legal_Tangle_of_Miseducation_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Legal_Tangle_of_Miseducation-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> though Gordon Williams, a prominent recorder, engineer, and mixer on <i>The Miseducation</i> described the album as a "powerfully personal effort by Hill" and stated, "It was definitely her vision."<sup id="cite_ref-The_Legal_Tangle_of_Miseducation_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Legal_Tangle_of_Miseducation-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> In response to the lawsuit, Hill claimed that New Ark took advantage of her success.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> New Ark requested partial writing credits, and monetary reimbursement.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> The suit was eventually settled out of court in February 2001 for a reported $5 million.<sup id="cite_ref-inside1_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inside1-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
