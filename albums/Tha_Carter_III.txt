<p><i><b>Tha Carter III</b></i> is the sixth studio album by American rapper <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne" title="Lil Wayne">Lil Wayne</a>, released on June 10, 2008, by <a href="/wiki/Cash_Money_Records" title="Cash Money Records">Cash Money</a> and <a href="/wiki/Universal_Motown" title="Universal Motown" class="mw-redirect">Universal Motown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It follows a long string of mixtape releases and guest appearances on other hip hop and R&amp;B artists records, helping to increase his exposure in the mainstream.<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffries2_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffries2-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's cover art features a baby picture of Wayne and is similar to covers of hip hop albums such as <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Illmatic" title="Illmatic">Illmatic</a></i> (1994) and <a href="/wiki/Notorious_B.I.G." title="Notorious B.I.G." class="mw-redirect">Notorious B.I.G.</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ready_to_Die" title="Ready to Die">Ready to Die</a></i> (1994).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Amid release delays and <a href="/wiki/Internet_leak" title="Internet leak">leaks</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffries2_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffries2-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Tha Carter III</i> became one of the most anticipated releases of 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-newxxl_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newxxl-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album debuted at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 1,005,545 copies in its first week. It reached sales of 2.88 million copies by the end of 2008 and produced four singles that achieved chart success, including the international hit "<a href="/wiki/Lollipop_(Lil_Wayne_song)" title="Lollipop (Lil Wayne song)">Lollipop</a>" and <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_charts" title="Billboard charts">Billboard</a></i> hits "<a href="/wiki/A_Milli" title="A Milli">A Milli</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Got_Money" title="Got Money">Got Money</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Mrs._Officer" title="Mrs. Officer">Mrs. Officer</a>". <i>Tha Carter III</i> received acclaim from music critics upon its release and earned Lil Wayne several accolades. It has been certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">triple-platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> and has sold 3.6 million copies in the United States.</p>

</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tha_Carter_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Tha Carter III</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s lead single, "<a href="/wiki/Lollipop_(Lil_Wayne_song)" title="Lollipop (Lil Wayne song)">Lollipop</a>", peaked at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">US <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, staying at the top for three weeks. It was Wayne's most successful solo single in his career, winning one <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Black_Entertainment_Television" title="Black Entertainment Television" class="mw-redirect">BET</a> Award, and an <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_Music_Awards" title="Video Music Awards" class="mw-redirect">VMA</a>. The song was praised as an "electro-bumpin'...infectious track",<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffries1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffries1-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> perceived as more of a "bubblegum" pop track than rap.<sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone.com_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone.com-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The second track on the album, "<a href="/wiki/Mr._Carter" title="Mr. Carter">Mr. Carter</a>", was nominated for a Grammy while also peaking within the Hot 100. It was praised for featuring <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, which was seen as Jay-Z passing the throne to Wayne.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard.com_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard.com-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ew.com_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ew.com-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The second single, "<a href="/wiki/A_Milli" title="A Milli">A Milli</a>", was a top ten hit and was praised as one of the best songs of 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The song garnered countless freestyles and remixes, while Wayne's original version was praised with "spectacular rhyme".<sup id="cite_ref-ew.com_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ew.com-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> "Dr. Carter", the sixth track, was also praised for lyrical content and humor as Wayne took on the persona of a doctor performing surgery on various patients (a metaphor for Wayne resurrecting hip-hop<sup id="cite_ref-billboard.com_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard.com-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone.com_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone.com-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> "Tie My Hands", featuring <a href="/wiki/Robin_Thicke" title="Robin Thicke">Robin Thicke</a>, was praised as a deep track featuring "political commentary" and "despair" with Thicke's performance being the most complementary to Wayne.<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffries1_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffries1-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> "Phone Home" also features various alien metaphors reminiscent of the film <i><a href="/wiki/E.T._the_Extra-Terrestrial" title="E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial">E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial</a></i> (1982).<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffries1_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffries1-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone.com_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone.com-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_promotion">Release and promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tha_Carter_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Release and promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaks">Leaks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tha_Carter_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Leaks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After most of the album leaked on the Internet in mid-2007, Lil Wayne used the leaked tracks, plus four new songs to make an album titled <i>The Leak</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-newxxl_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newxxl-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Leak</i> was to be officially released on December 18, 2007, with the actual album being delayed until June 10, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> When questioned about the unplanned leak, Lil Wayne said:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>We have to find out exactly what's out there. I'll probably just [collect] all the songs that's floating around and make my own mixtape called <i>The Leak</i> since people want the music so bad. To tell you the truth though, there's a song I did with <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> out there—of course you want to save that for your album, but the rest of them songs probably wouldn't have made the album. There's a song floating around that says 'produced by Timbaland'.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>On May 24, 2008, 10-second snippets of multiple songs were leaked onto AT&amp;T Media Mall.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> On May 30–31, <i>Tha Carter III</i> was leaked internationally. The first of the leaks were distributed on May 30 at around 8pm where five songs from the track list were available on the internet. Hours later on May 31 at 12am-1am the whole album was leaked and posted on various websites for free download. The DJ responsible for the leaks was DJ Chuck T who retaliated for an interview conducted by Wayne, where he discredited all DJs and the mixtape scene days before.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Lil Wayne later called <a href="/wiki/DJ_Drama" title="DJ Drama">DJ Drama</a>'s radio show Shade 45 Sirius Satellite Radio to explain that his comments were meant specifically for DJ Empire who leaked his materials periodically without his permission, consent, or knowledge; he also apologized for any misunderstandings between him and the numerous DJs that have aided him in the mixtape industry. He made it clear, however, that he wished for any feelings of dislike or resentment to remain.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tha_Carter_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album's lead single, "<a href="/wiki/Lollipop_(Lil_Wayne_song)" title="Lollipop (Lil Wayne song)">Lollipop</a>", topped the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> for 5 non-consecutive weeks, making it Wayne's most successful single in his career. It features <a href="/wiki/Rapper" title="Rapper" class="mw-redirect">rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/Static_Major" title="Static Major">Static Major</a>. The album's second single, "<a href="/wiki/A_Milli" title="A Milli">A Milli</a>", was another top ten. It reached #6 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100. It also won a Grammy for Best Rap Song. The video for the second single, "A Milli", was set to be released in May, and has since been mainstreamed. Multiple versions of the track were to be included on the album as "skit-like" tracks, featuring artists such as <a href="/wiki/Tyga" title="Tyga">Tyga</a>, Cory Gunz, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Chris_(rapper)" title="Hurricane Chris (rapper)">Hurricane Chris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lil_Mama" title="Lil Mama">Lil Mama</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Another artist, 13-year-old Lil Chuckee, was also set to appear on one of the "A Milli" skits. None of the skits made the final cut of the album. The third single is "<a href="/wiki/Got_Money" title="Got Money">Got Money</a>", featuring <a href="/wiki/T-Pain" title="T-Pain">T-Pain</a>. It reached #10 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100. The fourth single is "<a href="/wiki/Mrs._Officer" title="Mrs. Officer">Mrs. Officer</a>", featuring <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Valentino_(American_singer)" title="Bobby Valentino (American singer)" class="mw-redirect">Bobby Valentino</a>. It made the Top 20 in just four weeks. "Comfortable" was sent to American <a href="/wiki/Rhythmic_contemporary" title="Rhythmic contemporary">rhythmic contemporary</a> radio as the album's fifth and final single on September 29, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> "Lollipop", "A Milli", "Got Money", and another track, "<a href="/wiki/Mr._Carter" title="Mr. Carter">Mr. Carter</a>", were nominated for a Grammy. Lil Wayne also performed "<a href="/wiki/Tie_My_Hands" title="Tie My Hands" class="mw-redirect">Tie My Hands</a>" with <a href="/wiki/Robin_Thicke" title="Robin Thicke">Robin Thicke</a> at the 51st Grammy Awards.</p>
<p>The album also featured the releases of promo singles. "3 Peat" peaked at number 66 on the <i>Billboard</i> 100. "<a href="/wiki/You_Ain%27t_Got_Nuthin" title="You Ain't Got Nuthin">You Ain't Got Nuthin</a>" featuring <a href="/wiki/Fabolous" title="Fabolous">Fabolous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juelz_Santana" title="Juelz Santana">Juelz Santana</a> was released as a promo single, peaking at number 81 on the <i>Billboard</i> 100. "<a href="/wiki/Mr._Carter" title="Mr. Carter">Mr. Carter</a>", featuring <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, peaked at number 62 on the <i>Billboard</i> 100, number 27 on the Hot R&amp;B/Hip Hop Songs chart, and number 13 on the Top Rap Songs. It was nominated for a Grammy for Best Rap Performance by a Duo or a Group in 2009.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits">Lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tha_Carter_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lawsuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On July 24, 2008, <a href="/wiki/ABKCO_Records" title="ABKCO Records">Abkco Music Inc</a>. filed a <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">lawsuit</a> against Lil Wayne for <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trademark_infringement" title="Trademark infringement">unfair competition</a>, specifically referring to the track "Playing with Fire".<sup id="cite_ref-abkco_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abkco-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> In the lawsuit, Abkco claims that the song was obviously derived from <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Play_with_Fire_(The_Rolling_Stones_song)" title="Play with Fire (The Rolling Stones song)">Play with Fire</a>", to which Abkco owns the rights.<sup id="cite_ref-abkco_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abkco-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Subsequently, "Playing with Fire" was removed from the tracklist of <i>Tha Carter III</i> on all <a href="/wiki/Online_music_stores" title="Online music stores" class="mw-redirect">online music stores</a> and replaced with the <a href="/wiki/David_Banner" title="David Banner">David Banner</a> produced track, "Pussy Monster".<sup id="cite_ref-iTunespussymonster_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iTunespussymonster-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Amazonpussymonster_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amazonpussymonster-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rhapsodypussymonster_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhapsodypussymonster-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On March 2011, producer <a href="/wiki/Deezle" title="Deezle">Deezle</a> (Darius Harrison) sued Wayne and his parent labels Cash Money Records over unpaid royalties from Tha Carter III album.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> On May 2011, producer Bangladesh also filed a lawsuit against Weezy &amp; Co. over unpaid royalties as well.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> In early June 2011, another producer named <a href="/wiki/David_Kirkwood" title="David Kirkwood">David Kirkwood</a> filed a lawsuit against Young Money Entertainment and Cash Money Records on claims that the labels have failed to pay him over $1.5&#160;million dollars in royalties and production services for his work on the album, also including his songwriting on "Love Me or Hate Me", a bonus song featured only on the deluxe edition of the album.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Also in June 2011, Dallas producers <a href="/wiki/Play-N-Skillz" title="Play-N-Skillz">Play-N-Skillz</a> filed a lawsuit against him claiming Wayne owed them at least $1&#160;million in unpaid royalties for "<a href="/wiki/Got_Money" title="Got Money">Got Money</a>" from <i>Tha Carter III</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tha_Carter_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With opening day sales figures of approximately 423,000,<sup id="cite_ref-Lock_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lock-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> the album sold 1,005,545 copies in its first week in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> With its first week sales, it is the largest first week sales for any album in 2008 in the United States and the first album to reach the million mark in one week since <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Massacre" title="The Massacre">The Massacre</a></i> (2005).<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Tha Carter III</i> has also reached the top spot in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Albums_Chart" title="Canadian Albums Chart">Canadian Albums Chart</a>, selling nearly 21,000 units. Elsewhere, the album achieved moderate success, entering at only number 23 in the <a href="/wiki/UK_Album_Chart" title="UK Album Chart" class="mw-redirect">UK</a> and number 34 on the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Album_Chart" title="Irish Album Chart" class="mw-redirect">Irish Album Chart</a>. In the album's second week, it sold a reported 309,000 copies, helping <i>Tha Carter III</i> towards becoming Lil Wayne's most successful selling album to date.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Tha Carter III</i> had sold approximately 2.88 million copies in 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-top_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-top-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> after selling another 985,000 and 964,000 in two week span week June 24–July 8 and well over 697,000 the following week in a 7 day span, becoming 2008's best-selling album.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> By the end of 2008, it was named the best-selling album of the year in the United States by <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-top_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-top-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> On February 12, 2009, the album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">triple-platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), following shipments of three million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> As of July 2011, it has sold 3.6 million copies in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Tha Carter III</i> received universal acclaim from <a href="/wiki/Music_criticism" title="Music criticism">music critics</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album received an <a href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean" class="mw-redirect">average</a> score of 84, based on 26 reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> editor David Jeffries gave it four out of five stars and praised Wayne's "entertaining wordplay and plenty of well-executed, left-field ideas".<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffries1_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffries1-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Now_(magazine)" title="Now (magazine)" class="mw-redirect">NOW</a></i> gave it a five out of five rating and called it a "sub­versive masterpiece".<sup id="cite_ref-Now_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Now-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Alex Macpherson lauded Wayne's rapping, stating "Just trying to keep up with Wayne's mind as he proves the case is a thrill. He breaks language down into building blocks for new metaphors, exploiting every possible semantic and phonetic loophole for humour and yanking pop culture references into startling new contexts".<sup id="cite_ref-Macpherson_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macpherson-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> writer <a href="/wiki/Jody_Rosen" title="Jody Rosen">Jody Rosen</a> commended its themes and stated "This isn't a mixtape, it's a suite of songs, paced and sequenced for maxaqimum  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] impact."<sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone.com_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone.com-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Jonah Weiner of <i><a href="/wiki/Blender_(magazine)" title="Blender (magazine)">Blender</a></i> gave the album four-and-a-half out of five stars and called it "a weird, gripping triumph".<sup id="cite_ref-Weiner_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiner-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jon_Pareles" title="Jon Pareles">Jon Pareles</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> commented that Wayne "has clearly worked to make <span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span><i>Tha Carter III</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span> a statement of its own: one that moves beyond standard hip-hop boasting (though there's plenty of that) to thoughts that can be introspective or gleefully unhinged".<sup id="cite_ref-Pareles_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pareles-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dombal-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> In his consumer guide for <a href="/wiki/MSN_Music" title="MSN Music">MSN Music</a>, critic <a But every facet of the album comes animated and atomized by Wayne's absurdist drug-gobbling persona".<sup id="cite_ref-Breihan_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Breihan-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> Drew Hinshaw of <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a> gave it an eight out of 10 rating and stated "<i>Tha Carter III</i> is a monumental album full of powerful, self-defeating statements that obliterate rap's internal logic without offering too much more than indifferent bong logic in return. parts are otherworldly enough."<sup id="cite_ref-Perkins_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perkins-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> Julian Benbow of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> gave it a favorable review and said the album was "not an instant classic, but it is the best rap album since Kanye West dropped "Graduation" last year."<sup id="cite_ref-Benbow_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Benbow-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> Eric R. Danton of the <i><a href="/wiki/Hartford_Courant" title="Hartford Courant">Hartford Courant</a></i> also gave it a favorable review and said of Lil Wayne, "If his raspy, cartoonish voice didn't mark him as different, his quick wit, offhanded wordplay and quirky subject matter should have in a genre populated largely by grim-faced imitators."<sup id="cite_ref-Danton_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danton-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Other reviews are average or mixed: Chase Hoffberger of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Austin_Chronicle" title="The Austin Chronicle">The Austin Chronicle</a></i> gave the album three stars out of five and said, "It's Wayne's personality that both floats and sinks <i>TCIII</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-Hoffberger_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffberger-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> Kilian Murphy of <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Press" title="Hot Press">Hot Press</a></i> also gave it a score of three out of five and stated, "Gifted MC loses the run of himself without Mannie Fresh."<sup id="cite_ref-HotPress_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HotPress-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> Lewis P. of <a href="/wiki/Sputnikmusic" title="Sputnikmusic">Sputnikmusic</a> likewise gave it a score of three out of five and said the album "is scattershot, which oddly strengthens its faults, as if any lull in quality means that the next batch of producers can just reset the formula."<sup id="cite_ref-SputnikRev1_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SputnikRev1-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> (However, nearly three years later, in 2011, Alex Robertson of the same website gave the album a score of four-and-a-half out of five and said it was "sort of a miracle: it’s way too weird and confusing to be on the mainstream rap charts--to be that record that everyone knows about--but it is anyway. This album was in opposition to much of modern rap but somehow became popular and then proceeded to completely consume the genre and change its direction. [...] <i>Tha Carter III</i> is a contradictory, against-all-odds masterpiece, and Lil Wayne may never perfect this balance again. I sincerely question: will anyone?"<sup id="cite_ref-SputnikRev2_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SputnikRev2-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup>) Ajitpaul Manjat of <a href="/wiki/Tiny_Mix_Tapes" title="Tiny Mix Tapes">Tiny Mix Tapes</a> gave the album two-and-a-half stars out of five and stated that, "equipped with the stylish, but too-often substance-less <i>Tha Carter III,</i> Lil Wayne seems poised to flip the script on the “rapper racists” (radio stations, MTV) by evolving into the “biggest” rapper alive."<sup id="cite_ref-Manjat_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manjat-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tha_Carter_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Tha Carter III</i> was ranked number one in <i><a href="/wiki/Blender_(magazine)" title="Blender (magazine)">Blender</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s list of the 33 best albums of 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> Christgau ranked its deluxe edition as the second best album of 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was also ranked number three on <i>Rolling Stone</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s list of the top 50 albums of 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> It was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Album_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Album of the Year">Grammy Award for Album of the Year</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> and it won for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Best Rap Album</a> at the <a href="/wiki/2009_Grammy_Awards" title="2009 Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">2009 Grammy Awards</a>, while "<a href="/wiki/Lollipop_(Lil_Wayne_song)" title="Lollipop (Lil Wayne song)">Lollipop</a>" won for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Song" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Song">Best Rap Song</a> and "<a href="/wiki/A_Milli" title="A Milli">A Milli</a>" won for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance">Best Rap Solo Performance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> magazine ranked the album number 103 on its list of the Top 200 Albums of the Decade.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012, the album was ranked number 437 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s list of <a href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">the 500 Greatest Albums of All Time</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012 <a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a> named the album one of the classic albums of the last decade.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
