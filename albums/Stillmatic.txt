<p><i><b>Stillmatic</b></i> is the fifth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American rapper <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, released December 18, 2001 on <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a> in the United States. In contrast to his previous work's <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a> themes, it contains socially conscious and philosophical themes similar to those of his debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Illmatic" title="Illmatic">Illmatic</a></i> (1994). Nas' lyrics address topics such as ghetto life, U.S. domestic and foreign policies, and his <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z_vs._Nas_feud" title="Jay-Z vs. Nas feud" class="mw-redirect">feud</a> with rapper <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>.</p>
<p><i>Stillmatic</i> served as a commercial and critical success that helped re-establish Nas' career, following a period of critical disappointment with the releases of <i><a href="/wiki/I_Am%E2%80%A6_(Nas_album)" title="I Am… (Nas album)" class="mw-redirect">I Am…</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nastradamus" title="Nastradamus">Nastradamus</a></i> (1999).<sup id="cite_ref-RSguide_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSguide-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Leroy_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leroy-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It debuted at number 8 on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart with sales of 342,600, eventually peaking at number 5 and selling over 2,026,000 copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-More_Than_Words_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-More_Than_Words-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Upon its release, <i>Stillmatic</i> received generally positive reviews from most music critics.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stillmatic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though he had gained critical acclaim with his classic debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Illmatic" title="Illmatic">Illmatic</a></i> in 1994, Nas' image had been quickly deteriorating in the hip-hop community with his change of theme, from the philosophical topics of <i>Illmatic</i> to the <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a> and commercialized sound that became the focus of his later albums.<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> While his second album, <i><a href="/wiki/It_Was_Written" title="It Was Written">It Was Written</a>, " received fairly positive reviews and introduced him to a greater audience, the follow-ups</i> <a href="/wiki/I_Am%E2%80%A6_(Nas_album)" title="I Am… (Nas album)" class="mw-redirect">I Am...</a> <i>and</i> <a href="/wiki/Nastradamus" title="Nastradamus">Nastradamus</a> <i>were considered mediocre in comparison by critics.<sup id="cite_ref-RSguide_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSguide-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Some criticized his inability to continue his critical success, with <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> rapping in "<a href="/wiki/Takeover_(song)" title="Takeover (song)">Takeover</a>" that he had "a one hot album every ten year average." The release of</i> Stillmatic <i>was an attempt by Nas to reestablish his credibility in the hip-hop community, with the title signifying his intentions to continue where</i> Illmatic <i>left off.</i></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feud_with_Jay-Z">Feud with Jay-Z</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stillmatic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Feud with Jay-Z">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z_vs._Nas_feud" title="Jay-Z vs. Nas feud" class="mw-redirect">Jay-Z vs. Nas feud</a></div>
<p>Jay-Z had previously dissed Nas in his single "<a href="/wiki/Takeover_(song)" title="Takeover (song)">Takeover</a>, taken from his September 2001 release, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blueprint" title="The Blueprint">The Blueprint</a>." <sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> On</i> Stillmatic," Nas retaliated with the anticipated song, "<a href="/wiki/Ether_(song)" title="Ether (song)">Ether</a>," a response to "Takeover" which insinuated that <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> had stolen lyrics from <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> several times, that he had sold out, and that he was a <a href="/wiki/Misogynist" title="Misogynist" class="mw-redirect">misogynist</a>, among other things. Several hip-hop aficionados believe Nas won the feud based on this track, which many felt was much more vicious and ruthless than "Takeover," although this is still a subject of debate within hip-hop circles. Jay-Z would respond with the radio freestyle "<a href="/wiki/Supa_Ugly" title="Supa Ugly" class="mw-redirect">Supa Ugly</a>." <sup id="cite_ref-Hinds_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinds-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stillmatic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first single from <i>Stillmatic</i> was "<a href="/wiki/Rule_(song)" title="Rule (song)">Rule</a>" featuring R&amp;B singer <a href="/wiki/Amerie" title="Amerie">Amerie</a>. It was not heavily promoted but still managed to reach number 67 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Singles_%26_Tracks" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-singles_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-singles-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It did not receive a video and was issued on compact disc, so many are unaware that it was a single. "<a href="/wiki/Got_Ur_Self_A..." title="Got Ur Self A..." class="mw-redirect">Got Ur Self A...</a>" was believed to have been the first single from <i>Stillmatic.</i> "<a href="/wiki/Got_Ur_Self_A..." title="Got Ur Self A..." class="mw-redirect">Got Ur Self A...</a>," produced by <a href="/wiki/Megahertz" title="Megahertz" class="mw-redirect">Megahertz</a>, contains a sample from <a href="/wiki/Woke_Up_This_Morning" title="Woke Up This Morning">the theme song</a> on the <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> drama <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sopranos" title="The Sopranos">The Sopranos</a>." The third single was "<a href="/wiki/One_Mic" title="One Mic">One Mic</a>," which received acclaim for its content and video.</i></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stillmatic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stillmatic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album was released on December 18, 2001 and was certified Platinum by <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> on January 16, 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> By July 2008, the album sold over 2,026,000 copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-More_Than_Words_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-More_Than_Words-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Stillmatic</i> received generally positive reviews from music critics. At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album received an <a href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean" class="mw-redirect">average</a> score of 69, based on 12 reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite finding its production "half-realised", Alex Needham of <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> wrote that "lyrically, Nas is pretty much back on form".<sup id="cite_ref-Needham_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Needham-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Blender_(magazine)" title="Blender (magazine)">Blender</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Alex Pappademas described the album as "a surprising return to form" and stated "Even if it’s a fluke, <i>Stillmatic</i> still feels like a ren-Nas-sance".<sup id="cite_ref-Pappademas_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pappademas-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Steve Jones of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> gave it three-and-a-half out of four stars and stated, "dis songs aside, Nas' strength has always been his incisive lyrical analyses of current events."<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> In his review for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, Selwyn Seyfu Hinds wrote "<i>Stillmatic</i> isn't merely a reunion or rehash of <i><a href="/wiki/Illmatic" title="Illmatic">Illmatic</a></i> themes. The Nas on this record has grown, with the emotional expansion such maturation suggests. For one, he has never before drawn upon his anger, with a burning focus and controlled intensity that underscores nearly every song. Some of it can surely be ascribed to the <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z%E2%80%93Nas_feud" title="Jay-Z–Nas feud">Jay-Z battle</a>, but more seems due to the deeper, internal struggle Nas has waged against the fallout from his early, precocious success".<sup id="cite_ref-Hinds_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinds-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In contrast, <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine gave it two-and-a-half out of five stars, and stated "Striving to maintain street <a href="/wiki/Credibility" title="Credibility">cred</a> while reaching for pop success has left Nas vacillating clumsily on past projects, and this record is riddled with similar inconsistencies. One moment he casts himself as a gritty cat who feels most at home on a project bench, calling out neighborhood snakes, and ducking gunshots. The next, he's delivering dumbed-down verses over the <a href="/wiki/Trackmasters" title="Trackmasters">Trackmasters</a>' rinky-dink rendition of <a href="/wiki/Tears_for_Fears" title="Tears for Fears">Tears for Fears</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-McGuire_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGuire-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> John Bush from <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> wrote "Dropping many of the mainstream hooks and featured performers in order to focus his rapping, Nas proves he's still a world-class rhymer, but he does sound out of touch in the process of defending his honor. Despite the many highlights, a few of the tracks just end up weighing him down".<sup id="cite_ref-Bush_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Brett Berliner from <a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a> remarked "<i>Stillmatic</i> features the best rhymes from Nas since his debut, and possibly the best rhymes of the year, rivaled maybe only by <a href="/wiki/Ghostface_Killah" title="Ghostface Killah">Ghostface Killah</a>". He however was unfavorable of the album's guests, and some of its production, and went on to comment "<i>Stillmatic</i> was billed as a recreation of <i>Illmatic</i>, where the only rhyming guest was the underrated <a href="/wiki/AZ_(rapper)" title="AZ (rapper)">AZ</a>. However, 5 other guest rappers appear on the album, including Nas’ crew, the <a href="/wiki/Bravehearts" title="Bravehearts">Bravehearts</a>, and the mundane Millennium Thug. The guest rappers only detract from the album, and the guest singers that appear don’t add a whole lot either".<sup id="cite_ref-Berliner_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berliner-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Despite a mixed response from most mainstream music publications, <i>Stillmatic</i> achieved a fair amount of acclaim from within the hip hop community, who viewed it as a comeback album. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i> magazine gave it '5 mics', a rating they reserve only for classic hip-hop albums.<sup id="cite_ref-Source_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Source-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> HipHopDx also gave it a maximum five rating, and wrote "Easily one of the best albums of the year, <i>Stillmatic</i> runs the gamut of street poetry to storytelling and bravado. This CD is filled with instant classics".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Although panning several of the album's songs, Steve Juon from RapReviews gave it a 9 out of 10 rating and concluded "While no album may ever top his debut's brilliance, this one comes close enough to make even his most passionate haters happy to hear the raw essence of hip-hop revealed in all its true glory".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Giving <i>Stillmatic</i> a three and-a-half out of five rating, Elizabeth Mendez Berry of <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> was less favorable, calling it "infuriatingly inconsistent", but she went on to extol it as "an exercise in lyrical courage and musical might".<sup id="cite_ref-Berry_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berry-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Rolling_Stone_Album_Guide" title="The New Rolling Stone Album Guide" class="mw-redirect">The New Rolling Stone Album Guide</a></i> (2004), <i>Rolling Stone</i> journalist Chris Ryan gave the album three stars and called it "a return to form".<sup id="cite_ref-RSguide_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSguide-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Ryan wrote that it "finds Nas sticking with what works, creative storyraps and trenchant social commentary. He still errs when he makes attempts at club tracks, but the album is largely a success."<sup id="cite_ref-RSguide_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSguide-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Rock" title="Chris Rock">Chris Rock</a> compiled a list of his Top 25 Hip-Hop Albums of all time, to which he ranked <i>Stillmatic</i> at number 20, commenting "It's like <i><a href="/wiki/Mama_Said_Knock_You_Out" title="Mama Said Knock You Out">Mama Said Knock You Out</a></i> eleven years earlier, where a guy just reclaimed his spot with some great records".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<dt>Notes</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>The track "Braveheart Party" was removed from later pressings of <i>Stillmatic</i> at Mary J. Blige's request.<sup id="cite_ref-mtv_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The Japanese release of <i>Stillmatic</i> features three additional tracks: "No Idea's Original," "Everybody's Crazy" and "Black Zombies." Each can also be found on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Tapes" title="The Lost Tapes">The Lost Tapes</a></i>, a compilation album that was released in 2002.</li>
<li>A limited edition version of <i>Stillmatic</i> contains a bonus disc with snippets from five songs on <i>The Lost Tapes</i>.</li>
</ul>
