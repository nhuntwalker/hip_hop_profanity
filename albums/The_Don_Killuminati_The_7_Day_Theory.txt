<p><i><b>The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory</b></i> (commonly shortened to <i><b>The 7 Day Theory</b></i> or <i><b>Don Killuminati</b></i> and sometimes called <i><b>Makaveli</b></i>) is the fifth and final <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a>. Released under the new stage name Makaveli, it was his first studio album to be posthumously released.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was completely finished in a total of seven days during the month of August 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The lyrics were written and recorded in only three days and mixing took an additional four days. These are among the very last songs Shakur recorded before his fatal shooting on September 7, 1996. The album was originally due for release in March 1997, but due to his death, <a href="/wiki/Suge_Knight" title="Suge Knight">Suge Knight</a> released it four months earlier.</p>
<p>In 2005, MTV.com ranked <i>The 7 Day Theory</i> at #9 on their greatest hip hop albums of all time list<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and, in 2006, recognized it as a classic.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The emotion and anger showcased on the album has been admired by a large part of the hip-hop community, including other rappers.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Ronald "Riskie" Brent is the creator of <i>The 7 Day Theory</i> cover painting.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> George "Papa G" Pryce, Former Head of Publicity for Death Row, claimed that "Makaveli which we did was a sort of tongue-in-cheek, and it was not really to come out, [but] after Tupac was murdered, it did come out... Before that, it was going to be a sort of an underground."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The album peaked at number one on the <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart and the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, also making Shakur the second artist to have a number-one album while deceased ever since 1995 when Latin-pop singer, <a href="/wiki/Selena" title="Selena">Selena</a> posthumously released <i><a href="/wiki/Dreaming_of_You" title="Dreaming of You">Dreaming of You</a></i>, which also peaked at number one on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 as well.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The album generated the second-highest debut-week sales total of any album that year,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> selling 664,000 within its first week in stores. The album was certified 3x platinum in April 1997 and then 4x Platinum on June 15, 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It had sold 3,911,787 copies in the United States by 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Don_Killuminati:_The_7_Day_Theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though Shakur had been using the name Makaveli as far back as <i>All Eyez On Me</i>, the Makaveli album was conceived as a sort of introduction for Shakur's alternate stage name. Thus, the album’s sleeve contains the text, "Exit 2Pac, Enter Makaveli".</p>
<p>The album cover, which features Shakur on the cross in an attempt to convey his <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion" title="Crucifixion">crucifixion</a> by the media, is intended to imply an artistic resurrection. Within the next year in 1997, a music video entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Smile_(Scarface_song)" title="Smile (Scarface song)">Smile</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Scarface_(rapper)" title="Scarface (rapper)">Scarface</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Geto_Boys" title="Geto Boys">Geto Boys</a> on his album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Untouchable" title="The Untouchable">The Untouchable</a></i> featured Shakur as a performer, some showing his name listed as Makaveli. The video showed him depicted in a very similar type of crucifixion resembling the cover of this album.</p>
<p>While <i><a href="/wiki/All_Eyez_on_Me" title="All Eyez on Me">All Eyez on Me</a></i> was considered by Shakur "a celebration of life"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, <i>The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory</i> is a much darker album<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Makaveli's style of <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapping</a> is still emotional, but is intensified throughout this album. Some songs on the album contain both subtle and direct insults to Shakur's rivals at the height of the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast%E2%80%93West_Coast_hip_hop_rivalry" title="East Coast–West Coast hip hop rivalry">East Coast–West Coast feud</a>. Rappers insulted on the album include <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sean_Combs" title="Sean Combs">Puff Daddy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>. Although Shakur insulted rapper <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a> on "Intro/Bomb First (My Second Reply)" and "Against All Odds", rapper <a href="/wiki/Young_Noble" title="Young Noble">Young Noble</a>, who appeared on several songs on <i>The 7 Day Theory</i>, stated in an interview that the <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a> song "I Gave You Power" served as a main inspiration for Shakur's "Me and My Girlfriend".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Nas was to appear on <i>Makaveli: 7 day theory</i>, due to his and Shakur's reconciliation in 1996; Shakur was murdered before any collaboration could occur. <i>The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory</i> was recorded and mixed in a mere 7 days in August 1996. It was released November 5, 1996 and debuted at number one on both the pop album and R&amp;B/Hip-Hop album charts, with 663,000 albums sold in its first week.</p>
<p>The album was recorded at Can-Am Studios in <a href="/wiki/Tarzana,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Tarzana, Los Angeles, California" class="mw-redirect">Tarzana, Los Angeles, California</a> over 7 days in the month of August 1996.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> During those seven days 21 songs were completed, 12 of which made the final product. The album did not feature the star-studded guest list that <i>All Eyez on Me</i> did. Most of the guest verses are supplied by Shakur's group <a href="/wiki/The_Outlawz" title="The Outlawz" class="mw-redirect">The Outlawz</a>. The only verse that was not from one of the Outlawz was from <a href="/wiki/Bad_Azz_(rapper)" title="Bad Azz (rapper)">Bad Azz</a>. Young Noble of the Outlawz recalled:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>We had started writing the shit and we was taking long. 'Pac was like, "Who got something? Bad Azz you got something?" and it fit perfect, so it was meant for Bad Azz to be on that song. We had already been on a million 'Pac songs. That was his way of motivating us like, "If y'all ain't ready, then you don't make the song."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Don_Killuminati:_The_7_Day_Theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory</i> has also gained praise for its production. Although many of Shakur's usual producers were not involved in the project, the producers still managed to come through for the project. The only producer with whom Shakur had worked prior to this album was <a href="/wiki/QD3" title="QD3" class="mw-redirect">QD3</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Quincy_Jones" title="Quincy Jones">Quincy Jones</a> and brother of Shakur's girlfriend <a href="/wiki/Kidada_Jones" title="Kidada Jones">Kidada Jones</a>. Shakur also co-produces three tracks on the album. The other two producers were Hurt-M-Badd and Darryl “Big D” Harper. <a href="/wiki/E.D.I._Mean" title="E.D.I. Mean">E.D.I. Mean</a> of the Outlawz recalls:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>At the time Hurt-M-Badd, who was just an up-and-coming producer at Death Row, and Darryl Harper, who was an R&amp;B producer - Suge had him working on all the R&amp;B projects - they had a green room up in Can-Am [Studios] which everybody around Death Row called the "wack room" because they said "Ain't nothing but wack shit come out of there." But we was up in the studio one day and we trying to get music done - ain't none of us producers - we see them two niggas in the "Wack room" and 'Pac like, "Go get them niggas." So niggas go bring them, 'Pac just putting niggas to work like, "I need a beat here, I need y'all to do this, do that." And these are niggas that nobody at Death Row was fucking with. They'll tell you themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Before Tupac's death, he wanted to start his own record label, which he envisioned as Makaveli Records. Tupac fulfilled his three-album obligation to Death Row quickly in order to move on from Death Row Records and start Makaveli Records. According to personal bodyguard Frank Alexander, Tupac didn`t like Death Row. Artists slated to be on Makaveli Records included not only himself, but the rest of The Outlawz, as well as Storm and Bad Azz, among others.</p>
