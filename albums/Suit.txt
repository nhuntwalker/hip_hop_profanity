<p><i><b>Suit</b></i> is the fourth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> rapper <a href="/wiki/Nelly" title="Nelly">Nelly</a>. It was originally intended to be released on August 17, 2004, before being delayed and released on September 13, 2004 through <a href="/wiki/Universal_Records" title="Universal Records">Universal Records</a>. Production for the album was handled by several producers, including <a href="/wiki/The_Neptunes" title="The Neptunes">The Neptunes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazze_Pha" title="Jazze Pha">Jazze Pha</a>, Doe, AHM, Jayson "Koko" Bridges, Kuya Productions, Soulshock and Karlin, Ryan Bowser, Big Boi and Beat Bullies. Released in conjunction with <i><a href="/wiki/Sweat_(Nelly_album)" title="Sweat (Nelly album)">Sweat</a></i>, Nelly originally intended to release a single album before conceptualizing and releasing two albums simultaneously, both of which would contrast each other's themes. Nelly characterized <i>Sweat</i> as "more up-tempo" and "energetic" while describing <i>Suit</i> as more of "a grown-up and sexy vibe [...] it's more melodic".</p>
<p>The album produced three singles: "<a href="/wiki/My_Place" title="My Place">My Place</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Over_and_Over_(Nelly_song)" title="Over and Over (Nelly song)">Over and Over</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/%27N%27_Dey_Say" title="'N' Dey Say">'N' Dey Say</a>". Its lead single, "My Place", was a commercial success, topping the <a href="/wiki/Recorded_Music_NZ" title="Recorded Music NZ">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Chart" title="ARIA Chart" class="mw-redirect">Australian</a> and <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK</a> single charts, becoming Nelly's second number one on the former and latter charts. It peaked at number four on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>. "Over and Over" featuring <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Tim_McGraw" title="Tim McGraw">Tim McGraw</a> was also a success, peaking at number three on the Hot 100, and topping several charts worldwide, including the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Recorded_Music_Association" title="Irish Recorded Music Association">Irish</a>, Australian and UK Singles Charts. "My Place" and "Over and Over" were certified gold and platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), for shipments of 500,000 and one million copies, respectively. <i>Suit</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s final single, "'N' Dey Say", achieved moderate chart success, peaking at number sixty-four on the Hot 100 and number six on the UK Singles Chart.</p>
<p><i>Suit</i> was generally well received by <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">music critics</a>, who compared it with <i>Sweat</i>, praising both album's contrasting themes and musical content, though with some criticism also targeted towards their content, in regards to inconsistencies. <i>Suit</i> topped the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart in its opening week, selling 396,000 copies, becoming Nelly's third consecutive US number-one album. It went on to be certified three times platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), for shipments of three million copies. The album was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Grammy Award for Best Rap Album</a> at the <a href="/wiki/47th_Grammy_Awards" title="47th Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">47th Grammy Awards</a>, losing to <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_College_Dropout" title="The College Dropout">The College Dropout</a></i>.</p>

<p>While recording material for his third studio album, <a href="/wiki/Nelly" title="Nelly">Nelly</a> originally had the intention of producing one album. Songs were being recorded at a steady pace, with Nelly composing more ideas, to which he established the idea of two albums released simultaneously to house all the tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-mtv_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> On April 27, 2004, Nelly's representative initially described the upcoming albums as thematically dissimilar, "one is more melodic and party-oriented in the vein of records like "<a href="/wiki/E.I._(song)" title="E.I. (song)">E.I.</a>" and "Tip Drill", while the other was described as having a "harder edge".<sup id="cite_ref-mtv1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv1-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Nelly would release singles accompanied by music videos from both albums.<sup id="cite_ref-mtv1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv1-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Talking to <a href="/wiki/MTV_News" title="MTV News">MTV News</a>, Nelly went on to describe the differences between both albums; their titles of <i>Sweat</i> and <i>Suit</i> were announced on May 27, 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-mtv2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv2-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> He noted <i>Sweat</i> as "more up-tempo" and "energetic", while characterizing <i>Suit</i> as more of "a grown-up and sexy vibe [...] it's more melodic".<sup id="cite_ref-mtv_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Sweat</i> and <i>Suit</i> were originally intended to be released on August 17, 2004, but were pushed back and released on September 14 in the United States, Canada and Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-mtv2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv2-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amazon_us_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amazon_us-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amazon_canada_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amazon_canada-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amazon_japan_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amazon_japan-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Suit</i> was released on September 13, 2004 in the United Kingdom and Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-amazon_uk_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amazon_uk-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amazon_germany_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amazon_germany-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suit_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lead single "<a href="/wiki/My_Place" title="My Place">My Place</a>" interlopes composition from "Isn't It a Shame" by <a href="/wiki/Girl_group" title="Girl group">girl group</a> <a href="/wiki/Labelle" title="Labelle">Labelle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Randy_DeBarge" title="Randy DeBarge">Randy DeBarge</a>'s 1982 "<a href="/wiki/I_Like_It_(DeBarge_song)" title="I Like It (DeBarge song)">I Like It</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-liner_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liner-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> It also samples elements of the <a href="/wiki/The_Del-Vikings" title="The Del-Vikings">The Del-Vikings</a>' 1957 <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a> ballad "<a href="/wiki/Come_Go_with_Me" title="Come Go with Me">Come Go with Me</a>", which Jason Birchmeier of <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> recognized as a large influence to the song.<sup id="cite_ref-liner_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liner-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Bordering between the genres of <a href="/wiki/Hip-hop" title="Hip-hop" class="mw-redirect">hip-hop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Juon_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Juon-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> the song is primarily an "adult-oriented Southern-tinged hip-hop love song,"<sup id="cite_ref-Sanneh_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanneh-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> with guitars,<sup id="cite_ref-Sanneh_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanneh-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Synthesizers" title="Synthesizers" class="mw-redirect">synthesizers</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Sanneh_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanneh-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and "mixed high" and "mixed low" beats.<sup id="cite_ref-Sanneh_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanneh-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Other tracks that bare similar characteristics to "My Place" include "Paradise" (which also utilizes a "finger-picked guitar" throughout),<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> "Pretty Toes" and "She Don't Know My Name".<sup id="cite_ref-Sanneh_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanneh-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Birchmeier declared "My Place" as the successor to Nelly's 2002 "<a href="/wiki/Dilemma_(song)" title="Dilemma (song)">Dilemma</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> "Over and Over" is a break-up <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">ballad</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Browne_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> that combines Nelly’s <a href="/wiki/Sedation" title="Sedation">sedate</a> side with <a href="/wiki/Tim_McGraw" title="Tim McGraw">Tim McGraw</a>’s "twanging" <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a> and vocals,<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> with McGraw providing an <a href="/wiki/Overdubbing" title="Overdubbing">overdubbed</a> <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hook</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/%27N%27_Dey_Say" title="'N' Dey Say">'N' Dey Say</a>" samples <a href="/wiki/Spandau_Ballet" title="Spandau Ballet">Spandau Ballet</a>’s 1983 "<a href="/wiki/True_(Spandau_Ballet_song)" title="True (Spandau Ballet song)">True</a>";<sup id="cite_ref-liner_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liner-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> lyrically, the track and "Paradise" both engage in the themes of "hope and the promise of something better".<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> "Nobody Knows" featuring <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Hamilton_(musician)" title="Anthony Hamilton (musician)">Anthony Hamilton</a> was chosen as the "most sonically inventive track" on <i>Suit</i> by Todd Burns of <a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a>, who depicted its "<a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestral</a> stabs" and <a href="/wiki/MIDI" title="MIDI">MIDI</a> <a href="/wiki/Strings_(music)" title="Strings (music)" class="mw-redirect">strings</a>, which "interweave uneasily".<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suit_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/My_Place" title="My Place">My Place</a>" featuring <a href="/wiki/Jaheim" title="Jaheim">Jaheim</a> was released on July 20, 2004 as the album's lead single.<sup id="cite_ref-my_place_us_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-my_place_us-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> It written by Nelly, Eldra DeBarge, Etterlene Jordan, Kenneth Gamble, Leon Huff, Randy Edelman, William DeBarge, Jaheim Hoagland, Dorian Moore and produced by Doe. The song topped both the <a href="/wiki/Recorded_Music_NZ" title="Recorded Music NZ">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Chart" title="ARIA Chart" class="mw-redirect">Australian</a> single charts,<sup id="cite_ref-new_zealand_chart_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_zealand_chart-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-australia_chart_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-australia_chart-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> becoming Nelly's first number one on the former chart, and his second on the latter, following "<a href="/wiki/Dilemma_(song)" title="Dilemma (song)">Dilemma</a>". "My Place" remained on the charts for fifteen and thirteen weeks, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The song became his second number one on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> on the week of September 11, 2004, again following "Dilemma".<sup id="cite_ref-uk_chart_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk_chart-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> On the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> charts, it peaked at number four.<sup id="cite_ref-US100_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US100-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> "My Place" was certified gold by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA) and <a href="/wiki/Recorded_Music_NZ" title="Recorded Music NZ">Recorded Music NZ</a>, for shipments of 500,000 and 7,500 units, respectively. It was certified platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Recording_Industry_Association" title="Australian Recording Industry Association">Australian Recording Industry Association</a> (ARIA), for shipments of 70,000 copies.</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Over_and_Over_(Nelly_song)" title="Over and Over (Nelly song)">Over and Over</a>" featuring <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Tim_McGraw" title="Tim McGraw">Tim McGraw</a> was released as the second single on October 12, 2004. It was written by Nelly, Jayson "KoKo" Bridges, James D. Hargrove and Samuel McGraw and produced by Bridges. The song achieved commercial success, topping several charts, including the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Recorded_Music_Association" title="Irish Recorded Music Association">Irish Singles Chart</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-IRL_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRL-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Australian Singles Chart and UK Singles Chart.<sup id="cite_ref-australia_chart_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-australia_chart-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-uk_chart_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk_chart-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> It is the second single from the album to top the latter and former charts, following "<a href="/wiki/My_Place" title="My Place">My Place</a>". The song remained at number one in Australia for five consecutive weeks,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> and stayed atop in the UK for one week.<sup id="cite_ref-uk_chart_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk_chart-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> "Over and Over" peaked within the top five on the New Zealand Singles Chart and the Hot 100,<sup id="cite_ref-new_zealand_chart_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_zealand_chart-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Nelly's second top five hit on the Hot 100 from the album .<sup id="cite_ref-US100_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US100-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> "Over and Over" was certified two times platinum by the ARIA, denoting shipments of 140,000 copies. The RIAA awarded the song with a single platinum certification, for shipments of one million copies.</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/%27N%27_Dey_Say" title="'N' Dey Say">'N' Dey Say</a>" was released as the album's third and final single on May 23, 2005. The song was written by Nelly, Jayson Bridges and G. Kemp and produced by the former. It achieved its highest peak of number three on the <a href="/wiki/Ultratop" title="Ultratop">Belgian Singles Chart</a> (Flanders);<sup id="cite_ref-flanders_chart_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flanders_chart-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> it reached number six on the UK Singles Chart and number eight on the Belgian Singles Chart (Wallonia).<sup id="cite_ref-uk_chart_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk_chart-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wallonia_chart_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wallonia_chart-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The song reached the top twenty on the Irish Singles Chart,<sup id="cite_ref-IRL_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRL-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Official_Finnish_Charts" title="The Official Finnish Charts">Finnish Singles Chart</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FIN_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FIN-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> New Zealand Singles Chart and Australian Singles Chart.<sup id="cite_ref-new_zealand_chart_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_zealand_chart-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-australia_chart_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-australia_chart-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> "'N' Dey Say" peaked at number sixty-four on the Hot 100,<sup id="cite_ref-US100_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US100-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> achieving moderate chart success in other territories.</p>

<p><i>Suit</i> received generally favorable reviews from music critics. At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album has received an <a href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean" class="mw-redirect">average</a> score of 62, based on 16 reviews, which indicates "generally favorable reviews".<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Giving the album an A–, <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s David Browne lauded <i>Suit</i>, writing that Nelly "sounds convincing and appealing" throughout the album. Browne went on to further praise specific songs including the "summer-breezy" "Play it Off" and the "cozy hip-hop soul" housed "In My Life".<sup id="cite_ref-Browne_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> RapReviews.com's Steve Juon presented a similar response, giving the album a nine-out-of-ten rating.<sup id="cite_ref-Juon_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Juon-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Juon depicted <i>Suit</i> as being more formal compared to <i><a href="/wiki/Sweat_(Nelly_album)" title="Sweat (Nelly album)">Sweat</a></i> which takes creative risks. He praised the overall material quality within both <i>Suit</i> and <i>Sweat</i>, as well as their contrasting themes, going on to note the albums as "one of the best double albums" in hip-hop.<sup id="cite_ref-Juon_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Juon-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Jason Birchmeier of <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> was hesitant of the simultaneous release of two album's, as they may suffer from being one dimensional, but liked the musical content within both <i>Suit</i> and <i>Sweat</i>, noting the former's to be more diversified.<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Birchmeier summed up <i>Suit</i> as "a brief album with absolutely no filler", describing its tracks as "fun" and "catchy."<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In a mixed review, Jason King of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i> wrote that tracks on <i>Suit</i> have the "sole intention of ending up as your next ringtone".<sup id="cite_ref-King_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-King-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> King noted some songs to "crumble" due to their "simplistic approach", while tracks like "Flap Your Wings" as "addictive".<sup id="cite_ref-King_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-King-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a>'s Todd Burns praised several tracks on <i>Suit</i>, including "Over and Over" and "Nobody Knows", praising the former for combining Nelly's sedate side with <a href="/wiki/Tim_McGraw" title="Tim McGraw">Tim McGraw</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a> and vocals.<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite this, Burns saw the album's highlights as "minimal at best", and closed his review disapproving of the album's mainstream approach.<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> In a negative review, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Alexis Petridis showed distaste for both Nelly's vocal technique and <i>Suit</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-Petridis_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Petridis-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Petridis described the album's songs as "gummy R&amp;B ballads", while noting Nelly's <a href="/wiki/Egotism" title="Egotism">egotism</a> to overtake his abilities as an artist.<sup id="cite_ref-Petridis_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Petridis-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Suit</i> was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Grammy Award for Best Rap Album</a> at the <a href="/wiki/47th_Grammy_Awards" title="47th Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">47th Grammy Awards</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-grammy_nominee_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grammy_nominee-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> losing to <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_College_Dropout" title="The College Dropout">The College Dropout</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-grammy_winners_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grammy_winners-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suit_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In its opening week, <i>Suit</i> debuted atop the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 396,000 copies, becoming Nelly's third consecutive US number-one album following <i><a href="/wiki/Country_Grammar" title="Country Grammar">Country Grammar</a></i> (2000) and <i><a href="/wiki/Nellyville" title="Nellyville">Nellyville</a></i> (2002).<sup id="cite_ref-debut_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-debut-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> On the same week of <i>Suit</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s release, Nelly's <i><a href="/wiki/Sweat_(Nelly_album)" title="Sweat (Nelly album)">Sweat</a></i>, released in conjunction with the former album, debuted at number two on the <i>Billboard</i> 200, selling 342,000 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-debut_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-debut-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> The combination of both album's opening figures of 737,000 surpasses that of <i>Nellyville</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s, which debuted with 714,000 copies sold.<sup id="cite_ref-debut_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-debut-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> With the debuts, Nelly became the first act to achieve the feat of obtaining album's number one and two on the US <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip_Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip Hop Albums" class="mw-redirect">Top R&amp;B/Hip Hop Albums</a> chart simultaneously. He is the second artist, following American <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Guns_N%27_Roses" title="Guns N' Roses">Guns N' Roses</a>, to achieve the same feat but on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 chart.<sup id="cite_ref-debut_37-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-debut-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Guns N' Roses attained the accomplishment in 1991 with <i><a href="/wiki/Use_Your_Illusion_II" title="Use Your Illusion II">Use Your Illusion II</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Use_Your_Illusion_I" title="Use Your Illusion I">Use Your Illusion I</a></i>, with the former debuting at number one and the latter at number two, selling 770,000 and 685,000 copies, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-debut_37-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-debut-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> The following week of <i>Suit</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s release, it fell to number two on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 chart, selling 160,000 copies, being replaced by <a href="/wiki/Green_Day" title="Green Day">Green Day</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/American_Idiot" title="American Idiot">American Idiot</a></i> (2004).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> In its third week, <i>Suit</i> sold 118,000 copies, dropping to number five.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> In the album's fourth week of release, its sales increased to 122,000 copies, the only album of the week to have its sales rise; it sustained its position at number five.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> In its fifth week, <i>Suit</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s sales further increased to 144,000 units, gaining the title of Greatest Gainer, and rising from number five to three on the chart.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's sales continued to increase in its sixth week of release, selling 153,000 copies, moving from number three to two.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> It had passed one million copies in sales in the US that week.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> By November 1, 2004, <i>Suit</i> went on to be certified gold by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), denoting shipments of 500,000 copies. By January 19, 2005, the album was certified three times platinum by the RIAA, for shipments of three million copies.</p>
<p>On the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Albums_Chart" title="Canadian Albums Chart">Canadian Albums Chart</a>, <i>Suit</i> debuted at number one, becoming Nelly's first album to top the chart in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-canada_chart_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canada_chart-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> The album debuted at number six on the <a href="/wiki/Recorded_Music_NZ" title="Recorded Music NZ">New Zealand Albums Chart</a> on the week of September 20, 2004, remaining at number six for two weeks, and dropping out of the chart after thirteen weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-new_zealand_chart_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_zealand_chart-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> On the <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Charts" title="ARIA Charts">Australian Albums Chart</a>, <i>Suit</i> reached number seven, remaining on the chart for five weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-australia_chart_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-australia_chart-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Along with <i><a href="/wiki/Sweat_(Nelly_album)" title="Sweat (Nelly album)">Sweat</a></i>, <i>Suit</i> is Nelly's third consecutive album to peak within the top ten of the latter three charts.<sup id="cite_ref-new_zealand_chart_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_zealand_chart-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-australia_chart_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-australia_chart-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-canada_chart_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canada_chart-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> The album has been certified gold by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Recording_Industry_Association" title="Australian Recording Industry Association">Australian Recording Industry Association</a> (ARIA) and Recorded Music NZ, for shipments of 35,000 and 7,500 copies, respectively. The album reached the top ten on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_Albums_Chart" title="German Albums Chart" class="mw-redirect">German Albums Chart</a>, at number eight.<sup id="cite_ref-uk_chart_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk_chart-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-germany_chart_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-germany_chart-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> It has been certified platinum by the <a href="/wiki/British_Phonographic_Industry" title="British Phonographic Industry">British Phonographic Industry</a> (BPI), for shipments of 300,000 copies, on May 20, 2005. <i>Suit</i> peaked at number eleven on the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Hitparade" title="Swiss Hitparade">Swiss Albums Chart</a> on the week of September 26, 2004, dropping off the chart after eight weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-switzerland_chart_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-switzerland_chart-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> It remained on the <a href="/wiki/MegaCharts" title="MegaCharts">Dutch Albums Chart</a> for seven weeks, debuting and peaking at number seventeen.<sup id="cite_ref-netherlands_chart_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-netherlands_chart-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was less successful in other territories, charting within the top thirty on the <a href="/wiki/%C3%963_Austria_Top_40" title="Ö3 Austria Top 40">Austrian Albums Chart</a> and <a href="/wiki/VG-lista" title="VG-lista">Norwegian Albums Chart</a> at number twenty-nine.<sup id="cite_ref-austria_chart_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-austria_chart-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-norway_chart_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-norway_chart-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Suit</i> reached number thirty-four on the <a href="/wiki/Sverigetopplistan" title="Sverigetopplistan">Swedish Albums Chart</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-sweden_chart_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sweden_chart-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> number forty-three on the <a href="/wiki/Ultratop" title="Ultratop">Belgian Albums Chart</a> (Flanders) and number seventy-two on the <a href="/wiki/Syndicat_National_de_l%27%C3%89dition_Phonographique" title="Syndicat National de l'Édition Phonographique">French Albums Chart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-flanders_chart_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flanders_chart-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-france_chart_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-france_chart-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's lowest charting position was on the Belgian Albums Chart (Wallonia), at number eighty-three. It remained on it for three weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-wallonia_chart_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wallonia_chart-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
