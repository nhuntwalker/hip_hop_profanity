<p><i><b>No Way Out</b></i> is the debut studio album by American hip-hop artist <a href="/wiki/Sean_Combs" title="Sean Combs">Puff Daddy</a>, released on July 22, 1997 through record label <a href="/wiki/Bad_Boy_Records" title="Bad Boy Records">Bad Boy Records</a>. The label also receives official crediting on the album as "The Family", and the album's guest appearances are largely made up of the label's artists. Production is largely handled by Puff Daddy (real name Sean Combs) and various members of production group The Hitmen. The album, executively produced by Combs himself, <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deric_Angelettie" title="Deric Angelettie" class="mw-redirect">Deric "D-Dot" Angelettie</a> of The Hitmen, is predominantly rap-based, although it features generally introspective content, largely focused on close friend <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G.#March_1997_shooting_and_death" title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.'s killing</a> on March 9, 1997: however, the album features several more aggressive and positive songs recorded before the shooting. Originally titled <i>Hell Up in Harlem</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> the album underwent several changes after the death of his close friend emotionally affected Combs, who postponed the album in order to record several of the album's more emotional songs.</p>
<p>Upon its release, <i>No Way Out</i> was a significant commercial success, particularly in the United States where it reached number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> in its first week of release, selling 561,000 copies. It also produced five commercially successful singles, the most successful of these being the worldwide hit "<a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Be_Missing_You" title="I'll Be Missing You">I'll Be Missing You</a>", a tribute song aimed at The Notorious B.I.G., which became the first rap song ever to debut at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, and remained at the top of the chart for eleven consecutive weeks, whilst topping several other charts worldwide. The other four singles, the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)"><i>Billboard</i></a> hits "<a href="/wiki/Can%27t_Nobody_Hold_Me_Down" title="Can't Nobody Hold Me Down">Can't Nobody Hold Me Down</a>", "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_All_About_the_Benjamins" title="It's All About the Benjamins">It's All About the Benjamins</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Been_Around_the_World" title="Been Around the World">Been Around the World</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Victory_(Puff_Daddy_song)" title="Victory (Puff Daddy song)">Victory</a>", were also commercially successful, and all except the latter peaked in the top two positions of the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100.</p>
<p>The album also received generally mixed to positive reviews from music critics, who generally wrote positively about its emotional feel and its production, but were divided in their reviews towards Puff Daddy's rapping and songwriting. On September 7, 2000, the album was certified septuple platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> for sales of 7,000,000+ copies:<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> to date, it remains Combs' most commercially successful album. The album earned Combs five nominations at the <a href="/wiki/40th_Grammy_Awards" title="40th Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">40th Grammy Awards</a> in 1998, winning the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Grammy Award for Best Rap Album</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Grammy1998_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grammy1998-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_recording">Background and recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=No_Way_Out_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When asked about the meaning behind the album’s name, Puff Daddy said it was because after The Notorious B.I.G.’s death on March 9 earlier in the year, he felt that there was “No Way Out” of the way things were. The album was filled with some of the emotions Puff Daddy felt in mourning his close friend the Notorious B.I.G. In the song “Is This the End?” Puff Daddy talks about experiencing the drive-by in Los Angeles that took his friend's life. The album was originally titled <i>Hell Up in Harlem</i> until <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>'s death. The album topped the album charts in the US with 561,000 units sold in its first week of release, debuting at #1. On September 23, 1997, <i>No Way Out</i> was number one on Jet Magazine’s top 20 albums. The album topped music charts as number one for 6 months after its initial release. The album won the 1998 <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> for Best Rap Album. Alongside the album's Grammy, one of the album's hits, "It's All About the Benjamins" won the Viewer's Choice Award at the MTV Video Music Awards in September 1998. On September 7, 2000 the RIAA certified the album 7× multi-Platinum.</p>
<p>At the end of the 1998, out of the 10 songs to reach number one of the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100 list, four of these belonged to Bad Boy Records, Puff Daddy's record label which released his album. Out of the 52 weeks of the year, Bad Boy Records had a hit at number one for 22 weeks. The album had two <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100 number-one singles, "<a href="/wiki/Can%27t_Nobody_Hold_Me_Down" title="Can't Nobody Hold Me Down">Can't Nobody Hold Me Down</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Be_Missing_You" title="I'll Be Missing You">I'll Be Missing You</a>", and two number two singles, "<a href="/wiki/Been_Around_the_World" title="Been Around the World">Been Around the World</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_All_About_the_Benjamins" title="It's All About the Benjamins">It's All About the Benjamins</a>". The longest reign of any of the label's four hits was the hit "I'll Be Missing You", which topped the charts for 11 weeks. The inspiration for the album's hit "I'll Be Missing You" comes from The Police's 1983 hit "<a href="/wiki/Every_Breath_You_Take" title="Every Breath You Take">Every Breath You Take</a>". A year after the praise at both award ceremonies, Puff Daddy's success led him to be named as one of <i>Forbes</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span> 40 highest-paid entertainers alongside southern rapper Master P, and Oprah Winfrey. In the music video of "Been Around the World" Puff Daddy romances actress <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Lopez" title="Jennifer Lopez">Jennifer Lopez</a> in a fictional storyline. The video began to spread rumors of the two celebrities being in a very serious relationship. Furthermore, the music video for "<a href="/wiki/Victory_(Puff_Daddy_song)" title="Victory (Puff Daddy song)">Victory</a>" is one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_most_expensive_music_videos" title="List of most expensive music videos">most expensive videos ever</a> made.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> A song titled "No Way Out" by Puff Daddy appears on the soundtrack to the film <i><a href="/wiki/Money_Talks" title="Money Talks">Money Talks</a></i>, but is not included on the album.</p>
