<p><i><b>The Dynasty: Roc La Familia</b></i> is the fifth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> released by American rapper <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Its lead single, "<a href="/wiki/I_Just_Wanna_Love_U_(Give_It_2_Me)" title="I Just Wanna Love U (Give It 2 Me)">I Just Wanna Love U (Give It 2 Me)</a>", produced by <a href="/wiki/The_Neptunes" title="The Neptunes">The Neptunes</a>, was one of Jay-Z's most successful singles peaking at number 11 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>. The album debuted at #1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> with 557,789 copies sold in its first week and more than 2.3 million copies sold to date. The album generally received positive reviews from music critics and the album ended up becoming the 20th highest selling R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Album of the 2000–2010 decade according to <i>Billboard</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Dynasty:_Roc_La_Familia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally a <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a> compilation album, a showcase for Roc-A-Fella artists such as <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Bleek" title="Memphis Bleek">Memphis Bleek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beanie_Sigel" title="Beanie Sigel">Beanie Sigel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amil_(rapper)" title="Amil (rapper)">Amil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freeway_(rapper)" title="Freeway (rapper)">Freeway</a>, which eventually became another Jay-Z solo album (or at least marketed as one to increase sales), although the Roc-A-Fella artists are abundant on this album. Big name guest appearances have been limited to three – <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, <a href="/wiki/R._Kelly" title="R. Kelly">R. Kelly</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scarface_(rapper)" title="Scarface (rapper)">Scarface</a> – to make room for the Roc-A-Fella family. Rather than using big-name producers such as <a href="/wiki/Swizz_Beatz" title="Swizz Beatz">Swizz Beatz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timbaland" title="Timbaland">Timbaland</a>, who had featured prominently on <i><a href="/wiki/Vol._2..._Hard_Knock_Life" title="Vol. 2... Hard Knock Life">Vol. 2</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Vol._3..._Life_and_Times_of_S._Carter" title="Vol. 3... Life and Times of S. Carter">Vol. 3</a></i>, Jay-Z had decided to feature more up-and-coming producers at the time of this album, such as <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bink_(record_producer)" title="Bink (record producer)">Bink!</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Neptunes" title="The Neptunes">The Neptunes</a>. Partially as a result of this exposure, these producers would achieve notable success in the future. This was also the first album of Jay-Z's not to feature work by long time producer <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>. Given Just Blaze and Kanye West's penchant for utilizing soul samples in their beats, the album features a smoother sound than Jay-Z's previous efforts, though <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">Bay Area</a>-based producer <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rock" title="Rick Rock">Rick Rock</a> includes a more minimalist sound in his four productions.</p>
