<p><i><b>The Chronic</b></i> is the debut solo studio album by American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> recording artist <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, released December 15, 1992, on his own record label <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a>, and distributed by <a href="/wiki/Priority_Records" title="Priority Records">Priority Records</a>. Recording sessions for the album took place in June 1992 at Death Row Studios in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> and at <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Grundman_Mastering" title="Bernie Grundman Mastering" class="mw-redirect">Bernie Grundman Mastering</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Hollywood, Los Angeles, California" class="mw-redirect">Hollywood</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The album is named after a slang term for high-grade <a href="/wiki/Marijuana" title="Marijuana" class="mw-redirect">marijuana</a>, and its cover is a homage to <a href="/wiki/Zig-Zag_(company)" title="Zig-Zag (company)">Zig-Zag</a> rolling papers. It was recorded by Dr. Dre following his departure from hip hop group <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a> and its label <a href="/wiki/Ruthless_Records" title="Ruthless Records">Ruthless Records</a> over a financial dispute, and consequently features both subtle and direct insults at Ruthless and its owner, former N.W.A-member <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>. Although a solo album, it features many appearances by <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, who used the album as a launch pad for his own solo career.</p>
<p>Upon its release, <i>The Chronic</i> received positive reviews from most music critics and earned considerable sales success. The album peaked at number three on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> and had been certified triple platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> with sales of over three million copies in the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-NBAGT_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-NBAGT-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> which led to Dr. Dre becoming one of the top ten best-selling American performing artists of 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes-ThePopLife_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-ThePopLife-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Dr. Dre's production has been noted for founding and popularizing the <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a> sub-genre within <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a>. <i>The Chronic</i> has been widely regarded as one of the most important and influential albums of the 1990s and regarded by many fans and peers to be one of the most well-produced hip hop albums of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-RapCentral_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RapCentral-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Huey_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC25Years_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC25Years-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2003, the album was ranked number 138 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of <a href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The production on <i>The Chronic</i> was seen as innovative and ground-breaking, and received universal acclaim from critics. <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> commented on Dr. Dre's efforts, "Here, Dre established his patented G-funk sound: fat, blunted <a href="/wiki/Parliament_(band)" title="Parliament (band)">Parliament</a>-<a href="/wiki/Funkadelic" title="Funkadelic">Funkadelic</a> beats, soulful backing vocals, and live instruments in the rolling basslines and whiny synths"<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and that "For the next four years, it was virtually impossible to hear mainstream hip-hop that wasn't affected in some way by Dre and his patented G-funk."<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Unlike other hip hop acts (such as <a href="/wiki/The_Bomb_Squad" title="The Bomb Squad">The Bomb Squad</a>) that sampled heavily, Dr. Dre only utilized one or few samples per song.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a>'</i>s <i>The Immortals – The Greatest Artists of All Time</i>, where Dr. Dre was listed at number 54, <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> wrote on the album's production quality: "<i>The Chronic</i> is still the hip-hop equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Songs_in_the_Key_of_Life" title="Songs in the Key of Life">Songs in the Key of Life</a></i>. It's the benchmark you measure your album against if you're serious."<sup id="cite_ref-TheImmortals_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheImmortals-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Jon Pareles of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> described the production, writing "The bottom register is swampy synthesizer bass lines that openly emulate Parliament-Funkadelic; the upper end is often a lone keyboard line, whistling or blipping incessantly. In between are wide-open spaces that hold just a rhythm guitar, sparse keyboard chords."<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes-Dr.Dre_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-Dr.Dre-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Pareles observed that the songs "were smoother and simpler than East Coast rap, and [Dr. Dre and Snoop Dogg] decisively expanded the hip-hop audience into the suburbs."<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Until this point, mainstream hip hop had been primarily party music (for example, <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">Beastie Boys</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-BeastieBoys_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-BeastieBoys-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> or angry and politically charged (for example, <a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(band)" title="Public Enemy (band)" class="mw-redirect">Public Enemy</a> or <a href="/wiki/X-Clan" title="X-Clan" class="mw-redirect">X-Clan</a>), and had consisted almost entirely of samples and breakbeats.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-PublicEnemy_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-PublicEnemy-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMG-XClan_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-XClan-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Dr. Dre ushered in a new musical style and lyrics for hip hop. The beats were slower and mellower, borrowing from late 1970s and early 1980s funk music. By mixing these early influences with original live instrumentation, he created a distinctive genre known as <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes-Dr.Dre_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-Dr.Dre-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The album's lyrics caused some controversy, as the subject matter included homophobia and violent representations. It was noted that the album was a "frightening amalgam of inner-city street gangs that includes misogynist sexual politics and violent revenge scenarios".<sup id="cite_ref-Nelson_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>'s <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diss" class="extiw" title="wikt:diss">dissing</a> of former band-mate, <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>, resulted in vicious lyrics, which were mainly aimed at offending his enemy with homosexual implications, although it was noted to have "a spirited cleverness in the phrasing and rhymes; in other words, the song is offensive, but it's <i>creatively</i> offensive".<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-FWDD_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-FWDD-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Rapper <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, who had a significant role on the album, was praised for his lyrics and flow, and it was mentioned that "Coupled with his inventive rhymes, Snoop's distinctive style made him a superstar before he'd even released a recording of his own"<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-NBAGT_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-NBAGT-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and that his involvement was as important to the album's success as its production.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-SnoopDogg_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-SnoopDogg-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tour%C3%A9" title="Touré">Touré</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> remarks that "While Snoop delivers rhymes delicately, the content is anything but. Growing up poor, often surrounded by violence, and having served six months in the Wayside County jail outside of Los Angeles (for cocaine possession) gave Snoop Dogg experiences upon which he draws."<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes-SnoopDogg_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-SnoopDogg-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Snoop Dogg later commented on the "reality" of his lyrics, stating "My raps are incidents where either I saw it happen to one of my close homies or I know about it from just being in the ghetto. I can't rap about something I don't know. You'll never hear me rapping about no bachelor's degree. It's only what I know and that's that street life. It's all everyday life, reality."<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes-SnoopDogg_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-SnoopDogg-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Three singles were released from the album: "<a href="/wiki/Nuthin%27_but_a_%27G%27_Thang" title="Nuthin' but a 'G' Thang">Nuthin' but a 'G' Thang</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Fuck_wit_Dre_Day_(And_Everybody%27s_Celebratin%27)" title="Fuck wit Dre Day (And Everybody's Celebratin')">Fuck wit Dre Day</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Let_Me_Ride" title="Let Me Ride">Let Me Ride</a>". "Nuthin' but a 'G' Thang" was released as the first single on January 19, 1993. It peaked at number two on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and number one on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Singles_%26_Tracks" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Singles" title="Hot Rap Singles" class="mw-redirect">Hot Rap Singles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Singles_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Singles-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> It sold over a million copies and the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA) certified it <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">Platinum</a> on March 24, 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA-Singles_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-Singles-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The song was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Performance_by_a_Duo_or_Group" title="Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards_of_1994" title="Grammy Awards of 1994" class="mw-redirect">1994 Grammy Awards</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-RockOnTheNet_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RockOnTheNet-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> but lost to <a href="/wiki/Digable_Planets" title="Digable Planets">Digable Planets</a>' "Rebirth of Slick (Cool Like Dat)". Steve Huey of <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> named it "<i>the</i> archetypal G-funk single" and added "The sound, style, and performances of "Nuthin' but a 'G' Thang" were like nothing else on the early-'90s hip-hop scene."<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-NBAGT_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-NBAGT-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> He praised <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>'s performance, stating "[Snoop Dogg's] flow was laconic and relaxed, massively confident and capable of rapid-fire tongue-twisters, but coolly laid-back and almost effortless at the same time".<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-NBAGT_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-NBAGT-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Today it is one of the most critically and commercially lauded hip-hop/rap songs of all time. It is rated the 134th best song of all time by Acclaimedmusic.net, and the sixth best hip-hop/rap song,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> and voted in a VH1 poll as the 13th best song of the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"Fuck wit Dre Day (and Everybody's Celebratin')" was released as the second single on May 20, 1993 and like the previous single, it was a hit on multiple charts. It reached number eight on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and number six on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Singles_%26_Tracks" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Singles_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Singles-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> It sold over 500,000&#160;units and the RIAA certified it <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">Gold</a> on October 8, 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA-Singles_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-Singles-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Allmusic writer Steve Huey stated that the song was "a classic hip-hop single", citing Dr. Dre's production as "impeccable as ever, uniting his signature whiny synth melodies with a halting, descending bass line, a booming snare, and soulful female vocals in the background"<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-FWDD_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-FWDD-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> and alluded to Snoop Dogg, stating "Attitude was something Snoop had by the boatload, his drawling, laid-back delivery projecting unassailable control – it sounded lazy even though it wasn't, and that helped establish Snoop's don't-give-a-damn persona."<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-FWDD_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-FWDD-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The track contains direct insults to rappers East coast rapper <a href="/wiki/Tim_Dog" title="Tim Dog">Tim Dog</a>, <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> member <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" title="Luther Campbell">Luke</a>, and Dre's former accomplice <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>.</p>
<p>"Let Me Ride" was released as a <a href="/wiki/Cassette_single" title="Cassette single">cassette single</a> on September 13, 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> It experienced moderate success on the charts, reaching number&#160;34 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and number three on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Singles" title="Hot Rap Singles" class="mw-redirect">Hot Rap Singles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Singles_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Singles-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The song won Dr. Dre <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Best Rap Solo Performance" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Solo Performance</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards_of_1994" title="Grammy Awards of 1994" class="mw-redirect">1994 Grammy Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Grammy_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grammy-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> On this song and "Nuthin but a "G" Thang", <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine noted that Dr. Dre's verses were delivered with a "hypnotically intimidating ease" and made the songs feel like "dusk on a wide-open L.A. boulevard, full of possibility and menace".<sup id="cite_ref-TIME_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIME-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album has sold over four and half million copies in the United States and over eight million worldwide,<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-NBAGT_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-NBAGT-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RollingStoneTop500_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStoneTop500-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> and was certified three times <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">Platinum</a> by <a href="/wiki/RIAA" title="RIAA" class="mw-redirect">RIAA</a> on November 3, 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA-TheChronic_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-TheChronic-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> It is Dr. Dre's second best selling album, as his follow-up album, <i><a href="/wiki/2001_(Dr._Dre_album)" title="2001 (Dr. Dre album)">2001</a></i>, was certified sextuple Platinum.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA-2001_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-2001-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The album first appeared on music charts in 1993, peaking on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> at number three, and peaking on <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> at number one.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Sales_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Sales-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's three singles became top ten <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> singles.<sup id="cite_ref-bbsingles_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbsingles-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> "Nuthin' but a "G" Thang" peaked at number two on the <i>Billboard</i> <a href="/wiki/Hot_100" title="Hot 100" class="mw-redirect">Hot 100</a> and at number one on both the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Singles" title="Hot Rap Singles" class="mw-redirect">Hot Rap Singles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B Singles</a> charts.<sup id="cite_ref-bbsingles_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbsingles-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> "Fuck Wit Dre Day (And Everybody's Celebratin')" became a top ten single on four different charts, including the Hot R&amp;B Singles (number 6) and the Hot 100 (number 8).<sup id="cite_ref-bbsingles_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbsingles-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Chronic</i> re-entered the charts in 2003, peaking on the Ireland Albums Top&#160;75 at number&#160;48, and on the U.K. Albums Top&#160;75 in 2004 at number&#160;43.<sup id="cite_ref-acharts_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acharts-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>The Chronic</i> received generally positive reviews from contemporary <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">music critics</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Havelock Nelson wrote that the album "drops raw realism and pays tribute to hip-hop virtuosity."<sup id="cite_ref-Nelson_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> said that it "storms with rage, strolls with confidence, and reverberates with a social realism that's often ugly and horrifying".<sup id="cite_ref-EW_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EW-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i> claimed that Snoop Dogg's "<a href="/wiki/Slick_Rick" title="Slick Rick">Slick Rick</a>-esque style" produces "new ground for West Coast MCs" and that the album is "an innovative and progressive hip-hop package that must not be missed."<sup id="cite_ref-Matty_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matty-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> found "Dre's prowess as beat-master and street preacher" to be "undeniable".<sup id="cite_ref-Gundersen_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gundersen-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Jonathan Gold of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> wrote that, although the rappers lack "quick wit" and "rhythmic virtuosity", Dre's artistry is "on a par with <a href="/wiki/Phil_Spector" title="Phil Spector">Phil Spector</a>'s or <a href="/wiki/Brian_Wilson" title="Brian Wilson">Brian Wilson</a>'s." Gold argued that, because Dre recreates rather than samples beats and instrumental work, the finished album's fidelity is not inflected by that of "scratchy R&amp;B records that have been played a million times", unlike productions from East Coast hip hop.<sup id="cite_ref-Gold_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gold-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In a mixed review, <a href="/wiki/Greg_Kot" title="Greg Kot">Greg Kot</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> viewed the album as superficial, unrefined entertainment and felt that "Dre combines street potency with thuggish stupidity in equal measure."<sup id="cite_ref-Kot_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kot-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a>, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, dismissed it as "sociopathic <a href="/wiki/Easy-listening" title="Easy-listening" class="mw-redirect">easy-listening</a>" and "bad pop music" whose innovation—Dre's departure from sampling—is not inspired by contemporary <a href="/wiki/P-Funk" title="P-Funk">P-Funk</a>, but rather <a href="/wiki/Blaxploitation" title="Blaxploitation">blaxploitation</a> soundtracks, which led him to combine trite bass lines with imitations of "<a href="/wiki/Bernie_Worrell" title="Bernie Worrell">Bernie Worrell</a>'s high keyb <a href="/wiki/Sustain" title="Sustain">sustain</a>, a basically irritating sound that in context always signified fantasy, not reality—stoned self-loss or, at a best Dre never approaches, grandiose jive."<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> He felt that the brutal lyrical threats were vague and lacked detail,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> although he found Snoop Dogg's rhymes "<a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/drolly" class="extiw" title="wikt:drolly">drolly</a>" and not dull, unlike Dr. Dre.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Retrospective reviews of the album were also positive. <a href="/wiki/Jon_Pareles" title="Jon Pareles">Jon Pareles</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> said that <i>The Chronic</i> and <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Doggystyle" title="Doggystyle">Doggystyle</a></i> "made the gangsta life sound like a party occasionally interrupted by gunplay".<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a>'s Steve Huey compared Dr. Dre to his inspiration, <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(funk_musician)" title="George Clinton (funk musician)" class="mw-redirect">George Clinton</a>, stating "Dre's just as effortlessly funky, and he has a better feel for a hook, a knack that improbably landed gangsta rap on the pop charts".<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rhapsody_(online_music_service)" title="Rhapsody (online music service)">Rhapsody</a> writer Brolin Winning named the album as "an untouchable masterpiece of <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">California Gangsta Rap</a>" and that it had "track after track of G-Funk gems".<sup id="cite_ref-Rhapsody_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhapsody-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> On <i>Rolling Stone</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <i><a href="/wiki/500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">500 Greatest Albums of All Time</a></i>, it was noted that "Dre funked up the rhymes with a smooth bass-heavy production style and the laid-back delivery of then-unknown rapper <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Doggy Dogg</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-RollingStoneTop500_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStoneTop500-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine's Josh Tyrangiel states that Dr. Dre created "a sound that defined early 90's urban <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">L.A.</a> in the same way that <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a> defined 60's <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-TIME_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIME-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Laura Sinagra, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stone_Album_Guide" title="The Rolling Stone Album Guide">The Rolling Stone Album Guide</a></i> (2004), said that <i>The Chronic</i> "features system-busting <a href="/wiki/Funkadelic" title="Funkadelic">Funkadelic</a> beats designed to rumble your woofer while the matter-of-fact violence of the lyrics blows your smoke-filled mind".<sup id="cite_ref-Hoard_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoard-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1994, "Nuthin' but a "G" Thang" and "Let Me Ride" were nominated at the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards_of_1994" title="Grammy Awards of 1994" class="mw-redirect">36th Grammy Awards</a>, with the latter winning <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Best Rap Solo Performance" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Solo Performance</a> for Dr. Dre.<sup id="cite_ref-Grammy_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grammy-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> That year, readers of <i><a href="/wiki/Hip_Hop_Connection" title="Hip Hop Connection">Hip Hop Connection</a></i> voted it the fourth best album of all time, leading the magazine to speculate, "In a few years' time, it could even be remembered as <i>the</i> best rap album of all time."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Chronic</i> was included in <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> magazine's "100 Essential Albums of the 20th Century" and it was ranked at number six in their "Top&#160;10 Rap Albums of All Time". <i>Rolling Stone</i> ranked it at number 137 on their list of the <i><a href="/wiki/500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">500 Greatest Albums of All Time</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-RollingStoneTop500_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStoneTop500-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The record ranked at number eight in <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> magazine's "90 Greatest Albums of the '90s" and in 2005, it was ranked at number thirty-five in their "100 Greatest Albums, 1985–2005". <a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source magazine</a> originally gave the album four and a half mics out of five<sup id="cite_ref-Matty_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matty-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> and it was added to The Source's 100 Best Rap Albums. It was later revealed that while everybody at the magazine knew it was an instant classic, the music editor at that time had a strict policy of staying away from a perfect rating.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphpdx_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphpdx-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2005, <a href="/wiki/MTV_Networks" title="MTV Networks" class="mw-redirect">MTV Networks</a> listed <i>The Chronic</i> as the third greatest hip hop album in history.<sup id="cite_ref-MTV_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2006, <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine ranked it as one of the 100 greatest albums of all time<sup id="cite_ref-TIME_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIME-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> and it was listed in the book <i><a href="/wiki/1001_Albums_You_Must_Hear_Before_You_Die" title="1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die">1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1001A_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1001A-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> In a retrospective issue, <i><a href="/wiki/XXL_(magazine)" title="XXL (magazine)">XXL</a></i> magazine awarded <i>The Chronic</i> a perfect "XXL" rating.<sup id="cite_ref-XXL_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:269px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zig-Zag_Papers_%26_The_Chronic.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/48/Zig-Zag_Papers_%26_The_Chronic.jpg/267px-Zig-Zag_Papers_%26_The_Chronic.jpg" width="267" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/48/Zig-Zag_Papers_%26_The_Chronic.jpg/401px-Zig-Zag_Papers_%26_The_Chronic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/48/Zig-Zag_Papers_%26_The_Chronic.jpg 2x" data-file-width="415" data-file-height="238" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zig-Zag_Papers_%26_The_Chronic.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.24wmf6/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Comparison of <a href="/wiki/Zig-Zag_(company)" title="Zig-Zag (company)">Zig-Zag</a> rolling papers with <i>The Chronic</i> album cover</div>
</div>

<p>Having split from <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>, Dr. Dre's first solo album established him as one of the biggest hip hop stars of his era.<sup id="cite_ref-RapCentral_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RapCentral-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Music" title="Yahoo! Music">Yahoo! Music</a> writer S.L. Duff wrote of the album's impact on his status in hip hop at the time, stating "Dre's considerable reputation is based on this release, alongside his production technique on Snoop's <i>Doggy Style</i> and his early work with N.W.A. Whatever one thinks of the over-the-top bravado rapping, the tracks and beats Dre assembled are beyond reproach".<sup id="cite_ref-Duff_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duff-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Chronic</i> brought <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a> to the mainstream – a genre defined by slow bass beats and melodic synthesizers, topped by <a href="/wiki/P-Funk" title="P-Funk">P-Funk</a> samples, female vocals, and a <a href="/wiki/Laconic_phrase" title="Laconic phrase">laconic</a>, laid-back lyrical delivery referred to as a "lazy drawl". The album takes its name from a slang term for premium grade cannabis, Chronic. The album cover is an homage to <a href="/wiki/Zig-Zag_(company)" title="Zig-Zag (company)">Zig-Zag</a> rolling papers.<sup id="cite_ref-RapCentral_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RapCentral-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> said that, although he "can't stand" it, he respects <i>The Chronic</i> "for its influence and iconicity".<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album launched the careers of <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast hip hop</a> artists, including <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Doggy Dogg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daz_Dillinger" title="Daz Dillinger">Daz Dillinger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurupt" title="Kurupt">Kurupt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Warren_G" title="Warren G">Warren G</a>, Dr. Dre's stepbrother – all of whom pursued successful commercial careers.<sup id="cite_ref-RapCentral_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RapCentral-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Chronic</i> is widely regarded as the album that re-defined West Coast hip hop,<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> demonstrated <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a>'s commercial potential as a multi-<a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">platinum</a> commodity, and established G-funk as the most popular sound in hip hop music for several years after its release, with Dr. Dre producing major albums that drew heavily on his production style.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's success established <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a> as a dominant force in 1990s hip hop.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> It has been re-released 3 times, first as a remastered CD, then as a remastered <a href="/wiki/DualDisc" title="DualDisc">DualDisc</a> with enhanced stereo and four videos, and in 2009 as "The Chronic Re-Lit" with a bonus DVD containing a 30 minute interview and 7 unreleased tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The singles "Fuck wit Dre Day" and "Nuthin' but a "G" Thang" are in best-selling video game <i><a href="/wiki/Grand_Theft_Auto:_San_Andreas" title="Grand Theft Auto: San Andreas">Grand Theft Auto: San Andreas</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-BBC_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SOSD_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOSD-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> on the fictional radio station <a href="/wiki/Radio_Los_Santos" title="Radio Los Santos" class="mw-redirect">Radio Los Santos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
