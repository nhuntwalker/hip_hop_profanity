<p><i><b>The Documentary</b></i> is the debut <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by West Coast rapper and former <a href="/wiki/G-Unit" title="G-Unit">G-Unit</a> member, <a href="/wiki/Game_(rapper)" title="Game (rapper)" class="mw-redirect">Game</a>, released on January 18, 2005, through <a href="/wiki/Aftermath_Entertainment" title="Aftermath Entertainment">Aftermath Entertainment</a>, <a href="/wiki/G-Unit_Records" title="G-Unit Records">G-Unit Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interscope_Records" title="Interscope Records">Interscope Records</a>. After recovering from a shooting in late 2001, The Game pursued a rap career and was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> who signed him to his Aftermath Entertainment label. The album features production provided by but not limited to Dr. Dre, <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Timbaland" title="Timbaland">Timbaland</a>, and guest vocals from <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Faith_Evans" title="Faith Evans">Faith Evans</a>, among others.</p>
<p><i>The Documentary</i> debuted at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 586,000 units in the first week. The <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> certified the album two times <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">platinum</a> in March 2005 and it sold over five million copies worldwide. The album received generally favorable reviews, with music critics praising the album's production. Since the album's release, The Game has been credited as a driving force in reviving the West Coast hip hop scene, which had been overshadowed by artists from the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_hip_hop" title="East Coast hip hop">East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midwest_hip_hop" title="Midwest hip hop">Midwest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_hip_hop" title="Southern hip hop">South</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Documentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Game was born Jayceon Terrell Taylor on November 29, 1979 in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in a primarily <a href="/wiki/Crips" title="Crips">Crip</a> gang neighborhood known as Santana Blocc,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> although he grew up to become a member of the <a href="/wiki/Bloods" title="Bloods">Bloods</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> He was born into a life of gang-banging and hustling. In an October 2006 interview with <a href="/wiki/MTV_News" title="MTV News">MTV News</a> correspondent <a href="/wiki/Sway_Calloway" title="Sway Calloway">Sway Calloway</a>, Game described his family as "<a href="/wiki/Dysfunctional_family" title="Dysfunctional family">dysfunctional.</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> When later interviewed, Game stated that at a young age, he recalled seeing both of his parents preparing to do <a href="/wiki/Drive-by_shooting" title="Drive-by shooting">drive-bys</a>. His father was a Nutty Block <a href="/wiki/Crips" title="Crips">Crip</a> and his mother a Hoover Crippelette. <a href="/wiki/Illegal_drug_trade" title="Illegal drug trade">drugs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gun" title="Gun">guns</a> were all around Game growing up. At around the age of 6, Game stated that a friend of his was murdered in the neighborhood by a teenager, for his clothes and shoes.<sup id="cite_ref-IMDbBio_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMDbBio-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At the age of 7, Game went into <a href="/wiki/Foster_care" title="Foster care">foster care</a>. In foster care he was teased by other children, he didn't go unnoticed however. Game's intelligence was acknowledged by his caretakers and he usually helped his foster brothers and sisters with their homework. Around 1989, Game met his idol, Eric Wright also known as <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a> founder of the rap group <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>, this being a defining moment in his life. Game's adolescence was one of many hardships, at 13, one of Game's older brothers, Jevon who was just 21 at the time was shot at a gas station. His brother had just received a record deal. Game stated that he felt his father played a hand in this by not being there and felt that if he had, his brother would not have been shot. Jevon died the day after Game visited him in the hospital promising that things would be better and lost time would be made up. Two years later at the age of 15, teenage Jayceon was removed from the foster care system and back into his mother's home. Lynette (Game's mother) and Game had troubles in the beginning, Game's father was not around.<sup id="cite_ref-IMDbBio_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMDbBio-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Game attended <a href="/wiki/Compton_High_School" title="Compton High School">Compton High School</a> and most of the students that were gang affiliated were <a href="/wiki/Crips" title="Crips">Crips</a>. His older half brother George Taylor III, known as <a href="/wiki/Big_Fase_100" title="Big Fase 100" class="mw-redirect">Big Fase 100</a>, attended <a href="/wiki/Centennial_High_School_(Compton,_California)" title="Centennial High School (Compton, California)">Centennial High School</a> and was the leader of the Cedar Block <a href="/wiki/Pirus" title="Pirus">Piru</a> <a href="/wiki/Bloods" title="Bloods">Bloods</a>, Game was beginning to follow in his brother's footsteps. But Game's natural abilities in athletics led him into a spot as point guard on the <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a> team. He also ran <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track</a> and did various other sports. After graduating from Compton High School in 1999 and had received many scholarship offers from various colleges,<sup id="cite_ref-TheGameBio_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGameBio-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Game chose to go and attended <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_University" title="Washington State University">Washington State University</a> on a basketball scholarship to play basketball. His ticket and way out was short-lived after being found with drugs in his possession. Game's scholarship was revoked, before being suspended in his first semester because of drug allegations. However, the university's athletic department refutes these claims.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It was then that he started fully embracing street life and turned towards selling drugs and running with gangs.<sup id="cite_ref-EarlyLife_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EarlyLife-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> At the age of 18, he began to follow his brother's footsteps. Game and his brother Big Fase had an apartment on the outskirts of Compton in <a href="/wiki/Bellflower,_California" title="Bellflower, California">Bellflower</a>. Shortly after moving there, they quickly had a monopoly on the drug trade, that was short-lived however. On October 1, 2001 while at the apartment alone, Game heard a knock on the door at 2 a.m. Wanting to make a late night sale, he opened the door to see a regular customer. His regular however brought two others with him. He and another man fought. Reaching for his pistol, he was shot execution style by one of the assailants five times. After laying still for several minutes, Game used his cell phone and called the <a href="/wiki/Ambulance" title="Ambulance">ambulance</a>. Affected by his wounds, he went into a three-day <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">coma</a> as a result.<sup id="cite_ref-IMDbBio_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMDbBio-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While recovering in the hospital after being attacked in late 2001, Game told his brother to go out and buy all of the classic hip hop albums. He studied seminal hip hop albums such as <a href="/wiki/Nasty_Nas" title="Nasty Nas" class="mw-redirect">Nas</a>' 1994 debut <a href="/wiki/Illmatic" title="Illmatic">Illmatic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Reasonable_Doubt_(album)" title="Reasonable Doubt (album)">Reasonable Doubt</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ready_to_Die" title="Ready to Die">Ready to Die</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Doggystyle" title="Doggystyle">Doggystyle</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a>", <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/All_Eyez_on_Me" title="All Eyez on Me">All Eyez on Me</a></i> and albums by <a href="/wiki/Big_Daddy_Kane" title="Big Daddy Kane">Big Daddy Kane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ny_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ny-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> While Studying all the various influential rap albums in the course of five months, Game developed a strategy to become a rapper himself and with help from Big Fase, they founded <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Wall_Street_Records" title="The Black Wall Street Records">The Black Wall Street Records</a>. The label originally featured such artists as <a href="/wiki/Glasses_Malone" title="Glasses Malone">Glasses Malone</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vita_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vita (rapper) (page does not exist)">Vita</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nu_Jerzey_Devil" title="Nu Jerzey Devil" class="mw-redirect">Nu Jerzey Devil</a>, along with The Game himself.<sup id="cite_ref-Shadows_3_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shadows_3-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> His stage name was coined by his grandmother cause he stated that she used to call him Game saying he was game for anything.<sup id="cite_ref-Shadows_2_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shadows_2-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The Game first gained prominence in February 2002, when he attended a hip-hop summit hosted by <a href="/wiki/Russell_Simmons" title="Russell Simmons">Russell Simmons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Farrakhan" title="Louis Farrakhan">Louis Farrakhan</a> to participate in their freestyling event.<sup id="cite_ref-Larkin_1998_6653_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Larkin_1998_6653-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> After the event (in which he demonstrated his freestyling skills), he got a record deal with the independent label, <a href="/wiki/Get_Low_Recordz" title="Get Low Recordz">Get Low Recordz</a> owned by <a href="/wiki/JT_the_Bigga_Figga" title="JT the Bigga Figga">JT the Bigga Figga</a>. They flew together to San Francisco to record a demo CD, released a month later on the streets (later released as <a href="/wiki/Untold_Story_(album)" title="Untold Story (album)">Untold Story</a> on October 5, 2004 through Get Low Recordz) that would generate Game's first street buzz.</p>
<p>Soon after <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> was passed Game's demo CD and had been suitably impressed. According to Aftermath A&amp;R Angelo Sanders in an interview with <a href="/wiki/HitQuarters" title="HitQuarters">HitQuarters</a>, when Dre then met him, "He saw the swagger in him and thought he could do something with him." Later Game was signed to <a href="/wiki/Aftermath_Entertainment" title="Aftermath Entertainment">Aftermath Entertainment</a> only a week later through a joint deal with <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>'s <a href="/wiki/G-Unit_Records" title="G-Unit Records">G-Unit Records</a>. Although originally signed as an artist, <a href="/wiki/Interscope_Records" title="Interscope Records">Interscope Records</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Iovine" title="Jimmy Iovine">Jimmy Iovine</a> and Dr. Dre decided to have Game also work with 50 Cent and <a href="/wiki/G-Unit" title="G-Unit">G-Unit</a>. The arrangement was to help build a growing buzz around Game.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-observer_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-observer-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Since then, he made numerous cameo appearances in music videos by 50 Cent, <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Banks" title="Lloyd Banks">Lloyd Banks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Young_Buck" title="Young Buck">Young Buck</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fabolous" title="Fabolous">Fabolous</a>, first appearing on the music video of "<a href="/wiki/In_da_Club" title="In da Club">In da Club</a>", dancing with a girl. Game spent the next two-and-a-half years working on his debut album and being mentored by Dr. Dre.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Not having dropped an yet album despite being signed onto Aftermath Entertainment and Interscope Records for a while, Game still created hype around just his image alone. He appeared in ads for <a href="/wiki/Sean_Combs" title="Sean Combs">Sean Combs</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sean_John" title="Sean John">Sean John</a> clothing company and had an endorsement deal with <a href="/wiki/Boost_Mobile" title="Boost Mobile">Boost Mobile</a> appearing in a commercial alongside <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>. Game also appeared heavily on the mixtape circuit and guest starred on mixtapes for <a href="/wiki/DJ_Green_Lantern" title="DJ Green Lantern">DJ Green Lantern</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Diplomats" title="The Diplomats">The Diplomats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/G-Unit" title="G-Unit">G-Unit</a>. The first single released with Game on it was "<a href="/wiki/Certified_Gangstas" title="Certified Gangstas">Certified Gangstas</a>". Game guest starred on it alongside <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jones_(rapper)" title="Jim Jones (rapper)">Jim Jones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cam%27ron" title="Cam'ron">Cam'ron</a>. Though the single wasn't considered mainstream, the buzz increased around the west coast rapper.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Documentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After The Game signed with G-Unit, he recorded nine tracks with <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a> in his home studio in <a href="/wiki/Farmington,_Connecticut" title="Farmington, Connecticut">Farmington, Connecticut</a>. He then went back to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> and finished up the album with Dr. Dre.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Also in LA he worked with <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> on a song where Kanye did the chorus but the song was left on the cutting room floor.<sup id="cite_ref-www.hiphopdx.com_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.hiphopdx.com-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The Game worked on the album with the goal of reviving the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast hip hop</a> scene, which had been overshadowed in the past few years by artists from the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_hip_hop" title="East Coast hip hop">East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_hip_hop" title="Southern hip hop">South</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ny_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ny-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> In a 2005 <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> magazine interview, 50 Cent stated he was brought in by Interscope Records to work on the album, which he claimed was on the verge of being shelved and The Game being dropped from the label. However, in an interview with <a href="/wiki/Funkmaster_Flex" title="Funkmaster Flex">Funkmaster Flex</a>, The Game said his status was never uncertain nor was he close to being dropped.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> 50 Cent also claimed he wrote six of <i>The Documentary</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s eighteen tracks—"<a href="/wiki/Hate_It_or_Love_It" title="Hate It or Love It">Hate It or Love It</a>", "<a href="/wiki/How_We_Do_(song)" title="How We Do (song)">How We Do</a>", "Church for Thugs", "Special", "Higher", and "<a href="/wiki/Westside_Story_(song)" title="Westside Story (song)">Westside Story</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup>—and was not receiving proper credit for his work.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The Game denied the claim, saying 50 Cent helped write two songs.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Documentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">Lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Documentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Game recorded tracks based on his life experiences from his childhood to his success as a rapper. When asked about the album, he stated:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I grew up in a boys home and I was taken away from my parents when I was like 8 years old... Here I am, 24. When my album drops I will be 25 so that's 17 years I have been going through my struggle by myself. There are 17 tracks on my album and every track sheds light on a different situation I went through the last 17 years.<sup id="cite_ref-laststand_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laststand-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The rapper commented on the album's perception before its release, saying, "I know everybody was expecting gang-bang, 40-ounce, low-rider music, but that's not what I gave them... I'm telling a real story, and maybe there are people out there who can relate to my experiences."<sup id="cite_ref-ny_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ny-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> observed that "every song has a well-massaged hook and some immediate appeal, and verses that don't waste a lot of time getting to the point."<sup id="cite_ref-rs_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Documentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Documentary</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s big budget production from high profile hip hop producers was well received from critics.<sup id="cite_ref-stylus_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stylus-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The first half of the album contains "upbeat, gangsta boogie" tracks with the other half relegating "smoothed out R&amp;B maneuvers".<sup id="cite_ref-ign_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> At seven tracks, <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> co-executive produced the album with his "stripped-down cinematic" approach.<sup id="cite_ref-pfork_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pfork-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Westside_Story_(song)" title="Westside Story (song)">Westside Story</a>" contains an "evil sounding piano plink", "<a href="/wiki/Dreams_(The_Game_song)" title="Dreams (The Game song)" class="mw-redirect">Dreams</a>" has a "simultaneously smooth and eerie" beat, and "<a href="/wiki/Hate_It_or_Love_It" title="Hate It or Love It">Hate It or Love It</a>" unveils a "smoothed out R&amp;B funk vibe".<sup id="cite_ref-ign_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> "Higher" revolves around a pounding synth blast and "<a href="/wiki/How_We_Do_(song)" title="How We Do (song)">How We Do</a>" contains <a href="/wiki/Syncopated" title="Syncopated" class="mw-redirect">syncopated</a> hand claps<sup id="cite_ref-ign_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> with a beat described as "a hypnotic blast of sinister seduction powered by a deliciously primitive <a href="/wiki/Roland_TR-808" title="Roland TR-808">808 pattern</a> and a slinky synth."<sup id="cite_ref-av_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-av-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> "Don't Need Your Love" samples <a href="/wiki/Mary_J._Blige" title="Mary J. Blige">Mary J. Blige</a>'s "Not Gon Cry" and is one the album's more soulful songs.<sup id="cite_ref-dx_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dx-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> "Church for Thugs" delivers a "sing-song stylee over an accentuated sonic bed" and "<a href="/wiki/Put_You_on_the_Game" title="Put You on the Game">Put You on the Game</a>" is a club track containing "dark dirge[s] of synth".<sup id="cite_ref-ign_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although "Start from Scratch" features <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Marsha_Ambrosius" title="Marsha Ambrosius">Marsha Ambrosius</a>, the beat "eschews the traditional R&amp;B vibes" for more "aural intimidation". <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> called it "the most haunting inclusion on the album."<sup id="cite_ref-ign_29-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> "The Documentary" features a "busy backing track" of "crashing symphonics and tinny flares of synth", which one critic believed overshadowed the lyrics.<sup id="cite_ref-ign_29-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> "Runnin'" is a "dark, <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stygian" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:stygian">Stygian</a> tune augmented by tinges of R&amp;B mellowness."<sup id="cite_ref-ign_29-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> "No More Fun and Games" has a fast-paced beat<sup id="cite_ref-pop_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> that takes inspiration from early 1990s production.<sup id="cite_ref-rapreviews_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rapreviews-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> "We Ain't", which samples Dr. Dre's "The Watcher", takes <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>'s "chug laden synth gurgle" and is described as "one of the most menacingly catchy numbers on the entire album."<sup id="cite_ref-ign_29-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a> features on two "smoothed out"<sup id="cite_ref-ign_29-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> tracks; "Special" and the <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">g-funk</a>-inspired "Where I'm From".<sup id="cite_ref-yahoo_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> "Don't Worry" is an R&amp;B flavored track and despite its "minimal production", one reviewer wrote the song "still hits hard."<sup id="cite_ref-dx_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dx-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> The final track, "Like Father, Like Son", is driven by a "melodramatic, string-laden" beat.<sup id="cite_ref-pfork_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pfork-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title">Title</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Documentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album's title was originally "Nigga Witta Attitude Vol. 1" (a reference to <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> but was changed to "The Documentary" due to legal issues with an <a href="/wiki/Injunction" title="Injunction">injunction</a> filed at the request of <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>'s widow prevented him from using N.W.A.'s name in the album title.<sup id="cite_ref-laststand_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laststand-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The album was originally set for a late 2004 release, but since other high profile albums—including <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Encore_(Eminem_album)" title="Encore (Eminem album)">Encore</a></i>—were to be released around the same time, it was pushed back to January 18, 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> On September 28, 2004 The Game released a promotional album entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Westside_Story_(The_Game_album)" title="Westside Story (The Game album)" class="mw-redirect">Westside Story</a></i> through Aftermath Entertainment and G-Unit Records. The album was used to promote his debut studio album, <i>The Documentary</i>. In October 2004, The Game released his first independent album entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Untold_Story_(album)" title="Untold Story (album)">Untold Story</a></i> through <a href="/wiki/Get_Low_Recordz" title="Get Low Recordz">Get Low Recordz</a> owned by <a href="/wiki/JT_the_Bigga_Figga" title="JT the Bigga Figga">JT the Bigga Figga</a>, The album sold over 82,000 copies within its first three months.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> The album featured artists like <a href="/wiki/Sean_T_(rapper)" title="Sean T (rapper)" class="mw-redirect">Sean T</a>, <a href="/wiki/Young_Noble" title="Young Noble">Young Noble</a> (of the <a href="/wiki/Outlawz" title="Outlawz">Outlawz</a>), and JT the Bigga Figga.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Game also appeared on various mixtapes hosted by DJ's such as <a href="/wiki/DJ_Kayslay" title="DJ Kayslay" class="mw-redirect">DJ Kayslay</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Whoo_Kid" title="DJ Whoo Kid">DJ Whoo Kid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Clue%3F" title="DJ Clue?">DJ Clue?</a>. The Game also released a second mixtape <i>You Know What It Is Vol. 2</i> through his own record label and appeared on the video game <i><a href="/wiki/NBA_Live_2004" title="NBA Live 2004">NBA Live 2004</a></i> on a song produced by <a href="/wiki/Fredwreck" title="Fredwreck">Fredwreck</a> called "Can't Stop Me".<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> On November 15, 2004 The Game released a promotional Mixtape entitled <i>Charge It to the Game: The Mixtape</i> through Westside Records. The Mixtape was also used to promote his debut studio album.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Documentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Westside_Story_(song)" title="Westside Story (song)">Westside Story</a>" was the first official single to be released from <i>The Documentary</i>. It features <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>. The single entered the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> at number ninety-three, it charted at number 55 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> chart, and it charted at number 29 on the <a href="/wiki/Rhythmic_Top_40" title="Rhythmic Top 40" class="mw-redirect">Rhythmic Top 40</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> described it as "a kind of L.A. version of "<a href="/wiki/In_Da_Club" title="In Da Club" class="mw-redirect">In Da Club</a>" with "a simple keyboard part, a spare 808 beat and strings that manage to sound both stressed-out and catchy."<sup id="cite_ref-rs_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The Game has stated that this song is a tribute to <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a>, with a direct reference to him, saying "I got California love fuckin bitches to that Pac shit." The Game also makes references to Tupac's songs, <i><a href="/wiki/California_Love" title="California Love">California Love</a></i> and <i>Against All Odds</i>. Other references include: <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tha_Dogg_Pound" title="Tha Dogg Pound">Tha Dogg Pound (D.P.G.)</a> and their song <i><a href="/wiki/New_York,_New_York_(Tha_Dogg_Pound_song)" title="New York, New York (Tha Dogg Pound song)">New York</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Westside_Connection" title="Westside Connection">Westside Connection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> and his album <i><a href="/wiki/Thriller_(Michael_Jackson_album)" title="Thriller (Michael Jackson album)">Thriller</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Pooh" title="DJ Pooh">DJ Pooh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a>. There is a remix featuring <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a> singing the hook and a version with both Snoop Dogg and 50 Cent on it. The song was produced by <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scott_Storch" title="Scott Storch">Scott Storch</a>.</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/How_We_Do_(song)" title="How We Do (song)">How We Do</a>" was the second official single to be released from <i>The Documentary</i>. The song features rapper <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>. The single entered the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> at number 4, for four weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The single also charted at number 2 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> chart, it charted at number 2 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Tracks" title="Hot Rap Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot Rap Tracks</a> chart, it charted at number 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Rhythmic_Top_40" title="Rhythmic Top 40" class="mw-redirect">Rhythmic Top 40</a>, it charted at number 3 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Digital_Songs" title="Hot Digital Songs" class="mw-redirect">Hot Digital Songs</a>, it charted at number 38 on the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Tropical_Airplay" title="Latin Tropical Airplay" class="mw-redirect">Latin Tropical Airplay</a>, it charted at number 11 on the <a href="/wiki/Pop_100" title="Pop 100">Pop 100</a>, it charted at number 14 on the <a href="/wiki/Pop_100_Airplay" title="Pop 100 Airplay">Pop 100 Airplay</a>, also charting at number 14 on the <a href="/wiki/Top_40_Mainstream" title="Top 40 Mainstream" class="mw-redirect">Top 40 Mainstream</a>, and at number 16 on the <a href="/wiki/Top_40_Tracks" title="Top 40 Tracks">Top 40 Tracks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The single attained respectable international charting. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> at number 5, in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/German_Singles_Chart" title="German Singles Chart" class="mw-redirect">German Singles Chart</a> at number 9, in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Singles_Chart" title="Irish Singles Chart">Irish Singles Chart</a> at number 8, and in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Singles_Chart" title="Australian Singles Chart" class="mw-redirect">Australian Singles Chart</a> at number 18. The single was an instant hit with major air play. It was also moderately successful worldwide, reaching the top twenty in most countries.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> The music video was directed by <a href="/wiki/Hype_Williams" title="Hype Williams">Hype Williams</a>". The <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> certified the single <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">gold</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Hate_It_or_Love_It" title="Hate It or Love It">Hate It or Love It</a>" was the third official single to be released from <i>The Documentary</i>. The song features rapper <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>. The single was the most popular single from the album. The single entered the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> at number 2, it charted at number 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> chart, it charted at number 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Tracks" title="Hot Rap Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot Rap Tracks</a> chart, it charted at number 6 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Digital_Songs" title="Hot Digital Songs" class="mw-redirect">Hot Digital Songs</a> chart, it charted at number 39 on the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Tropical_Airplay" title="Latin Tropical Airplay" class="mw-redirect">Latin Tropical Airplay</a>, it charted at number 9 on the <a href="/wiki/Pop_100" title="Pop 100">Pop 100</a>, it charted at number 13 on the <a href="/wiki/Pop_100_Airplay" title="Pop 100 Airplay">Pop 100 Airplay</a>, it charted at number 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Rhythmic_Top_40" title="Rhythmic Top 40" class="mw-redirect">Rhythmic Top 40</a>, and at number 16 on the <a href="/wiki/Top_40_Mainstream" title="Top 40 Mainstream" class="mw-redirect">Top 40 Mainstream</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_42-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> The single attained respectable international charting. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> at number 4, in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/German_Singles_Chart" title="German Singles Chart" class="mw-redirect">German Singles Chart</a> at number 14, in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Singles_Chart" title="Irish Singles Chart">Irish Singles Chart</a> at number 5, in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Singles_Chart" title="Australian Singles Chart" class="mw-redirect">Australian Singles Chart</a> at number 23, and in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Singles_Chart" title="Danish Singles Chart" class="mw-redirect">Danish Singles Chart</a> at number 17.<sup id="cite_ref-danishcharts_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-danishcharts-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> The track uses a sample from the song "Rubber Band" performed by <a href="/wiki/The_Trammps" title="The Trammps">The Trammps</a> on their album The Legendary Zing Album. At the <a href="/wiki/2006_Grammy_Awards" title="2006 Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">2006 Grammy Awards</a>, it was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Performance_by_a_Duo_or_Group" title="Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Song" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Song">Best Rap Song</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a>, which was directed by the Saline Project, was nominated at the 2005 <a href="/wiki/MTV_Video_Music_Awards" title="MTV Video Music Awards" class="mw-redirect">MTV Video Music Awards</a> for <a href="/wiki/MTV_Video_Music_Award_for_Best_Rap_Video" title="MTV Video Music Award for Best Rap Video">Best Rap Video</a>, but lost to <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Number_One_Spot" title="Number One Spot">Number One Spot</a>". The <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> certified the single <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">gold</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Dreams_(The_Game_song)" title="Dreams (The Game song)" class="mw-redirect">Dreams</a>" was the fourth official single to be released from <i>The Documentary</i>. The single entered the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> at number 32, it charted at number 12 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> chart, it charted at number 5 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Tracks" title="Hot Rap Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot Rap Tracks</a> chart, it charted at number 74 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Digital_Songs" title="Hot Digital Songs" class="mw-redirect">Hot Digital Songs</a> chart, it charted at number 62 on the <a href="/wiki/Pop_100" title="Pop 100">Pop 100</a>, and charted at number 13 on the <a href="/wiki/Rhythmic_Top_40" title="Rhythmic Top 40" class="mw-redirect">Rhythmic Top 40</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_42-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The single attained international charting. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> at number 8, in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/German_Singles_Chart" title="German Singles Chart" class="mw-redirect">German Singles Chart</a> at number 71, in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Singles_Chart" title="Irish Singles Chart">Irish Singles Chart</a> at number 11, and in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Singles_Chart" title="Australian Singles Chart" class="mw-redirect">Australian Singles Chart</a> at number 42. It is produced by <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> and features a <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sample</a> of "No Money Down" by <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Butler_(singer)" title="Jerry Butler (singer)" class="mw-redirect">Jerry Butler</a>. Co-Written by Jimmy "Henchmen" Rosemonds' artist, "Beloved". The song is dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Yetunde_Price" title="Yetunde Price" class="mw-redirect">Yetunde Price</a> who was shot dead in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> One critic wrote The Game "sews together a soulful <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr." title="Martin Luther King Jr." class="mw-redirect">Martin Luther King Jr.</a> type speech with the acerbic wit and hustler charm of <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm X</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-pop_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Atwell" title="Phillip Atwell" class="mw-redirect">Phillip Atwell</a> directed the music video. The song is also known for having an incorrect lyric in 'I woke up from that coma 2001, about the same time Dre dropped 2001'. This references the <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> album <a href="/wiki/2001_(Dr._Dre_album)" title="2001 (Dr. Dre album)">2001</a>, actually released in the year 1999. The song was placed 16th on <a href="/wiki/About.com" title="About.com">about.com</a>'s Best Hip-Hop Songs of 2005. And had singer <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BDa" title="Mýa">Mýa Harrison</a> in the music video.</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Put_You_on_the_Game" title="Put You on the Game">Put You on the Game</a>" was the fifth and final official single from <i>The Documentary</i>. The single was the lowest charting single from the album, reaching only ninety-six on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Singles_%26_Tracks" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_42-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The single attained respectable international charting. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> at number 46, and in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> the single entered the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Singles_Chart" title="Irish Singles Chart">Irish Singles Chart</a> at number 22. The single was produced by <a href="/wiki/Timbaland" title="Timbaland">Timbaland</a> and co-produced by <a href="/wiki/Danja_(producer)" title="Danja (producer)" class="mw-redirect">Danja</a>. This was the first single released by The Game after his truce with 50 Cent fell apart and after The Game released <i>You Know What It Is Vol. 3</i>. As of recently a lawsuit has been filed from a Major India Record Company Saregama India, Timbaland, co-producer Nate "Danja" Hills, The Game, Interscope Records, along with a few other Production companies are being sued for using an uncleared sample from artist.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> features The Game with many appearances by his current and former <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Wall_Street_Records" title="The Black Wall Street Records">Black Wall Street</a> affiliates. The video shows various places in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County,_California" title="Los Angeles County, California">Los Angeles County</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Los_Angeles" title="Downtown Los Angeles">downtown Los Angeles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton</a>. The video shows many Los Angeles landmarks such as <a href="/wiki/Staples_Center" title="Staples Center">Staples Center</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Watts_Towers" title="Watts Towers">Watts Towers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_International_Airport" title="Los Angeles International Airport">LAX</a>. The Game refers to a number of artists, songs and albums in "Put You on The Game", including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/N.W.A." title="N.W.A." class="mw-redirect">N.W.A.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Makaveli</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(band)" title="Public Enemy (band)" class="mw-redirect">Public Enemy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flavor_Flav" title="Flavor Flav">Flavor Flav</a>, <a href="/wiki/G-Unit" title="G-Unit">G-Unit</a>, <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> and his song "<a href="/wiki/Let_Me_Ride" title="Let Me Ride">Let Me Ride</a>", <a href="/wiki/Eve_(entertainer)" title="Eve (entertainer)">Eve</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>. During the DVD <i><a href="/wiki/Stop_Snitchin,_Stop_Lyin" title="Stop Snitchin, Stop Lyin">Stop Snitchin, Stop Lyin</a></i> The Game watches the video for this song pausing at specific spots in the video which shows a dead man at on the ground blocked off by police tape. The Game then repeatedly states that he is wearing G-Unit sneakers, a diss at 50 Cent saying his career is dead. Damon Johnson directed the music video. <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> called it a "club track so <a href="/wiki/Crunk" title="Crunk">crunkalicious</a>, it's almost shocking that a California newbie — not a <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a> — was the recipient of its deep-fried <a href="/wiki/Southern_hip_hop" title="Southern hip hop">Southern</a> charms."<sup id="cite_ref-ew_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ew-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> The song was placed 44th on <a href="/wiki/About.com" title="About.com">about.com</a>'s Best Hip-Hop Songs of 2005.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Documentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Documentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Game is often credited as a driving force in bringing the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast hip hop</a> scene back to recognition.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> Before its release, he expressed his desire to have high opening week sales, saying, "I want to sell a million albums in my first week. And if I only sell one album the following week, I'm good."<sup id="cite_ref-ny_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ny-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> He also admitted feeling nervous about not being able to live up to the industry expectations, saying, "Yeah, I've got some butterflies. I'm worried about my first-week numbers, and I'm worried about living up to the hype."<sup id="cite_ref-ny_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ny-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The album debuted at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 586,000 copies in the first week.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> certified the album two times <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">platinum</a> on March 23, 2005,<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> and it was the tenth best-selling record of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> The album has since sold over 2.5 million units in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Documentary</i> peaked at the top fifteen in most European charts it entered. It peaked at number seven on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a> and remained on the chart for thirty-three weeks. It reached the top ten in the Netherlands, France, Ireland, Switzerland, and the top twenty in Belgium, Germany, and Norway.<sup id="cite_ref-acharts_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acharts-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> The album topped the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Albums_Chart" title="Canadian Albums Chart">Canadian Albums Chart</a> for three weeks<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> and on March 8, 2005, it was certified platinum with 1,000,000 units shipped.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> It has since sold over five million copies worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Upon its release, <i>The Documentary</i> received generally positive reviews from music critics. On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, <i>The Documentary</i> received an aggregate score of 72 out of 100 based on 19 reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-MC_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MC-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> called it "the best West Coast street-rap album since <a href="/wiki/DJ_Quik" title="DJ Quik">DJ Quik</a>'s 2002 LP <i><a href="/wiki/Under_tha_Influence" title="Under tha Influence">Under tha Influence</a></i>" and described the production as "a rich, triumphant sonic tapestry".<sup id="cite_ref-pfork_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pfork-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> wrote the album was an "excellent debut" that "hints at a lot of potential" and observed the "most remarkable aspect of the Game is how he can be such a blatant product of gangsta rap... and leave a mark so fast."<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> noted The Game was "going for emotional impact rather than dazzling wordplay or laughs"<sup id="cite_ref-rs_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a> described him as "a self-conscious, malicious, nihilistic gangsta rapper with a heart and lyrical content".<sup id="cite_ref-pop_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> believed the album was "dull even when he isn't describing his medical problems, this no-talent is masscult rock at its most brazen".<sup id="cite_ref-rob_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rob-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a> praised the production for being "a sonic classic of slow-rolling G-funk and glossy hyper-soul", but panned The Game for his name dropping, suggesting if he "cut all the references to rappers and albums... it'd be a good 15 to 20 minutes shorter—and probably a lot more compelling."<sup id="cite_ref-av_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-av-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> declared it "one of the best rap albums of the year"<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> suggested "with the brightest hip-hop stars aligning for him, the Game may have willed himself a popular masterpiece."<sup id="cite_ref-ew_50-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ew-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> criticized the large number of guest appearances, stating "the propensity of guest artists makes it hard to actually get a grasp on the rising star's own voice."<sup id="cite_ref-ign_29-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/MusicOMH" title="MusicOMH">MusicOMH</a> observed "like many rap albums The Documentary is too long, but it maintains a high level of interest" and overall, it was "an impressive effort" that "introduces a strong presence to the West Coast".<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a></i> wrote "no one disappoints" and despite the record being "so obviously and deeply grounded in marketing, it’s still an outstandingly solid and enjoyable" debut.<sup id="cite_ref-stylus_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stylus-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> noted The Game's "tough but straightforward rhyme style is appealing but not, usually, enthralling... This is a rapper who almost never forgets himself, who almost never loses himself in syllables just for the fun of it."<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i> criticized the rapper's lyrical skills, saying, "the Game's rhymes are about six degrees from totally artless".<sup id="cite_ref-vv_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vv-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Music" title="Yahoo! Music">Yahoo! Music</a> also panned the lyrics for "almost totally lacking in shock value, humour or insight", but praised the production, writing that "musically, this is probably the greatest major label hip-hop album of recent years – a near faultless succession of hi-tech beats and ominously catchy hooks".<sup id="cite_ref-yahoo_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Documentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album appeared on numerous music critics' and publications' end-of-year albums lists. <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> placed the album at number 35 on their list of Top 50 Albums of 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-pitchfork.com_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitchfork.com-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/48th_Grammy_Awards" title="48th Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">48th Annual Grammy Awards</a>, The Game was nominated with a total of two nominations, including <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Song" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Song">Grammy Award for Best Rap Song</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Performance_by_a_Duo_or_Group" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group">Grammy Award for Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group</a> for the smash single "<a href="/wiki/Hate_It_or_Love_It" title="Hate It or Love It">Hate It or Love It</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Grammy_2006_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grammy_2006-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012 <a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a> named the album one of the classic albums of the last decade.<sup id="cite_ref-www.complex.com_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.complex.com-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
