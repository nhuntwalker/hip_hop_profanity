<p><i><b>Hello Nasty</b></i> is the fifth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">Beastie Boys</a>. It was released on July 14, 1998 via <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a> and sold 681,000 copies in its first week, debuting at #1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a> album sales chart. The album took home two awards at the 1999 Grammys, in the categories of Best Alternative Music Album and Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group for "Intergalactic".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hello_Nasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Hello Nasty</i> was released on July 14, 1998—four years after the band's previous album, <i><a href="/wiki/Ill_Communication" title="Ill Communication">Ill Communication</a></i>—and marked the addition of DMC champion <a href="/wiki/Mix_Master_Mike" title="Mix Master Mike">Mix Master Mike</a> to the group's line-up.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_review_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_review-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> "Song for Junior" features <a href="/wiki/Miho_Hatori" title="Miho Hatori">Miho Hatori</a> on vocals, and "Dr. Lee, PhD" guest stars <a href="/wiki/Dub_music" title="Dub music" class="mw-redirect">dub</a> musician <a href="/wiki/Lee_Scratch_Perry" title="Lee Scratch Perry" class="mw-redirect">Lee Scratch Perry</a> on both vocals and percussion.<sup id="cite_ref-Rolling_Stone_Review_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolling_Stone_Review-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Hello Nasty</i> also marked <a href="/wiki/Eric_Bobo" title="Eric Bobo" class="mw-redirect">Eric Bobo</a>'s last appearance as percussionist in the band, as well as the last time the Beastie Boys worked with a co-producer.<sup id="cite_ref-Rolling_Stone_Review_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolling_Stone_Review-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The title for the album was allegedly inspired by the receptionist of their NY based publicity firm Nasty Little Man who would answer the phone with the greeting "Hello Nasty".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Hello Nasty</i> received mostly positive reviews upon release. Caroline Sullivan writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> awarded the album "Pop CD Of The Week" claiming that it "fills a gap created by the current profusion of serious rock bands like Radiohead; elbowing its way up front, [and letting] rip with adolescent vigour."<sup id="cite_ref-The_Guardian_Review_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Guardian_Review-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> She went on to summarize the record as "the perfect party soundtrack by the perfect party band."<sup id="cite_ref-The_Guardian_Review_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Guardian_Review-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Although <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> felt that the album's ending was "a little anticlimactic", he also saw <i>Hello Nasty</i> as a progressive step forward from the group's 1992 LP <i><a href="/wiki/Check_Your_Head" title="Check Your Head">Check Your Head</a></i>, and praised the input of the group's new recruit, <a href="/wiki/Mixmaster_Mike" title="Mixmaster Mike" class="mw-redirect">Mixmaster Mike</a>; "Hiring DJ Mixmaster Mike turned out to be a masterstroke; he and the Beasties created a sound that strongly recalls the spare electronic funk of the early '80s, but spiked with the samples and post-modern absurdist wit that have become their trademarks."<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_review_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_review-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In his B+ rated review for <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/David_Browne" title="David Browne">David Browne</a> highlighted the album's multi-genre sound, along with the group's use of a wide range of musical styles, as its most engaging aspect;<sup id="cite_ref-EW_review_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EW_review-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Hello Nasty</i> is a sonic smorgasbord in which the Beasties gorge themselves with reckless abandon. They dabble in lounge-pop kitsch (the loser put-down <i>Song for the Man</i>), make like a summit of Santana and Traffic (the Latin-flavored "Song for Junior"), and subtly incorporate a drum-and-bass shuffle into the mix ("Flowin' Prose"). The melange makes for a looser, more free-spirited record than their earlier albums; the music invites you in, rather than threatening to shut you out.<sup id="cite_ref-EW_review_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EW_review-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
