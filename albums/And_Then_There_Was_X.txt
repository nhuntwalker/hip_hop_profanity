<p><i><b>...And Then There Was X</b></i> is the third album by American <a href="/wiki/Rapper" title="Rapper" class="mw-redirect">rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/DMX_(rapper)" title="DMX (rapper)">DMX</a>, released December 21, 1999 on <a href="/wiki/Ruff_Ryders_Entertainment" title="Ruff Ryders Entertainment">Ruff Ryders Entertainment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Recordings" title="Def Jam Recordings">Def Jam Recordings</a>. In 2000, it was certified 4x Platinum and then later 5x Platinum. The album was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Album" title="Best Rap Album" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Album</a> at the 2001 Grammys.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Songs">Songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=...And_Then_There_Was_X&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first single of the album, the club song '<a href="/wiki/What%27s_My_Name%3F_(DMX_song)" title="What's My Name? (DMX song)">What's My Name</a>' was released, which received much airplay on urban radio and television. It reached #67 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> chart.</p>
<p>The second single was the party anthem "<a href="/wiki/Party_Up_(Up_in_Here)" title="Party Up (Up in Here)">Party Up (Up in Here)</a>", which helped increased album sales. The album also featured more meaningful, introspective tracks such as "<i>Fame</i>" and "<i>Here We Go Again</i>", a heartfelt account of emotional dispute with his fictional protégé, 'Shorty', who he quotes as 'fucking up big time', forcing X to leave him to fend by himself in the streets.</p>
<p>Typical DMX tracks include the obligatory ladies track, "<a href="/wiki/What_These_Bitches_Want" title="What These Bitches Want">What These Bitches Want</a>", featuring smooth vocals from R&amp;B star <a href="/wiki/Sisq%C3%B3" title="Sisqó">Sisqó</a>. The song was released as a third single in its edited form as "What You Want", to moderate radio airplay and a high-budget video from director <a href="/wiki/Hype_Williams" title="Hype Williams">Hype Williams</a>. Also, the standard X aggressive joints include "Don't You Ever", "Coming For Ya" and "The Professional", wherein DMX documents a criminal's activities throughout the city.</p>

<p>Although not credited as his best work, <i>...And Then There Was X</i> was well received by fans and critics alike, however, to some criticisms, the production style geared towards a more commercial and radio-friendly sound; a stark contrast to his previous work, which involved strong gothic and religious undertones (mainly connections between hell and his bad-deeds), boasting bloody and gruesome images over his album covers e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Flesh_of_My_Flesh,_Blood_of_My_Blood" title="Flesh of My Flesh, Blood of My Blood">Flesh of My Flesh, Blood of My Blood</a></i> cover.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=...And_Then_There_Was_X&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album sold very well selling roughly 698,000 units in its first week and went on to be certified 5x <a href="/wiki/Platinum_record" title="Platinum record" class="mw-redirect">Platinum</a> making it DMX's best-selling album to date.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The album debuted at #1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_chart" title="Billboard chart" class="mw-redirect">Billboard chart</a>, firmly ranking DMX within hip hop's only artist to have their first three albums enter the Billboard Top 200 Chart at the #1 spot.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Versions">Versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=...And_Then_There_Was_X&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>...And Then There Was X</i> was also released as a "clean" version that replaces explicit drug content and profanity with sound effects. Strangely, no violence was edited out except for gunshot sound effects in the background of some tracks and some content on the track "The Professional". Skits were also completely removed from the censored version, including the intro track. However, the track "Party Up (Up in Here)" appearing on this album is less censored than the radio/video edit.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=...And_Then_There_Was_X&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The songs "Angel", "D-X-L (Hard White)" and the bonus track "Good Girls, Bad Guys" featured in the <a href="/wiki/2001_in_film" title="2001 in film">2001</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Exit_Wounds" title="Exit Wounds">Exit Wounds</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>"Prayer III" was used in <a href="/wiki/John_Cena" title="John Cena">John Cena</a>'s entrance vignette at <a href="/wiki/WrestleMania_XXVII" title="WrestleMania XXVII">WrestleMania XXVII</a>.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>"Good Girls, Bad Guys" was featured in the 2001 comedy film <i><a href="/wiki/How_High" title="How High">How High</a></i></li>
</ul>
