<p><i><b>The Score</b></i> is the second and final studio album by the <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> trio <a href="/wiki/Fugees" title="Fugees">Fugees</a>, released worldwide February 13, 1996 on <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>. The album features a wide range of samples and instrumentation, with many aspects of <a href="/wiki/Alternative_hip_hop" title="Alternative hip hop">alternative hip hop</a> that would come to dominate the hip hop music scene in the mid-late 1990s. The <i>Score'</i>s production was handled mostly by the Fugees themselves and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Duplessis" title="Jerry Duplessis">Jerry Duplessis</a>, with additional production from <a href="/wiki/Salaam_Remi" title="Salaam Remi">Salaam Remi</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Fort%C3%A9" title="John Forté">John Forté</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diamond_D" title="Diamond D">Diamond D</a>, and Shawn King. The album's guest raps are from <a href="/wiki/Outsidaz" title="Outsidaz">Outsidaz</a>' members <a href="/wiki/Rah_Digga" title="Rah Digga">Rah Digga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Young_Zee" title="Young Zee" class="mw-redirect">Young Zee</a> and Pacewon, as well as Omega, John Forté, and Diamond D. Most versions of the album feature four bonus tracks, including three remixes of "Fu-Gee-La", and a short acoustic Wyclef Jean solo track entitled "Mista Mista."</p>
<p>Upon its release, <i>The Score</i> was a commercial success, peaking at the number one spot on both the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip_Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip Hop Albums" class="mw-redirect">Top R&amp;B/Hip Hop Albums</a> chart (it was a number-one album on the latter in 1996 on the year-end chart). The singles "<a href="/wiki/Killing_Me_Softly_With_His_Song#The_Fugees_version" title="Killing Me Softly With His Song" class="mw-redirect">Killing Me Softly</a>," "<a href="/wiki/Fu-Gee-La" title="Fu-Gee-La">Fu-Gee-La</a>," and "<a href="/wiki/Ready_or_Not_(Fugees_song)" title="Ready or Not (Fugees song)">Ready or Not</a>" also achieved notable chart success, and helped the group achieve worldwide recognition. On October 3, 1997, <i>The Score</i> was certified six times platinum in sales by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA). In addition to receiving mostly favorable reviews upon its release, the album has garnered a considerable amount of acclaim over the years, with many music critics and publications noting it as one of the greatest albums of the 1990s, as well as one of the greatest hip hop albums of all time. In 1998, the album was included in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a>'</i>s 100 best rap albums list, and in 2003, it was ranked number 477 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of <a href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Score_(Fugees_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the Fugees' previous album <i><a href="/wiki/Blunted_on_Reality" title="Blunted on Reality">Blunted on Reality</a></i> proved to be critically and commercially unsuccessful, Christ Schwartz, head of Ruffhouse Records, decided to give the group another chance. In early 1995, he gave them a $135,000 advance and granted them complete artistic control for a follow-up album.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The group used the money for recording equipment and set up a studio in Wyclef's uncle's basement, which they referred to as Booga Basement.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Recording for the album began in June 1995, and extended into November<sup id="cite_ref-Furman86_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Furman86-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> at what Wyclef described as a "relaxed pace" by stating "It was done calmly, almost unconsciously. There wasn't any pressure - it was like "let's make some music," and it just started forming into something amazing. It sounded like a feel-good hip hop record to us, and it was different than what anyone was doing at the time. It was three kids from an urban background expressing themselves."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In regards to <i>The Score'</i>s unified themes and production, Lauryn commented "It's an audio film. It's like how radio was back in the 1940s. It tells a story, and there are cuts and breaks in the music. It's almost like a hip hop version of <i><a href="/wiki/Tommy_(album)" title="Tommy (album)">Tommy</a></i>, like what <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a> did for rock music."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Score_(Fugees_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Score</i> was produced by a variety of producers including each member of the Fugees as well as <a href="/wiki/Diamond_D" title="Diamond D">Diamond D</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salaam_Remi" title="Salaam Remi">Salaam Remi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Duplessis" title="Jerry Duplessis">Jerry Duplessis</a>. Although most tracks are built on <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampled</a> <a href="/wiki/Melodies" title="Melodies" class="mw-redirect">melodies</a>, live instrumentation and <a href="/wiki/DJing" title="DJing" class="mw-redirect">DJing</a> are incorporated into multiple tracks. Wyclef Jean plays the guitar on "Family Business," while DJ Scribble <a href="/wiki/Scratching" title="Scratching">scratches</a> on "Manifest/Outro." Nevertheless, samples are the predominant production tool on <i>The Score</i>. "Fu-Gee-La" incorporates a sample of <a href="/wiki/Teena_Marie" title="Teena Marie">Teena Marie</a>'s "Ooh La La La," and is <a href="/wiki/Interpolation_(music)" title="Interpolation (music)">interpolated</a> in the song's chorus. "<a href="/wiki/Ready_or_Not_(Fugees_song)" title="Ready or Not (Fugees song)">Ready or Not</a>" also contains a sample that is interpolated; "Ready or Not (Here I Come)" by <a href="/wiki/The_Delfonics" title="The Delfonics">The Delfonics</a>. "Manifest/Outro" contains a sample from "Rock Dis Funky Joint" by <a href="/wiki/Poor_Righteous_Teachers" title="Poor Righteous Teachers">Poor Righteous Teachers</a>, while the title track contains vocal samples from every track on the entire album.</p>
<p>Three official singles were released in promotion of <i>The Score</i>. The first of which was "Fu-Gee-La." The single was released January 9, 1996 and peaked at number 29 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, number 13 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a>, and number 2 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Songs" title="Hot Rap Songs">Hot Rap Songs</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-Singles_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Singles-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The song was produced by <a href="/wiki/Salaam_Remi" title="Salaam Remi">Salaam Remi</a> and includes elements of "<a href="/wiki/Ooo_La_La_La" title="Ooo La La La">Ooo La La La</a>" as performed by <a href="/wiki/Teena_Marie" title="Teena Marie">Teena Marie</a>.</p>

<p>The second single, "Killing Me Softly" was released May 31, 1996. Proving to be the most successful single from the album, it instantly reached number two on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100, as well as number one on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Singles_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Singles-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Initially, the song was to be titled "Killing Them Softly," though alluding to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Fox_(composer)" title="Charles Fox (composer)">Charles Fox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norman_Gimbel" title="Norman Gimbel">Norman Gimbel</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Killing_Me_Softly_with_His_Song" title="Killing Me Softly with His Song">Killing Me Softly with His Song</a>," it was originally not intended to be a cover, however, the original writers refused permission for the rewrite of their song, but did allow the Fugees to do a cover version.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1997, "Killing Me Softly" won the Fugees a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> for Best R&amp;B Performance by Duo or group.<sup id="cite_ref-Awards_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Awards-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The third and final single released for the album was "Ready or Not," released September 2, 1996. The song was the least successful of all the singles from the album, only peaking at number 34 on the Rhythmic Top 40 chart.<sup id="cite_ref-Singles_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Singles-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The track interpolates "Ready or Not, Here I Come (Can't Hide from Love)" as performed by <a href="/wiki/The_Delfonics" title="The Delfonics">The Delfonics</a>, and also samples <a href="/wiki/Enya" title="Enya">Enya</a>'s "Boadicea". Initially this sample was uncredited, and Enya was prepared to sue for copyright infringement, however decided not to when she discovered that the Fugees were not <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rappers" title="Gangsta rappers" class="mw-redirect">gangsta rappers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> In a later interview, Fugee member Pras described the recording of "Ready or Not" stating "The three of us was each going through some pain. Lauryn was crying when she did her vocals. It was unbelievable. To see her singing with tears coming out of her eyes, it made me want to cry too."<sup id="cite_ref-Furman86_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Furman86-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Upon its release, <i>The Score</i> received mostly positive reviews from most music critics. <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> writer James Bernard commented "What a shock: a smooth, well-produced rap album that doesn't have <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>'s fingerprints on it [...] <i>The Score</i> showcases their acrobatic lyrical techniques and restless intelligence, and unlike much East Coast rap, <i>The Score</i> feels warm and intimate — partly because the instruments are live but also because the Fugees sound so relaxed and casual."<sup id="cite_ref-EW_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EW-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i> stated "Forget <a href="/wiki/Blunted_on_Reality" title="Blunted on Reality">their debut</a> [...] this isn't another terrible thing to waste. It's so beautiful and funny its courage could make you weep."<sup id="cite_ref-RC_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RC-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Steve Huey from Allmusic wrote that "Even when they're not relying on easily recognizable tunes, their original material is powered by a raft of indelible hooks [...] <i>The Score</i> balances intelligence and accessibility with an easy assurance, and ranks as one of the most distinctive hip-hop albums of its era."<sup id="cite_ref-AM_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AM-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>He and others also touted it for its eclecticism, group rapping with good chemistry and social consciousness. Cheo Hodari Coker from <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> wrote that "<i>The Score</i> succeeds on all counts. As a cohesive unit, Hill, Wyclef Jean and Prakazrel Michel complement one another like no rap trio since <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="A Tribe Called Quest">A Tribe Called Quest</a>. Their specialty is matching a gymnastic rhyme flow and rock-solid beats with expert crooning."<sup id="cite_ref-LAT_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LAT-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> commented "A sense of organic interaction is the hallmark of this album [....] the album's most important factor is its beats; chest-shaking, obscure-texture-having, freestyle-friendly beats."<sup id="cite_ref-Spin_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spin-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)" class="mw-redirect">Q</a></i> described the album as "An impressively panoramic soundscape."<sup id="cite_ref-Q_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Q-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In a mixed review, <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> writer <a href="/wiki/Ann_Powers" title="Ann Powers">Ann Powers</a> commented "The Fugees' roots in reggae gives them a solid base in song and a basic philosophy that's richer than the money-or-nothing ethic that dulls much of rap these days. Without being sanctimonious, <i>The Score</i> paints the ghetto as a mythical landscape, one that can inspire pride as well as sorrow. Like <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, the Fugees view the world as their movie, complete with stunts and special effects."<sup id="cite_ref-RS1_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RS1-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
