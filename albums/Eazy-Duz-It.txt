<p><i><b>Eazy-Duz-It</b></i> is the debut album of <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>, released on September 13, 1988, through both <a href="/wiki/Ruthless_Records" title="Ruthless Records">Ruthless</a> and <a href="/wiki/Priority_Records" title="Priority Records">Priority Records</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Record_producer" title="Record producer">production</a> by <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Yella" title="DJ Yella">DJ Yella</a> was deemed dense and funky by critic Jason Birchmeier.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The pieces were written primarily by <a href="/wiki/MC_Ren" title="MC Ren">MC Ren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_D.O.C." title="The D.O.C.">The D.O.C.</a>. The album's title track features Eazy rapping about himself and things that he does. "Boyz n the Hood" and "No More&#160;?'s" are about life in <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton, California</a> and the gangster lifestyle.</p>
<p>The album charted on two different charts and went 2x Platinum in the United States despite minimal promotion by radio and television. Three <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">singles</a> were released from the album, each charting in the US. The Remastered version contains the out of print 1992 EP 5150.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording_and_production">Recording and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eazy-Duz-It&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recording and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Eazy-Duz-It</i> was recorded at Audio Achievements in <a href="/wiki/Torrance,_California" title="Torrance, California">Torrance, California</a> from 1987 to 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's writing was a four-pronged effort involving <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>, <a href="/wiki/MC_Ren" title="MC Ren">MC Ren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_D.O.C." title="The D.O.C.">The D.O.C.</a>. MC Ren's writing style was described by Marcus Reeves, author of <i><a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9780865479975" title="Special:BookSources/9780865479975">Somebody Scream!: Rap Music's Rise to Prominence in the Aftershock of Black Power</a></i> (2009), as "elaborate storytelling and acrobatic verbiage", while the D.O.C.'s included "syllabically punchy boasts" and Ice Cube wrote, "masterfully insightful first-person narratives." Ice Cube's writing was often inspired by <a href="/wiki/Comedian" title="Comedian">comedians</a> like <a href="/wiki/Richard_Pryor" title="Richard Pryor">Richard Pryor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudy_Ray_Moore" title="Rudy Ray Moore">Rudy Ray Moore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album's <a href="/wiki/Record_producer" title="Record producer">production</a>, almost solely done by <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Yella" title="DJ Yella">DJ Yella</a>, was praised by several critics. Jason Birchmeier from <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> gave a considerable amount of attention to the album's production, saying that "Dr. Dre and Yella meld together <a href="/wiki/P-Funk" title="P-Funk">P-Funk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam" title="Def Jam" class="mw-redirect">Def Jam</a>-style <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a>, and the leftover electro sounds of mid-[19]80s Los Angeles, creating a dense, funky, and thoroughly unique style of their own."<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Birchmeier would also write that some songs—"Eazy Duz It", "We Want Eazy", "Eazy-er Said Than Dunn", and "Radio"—are all heavily produced and have "layers upon layers of <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">samples</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beat_(music)" title="Beat (music)">beats</a> competing with Eazy-E's rhymes for attention."<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rapper" title="Rapper" class="mw-redirect">Rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> also touted Dr. Dre's production on the album.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eazy-Duz-It&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Glen Boyd of <a href="/wiki/Blogcritics" title="Blogcritics">Blogcritics</a> said that the album has "Deep-ass bass lines, old-school funk samples, and plenty of street smart ghetto attitude are what powers this record."<sup id="cite_ref-Seattle_Post-Intelligencer_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seattle_Post-Intelligencer-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Jerry Heller wrote that Eazy raps more up front on the album then he does on <i>Straight Outta Compton</i>, and insists that the album's lyrics contain more sexual humor than gangsta vibe.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album's title track, "Eazy-Duz-It", opens with a woman acclaiming Eazy-E's style. Eazy then interrupts saying "Bitch shut the fuck up, get the fuck outta here." This is followed by a bass line provided by <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>. Soon, Eazy begins to rap about himself and things that he does. The song declares that Eazy is a "hardcore villain" who collects money from his <a href="/wiki/Prostitution" title="Prostitution">prostitutes</a>, and feels great when his "pockets are fat."<sup id="cite_ref-Words_of_Ice_Cube_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Words_of_Ice_Cube-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The chorus, repeated three times, states that he "is a gangsta having fun". The piece is laden with the aural mainstays of gangsta rap, including gunshots, and references to several <a href="/wiki/Drugs" title="Drugs" class="mw-redirect">drugs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Words_of_Ice_Cube_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Words_of_Ice_Cube-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>"Boyz n the Hood" was written by Ice Cube, with some contribution by Eazy-E. The song is about growing up in Compton, California, and describes the gangster lifestyle. It conceives the "ghetto landscape as a generalized abstract construct… [and] also introduces a localized nuance that conveys a certain proximity, effectively capturing a narrowed sence of place through which young thugs and their potential crime victims move in tandem," as put by cultural historian Murray Forman.<sup id="cite_ref-Words_of_Ice_Cube_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Words_of_Ice_Cube-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"No More&#160;?'s" is similar to "Boyz n the Hood" in its theme. The piece begins with an interview between Eazy and a female journalist, who asks about his childhood. Eazy explains (in verse) that he was ruthless, in a gang, "specialized in gankin," (loosely, to steal from) and had no respect for rules. He is then asked if he has ever been in an <a href="/wiki/Armed_robbery" title="Armed robbery" class="mw-redirect">armed robbery</a>. He responds "you mean a 211?". The following verses tell of Eazy's exploits as a thief and thug.<sup id="cite_ref-Words_of_Ice_Cube_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Words_of_Ice_Cube-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eazy-Duz-It&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eazy-Duz-It&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album received little attention from radio and television stations, but got support from <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>'s hip-hop underground.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> On May 20, 1989, it peaked at number 41 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, and since 1989, was in various places on the chart for 90 weeks. It peaked at number 12 on the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart on March 11, 1989. Since the album's release, it has charted on the chart on 51 different weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> On February 15, 1989, the album was certified Gold (500,000 sales) by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>, and on June 1, 1989, it was certified Platinum (1,000,000 sales). It received its peak certification of Double Platinum (2,000,000 sales) on September 1, 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1989, it had sold over 650,000 copies,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> and by early 1995, <i>Eazy-Duz-It</i> had sold between 2 million and 2.5 million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-la_times_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-la_times-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> On February 11, 1989, "We Want Eazy" charted on the R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs at number 43. It stayed on the chart for 15 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The song also charted at number seven on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Songs" title="Hot Rap Songs">Hot Rap Songs</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_Billboard_singles_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_Billboard_singles-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> "Eazy-er Said Than Dunn", the album's third single, peaked at number 84 on the R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs charts on May 6, 1989, where it would maintain some lower position on the chart for six weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> "Eazy-Duz-It" charted on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Dance_Singles_Sales" title="Hot Dance Singles Sales" class="mw-redirect">Hot Dance Singles Sales</a> chart at number 39.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_Billboard_singles_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_Billboard_singles-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a>'s Jason Birchmeier praised the album, awarding the album four out of five stars. Birchmeier noted that "the album plays like a humorous, self-centered twist on Straight Outta Compton with Eazy-E, the most charismatic member of N.W.A, front and center while his associates are busy behind the scenes, producing the beats and writing the songs."<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> He compared it to <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton" title="Straight Outta Compton">Straight Outta Compton</a></i>, which Eazy also performs on, saying that <i>Straight Outta Compton</i> is "more revolutionary," but claimed <i>Eazy-Duz-It</i> to be <i>Straight Outta Compton</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s "great companion" and to have showcased N.W.A's style.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Music_journalist" title="Music journalist" class="mw-redirect">Music journalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave the album a C+, saying that Eazy "hides" behind his record label with such quotes as "I might be a woman beater but I'm not a pussy eater."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Soren Baker from the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> called it a "landmark albums brimming with violence, profanity, sexually explicit content and antigovernment themes," and said that it established Eazy as a "major player in the rap industry"<sup id="cite_ref-la_times_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-la_times-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Daniel Kreps of the <i>Los Angeles Times</i> called it a "solo masterpiece," and said that it was evidence that Eazy was one of the best rappers ever.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Dan Snierson of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> described the album as "an obscenity-littered depiction of violent, hollowed-out life in Compton."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Shan Fowler from <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a> said that it received "underground success."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Glen Boyd reviewed the album on the <a href="/wiki/Online_newspaper" title="Online newspaper">online newspaper</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Seattle_Post-Intelligencer" title="Seattle Post-Intelligencer">Seattle Post-Intelligencer</a></i>, noting that it "paved the way for all of the groundbreaking music which came later." Boyd also said that songs like "Boyz In The Hood" and "Radio" would establish "the street buzz that N.W.A would later ride to platinum selling success as the first true West Coast rap superstars."<sup id="cite_ref-Seattle_Post-Intelligencer_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seattle_Post-Intelligencer-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Jon Wiederhorn from <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> wrote that it "demonstrated Eazy's knack for provocative lyrics," and also said that it paved the way to <i>Straight Outta Compton</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
