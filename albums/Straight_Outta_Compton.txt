<p><i><b>Straight Outta Compton</b></i> is the debut <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>, released August 8, 1988 on group member <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>'s record label <a href="/wiki/Ruthless_Records" title="Ruthless Records">Ruthless Records</a>. Its title refers to the group's native town, <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton, California</a>. Production for the album was handled by <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Yella" title="DJ Yella">DJ Yella</a> giving co-production. The album has been viewed as the pioneering record of <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a>; with its ever-present profanity and violent lyrics, it helped to give birth to this then-new sub-genre of hip hop. It has been considered groundbreaking by music writers and has had an enormous impact on the evolution of <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast hip hop</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Straight Outta Compton</i> redefined the direction of hip hop, which resulted in lyricism concerning the <a href="/wiki/Gangster" title="Gangster">gangster</a> lifestyle becoming the driving force in sales figures<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. It was later <a href="/wiki/Re-release" title="Re-release" class="mw-redirect">re-released</a> on September 24, 2002, remastered and containing four bonus tracks. An extended version of the album was released on December 4, 2007, the 20th anniversary of the original album.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Straight_Outta_Compton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album reached double platinum sales status, becoming the first album to reach platinum status with no airplay support and without any major tours.<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As the hip hop community worldwide received the album with a high note, the members of <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a> became the top stars for the emerging new era of <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a> while popularizing the lyrics of <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a> and <a href="/wiki/MC_Ren" title="MC Ren">MC Ren</a>. The album also helped to spawn many young <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">MCs</a> and gangsta hip hop groups from areas such as <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton, California</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Central_Los_Angeles" title="South Central Los Angeles" class="mw-redirect">South Central Los Angeles</a>, as many thought they had the same story to tell and the ability to pursue the career track that N.W.A had taken,<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> hence groups such as <a href="/wiki/Compton%27s_Most_Wanted" title="Compton's Most Wanted">Compton's Most Wanted</a> coming into being.<sup id="cite_ref-mp3.com_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mp3.com-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Because of the recurring violent and sexual lyrics and profanity, often specifically directed at governmental organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Police_Department" title="Los Angeles Police Department">LAPD</a>, N.W.A always enjoyed a particular reputation with <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senators" title="U.S. Senators" class="mw-redirect">U.S. Senators</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a> (FBI), as noted in the LP's published notes. This situation persisted over the years with the group's visible head, <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>. One of the reasons for this was "<a href="/wiki/Fuck_tha_Police" title="Fuck tha Police">Fuck tha Police</a>", the highly controversial track from the album that resulted in the FBI and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Secret_Service" title="U.S. Secret Service" class="mw-redirect">U.S. Secret Service</a> sending a letter to Ruthless Records informing the label of their displeasure with the song's message, and N.W.A was banned from performing at several venues.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Bio_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Bio-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Eazy-e_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eazy-e-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The FBI letter only helped further popularize the album and N.W.A, and in the group's 1990 song "<a href="/wiki/100_Miles_and_Runnin%27_(song)" title="100 Miles and Runnin' (song)">100 Miles and Runnin'</a>, the follow-up to <i>Straight Outta Compton</i>, while the music video shows the crew running from the police, Dr. Dre raps "and now the FBI is all over my dick!" as a response to the FBI's warnings. Also, in his 1990 song "Amerikkka's Most Wanted", Ice Cube mocks the FBI with the line "With a pay-off, cop gotta lay off, FBI on my dick, stay off".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The lyrics on the album were mainly written by <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a> and <a href="/wiki/MC_Ren" title="MC Ren">MC Ren</a>. Some critics of the album expressed their view that the record glamorized Black-on-Black crime, but others stated that the group was simply showing the reality of living in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton, California</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Central_Los_Angeles" title="South Central Los Angeles" class="mw-redirect">South Central Los Angeles</a>. Steve Huey in a retrospective review for <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> feels that the lyrics are more about "raising hell" than social criticism, but also feels the album is "refreshingly uncalculated" due to its humor; something he feels is rare in hardcore rap.<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Many critics feel that the album's lyrics glamorize gang violence. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> writer <a href="/wiki/David_Mills_(writer)" title="David Mills (writer)" class="mw-redirect">David Mills</a> wrote: "The hard-core street rappers defend their violent lyrics as a reflection of 'reality.' But for all the gunshots they mix into their music, rappers rarely try to dramatize that reality — a young man flat on the ground, a knot of lead in his chest, pleading as death slowly takes him in. It's easier for them to imagine themselves pulling the trigger". However, <i>Wichita Eagle-Beacon</i> editor Bud Norman noted that "They [N.W.A] don't make it sound like much fun... They describe it with the same nonjudgmental resignation that a Kansan might use about a tornado."<sup id="cite_ref-eNotes_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eNotes-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Straight_Outta_Compton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The production on the album was generally seen as top-quality for the time,<sup id="cite_ref-Butler_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Butler-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>'s production performing well with his <a href="/wiki/Instrumental" title="Instrumental">instrumentals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drum_machine" title="Drum machine">drum machine</a> beats, and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Yella" title="DJ Yella">DJ Yella</a>'s turntable scratches and overall co-production seen as proficient by hip hop critics. Some critics find it somewhat sparse and low-budget given the significance of the album and compared with other producers of the time such as <a href="/wiki/Marley_Marl" title="Marley Marl">Marley Marl</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Huey_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huey-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Straight_Outta_Compton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album's most controversial track, "<a href="/wiki/Fuck_tha_Police" title="Fuck tha Police">Fuck tha Police</a>", was partly responsible for the fame of <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a> as the "World's Most Dangerous Group",<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and it did not appear on the censored version of the album.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The song "<a href="/wiki/Gangsta_Gangsta_(N.W.A._song)" title="Gangsta Gangsta (N.W.A. song)" class="mw-redirect">Gangsta Gangsta</a>" talks about the danger and violence in <a href="/wiki/South_Los_Angeles" title="South Los Angeles">South Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton</a>. "<a href="/wiki/Express_Yourself_(N.W.A._song)" title="Express Yourself (N.W.A. song)" class="mw-redirect">Express Yourself</a>" speaks of the ideas of free expression and the constraints placed on performers by radio censorship. Every N.W.A member except <a href="/wiki/DJ_Yella" title="DJ Yella">DJ Yella</a> recorded a solo song. <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, who mostly produced rather than performed, did a solo effort in the single "Express Yourself." <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a> performed on "I Ain't tha 1" and "A Bitch Iz a Bitch". <a href="/wiki/MC_Ren" title="MC Ren">MC Ren</a> made his solo performance in the songs "If It Ain't Ruff" and "Quiet on tha Set". <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>'s only solo recording was a remix of the song "8 Ball," which appeared on N.W.A's previous album <i><a href="/wiki/N.W.A_and_the_Posse" title="N.W.A and the Posse" class="mw-redirect">N.W.A and the Posse</a></i>. The only guests on the album were Ruthless Records ghostwriter <a href="/wiki/The_D.O.C." title="The D.O.C.">the D.O.C.</a>, who appeared on "Parental Discretion Iz Advised," <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rhyming</a> the intro, and founding N.W.A member <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Prince" title="Arabian Prince">Arabian Prince</a>, who contributed minor vocals on "Something 2 Dance 2."</p>
<p>Seven tracks from the album were released on <i><a href="/wiki/Greatest_Hits_(N.W.A)" title="Greatest Hits (N.W.A)" class="mw-redirect">N.W.A's Greatest Hits</a></i>: "<a href="/wiki/Gangsta_Gangsta_(N.W.A._song)" title="Gangsta Gangsta (N.W.A. song)" class="mw-redirect">Gangsta Gangsta</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Fuck_tha_Police" title="Fuck tha Police">Fuck tha Police</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton_(song)" title="Straight Outta Compton (song)">Straight Outta Compton</a> (extended mix)," "If It Ain't Ruff," "I Ain't tha 1," "Express Yourself," and a <a href="/wiki/Bonus_track" title="Bonus track">bonus track</a> from the remastered version, "A Bitch Iz a Bitch".</p>

<p>The album first appeared on music charts in 1989, peaking on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> Top LPs</a> chart at number 37, and peaking on <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_charts" title="Billboard charts">Billboard</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top Soul LPs</a> at number nine.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG-Sales_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Sales-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> It re-entered the charts in 2003, peaking on the UK Albums Top 75 at number thirty-five, and on the Ireland Albums Top 75 at number twenty.<sup id="cite_ref-acharts_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acharts-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album has sold over three million copies<sup id="cite_ref-Eazy-e_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eazy-e-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LA-Times_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LA-Times-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and was certified double <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">Platinum</a> on March 27, 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> It was <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>'s best selling album, as their debut, <i><a href="/wiki/N.W.A_and_the_Posse" title="N.W.A and the Posse" class="mw-redirect">N.W.A and the Posse</a></i>, was certified Gold.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Their final album, <i><a href="/wiki/Niggaz4Life" title="Niggaz4Life">Niggaz4Life</a></i>, was certified platinum.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Priority_Records" title="Priority Records">Priority Records</a>' calculations, 80% of sales were in the suburbs, beyond the boundaries of black neighborhoods.<sup id="cite_ref-LA-Times_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LA-Times-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Straight_Outta_Compton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon its release, the album was generally well received by most music critics. Greg Kot of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> gave <i>Straight Outta Compton</i> three and a half out of four stars and praised its production.<sup id="cite_ref-Kot_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kot-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Richmond_Times-Dispatch" title="Richmond Times-Dispatch">Richmond Times-Dispatch</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Mark Holmberg described the album as "a preacher-provoking, mother-maddening, reality-stinks diatribe that wallows in gangs, doping, drive-by shootings, brutal sexism, cop slamming and racism".<sup id="cite_ref-eNotes_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eNotes-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> noted that <i>Straight Outta Compton</i> "introduced some of the most grotesquely exciting music ever made", and added that "Hinting at gang roots, and selling themselves on those hints, they project a gangster mystique that pays no attention where criminality begins and marketing lets off".<sup id="cite_ref-eNotes_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eNotes-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Following its 2002 re-release, Jon Caramanica of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine cited <i>Straight Outta Compton</i> as one of hip-hop's most crucial albums, calling it a "bombastic, cacophonous car ride through Los Angeles' burnt-out and ignored hoods."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"The lyrics on this record are unrelenting in their unpleasantness," complained Peter Clarke in <i><a href="/wiki/Hi-Fi_News_%26_Record_Review" title="Hi-Fi News &amp; Record Review">Hi-Fi News &amp; Record Review</a></i>, awarding the album a rock-bottom "D:4" rating. "The cumulative effect is like listening to an endless fight next door. The music on this record is without a hint of dynamics or melody."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Straight_Outta_Compton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"It's definitely the best rap record I've ever heard," remarked <a href="/wiki/Sin%C3%A9ad_O%27Connor" title="Sinéad O'Connor">Sinéad O'Connor</a>. "Of course, I can see why people might be offended by the lyrics. But as a human being and not as a public figure, I'm not offended at all. I realise from reading interviews with people like Ice Cube, when they explain that they're not talking about women in general but about particular women they know, it makes a lot of sense. I think the sound of the record is brilliant. I really like hardcore hip-hop and reggae stuff, so it's right up my flight of stairs."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"Rappers haven't always referred to themselves as 'niggers' on record," remarked <i><a href="/wiki/Hip_Hop_Connection" title="Hip Hop Connection">Hip Hop Connection</a></i>, placing it at No.3 on their countdown of rap's best albums. "It came as something of a shock then that here were five politically astute black men calling themselves niggers and their women bitches at a time when Afrocentric rap was the current vogue… <i>Straight Outta Compton</i> sounded so exciting, insignificant details such as realism and integrity could be overlooked."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, the TV network <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">VH1</a>, named <i>Straight Outta Compton</i> the 62nd greatest album of all time.</p>
<p>It was ranked ten in <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> magazine's "100 Greatest Albums, 1985–2005".</p>
<p>In 1998, the album was selected as one of The Source's 100 Best Rap Albums.</p>
<p>It is the group's only album on <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a>'s list of the <a href="/wiki/500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">500 Greatest Albums of All Time</a> (ranked #144), and the first hip-hop album ever to get a 5-star rating from them in their initial review, and when comedian <a href="/wiki/Chris_Rock" title="Chris Rock">Chris Rock</a> wrote an article for the magazine about the 25 Greatest Hip-Hop Albums of all time in 2005, <i>Straight Outta Compton</i> was number one on his list.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album is ranked the 109th best of all time by Acclaimedmusic.net.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, the album was listed in the book <i><a href="/wiki/1001_Albums_You_Must_Hear_Before_You_Die" title="1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die">1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1001Albums_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1001Albums-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The same year, <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine ranked it as one of the 100 greatest albums of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-TIME_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIME-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Q_magazine" title="Q magazine">Q</a></i> magazine voted it one of the 'Top 50 Titles Of 1989. <i><a href="/wiki/Alternative_Press" title="Alternative Press">Alternative Press</a></i> (7/95, p.&#160;88) ranked it #45 in AP's list of the 'Top 99 Of '85–'95'. <a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)"><i>Vibe</i></a> (12/99, p.&#160;164) included it in Vibe's 100 Essential Albums of the 20th century. In 2004, <i>DigitaArts</i> included the album's cover in its list of the 25 Best Albums Covers.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012, <i><a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant</a></i> listed the album at #18 on its list of "Best Albums of the 1980s" saying "The juxtaposition of midtempo, Cali-languid grooves and violent wordplay positioned Straight Outta Compton as the sound of the West Coast firing on New York's Fort Sumpter in what would become '90s culture's biggest Uncivil War."<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Straight_Outta_Compton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album cover and title has parodied by American cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Bill_Holbrook" title="Bill Holbrook">Bill Holbrook</a> for his <i><a href="/wiki/Kevin_and_Kell" title="Kevin and Kell">Kevin and Kell</a></i> 2004 collection as "Straight Outta Computers";<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Welsh</a> novelty hip-hop group Goldie Lookin' Chain for their 2005 album, "Straight Outta Newport" <sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> and on <a href="/wiki/%22Weird_Al%22_Yankovic" title="&quot;Weird Al&quot; Yankovic">"Weird Al" Yankovic</a>'s 2006 album, <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Lynwood" title="Straight Outta Lynwood">Straight Outta Lynwood</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">Punk rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/NOFX" title="NOFX">NOFX</a> released a song titled Straight Outta Massachusetts on their <a href="/wiki/Cokie_the_Clown" title="Cokie the Clown">Cokie the Clown</a> <a href="/wiki/Extended_Play" title="Extended Play" class="mw-redirect">EP</a>.</p>
