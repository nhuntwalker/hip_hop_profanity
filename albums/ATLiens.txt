<p><i><b>ATLiens</b></i> is the second <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> duo <a href="/wiki/OutKast" title="OutKast" class="mw-redirect">OutKast</a>, released on August 27, 1996, by <a href="/wiki/LaFace_Records" title="LaFace Records">LaFace Records</a>. The duo wanted to improve on their 1994 debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik" title="Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik">Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik</a></i> and gain respect for their growing <a href="/wiki/Southern_hip_hop" title="Southern hip hop">Southern hip hop</a> scene. OutKast recorded <i>ATLiens</i> in sessions at several Atlanta studios—Bosstown Recording Studios, Doppler Recording Studios, <a href="/wiki/PatchWerk_Recording_Studios" title="PatchWerk Recording Studios">PatchWerk Recording Studio</a>, Purple Dragon Studios, and Studio LaCoCo—as well as <a href="/wiki/Chung_King_Studios" title="Chung King Studios">Chung King Recording Studio</a> and Sound On Sound Recording in New York City.</p>
<p>The record features outer space-inspired production sounds, with OutKast and producers <a href="/wiki/Organized_Noize" title="Organized Noize">Organized Noize</a> incorporating elements of <a href="/wiki/Dub_(music)" title="Dub (music)">dub</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a> into the compositions. Several songs feature the duo's first attempts at producing music by themselves. Lyrically, the group discusses a wide range of topics including urban life as hustlers, existential introspection, and extraterrestrial life. The album's title is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of "ATL" (an <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation" title="Abbreviation">abbreviation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia" class="mw-redirect">Atlanta, Georgia</a>) and "<a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">aliens</a>", which has been interpreted by critics as a commentary about the feeling of being isolated from American culture.</p>
<p><i>ATLiens</i> debuted at number two on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, and it sold nearly 350,000 copies in its first two weeks of release. The album was very well received by music critics upon its release, who praised the record's lyrical content. It has been certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">double platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), for shipments of two million copies in the United States. The album spawned the singles "<a href="/wiki/Elevators_(Me_%26_You)" title="Elevators (Me &amp; You)">Elevators (Me &amp; You)</a>", "<a href="/wiki/ATLiens_(song)" title="ATLiens (song)">ATLiens</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Jazzy_Belle" title="Jazzy Belle">Jazzy Belle</a>". Since its release, <i>ATLiens</i> has been listed by several magazines and critics as one of the greatest hip hop albums of all time.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATLiens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1994, the American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a> duo <a href="/wiki/Outkast" title="Outkast">OutKast</a> released their debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik" title="Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik">Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik</a></i>, which was recorded when members <a href="/wiki/Big_Boi" title="Big Boi">Big Boi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_3000" title="André 3000">André 3000</a> were eighteen years old. Bolstered by the success of the single "<a href="/wiki/Player%27s_Ball" title="Player's Ball">Player's Ball</a>", the record established OutKast as prominent figures in the Southern hip hop scene.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson3233_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson3233-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> After the album was certified platinum, LaFace Records gave OutKast more creative control and advanced money for their 1996 follow-up album <i>ATLiens</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The duo took the opportunity to recreate their image. On a trip to <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> with producer <a href="/wiki/Mr._DJ" title="Mr. DJ">Mr. DJ</a>, the two decided to abandon their <a href="/wiki/Cornrows" title="Cornrows">cornrow</a> hairstyles in favor of a more natural aesthetic, vowing to stop combing their hair.<sup id="cite_ref-Westhoff135_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Westhoff135-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dungeon_Family" title="Dungeon Family">Dungeon Family</a> member <a href="/wiki/Big_Rube" title="Big Rube">Big Rube</a> observed an increase in the duo's confidence after returning from their first tour, remarking, "They started understanding the power they had in their music. They started showing a swagger that certain artists have—the ones that are stars."<sup id="cite_ref-sarig139_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig139-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The members also underwent changes in their personal lives; in 1995, Big Boi's girlfriend gave birth to their first child and André 3000 and <a href="/wiki/Total_(group)" title="Total (group)">Total</a>'s Keisha Spivey ended their two-year relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig140_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig140-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Despite its success, <i>Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik</i> had some detractors, including hip hop tastemakers who were unaccustomed to the album's style.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson35_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson35-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> As the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_hip_hop" title="East Coast hip hop">East Coast</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast</a> hip hop scenes were already well-established at the time, many did not view the South as a legitimate and respectable scene.<sup id="cite_ref-Westhoff133_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Westhoff133-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> At the 1995 Source Awards, an award ceremony held by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i> magazine, OutKast won in the "Best Newcomer" category,<sup id="cite_ref-GRD_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GRD-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> but were booed upon taking the stage and delivering their acceptance speech; Big Boi managed to deliver his shout outs, while André 3000 was nervous and only said, "The South got somethin' to say."<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson46061_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson46061-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The latter recalled how the album was received by some listeners, "People thought that the South basically only had <a href="/wiki/Bass_music" title="Bass music">bass music</a>. At first people were looking at us like 'Um, I don't know.'"<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson35_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson35-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Taken aback by the backlash, André 3000 and Big Boi channelled their frustration in the studio to improve upon their debut.<sup id="cite_ref-Westhoff133_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Westhoff133-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording_and_production">Recording and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATLiens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Patchwerk_995.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Patchwerk_995.gif/270px-Patchwerk_995.gif" width="270" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Patchwerk_995.gif/405px-Patchwerk_995.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/Patchwerk_995.gif 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="333" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Patchwerk_995.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.24wmf6/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/PatchWerk_Recording_Studios" title="PatchWerk Recording Studios">PatchWerk Recording Studio</a> in Atlanta, one of the album's recording locations</div>
</div>

<p>After acquiring their own recording studio, the duo immediately started working on new material and assimiliated themselves with music recording and studio equipment, as they sought to become more ambitious artists and less dependent on other producers.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson42_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson42-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The two also became more accustomed to playing live, particularly Big Boi, and André 3000 significantly changed his lifestyle, as he adopted a more eccentric fashion sense, became a vegetarian, and stopped smoking marijuana.<sup id="cite_ref-Nickson46_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nickson46-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Having dropped out months before graduation, André 3000 also returned to high school to earn his diploma during the recording of <i>ATLiens</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Vibrations_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibrations-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Before beginning work on <i>ATLiens</i>, André 3000 purchased an <a href="/wiki/E-mu_SP-1200" title="E-mu SP-1200">SP1200</a> drum machine, an <a href="/wiki/Music_Production_Center" title="Music Production Center">MPC3000</a> sampler, a <a href="/wiki/TASCAM" title="TASCAM">TASCAM</a> mixing board, and turntables with stacks of classic records.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig140_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig140-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Although he had never produced a song before, he used techniques learned from observing the Dungeon Crew at work. "<a href="/wiki/Elevators_(Me_%26_You)" title="Elevators (Me &amp; You)">Elevators (Me &amp; You)</a>" was the first song the duo created together for the album.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig140_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig140-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The duo refrained from <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampling</a> on the album, with Big Boi explaining "I feel like you cheat the listener when you sample. If it's an old school jam, leave it to the old. We wanna have our own school of music."<sup id="cite_ref-Vibrations_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibrations-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>OutKast recorded the album in sessions at several Atlanta studios—Bosstown Recording Studios, Doppler Recording Studios, <a href="/wiki/PatchWerk_Recording_Studios" title="PatchWerk Recording Studios">PatchWerk Recording Studio</a>, Purple Dragon Studios, and Studio LaCoCo—as well as <a href="/wiki/Chung_King_Studios" title="Chung King Studios">Chung King Recording Studio</a> and Sound On Sound Recording in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> For <i>ATLiens</i>, the band aimed for a consistent set of songs in which a listener would not need to skip tracks; OutKast wrote around 35 songs for the album and reduced them to fourteen.<sup id="cite_ref-Home_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Home-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The duo's songwriting style for the album had no solid structure and was mostly spontaneous; Big Boi noted, "Stuff'll just come to you. I'll be sittin' in the truck, and I'll start rhymin'. People look at me like I'm crazy, but that's how it starts."<sup id="cite_ref-Home_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Home-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Two-thirds of the album is produced by <a href="/wiki/Organized_Noize" title="Organized Noize">Organized Noize</a>, OutKast's primary production team. The rest is produced by Earthtone III, a production team that includes OutKast themselves and <a href="/wiki/Mr._DJ" title="Mr. DJ">Mr. DJ</a>. André 3000 and Big Boi produced the songs "Jazzy Belle" and "Elevators (Me &amp; You)".<sup id="cite_ref-Wang133_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang133-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> It also has a notably more laid-back, spacey production sound, which they would continue to a certain extent on their follow-up album <i><a href="/wiki/Aquemini" title="Aquemini">Aquemini</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-aqueminirev_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aqueminirev-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Although the group drew from <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(musician)" title="George Clinton (musician)">George Clinton</a>'s outer-space inspired compositions, the band utilizes a more laid-back style as opposed to Clinton's hard funk leanings.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusicrev_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicrev-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Many tracks feature strong echo and reverb, taking influence from <a href="/wiki/Dub_(music)" title="Dub (music)">dub</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusicrev_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicrev-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Andrea Comer of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hartford_Courant" title="The Hartford Courant" class="mw-redirect">The Hartford Courant</a></i> perceives an "extraterrestrial feel" in the record's production.<sup id="cite_ref-Comer_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Comer-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>OutKast also incorporated elements of <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a> into the music; being from the South, the group felt obligated to "stay close to [their] slave roots".<sup id="cite_ref-Vibrations_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibrations-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's introduction track "You May Die" has been described as "churchy".<sup id="cite_ref-Harrington_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrington-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> "Elevators (Me &amp; You)" contains atmospheric elements including echoes, dub-influenced bass, organ riffs, and telephone tones.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig14041_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig14041-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> "13th Floor/Growing Old" contains a <a href="/wiki/Spoken_word" title="Spoken word">spoken word</a> introduction from Big Rube, somber soul vocals from <a href="/wiki/Debra_Killings" title="Debra Killings">Debra Killings</a>, and a "<a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>-ish" piano riff,<sup id="cite_ref-Wang133_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang133-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sarig142_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig142-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> while "Wheelz of Steel" features "furious" turntable scratching by Mr. DJ.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig142_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig142-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> "Extraterrestrial" offers a break in the continuity of the record as it features no drum <a href="/wiki/Beat_(music)" title="Beat (music)">beat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hess461462_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess461462-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">Lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATLiens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lyrically, André 3000 and Big Boi abandon the "hard-partying playa characters" of their debut album in favor of more spacey, funky, and futuristic personas on <i>ATLiens</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusicrev_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicrev-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> With their lyrics, the duo hoped to reflect on maturity in the wake of the birth of Big Boi's daughter.<sup id="cite_ref-carries_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carries-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> André 3000 explained, "It's like everybody's talking about sipping champagne and being big time, so we just took it upon ourselves to do something new&#160;... I want my children to say, 'Daddy really said something, he wasn't just trying to brag on himself.'"<sup id="cite_ref-carries_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carries-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Many songs on <i>ATLiens</i> feature more unconventional subject matter for hip hop. The lyrical content ranges from addressing urban life as hustlers and pimps to extraterrestrial life and space travel.<sup id="cite_ref-Hess461462_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess461462-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The title track's chorus expresses Southern pride, while its verses feature André 3000 explaining his newly-adopted drug-free lifestyle.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig14142_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig14142-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"Elevators (Me &amp; You)" illustrates OutKast's rise to fame, and was inspired by a show the band played at <a href="/wiki/Howard_University" title="Howard University">Howard University</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sean_Combs" title="Sean Combs">P. Diddy</a> in the audience.<sup id="cite_ref-Home_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Home-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The song also discusses the unlikely partnership of André 3000 and Big Boi, and uses the metaphor of an elevator for the ups and downs of fame.<sup id="cite_ref-Bradley498_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley498-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The final verse illustrates André 3000 dealing with a fan who pretends to have been childhood friends with him.<sup id="cite_ref-Bradley498_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley498-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> It also references Southern culture, including mentions of <a href="/wiki/Cadillac" title="Cadillac">Cadillacs</a> and extended family gatherings.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig141_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig141-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> "Babylon" reflects on <a href="/wiki/Religion_and_sexuality" title="Religion and sexuality">religious attitudes towards sex</a> and illustrates André 3000's upbringing and his forbidden attractions throughout childhood.<sup id="cite_ref-Hess461462_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess461462-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> "Jazzy Belle" discusses the group's "increasingly enlightened" view regarding women: "Went from yellin'&#160;... bitches and hoes to queen thangs".<sup id="cite_ref-sarig142_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig142-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's closer "13th Floor/Growing Old" is a meditation on aging and emphasizes Southern hip hop's legitimacy.<sup id="cite_ref-sarig142_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sarig142-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_promotion">Release and promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATLiens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Release and promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>ATLiens</i> was released on August 27, 1996, by <a href="/wiki/LaFace_Records" title="LaFace Records">LaFace Records</a>. In September, LaFace created a promotion for the record in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_LLC" title="Blockbuster LLC">Blockbuster</a> in which customers could enter to win a 1970s Cadillac car, emphasizing OutKast and Cadillac's connection with the Southern lifestyle.<sup id="cite_ref-carries_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carries-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The record's inner booklet features a 24-page comic strip foldout starring the members, who must defend "positive music" against the villain Nosamulli.<sup id="cite_ref-carries_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carries-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The strip continues in the artwork for each single released from <i>ATLiens</i> except for "Elevators (Me &amp; You)".<sup id="cite_ref-carries_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carries-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album's title is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of "ATL" (an <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation" title="Abbreviation">abbreviation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia" class="mw-redirect">Atlanta, Georgia</a>) and "<a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">aliens</a>". In his book <i>Icons of Hip Hop: An Encyclopedia of the Movement, Music, and Culture</i>, author Mickey Hess interprets the album's title as "partly a statement about being from Atlanta, while also signifying on the theme of the group's name (by using the term <i>aliens</i>) framing themselves as societal outcasts."<sup id="cite_ref-Hess461_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess461-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Mark Bould, author of <i>The Routledge Companion to Science Fiction</i>, observes that the title symbolizes OutKast's "estrangement from American society", suggesting that "the inner city of their formative years is out of this world and its hostile conditions."<sup id="cite_ref-Bould191_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bould191-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>ATLiens</i> was well received by contemporary music critics. Andrea Comer of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hartford_Courant" title="The Hartford Courant" class="mw-redirect">The Hartford Courant</a></i> felt that OutKast's "lyrical acumen shines through" despite "Heltah-Skeltah mumbling and Southern slang", and stated, "after a few rotations, the alien feeling wears away, and [the album is] just out of this world."<sup id="cite_ref-Comer_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Comer-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Sonia Murray of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlanta_Journal-Constitution" title="The Atlanta Journal-Constitution">The Atlanta Journal-Constitution</a></i> called the album "more thoughtful" than its predecessor, noting, "What the second album lacks in adventurous arrangements it more than makes up for in lyrical dominance."<sup id="cite_ref-Murray_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i> observed "growth" from OutKast and Organized Noize, and stated, "Big Boi and Dre have gone out of this world into a new dimension of sight, sound and mind".<sup id="cite_ref-Source_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Source-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Kevin Powell of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> felt that, like OutKast's debut album, <i>ATLiens</i> is "a gritty document of what's happening here and now, an up-to-the-minute briefing on Southern black ghetto life on which OutKast members Andre and Big Boi cast their feelings of alienation in familiar, realistic characterizations". Powell asserted that unlike East Coast hip hop's "hedonistic materialism" or "the gunplay and pimpism" of West Coast hip hop, "Andre and Big Boi display a unique ability to describe ghetto life while offering up life-affirming possibilities, something all too rare in today's hip-hop nation."<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Richard Harrington of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> enjoyed the record's "more serious and focused lyrical sensibility", explaining, "The raps are generally inventive, clever without being cloying, more proof (if any were needed) that hip-hop innovation isn't just an East-West thang."<sup id="cite_ref-Harrington_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrington-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATLiens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album debuted at number two on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> and it sold nearly 350,000 copies in its first two weeks of release.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> It ultimately spent 33 weeks on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 chart.<sup id="cite_ref-bb200_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb200-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Three singles were released for the album—"<a href="/wiki/Elevators_(Me_%26_You)" title="Elevators (Me &amp; You)">Elevators (Me &amp; You)</a>" on July 5, 1996,<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/ATLiens_(song)" title="ATLiens (song)">ATLiens</a>" in November 1996, and "<a href="/wiki/Jazzy_Belle" title="Jazzy Belle">Jazzy Belle</a>" in April 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-Strong_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Strong-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> "Elevators (Me &amp; You)" reached number 12 and spent 20 weeks on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_100" title="Hot 100" class="mw-redirect"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-hot100_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hot100-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> It was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">gold</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA) on September 13, 1996, for shipments of 500,000 copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> "ATLiens" reached number 35 and spent 17 weeks on the Hot 100, and "Jazzy Belle" spent 14 weeks and peaked at number 52 on the Hot 100.<sup id="cite_ref-hot100_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hot100-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On November 6, the album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">platinum</a> by the RIAA on November 6, 1996, for shipments of one million copies in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> By 1998, it had sold over 1.2 million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> On June 24, 2003, the RIAA certified <i>ATLiens</i> double platinum, having shipped two million copies in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_42-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATLiens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although <i>ATLiens</i> was well received, it would not be until they released <i><a href="/wiki/Stankonia" title="Stankonia">Stankonia</a></i> (2000) that OutKast would receive significant mainstream popularity.<sup id="cite_ref-boiii_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boiii-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> In a retrospective review, <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> editor Steve Huey viewed the album as OutKast's "most focused work" and commented that "In addition to the striking musical leap forward, Dre and Big Boi continue to grow as rappers; their flows are getting more tongue-twistingly complex, and their lyrics more free-associative".<sup id="cite_ref-allmusicrev_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicrev-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> RapReviews critic Steve Juon recommended it to listeners who "want to be challenged by [their] hip-hop" and wrote of the album's aesthetic:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It's deep. So deep that listening to <i>ATLiens</i> you might feel like drowning, but the smooth vo-cals of Big Boi and the earthy flows of Andre always push you back up to the surface. They are players in the truest sense of the word; not just playing for ends but playing to win in the ultimate battle of life over death, good over bad, and righteousness over evil. Yet, it's not that heavy either. This album is nod your head music, shake your ass music. It makes you think and groove at the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-Juon_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Juon-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stone_Album_Guide" title="The Rolling Stone Album Guide">The Rolling Stone Album Guide</a></i> (2004), Roni Sarig felt that, strong rapping notwithstanding, the album's music "suffers as the duo make their first attempt at self-producing" and stated, "Although <i>ATLiens</i> promised expanded vistasa with its interstellar motif, the record delivered something of a sophomore slump&#160;... At best, <i>ATLiens</i> is the sound of an ambitious group searching for its voice."<sup id="cite_ref-RSguide_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSguide-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, the album was selected as one of <i>The Source</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s "100 Best Rap Albums".<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2000, <i><a href="/wiki/Exclaim!" title="Exclaim!">Exclaim!</a></i> listed the album on their "100 Records That Rocked 100 Issues of <i>Exclaim!</i>" list.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Hip_Hop_Connection" title="Hip Hop Connection">Hip Hop Connection</a></i> ranked it number six on their list of "The 100 Greatest Rap Albums 1995–2005".<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a></i> ranked the album fifth on their list of "The 50 Greatest Sophomore Albums in Hip-Hop History",<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> its title 15th on "The 50 Best Rap Album Titles Ever",<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> and the title track's beat 91st on "The 100 Greatest Hip-Hop Beats of All Time".<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> Rappers <a href="/wiki/Wiz_Khalifa" title="Wiz Khalifa">Wiz Khalifa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dom_Kennedy" title="Dom Kennedy">Dom Kennedy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> and DJ Jesse Marco have named <i>ATLiens</i> as one of their favorite albums.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
