<p><i><b>It Was Written</b></i> is the second <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American rapper <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, released July 2, 1996 on <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a> in the United States. Following the moderate sales success of his acclaimed debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Illmatic" title="Illmatic">Illmatic</a></i> (1994), Nas chose to focus his efforts in a more mainstream direction. Primarily produced by <a href="/wiki/Trackmasters" title="Trackmasters">Trackmasters</a>, it is a departure for him from the raw, underground tone of his debut album towards a more polished, mainstream sound. The album features <a href="/wiki/Mafioso_rap" title="Mafioso rap" class="mw-redirect">mafioso</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta</a> themes, and marks the first appearance of Nas's short-lived supergroup <a href="/wiki/The_Firm_(hip_hop_group)" title="The Firm (hip hop group)">The Firm</a>, which included rappers <a href="/wiki/Foxy_Brown_(rapper)" title="Foxy Brown (rapper)">Foxy Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/AZ_(rapper)" title="AZ (rapper)">AZ</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cormega" title="Cormega">Cormega</a>.</p>
<p>The album proved to be Nas’s most commercially successful release, debuting at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart. It also heralded Nas’s mainstream popularity and followed the success of other mafioso rap albums such as <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx%E2%80%A6" title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx…" class="mw-redirect">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx…</a></i> (1995) and <i><a href="/wiki/Reasonable_Doubt_(album)" title="Reasonable Doubt (album)">Reasonable Doubt</a></i> (1996). However, his stylistic changes and increased mainstream success fostered accusations of <a href="/wiki/Selling_out" title="Selling out">selling out</a> within the hip hop community. It has been viewed by music writers as one of Nas's best works and remains Nas' best-selling release, with over 4 million copies in the United States alone.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_recording">Background and recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=It_Was_Written&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the critical acclaim of his debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Illmatic" title="Illmatic">Illmatic</a></i> (1994), Nas chose to concentrate his efforts in a more mainstream direction, in contrast to the raw, unpolished and underground tone of his debut.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite its significant impact on hip hop at the time, <i>Illmatic</i> did not experience the larger sales of most major releases at the time in hip hop, such as Snoop Dogg's <i><a href="/wiki/Doggystyle" title="Doggystyle">Doggystyle</a></i> (1993).<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> This was due in part to Nas's shy personality and uninvolvement in promoting the record.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Nas began to make appearances on other artists' work, including <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a>'s "4,5,6" (1995) and Raekwon's "Verbal Intercourse" on his album <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx%E2%80%A6" title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx…" class="mw-redirect">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx…</a></i> (1995), which made Nas the first non <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a> member to appear on one of its solo recordings.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> He began to dub himself as Nas Escobar on these guest appearances.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, his excessive spending habits left him with little money, and Nas was forced to ask for a loan to purchase clothes to wear to <a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)#The_Source_Awards" title="The Source (magazine)">the Source Awards</a> ceremony in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The success of fellow <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_hip_hop" title="East Coast hip hop">East Coast</a> act <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> and promoter <a href="/wiki/Puff_Daddy" title="Puff Daddy" class="mw-redirect">Puff Daddy</a> at the awards show sent a message to Nas to change his commercial approach, resulting in his hiring of <a href="/wiki/Steve_Stoute" title="Steve Stoute">Steve "The Commissioner" Stoute</a> as manager.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> While <i>Illmatic</i> attained <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">gold status</a> in the United States, Stoute convinced Nas to aim his efforts in a more mainstream, commercial direction for his second album, after which Nas enlisted the production team <a href="/wiki/Trackmasters" title="Trackmasters">Trackmasters</a>, who were known at the time for their mainstream success.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Other producers for the album included <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Havoc_(Mobb_Deep)" title="Havoc (Mobb Deep)" class="mw-redirect">Havoc</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a>, <a href="/wiki/L.E.S._(producer)" title="L.E.S. (producer)" class="mw-redirect">L.E.S.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Live_Squad" title="Live Squad">Live Squad</a>, and <a href="/wiki/MC_Serch" title="MC Serch">MC Serch</a> as <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">executive producer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-credits_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-credits-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Premier and L.E.S. had previously served as producers for Nas's <i>Illmatic</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-credits_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-credits-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was <a href="/wiki/Audio_mastering" title="Audio mastering">mastered</a> by Tom Coyne at Sterling Sound in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-S12_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S12-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The album cover was designed by artist Aimée Macauley, and the photography was taken by <a href="/wiki/Danny_Clinch" title="Danny Clinch">Danny Clinch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-credits_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-credits-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>In contrast to <i>Illmatic</i>, the album contains a more detailed and elaborate production,<sup id="cite_ref-Stanley_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanley-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> while it shares similarity to the <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a> sound, relying heavily on <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampled</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music_loop" title="Music loop" class="mw-redirect">looped</a> <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a> grooves.<sup id="cite_ref-Yahoo_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yahoo-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>It Was Written</i> has Nas experimenting with a theatrical <a href="/wiki/Mafioso_rap" title="Mafioso rap" class="mw-redirect">mafioso</a> concept under the alias of "Nas Escobar" (inspired by the Colombian drug lord <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Escobar" title="Pablo Escobar">Pablo Escobar</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's subject matter has been noted for its focus on materialistic excess and other mafioso lyrical themes.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Jon Pareles of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> wrote of Nas's shift in lyrical themes from <i>Illmatic</i>, stating he "repeatedly cites the Colombian drug kingpin Pablo Escobar and drops brand names of clothes, cars, liquor and guns."<sup id="cite_ref-Pareles_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pareles-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Nas also references lines from his previous material, a common element in his music that has been analyzed by one music writer as "return[ing] to his professional beginnings in those references."<sup id="cite_ref-literary_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-literary-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=It_Was_Written&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album opens with "Album Intro" in which a slave rebellion is heard,<sup id="cite_ref-albumintro_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-albumintro-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and it contains samples of <a href="/wiki/Sam_Cooke" title="Sam Cooke">Sam Cooke</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/A_Change_Is_Gonna_Come_(song)" title="A Change Is Gonna Come (song)" class="mw-redirect">A Change Is Gonna Come</a>" (1964) and <a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Generation_(band)" title="The Lost Generation (band)">The Lost Generation</a>'s "The Sly, the Slick, and the Wicked" (1970).<sup id="cite_ref-credits_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-credits-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> (<span class="unicode haudio" style="white-space:nowrap;"><span class="fn"><a href="/wiki/File:Album_Intro.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;<a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/ec/Album_Intro_%28Nas_song%2C_from_%27It_Was_Written%27_-_sample%29.ogg" class="internal" title="Album Intro (Nas song, from 'It Was Written' - sample).ogg">sample</a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Album_Intro.ogg" title="File:Album Intro.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>) The opening song "<a href="/wiki/The_Message_(Nas_song)" title="The Message (Nas song)">The Message</a>" features production by the Trackmasters, Poke &amp; Tone, and <a href="/wiki/Scratching" title="Scratching">scratching</a> from <a href="/wiki/Kid_Capri" title="Kid Capri">Kid Capri</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-S12_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S12-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> One critic described the song as a "bloody narrative", and cited it as "one of the most visually evocative songs of Nas' career".<sup id="cite_ref-Chennault_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chennault-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The song's title references the classic hip hop single "<a href="/wiki/The_Message_(Nas_song)" title="The Message (Nas song)">The Message</a>" (1982) by <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_Flash_and_the_Furious_Five" title="Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five">Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-literary_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-literary-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Legendary producer <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a> had one production credit on the <a href="/wiki/Jazz_fusion" title="Jazz fusion">jazz fusion</a>-styled "I Gave You Power",<sup id="cite_ref-Coleman_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coleman-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> a song which depicts a first-person narrative from the perspective of a gun.<sup id="cite_ref-Berliner_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berliner-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The song is accompanied by falling piano notes and stuttering drums.<sup id="cite_ref-Krisex_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krisex-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album contains the singles "<a href="/wiki/If_I_Ruled_The_World_(Imagine_That)" title="If I Ruled The World (Imagine That)" class="mw-redirect">If I Ruled the World</a>", which features guest vocals from <a href="/wiki/Lauryn_Hill" title="Lauryn Hill">Lauryn Hill</a>, and "<a href="/wiki/Street_Dreams_(song)" title="Street Dreams (song)">Street Dreams</a>". Music critic J.R. Reynolds wrote that the former has Nas "rapping his way to <a href="/wiki/Anarchy" title="Anarchy">anarchy</a> in an imagined world where he kicks discipline to the curb and good times rule."<sup id="cite_ref-Yahoo_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yahoo-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In the song, he states that he would "open every cell in <a href="/wiki/Attica_Correctional_Facility" title="Attica Correctional Facility">Attica</a>/send them to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Chennault_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chennault-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The latter is an account on the impact of drugs in Nas's neighborhood.<sup id="cite_ref-Ehrlich_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The song contains smooth bass lines and frail drums,<sup id="cite_ref-Krisex_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krisex-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and it features an <a href="/wiki/Interpolation_(music)" title="Interpolation (music)">interpolation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Eurythmics" title="Eurythmics">Eurythmics</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Sweet_Dreams_(Are_Made_of_This)_(song)" title="Sweet Dreams (Are Made of This) (song)" class="mw-redirect">Sweet Dreams (Are Made of This)</a>" (1983).<sup id="cite_ref-Berliner_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berliner-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The album also features guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Firm_(hip_hop_group)" title="The Firm (hip hop group)">The Firm</a>, a supergroup which was initially composed of Nas, <a href="/wiki/AZ_(rapper)" title="AZ (rapper)">AZ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cormega" title="Cormega">Cormega</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foxy_Brown_(rapper)" title="Foxy Brown (rapper)">Foxy Brown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The group makes an appearance on the song "Affirmative Action", a tale of robbery and three characters with mob connections.<sup id="cite_ref-Katz_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Katz-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Brett Berliner of <a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a> described the song's beat as "extremely mafioso, sounding straight out of <i><a href="/wiki/Goodfellas" title="Goodfellas">Goodfellas</a></i>, with strings and crescendos", while he cited the song as "one of the best <a href="/wiki/Posse_cut" title="Posse cut">posse tracks</a> of all time."<sup id="cite_ref-Berliner_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berliner-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Mobb Deep's Havoc produced two tracks for the album, "The Set Up", a story about revenge, and "Live Nigga Rap", a <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_(music)" title="Freestyle (music)" class="mw-redirect">freestyle</a> performed by Nas and Mobb Deep with hard, gloomy percussion.<sup id="cite_ref-Krisex_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krisex-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> "Black Girl Lost" is a sympathetic account on the struggle of African-American women.<sup id="cite_ref-Ehrlich_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> It features vocals by R&amp;B singer Joel "Jo-Jo" Hailey of <a href="/wiki/Jodeci" title="Jodeci">Jodeci</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Krisex_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krisex-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Music critic Krisex wrote of Nas's lyricism, stating "The L.E.S.-produced song woos heavy rotation while the MC makes the type of passionate perusals that leave lyrical aficionadeos genuflecting at his mike stand."<sup id="cite_ref-Krisex_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krisex-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The song's title comes from the book of the same name by pulp writer <a href="/wiki/Donald_Goines" title="Donald Goines">Donald Goines</a>; his literary work has served as a popular source of reference for many gangsta rap artists.<sup id="cite_ref-literary_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-literary-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> "Nas Is Coming" is a collaboration between Nas and West Coast rapper <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>. One writer cited it as "more of a gangsta, mainstream tune than anything Nas has ever recorded."<sup id="cite_ref-Katz_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Katz-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The song's opening conversation, a <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_skit" title="Hip hop skit">skit</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Katz_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Katz-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> is a discussion between Nas and Dr. Dre about hip hop artists and fans over-concerned with the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast-West_Coast_hip_hop_rivalry" title="East Coast-West Coast hip hop rivalry" class="mw-redirect">East Coast-West Coast rivalry</a>, and that the two are producing a song that does not revolve around or contribute to the beef.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=It_Was_Written&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=It_Was_Written&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>It Was Written</i> was released July 2, 1996 in the United States, Canada and Europe on <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> while featuring distribution in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> on Simply Vinyl Records as a <a href="/wiki/Double_album" title="Double album">double</a> <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_LP" title="Vinyl LP" class="mw-redirect">vinyl LP</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-S12_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S12-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>It Was Written</i> proved to be Nas’s most commercially successful album to date,<sup id="cite_ref-asof2009_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asof2009-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> selling 268,000 copies at the time of its chart debut.<sup id="cite_ref-Basham_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basham-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> It peaked at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> and debuted at the top of the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-billboard_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> remaining on the latter chart at number one for four consecutive weeks, in the top 20 for eleven weeks, and a total of thirty-four weeks in the top 200.<sup id="cite_ref-Basham_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basham-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album's first single "If I Ruled the World (Imagine That)" peaked at number 15 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Singles" title="Hot Rap Singles" class="mw-redirect">Hot Rap Singles</a> chart, number 17 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Singles_%26_Tracks" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks</a> chart and number 53 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> singles chart.<sup id="cite_ref-singles_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-singles-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The second single "Street Dreams" hit number one on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Dance_Music/Maxi-Singles_Sales" title="Hot Dance Music/Maxi-Singles Sales" class="mw-redirect">Hot Dance Music/Maxi-Singles Sales</a> chart, as well as number one on the Hot Rap Singles chart, while it peaked at number 18 on the Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks and number 27 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100.<sup id="cite_ref-singles_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-singles-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> A remix version of "Street Dreams" was released on November 26, 1996 featuring production by Poke &amp; Tone and guest vocals from R&amp;B singer <a href="/wiki/R._Kelly" title="R. Kelly">R. Kelly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> On September 6, 1996, <i>It Was Written</i> was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">double platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), for shipments of two million copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> On January 8, 1997, "Street Dreams" was certified gold in sales by the RIAA for shipments of 500,000 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>It Was Written</i> received generally positive reviews from critics.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i> called it an "audio anthology of ghetto stories told by one of hip-hop's most prolific writers."<sup id="cite_ref-Shortie_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shortie-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> magazine's Krisex criticized the album's "consistently aggressive attempts at pop music", but also wrote that Nas "shines through".<sup id="cite_ref-Krisex_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krisex-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite calling the album "adequate" and commending Nas for his lyricism and <a href="/wiki/Flow_(music)" title="Flow (music)" class="mw-redirect">flow</a>, Krisex concluded that <i>It was Written</i> "isn't nearly as satisfying as his first one."<sup id="cite_ref-Krisex_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krisex-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> wrote that "Nas' neat, considered lyrics treat the violence that surrounds him with a mixture of remorse, resignation and ebullience."<sup id="cite_ref-NME_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NME-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Christopher_John_Farley" title="Christopher John Farley">Christopher John Farley</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> stated "The lyrics in <i>It Was Written</i> could be sharper, but the music, energetic and engaging on many tracks, helps drive his message home."<sup id="cite_ref-Farley_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farley-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)" class="mw-redirect">Q</a></i> magazine called Nas's performance "angry, lean and full of drive."<sup id="cite_ref-Qmag_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Qmag-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Both the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> were favorable of the album's sound and gave the album 3½ out of 4 stars.<sup id="cite_ref-Kot_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kot-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Williams_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> writer Cheo Coker called the album "poetic", writing that it "demonstrates a continuing lyrical maturity that makes his already potent beats and rhymes all the more compelling".<sup id="cite_ref-Coker_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coker-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> magazine preferred the "reach" of <i>It Was Written</i> to Nas's "more suavely rapped debut", praised the production, and described the songs' choruses as "grainy, pop-savvy".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album's release followed the commercial success of other mafioso-themed rap albums with similar subject matter, including Raekwon's <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx%E2%80%A6" title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx…" class="mw-redirect">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx…</a></i> (1995), Jay-Z's <i><a href="/wiki/Reasonable_Doubt" title="Reasonable Doubt" class="mw-redirect">Reasonable Doubt</a></i> (1996) and AZ's <i><a href="/wiki/Doe_or_Die" title="Doe or Die">Doe or Die</a></i> (1995).<sup id="cite_ref-hnic_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hnic-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> Music writers criticized its mainstream, R&amp;B and pop-leaning sound, as well as the enlistment of a top production unit and popular guest artists.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hnic_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hnic-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's lyrics and themes were also poorly received and heavily dismissed as an attempt by Nas to follow the popularity of gangsta and mafioso rap.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hnic_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hnic-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Mark Coleman wrote negatively of Nas's themes and called it "the latest blatant example of trashy tough-guy talk", writing "Certainly he strikes a note of creepy realism in his stories of heavyweight dealing and literally cutthroat competition. 'The Set Up', 'Shootouts' and 'Affirmative Action' [...] are chilling in their how-many-grams-to-a-kilo detail and utter amorality. On 'Watch Dem Niggas', Nas cites as inspirations both the boxing coach <a href="/wiki/Cus_D%27Amato" title="Cus D'Amato">Cus D'Amato</a> and the murderous drug lord <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Escobar" title="Pablo Escobar">Pablo Escobar</a>. What is this guy thinking?".<sup id="cite_ref-Coleman_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coleman-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Jon Pareles of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> commented that he "continually shifts perspective" and called it "late-stage gangsta rap, starting to buckle under its own contradictions."<sup id="cite_ref-Pareles_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pareles-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave the album a "neither" (<span style="white-space:nowrap" title="(neither)"><img alt="(neither)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Rating-Christgau-neither.png/14px-Rating-Christgau-neither.png" width="14" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Rating-Christgau-neither.png/21px-Rating-Christgau-neither.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Rating-Christgau-neither.png/28px-Rating-Christgau-neither.png 2x" data-file-width="144" data-file-height="144" /></span>) rating,<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> indicating "may impress once or twice with consistent craft or an arresting track or two. Then it won't."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album was ranked number 41 in <i>NME</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s critics' poll of 1996,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> and Jim Farber of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Daily_News" title="New York Daily News" class="mw-redirect">New York Daily News</a></i> named it the sixth best album of 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> German-based magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Spex_(magazine)" title="Spex (magazine)">Spex</a></i> ranked it number four on its "End of Year" list, while the UK-based magazine <i><a href="/wiki/The_Face_(magazine)" title="The Face (magazine)">The Face</a></i> named it the twenty-fourth best album of 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> "If I Ruled the World (Imagine That)" was ranked number 29 on <i>NME</i>'s Singles of the Year list,<sup id="cite_ref-ruled_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ruled-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> and number 20 on <i>The Village Voice</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Pazz_%26_Jop" title="Pazz &amp; Jop">Pazz &amp; Jop</a> critics' poll.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> It was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance">Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance</a> in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-tower_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tower-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retrospect">Retrospect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=It_Was_Written&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Retrospect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since its release, <i>It Was Written</i> remains Nas' best-selling release to date.<sup id="cite_ref-Basham_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basham-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> By 2001, it had sold more than 2.13 million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-Basham_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basham-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Leo Stanley of <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> later praised Nas's lyricism and ghetto-themed vignettes, along with the album's detailed production.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanley_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanley-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a>'s <i>On Second Thought</i> publication, critic Brett Berliner re-examined the album, discussing its initial impact, and cited it as "one of the first hip-hop albums to straddle the critical and commercial divide successfully."<sup id="cite_ref-Berliner_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berliner-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> While comparing <i>It Was Written</i> to <i>Illmatic</i>, Berliner stated "It’s a seriously good album with a bit of filler, worth of almost all of the praise Illmatic got. This is Nas’ second classic, and should be considered one of the best albums of all time."<sup id="cite_ref-Berliner_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berliner-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/About.com" title="About.com">About.com</a> later ranked "If I Ruled the World (Imagine That)" number 24 on its list of 50 Great Hip Hop Songs,<sup id="cite_ref-ruled_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ruled-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> while it also named the song the fourth best R&amp;B/Rap Collaboration.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While later reviews of the album saw <i>It Was Written</i> in a different light than previous reviews had, the subject matter was still seen as a major flaw of the album. Music critics perceived Nas' violent, fantastical mafioso stories as lacking the emotion and truthfulness of his debut album. Writer Joe Katz wrote in a review of the album for <a href="/wiki/Sputnikmusic" title="Sputnikmusic">Sputnikmusic</a>, stating "The lyrics were there, but some of the emotion was gone. Maybe it was the world around Nas changing, or maybe it was changing in himself, but no one can listen to this after <i>Illmatic</i> and not see a change ... If <i>Illmatic</i> was the kind of nightmare you can't wake up from and don't really want too, then <i>IWW</i> is a regular dream, not quite "real" but enjoyable all the same."<sup id="cite_ref-Katz_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Katz-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> While <i>Illmatic</i> is often held as Nas's <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a>, <i>It Was Written</i> was not only perceived by critics as part of a sophomore jinx, but was also the first of Nas's subsequent work to be heavily judged and compared to the former.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> However, <i>It Was Written</i> has been noted as his commercial breakthrough by critics, as it boosted the rapper's image in the mainstream and helped attract a much larger fan-base.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_influence">Legacy and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=It_Was_Written&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=It_Was_Written&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Nas is Coming" began a brief collaboration between Nas and West Coast hip hop producer <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>. The alliance also resulted in the formation of The Firm, who make their debut on track number eight, "Affirmative Action".<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The pairing of the East Coast rapper and the West Coast producer, during the period of the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast-West_Coast_rivalry" title="East Coast-West Coast rivalry" class="mw-redirect">East Coast-West Coast rivalry</a>, brought criticism from both sides.<sup id="cite_ref-Lang_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lang-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-brown_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> More controversy ensued when, during the recording of The Firm's album, Cormega was fired from the group by Nas's manager, Steve Stoute, and replaced with a young rapper named <a href="/wiki/Nature_(rapper)" title="Nature (rapper)">Nature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Cormega recorded an underground single, "Fuck Nas and Nature", which began a rivalry with Nas that persisted—with brief periods of reconciliation—to the end of 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-reunites_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reunites-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, West Coast-based rapper <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a> took offense to the opening line of the song "<a href="/wiki/The_Message_(Nas_song)" title="The Message (Nas song)">The Message</a>", and in retaliation insulted Nas on a song titled "Against All Odds" from his posthumously-released album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Don_Killuminati:_The_7_Day_Theory" title="The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory">The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory</a></i> (1996).<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In an interview for <i><a href="/wiki/King_(magazine)" title="King (magazine)">King</a></i> magazine, Nas later confirmed that the song was intended as a diss towards <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>, with the line "There's one life, one love, so there can only be one King."<sup id="cite_ref-king_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-king-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Nas and Shakur eventually met and reconciled prior to the latter's fatal shooting. As result of his death, Shakur did not have the opportunity to remove the insults to Nas in "Against All Odds" on <i>The 7 Day Theory</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>It Was Written</i> has been credited, along with Raekwon's <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx%E2%80%A6" title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx…" class="mw-redirect">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx…</a></i> (1995), with helping usher in the era of the <a href="/wiki/Mafioso_rap" title="Mafioso rap" class="mw-redirect">mafioso rap</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chennault_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chennault-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> According to writer Sam Chennault, while the hip hop subgenre would "run out of steam quickly, this release is a gem."<sup id="cite_ref-Chennault_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chennault-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Chennault also discussed the significance of <i>It Was Written</i> during the period of its release, stating "after mastering stark street corner realism on <i>Illmatic</i>, Nas delivered a loose concept album that was, at the time, groundbreaking in its scope, approach and execution."<sup id="cite_ref-Chennault_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chennault-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> According to rapper <a href="/wiki/Young_Noble" title="Young Noble">Young Noble</a>, a close friend of Tupac Shakur, the song "I Gave You Power" served as the main inspiration for Shakur's "<a href="/wiki/The_Don_Killuminati:_The_7_Day_Theory" title="The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory">Me and My Girlfriend</a>" (1996).<sup id="cite_ref-literary_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-literary-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> American hip hop artist <a href="/wiki/Lupe_Fiasco" title="Lupe Fiasco">Lupe Fiasco</a> has cited <i>It Was Written</i> as his favorite album and his primary source of inspiration.<sup id="cite_ref-lupe_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lupe-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Fiasco has stated that he based his debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Lupe_Fiasco%27s_Food_%26_Liquor" title="Lupe Fiasco's Food &amp; Liquor">Food &amp; Liquor</a></i> (2006) on the conceptual style and "moods" of <i>It Was Written</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-smiling_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smiling-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> When asked of his musical influences in an interview with <a href="/wiki/AllHipHop" title="AllHipHop">AllHipHop</a>.com, Fiasco stated "You know I really tried to go back and recreate [Nas’] <i>It Was Written</i>, you know what I'm saying like that? [I would play] <i>It Was Written</i> and then I would play my album, and it was like, ‘Do we got [this] record, do we got that record?’"<sup id="cite_ref-lupe_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lupe-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> When asked in an interview with NobodySmiling.com about why he is comfortable admitting the album as a source of inspiration, Fiasco stated:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Cause it’s a classic. Like, people study-you study the masters, you know what I’m saying? Everybody that’s rapping studied someone to learn how to rap. They had a rapper who was they favorite rapper that they wanted to be like and wanted to rap like, know what I’m saying? For me, I just look at it like I studied a master piece. I modeled my album after a master piece; and not song for song; not line for line; not beat for beat. It was more-for me it was like mood for mood. The way he set the mood on that album to me was just like incredible. And at the time in my life-like, I fell in love with ‘<i>It Was Written</i>’ when I was seventeen, eighteen-a very impressionable time-so I was like I love that album. That’s my favorite Hip Hop album, so it’s like why not base your album on ‘<i>It Was Written</i>’?<sup id="cite_ref-smiling_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smiling-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="templatequotecite">—Lupe Fiasco</div>
</blockquote>
<p>American reggae and hip hop artist <a href="/wiki/Matisyahu" title="Matisyahu">Matisyahu</a> regards <i>It Was Written</i> as one of his influences as well.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> He cites the introduction of <i>It Was Written</i>, in which slaves rebel against their owner, as having a major influence on him. According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, "Matisyahu, too, felt enslaved. By what? He didn't know. Just felt the chains. The lyrics rocked him. The beat did, too."<sup id="cite_ref-albumintro_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-albumintro-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Matisyahu stated that after listening to <i>It Was Written</i>, "I connected with hip-hop, the hardness of it, the driving beat. It’s music with space, that has gaps in every little thing that happens."<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_work_by_Nas">Subsequent work by Nas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=It_Was_Written&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Subsequent work by Nas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While <i>It Was Written</i> earned a more favorable reputation among critics since its initial mixed reception, Nas's subsequent releases have continued to be <a href="/wiki/Sophomore_slump" title="Sophomore slump">weighed against</a> his critically acclaimed <i>Illmatic</i>, despite all of them outselling his debut.<sup id="cite_ref-Hess_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hill_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hill-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Against this standard, they have often been critically deemed as mediocre follow-ups.<sup id="cite_ref-Hill_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hill-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>It Was Written</i> was the first of Nas's albums to have been labeled as 'selling out' by fans of <i>Illmatic</i>, due to his crossover sensibilities and radio-friendly hits aimed at the pop charts.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, none of his following releases have been able to reach the sales success of <i>It Was Written</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-asof2009_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asof2009-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Basham_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basham-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The follow-up, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Am%E2%80%A6_(Nas_album)" title="I Am… (Nas album)" class="mw-redirect">I Am…</a></i> (1999), fared almost as well as <i>It Was Written</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Basham_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basham-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> serving as Nas's only other album to reach double platinum status.<sup id="cite_ref-asof2009_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asof2009-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> After the releases of <i>I Am…</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nastradamus" title="Nastradamus">Nastradamus</a></i> (1999), which underwent considerable editing due to bootlegging of the recording sessions, many fans and critics feared that his career was deteriorating.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite the chart-topping success of <i>I Am…</i>, hip hop audiences were not ready for the more prophetic themes of <i>Nastradamus</i>, as it only sold 232,000 copies by its first week (less than half of <i>I Am…</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s first-week figures).<sup id="cite_ref-Basham_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basham-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>By 2001, <i>Illmatic</i> and <i>It Was Written</i> were both selling at a rate of over 3,000 copies a week, while <i>Nastradamus</i> was earning an average of little more than 2,000 copies a week, despite its relative newness.<sup id="cite_ref-Basham_19-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basham-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Both <i>I Am…</i> and <i>Nastradamus</i> received further criticism for their commercially-oriented sound.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Reflecting this widespread perception in the hip hop community and adding to his ongoing <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z_vs._Nas_feud" title="Jay-Z vs. Nas feud" class="mw-redirect">feud with Jay-Z</a> at the time, Jay-Z mocked him in the song "<a href="/wiki/Takeover_(song)" title="Takeover (song)">Takeover</a>" (2001) for assuming a "Pablo Escobar" persona and having a "one hot album [<i>Illmatic</i>] every ten year average".<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> Nas, however, made something of a comeback with his fifth album <i><a href="/wiki/Stillmatic" title="Stillmatic">Stillmatic</a></i> (2001) and the follow-up <i><a href="/wiki/God%27s_Son" title="God's Son">God's Son</a></i> (2002),<sup id="cite_ref-Cowie_1-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowie-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> which both sold in excess of 1 million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-asof2009_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asof2009-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Afterwards, his subsequent albums tended to receive more positive reviews, including the platinum-selling<sup id="cite_ref-asof2009_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asof2009-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Street%27s_Disciple" title="Street's Disciple">Street's Disciple</a></i> (2004) and his <a href="/wiki/Untitled_Nas_album" title="Untitled Nas album">untitled ninth album</a> (2008).<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> Nevertheless, <i>It Was Written</i> has remained as Nas's best-selling album.<sup id="cite_ref-Basham_19-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basham-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
