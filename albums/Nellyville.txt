<p><i><b>Nellyville</b></i> is the second <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/Nelly" title="Nelly">Nelly</a>, released on June 25, 2002 through <a href="/wiki/Universal_Records" title="Universal Records">Universal Records</a>. Production for the album was handled mainly by reprising producer <a href="/wiki/Jason_%22Jay_E%22_Epperson" title="Jason &quot;Jay E&quot; Epperson">Jason "Jay E" Epperson</a>, with additional production by Kevin Law, C-Love, Waiel "Wally" Yaghnam, <a href="/wiki/The_Neptunes" title="The Neptunes">The Neptunes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trackboyz" title="Trackboyz">Trackboyz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Bowser" title="Ryan Bowser">Ryan Bowser</a> and <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a>. Six singles were released from the album: "<a href="/wiki/Number_1_(Nelly_song)" title="Number 1 (Nelly song)">#1</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Hot_in_Herre" title="Hot in Herre">Hot in Herre</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Dilemma_(song)" title="Dilemma (song)">Dilemma</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Ones_(song)" title="Air Force Ones (song)">Air Force Ones</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Work_It_(Nelly_song)" title="Work It (Nelly song)">Work It</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Pimp_Juice" title="Pimp Juice">Pimp Juice</a>". "Hot in Herre" and "Dilemma" both topped the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart, for seven and ten weeks, respectively.</p>
<p><i>Nellyville</i> debuted at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 714,000 copies in its opening week. It stayed atop the chart for four non-consecutive weeks and went on to be certified six times platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), for shipments of over six million copies. Internationally, <i>Nellyville</i> peaked at number two in <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Charts" title="ARIA Charts">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Albums_Chart" title="Canadian Albums Chart">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Media_Control_Charts" title="Media Control Charts">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recorded_Music_NZ" title="Recorded Music NZ">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Official_Charts_Company" title="Official Charts Company">United Kingdom</a>. By March 16, 2011, the album went on to sell 6,488,000 copies in the US, and is the fourteenth best-selling rap album of all time.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_production">Background and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nellyville&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Executive producers for <i>Nellyville</i> include <a href="/wiki/Nelly" title="Nelly">Nelly</a>, Kevin Law and C-Love. The majority of <a href="/wiki/Recording_engineering" title="Recording engineering" class="mw-redirect">recording</a> was done by Steve Eigner, who was assisted by Marc Lee. Rich Travali, assisted by Jeff Robinette, Jamie Duncan and Dan Milazzo performed the <a href="/wiki/Mixing_engineer" title="Mixing engineer">mixing</a>. <a href="/wiki/Audio_mastering" title="Audio mastering">Audio mastering</a> was done by Herb Powers, with <a href="/wiki/Artists_and_repertoire" title="Artists and repertoire">A&amp;R</a> directed by Law and coordinated by Jennifer Havey. Art direction was done by Sandy Brummels with Robert Sims undertaking design and Jonathan Mannion handling photography. Road management was executed by Keith Brent, with additional management done so by Tony Davis, Courtney Benson and Team Lunatics. Legality was handled by Richard Grable and Tim Mandelbaum. Several artists made an appearance on the album, including <a href="/wiki/Justin_Timberlake" title="Justin Timberlake">Justin Timberlake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kelly_Rowland" title="Kelly Rowland">Kelly Rowland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beanie_Sigel" title="Beanie Sigel">Beanie Sigel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freeway_(rapper)" title="Freeway (rapper)">Freeway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dani_Stevenson" title="Dani Stevenson">Dani Stevenson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-liner_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liner-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Nellyville</i> received generally positive reviews from music critics. At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album has received an <a href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean" class="mw-redirect">average</a> score of 70, based on 15 reviews, which indicates "generally favorable reviews".<sup id="cite_ref-MCNellyville_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCNellyville-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Wayne Franklin of <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a> held low expectations for the album prior to listening to it, following Nelly's feud with rapper <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">KRS-One</a>, but found its music to be "incredible".<sup id="cite_ref-Wayne_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wayne-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Wayne lauded the track "<a href="/wiki/Air_Force_One" title="Air Force One">Air Force One</a>", describing it as an "unbelievable ode" to hip hop, while noting several other songs as stand-outs.<sup id="cite_ref-Wayne_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wayne-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a>'s Jason Birchmeier gave the album three-and-a-half stars out of five, noting "Hot in Herre", "Dilemma" and "Roc the Mic" as "three well-calculated, standout moments".<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Birchmeier praised "Hot in Herre"'s production, "Roc the Mic"'s hip hop composition and the "Dilemma"'s interpolation of <a href="/wiki/Patti_LaBelle" title="Patti LaBelle">Patti LaBelle</a>'s "Love, Need and Want You".<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> RapReviews.com's Steve Juon gave <i>Nellyville</i> an 8.5 out of ten, citing the album's consistency and production, praising "Hot in Herre" for its Midwestern drawl and "harmonious musical flow".<sup id="cite_ref-Juon_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Juon-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Giving the album a B+, Tom Sinclair of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> praised several new additions to Nelly's vocal style, summarizing: "If you like your rap loose and funny, Nelly's the man for you".<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Dele Fadele saw <i>Nellyville</i> as Nelly's "reply to the haters"; Fadele described the album as a "glossy, well-produced album of populist anthems with a gangsta undertow" that expands his perspective of the world and "celebrates success".<sup id="cite_ref-Fadele_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fadele-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Though Fadele wasn't impressed at the <a href="/wiki/Justin_Timberlake" title="Justin Timberlake">Justin Timberlake</a> featured track "Work It", calling the song a "silly pop-directed collaboration".<sup id="cite_ref-Fadele_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fadele-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Nathan Rabin praised <i>Nellyville</i> for achieving similar universal appeal to Nelly's previous effort <i><a href="/wiki/Country_Grammar" title="Country Grammar">Country Grammar</a></i>, which achieved it with songs such as its <a href="/wiki/Country_Grammar_(Hot_Shit)" title="Country Grammar (Hot Shit)">self-titled track</a> and "<a href="/wiki/E.I._(song)" title="E.I. (song)">E.I.</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Rabin criticized Timberlake's "high-schooler doing a <a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees" title="Bee Gees">Bee Gees</a> impersonation" on "Work It", though noted the song as the only "glaring misstep" on the album.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/People_(magazine)" title="People (magazine)">People</a></i> lauded "Hot in Herre" for its "funky go-go groove", declaring it the "perfect summer jam", but were ambivalent towards the rest of the album's beats for being slightly repetitive.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Rob Marriott described Nelly as a "gifted, witty MC" with the "catchiest rhymes to ever hit the pop charts" but wrote that <i>Nellyville</i> sounds "weighted down" by the commercial pressure of having to have another multi-platinum album.<sup id="cite_ref-Rob_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rob-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nellyville&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/2003_Grammy_Awards" title="2003 Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">2003 Grammy Awards</a>, <i>Nellyville</i> was nominated for Album of the Year, "Hot in Herre" for Best Male Rap Solo Performance and "Dilemma" for Best Rap/Sung Collaboration; both songs won their respective nominations, with <i>Nellyville</i> losing out to <a href="/wiki/Norah_Jones" title="Norah Jones">Norah Jones</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Come_Away_with_Me" title="Come Away with Me">Come Away with Me</a></i> (2002).<sup id="cite_ref-grammy_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grammy-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Nelly received three <a href="/wiki/BET_Awards_2003" title="BET Awards 2003">BET Award</a> nominations: Best Male Hip-Hop Artist, Best Collaboration (for "Dilemma") and Video of the Year (for "Hot in Herre").<sup id="cite_ref-bet_awards_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bet_awards-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/American_Music_Awards_of_2003" title="American Music Awards of 2003">American Music Awards</a>, Nelly was nominated for <a href="/wiki/American_Music_Award_for_Favorite_Pop/Rock_Male_Artist" title="American Music Award for Favorite Pop/Rock Male Artist">Favorite Pop/Rock Male Artist</a> and the Fan's Choice Award, with <i>Nellyville</i> nominated for <a href="/wiki/American_Music_Award_for_Favorite_Pop/Rock_Album" title="American Music Award for Favorite Pop/Rock Album">Favorite Pop/Rock Album</a> and Favorite Hip/Hop/R&amp;B Album, with Nelly winning the Fan's Choice Award.<sup id="cite_ref-ama_awards_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ama_awards-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Nelly received six awards at the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Music_Award" title="Billboard Music Award">2002 Billboard Music Awards</a>, winning the awards for Artist of the Year, Male Artist of the Year, Hot 100 Singles Male Artist of the Year, R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Male Artist of the Year, Rap Artist of the Year and Rap Track of the Year (for "Hot in Herre").<sup id="cite_ref-billboard_awards_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard_awards-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> On the 2002 <a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)"><i>Billboard</i></a> year-end charts, the album was ranked third on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 and the Top R&amp;B/Hip Hop Albums charts.<sup id="cite_ref-bb200_year-end_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb200_year-end-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-r.26b_year-end_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-r.26b_year-end-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nellyville&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album debuted at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 714,000 copies, outperforming Nelly's previous effort <i><a href="/wiki/Country_Grammar" title="Country Grammar">Country Grammar</a></i> (2000), which debuted at number three selling 235,000 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard200wk1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard200wk1-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> With the single "<a href="/wiki/Hot_in_Herre" title="Hot in Herre">Hot in Herre</a>" number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> that week, Nelly was in possession of the top spot on ten different <i>Billboard</i> charts.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard200wk1_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard200wk1-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The following two weeks of its release, <i>Nellyville</i> remained atop the <i>Billboard</i> 200, selling 447,000 and 340,000 copies, respectively, surpassing 1.5 million copies in sales in its third week.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> In its fourth week, <i>Nellyville</i> replaced atop the chart by <a href="/wiki/Dave_Matthews_Band" title="Dave Matthews Band">Dave Matthews Band</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Busted_Stuff" title="Busted Stuff">Busted Stuff</a></i>, which debuted with 622,000 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard200wk4_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard200wk4-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The former album was positioned at number two on the <i>Billboard</i> 200, with its sales decreasing to 305,000 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard200wk4_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard200wk4-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> In the album's fifth week, it sold 271,000 copies, dropping from number two to three,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> and in its sixth week sold 244,000 copies remaining at number three.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Nellville</i> moved up to number two and sold 208,000 units in its seventh week of release and in its eighth week, topped the <i>Billboard</i> 200, accumulating to four non-consecutive weeks atop.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The album sold 183,000 copies that week.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard200wk8_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard200wk8-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Nellyville</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s four-week number one run and sales were, according to <i>Billboard</i>, largely in part to the success of the album's singles, "<a href="/wiki/Hot_in_Herre" title="Hot in Herre">Hot in Herre</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Dilemma_(song)" title="Dilemma (song)">Dilemma</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-billboard200wk8_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard200wk8-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The former track was number one on the Hot 100 prior to the album's release, maintaining a third week atop the chart when <i>Nellville</i> made its debut.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard200wk1_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard200wk1-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The song topped the Hot 100 for seven consecutive weeks before being replaced by its successor, "Dilemma", which topped the chart for ten non-consecutive weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard200wk8_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard200wk8-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> On June 25, 2002, <i>Nellyville</i> was certified six times platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), for shipments of six million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-us_cert_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-us_cert-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> By March 16, 2011, the album went on to sell 6,488,000 copies in the US,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> and became the fourteenth best-selling rap album of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-CGsales_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CGsales-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

chart for thirty-eight weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-uk_chart_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk_chart-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> On July 1, 2002 the album was certified two href="#cite_note-australia_chart-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The album dropped off the chart after thirty-one weeks, and was certified three times platinum by the <a
