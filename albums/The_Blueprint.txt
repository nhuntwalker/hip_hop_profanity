<p><i><b>The Blueprint</b></i> is the sixth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American rapper <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, released September 11, 2001 on <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a> in the United States. Its release was set a week earlier than initially planned in order to combat <a href="/wiki/Bootleg_recording" title="Bootleg recording">bootlegging</a>. Recording sessions for the album took place during 2001 at <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Center" title="Manhattan Center">Manhattan Center Studios</a> and Baseline Studios in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. Contrasting the radio-friendly sound of Jay-Z's previous work, <i>The Blueprint</i> features <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampling</a> and production handled primarily by <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> and <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoard_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoard-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> At the time of its recording, Jay-Z was awaiting two criminal trials, one for gun possession and another for assault, and had become one of hip hop's most <a href="/wiki/Diss_track" title="Diss track">dissed</a> artists, receiving insults from rappers such as <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prodigy_(rapper)" title="Prodigy (rapper)">Prodigy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Strauss_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Strauss-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Baker_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The album is also famous for its producer Kanye West's big breakout as a major producer. Kanye West produced 4 of the 13 tracks on the album, including the iconic track "<a href="/wiki/Izzo_(H.O.V.A.)" title="Izzo (H.O.V.A.)">Izzo (H.O.V.A.)</a>" and the controversial track which included diss lyrics aimed at rappers Nas and Prodigy, <a href="/wiki/Takeover_(song)" title="Takeover (song)">"Takeover"</a>.</p>
<p>In spite of its release coinciding with the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">9/11 attacks</a>, <i>The Blueprint</i> sold over 420,000 copies in its opening week,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> becoming Jay-Z's fourth consecutive album to reach number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart. It was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">double platinum</a> as sales stand at over two million units in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The album received a perfect "XXL" rating from <i><a href="/wiki/XXL_(magazine)" title="XXL (magazine)">XXL</a></i> magazine,<sup id="cite_ref-XXL_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> while <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i> awarded <i>The Blueprint</i> a classic 5 mic rating.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Blueprint</i> received universal acclaim from music critics, based on an aggregate score of 88/100 from <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Reviews_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reviews-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2003, the album was ranked number 464 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of <a href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> in a revised list in 2012, it was ranked number 252.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2010, <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> ranked it number 5 on their Top 200 Albums of the 2000s list.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Sales as of February 2012 stand at 2.7 million.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blueprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Blueprint</i> was reportedly cut in two weeks, with Jay-Z allegedly writing the lyrics in two days.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> At the time, he was awaiting two criminal trials for gun possession and <a href="/wiki/Assault" title="Assault">assault</a>. He was also engaged in feuds with various rappers, in particular <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a> member <a href="/wiki/Prodigy_(rapper)" title="Prodigy (rapper)">Prodigy</a>. In the song "<a href="/wiki/Takeover_(song)" title="Takeover (song)">Takeover</a>", Jay-Z attacks the two <a href="/wiki/Queensbridge" title="Queensbridge" class="mw-redirect">Queensbridge</a> rappers, using a sample of the song "<a href="/wiki/Five_to_One" title="Five to One">Five to One</a>" by <a href="/wiki/The_Doors" title="The Doors">The Doors</a><sup id="cite_ref-XXL_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and an interpolation of <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Fame_(David_Bowie_song)" title="Fame (David Bowie song)">Fame</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Leroy_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leroy-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> On <i>The Blueprint</i>, Jay-Z and his producers used vintage <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a> as inspiration, including a vocal <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sample</a> on almost every track from such artists as <a href="/wiki/Al_Green" title="Al Green">Al Green</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Bland" title="Bobby Bland">Bobby "Blue" Bland</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Ruffin" title="David Ruffin">David Ruffin</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Jackson_5" title="The Jackson 5">The Jackson 5</a>. Exceptions include "<a href="/wiki/Jigga_That_Nigga" title="Jigga That Nigga">Jigga That Nigga</a>," "Hola Hovito," and "Renegade," a track produced by and featuring the rapper <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>, and the only track on the album featuring another rapper on verses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Blueprint_Lounge_Tour">Blueprint Lounge Tour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blueprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Blueprint Lounge Tour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In late August, Jay-Z announced a September–October tour in small venues.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Because of the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>, the first two performances were rescheduled. <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> were subsequently added, and Jay-Z donated a dollar of the cost of every ticket sold from the tour to relief organizations.</p>

<p><i>The Blueprint</i> contained a unique and balanced blend of soulful samples that had both street credibility and mainstream appeal, thereby garnering praise from all quarters of the hip hop community and receiving special recognition from critics.<sup id="cite_ref-Rabin_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rabin-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Most consider <i>The Blueprint</i> to be one of Jay-Z's best albums, holding it on a level close to that of his debut, <i><a href="/wiki/Reasonable_Doubt_(album)" title="Reasonable Doubt (album)">Reasonable Doubt</a></i>. Upon its release, <i>The Blueprint</i> was rated as <a href="/wiki/VIBE" title="VIBE" class="mw-redirect">Vibe Magazine</a>'s <i>"Best Album of the year"</i>, and even received a 5 mic (out of 5) rating from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i> (a distinction reserved for hip hop classics).<sup id="cite_ref-The_Source_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Source-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> named it the 2nd best album of 2000-2004. At Metacritic, which assigns a normalized rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album received an average score of 88, which indicates "universal acclaim", based on 12 reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The popularity and commercial success of the album established <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> and <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a> as two of hip-hop’s most celebrated producers. Furthermore, <i>The Blueprint</i> signaled a major stylistic shift in hip hop production towards a more <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">Soulcentric</a> and sample-reliant sound, creating a number of imitators who attempted to emulate the album's atmospheric style. Prior to <i>The Blueprint</i>, mainstream hip-hop producers had largely eschewed <a href="/wiki/Music_sampling" title="Music sampling" class="mw-redirect">music sampling</a> in favor of the keyboard-driven <i><a href="/wiki/Timbaland#The_.22Timbaland_sound.22:_1996_-_2000" title="Timbaland">Timbaland sound</a></i> (characterized by a shifting, syncopated rhythm, similar to <a href="/wiki/Samba" title="Samba">samba</a> or <a href="/wiki/Drum_and_bass" title="Drum and bass">jungle music</a>), due to the financial and legal issues associated with copyright laws. <i>The Blueprint</i>, however, revived musical sampling as a common practice in <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop music</a> and dislodged the digital keyboard-driven production style as the dominant sound in hip-hop music.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Kanye West would later incorporate some of the production and sampling techniques he used on this album into his own solo albums. <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> put it on its end-of-the-decade, "best-of" list, saying, "One of the greatest poets ever to pick up a mic released his magnum opus in 2001. One retirement and one un-retirement later, it's still his finest hour."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonus_tracks">Bonus tracks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Blueprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bonus tracks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with <i><a href="/wiki/Vol._3..._Life_and_Times_of_S._Carter" title="Vol. 3... Life and Times of S. Carter">Vol. 3... Life and Times of S. Carter</a></i>, Jay-Z put two hidden bonus tracks at the end of the final track. "Blueprint (Momma Loves Me)" is 3:41 by itself. Twenty-five seconds of silence follows after and the bonus track "Breathe Easy (Lyrical Exercise)" begins. That song fades and is immediately followed by "Girls, Girls, Girls (Part 2)". It is reported that the latter song features uncredited vocals by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>. The final track as a whole is 12:07. On the <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">iTunes Store</a>, however, these bonus tracks are released as separate tracks, thus making the album 15 tracks long.</p>
