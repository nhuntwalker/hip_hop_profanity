<p><i><b>Paul's Boutique</b></i> is the second <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">Beastie Boys</a>, released on July 25, 1989, on <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a>. Featuring production by the <a href="/wiki/Dust_Brothers" title="Dust Brothers">Dust Brothers</a>, the recording sessions for the album took place in <a href="/wiki/Matt_Dike" title="Matt Dike" class="mw-redirect">Matt Dike</a>'s apartment and the <a href="/wiki/Record_Plant#Los_Angeles" title="Record Plant">Record Plant</a> in Los Angeles from 1988 to 1989, after which the recordings underwent <a href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(recorded_music)" title="Audio mixing (recorded music)">mixing</a> at the Record Plant in Los Angeles. Subsequent remixes were done at the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Record_Plant_Studios" title="Record Plant Studios" class="mw-redirect">Record Plant Studios</a>.</p>
<p><i>Paul's Boutique</i> was initially considered a commercial failure by the executives at Capitol Records, as its sales did not match that of the group's previous record, <i><a href="/wiki/Licensed_to_Ill" title="Licensed to Ill">Licensed to Ill</a></i>, and the label eventually decided to stop promoting the album. The album's popularity continued to grow, however, and it has since been touted as a breakthrough achievement for the Beastie Boys. Highly varied lyrically and sonically, <i>Paul's Boutique</i> secured the Beastie Boys' place as critical favorites in the hip-hop genre. The album's rankings near the top of many publications' "best albums" lists in disparate genres has given <i>Paul's Boutique</i> critical recognition as a landmark album in hip hop.<sup id="cite_ref-acclaimed_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acclaimed-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On January 27, 1999, <i>Paul's Boutique</i> was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">double platinum</a> in sales by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2003, the album was ranked number 156 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of <a href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was re-released in a 20th anniversary package remastered in 24-bit audio and featuring a commentary track on January 27, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul%27s_Boutique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Derided as <a href="/wiki/One-hit_wonder" title="One-hit wonder">one-hit wonders</a> and estranged from their original producer, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rubin" title="Rick Rubin">Rick Rubin</a>, and record label, <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam" title="Def Jam" class="mw-redirect">Def Jam</a>, the Beastie Boys were in self-imposed exile in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> during early 1988 and were written off by most music critics before even beginning to record their second studio album, <i>Paul's Boutique</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-boutique_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boutique-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Following the commercial success of <i>Licensed to Ill</i>, the Beastie Boys were focusing on making an album with more creative depth and less commercial material.<sup id="cite_ref-boutique_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boutique-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The group's previous album had been enormously popular and received critical acclaim among both mainstream and hip hop music critics, although its simple, heavy beats and comically juvenile lyrics led it to be labeled as <a href="/wiki/Frat" title="Frat" class="mw-redirect">frat</a> <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-boutique_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boutique-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The group signed with <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/EMI_Records" title="EMI Records">EMI Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-boutique_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boutique-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Put together on <a href="/wiki/Sampler_(musical_instrument)" title="Sampler (musical instrument)">samplers</a> with tiny memories, small fragments of staggeringly disparate musics drop in, then are snatched away abruptly; rhythms and melodies remain in focus as <a href="/wiki/Texture_(music)" title="Texture (music)">textures</a> and sounds constantly <a href="/wiki/Level_(music)" title="Level (music)">shift</a>.</p>

<div style="position: absolute; left: 0px; top: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">“</div>
<div style="position: absolute; right: 0px; bottom: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">”</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: center;">— <i><a href="/wiki/Mojo_(magazine)" title="Mojo (magazine)">Mojo</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-Mojo_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mojo-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
<p><i>Paul's Boutique</i> was produced with the Dust Brothers, whose extensive, innovative use of <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampling</a> helped establish the practice of multi-layered sampling as an art in itself. While the Dust Brothers were set on making a hit record, the duo agreed with the group on producing a more experimental and sonically different record.<sup id="cite_ref-boutique_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boutique-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In total, 105 songs were sampled on the album, including 24 individual samples on the last track alone. The <a href="/wiki/Backing_track" title="Backing track">backing tracks</a> were allegedly produced with the intention of being released as a Dust Brothers instrumental album, but the Beastie Boys convinced the duo to use the tracks as the basis of their follow up to <i>Licensed to Ill</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-boutique_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boutique-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-samples_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-samples-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Contrary to popular belief, most of the sampling for <i>Paul's Boutique</i> was cleared, but at dramatically lower costs compared to today's prevailing rates.<sup id="cite_ref-samples_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-samples-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> A 2005 article by Paul Tingen about The Dust Brothers reveals that "most of the samples used on <i>Paul's Boutique</i> were cleared, easily and affordably, something that [...] would be 'unthinkable' in today's litigious music industry."<sup id="cite_ref-samples_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-samples-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Mario "Mario C" Caldato, Jr., engineer on the album, later said in an interview that "after [Beastie Boys] did Paul's Boutique we realized we had spent a lot of money in the studio. We had spent about a $1/4 million in rights and licensing for samples."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> This type of sampling was only possible before <a href="/wiki/Grand_Upright_Music,_Ltd._v._Warner_Bros._Records_Inc." title="Grand Upright Music, Ltd. v. Warner Bros. Records Inc.">Grand Upright Music, Ltd. v. Warner Bros. Records Inc.</a>, the landmark <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">lawsuit</a> against <a href="/wiki/Biz_Markie" title="Biz Markie">Biz Markie</a> by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_O%27Sullivan" title="Gilbert O'Sullivan">Gilbert O'Sullivan</a>, which changed the process and future of hip hop sampling.</p>
<p>Speaking about the album 20 years on, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Yauch" title="Adam Yauch">Adam Yauch</a> told <i><a href="/wiki/Clash_(magazine)" title="Clash (magazine)">Clash</a></i> magazine,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The Dust Brothers had a bunch of music together, before we arrived to work with them. As a result, a lot of the tracks come from songs they'd planned to release to clubs as instrumentals – "Shake Your Rump," for example. They'd put together some beats, basslines and guitar lines, all these loops together, and they were quite surprised when we said we wanted to rhyme on it, because they thought it was too dense. They offered to strip it down to just beats, but we wanted all of that stuff on there. I think half of the tracks were written when we got there, and the other half we wrote together." <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>All of the songs for <i>Paul's Boutique</i> were recorded in Matt Dike's living room in Los Angeles,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> with the exception of "<a href="/wiki/Hello_Brooklyn" title="Hello Brooklyn" class="mw-redirect">Hello Brooklyn</a>". The fifth part of the album's finale <a href="/wiki/Suite_(music)" title="Suite (music)">suite</a> "<a href="/wiki/B-Boy_Bouillabaisse" title="B-Boy Bouillabaisse">B-Boy Bouillabaisse</a>" was recorded at the apartment building of the Beastie Boy-member Adam Yauch, aka MCA, in Koreatown, Los Angeles. The location of recording was credited in the album <a href="/wiki/Liner_notes" title="Liner notes">liner notes</a> as the Opium Den.<sup id="cite_ref-opium_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-opium-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The recordings for <i>Paul's Boutique</i> were later <a href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(recorded_music)" title="Audio mixing (recorded music)">mixed</a> by the Dust Brothers at <a href="/wiki/Record_Plant_Studios" title="Record Plant Studios" class="mw-redirect">Record Plant Studios</a> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-boutique_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boutique-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul%27s_Boutique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Upon its release, <i>Paul's Boutique</i> was alienated commercially for its experimental and dense <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampling</a> and lyricism, in contrast to the Beastie Boys' previous album, <i>Licensed to Ill</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> It was a commercial disappointment,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> peaking at only #24 on the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The album received a <a href="/wiki/Gold_record" title="Gold record" class="mw-redirect">gold</a> certification by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> on September 22 of its release year.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Paul's Boutique</i> would go on to sell over 2&#160;million copies by 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>In a contemporary review, David Handelman of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> gave the album four out of five stars and complimented its "loose, fun feel", writing that "are buoyed by the deft interplay of the three voices and a poetic tornado of imagery." Handelman found the musical samples "equally far-flung" and felt that the album is "littered with bullshit tough-guy bravado, but it's clever and hilarious bullshit".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In his review for <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy_(magazine)" title="Playboy (magazine)" class="mw-redirect">Playboy</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> felt that, although it "doesn't jump you the way great rap usually does," "the Beasties and <a href="/wiki/Tone_Loc" title="Tone Loc" class="mw-redirect">Tone-Loc</a>'s Dust Brothers have worked out a sound that sneaks up on you with its stark beats and literal-minded samples, sometimes in a disturbing way". Christgau commended them for "bearing down on the cleverest rhymes in the biz" and commented that "the Beasties concentrate on tall tales rather than boasting or dissing. In their irresponsible, exemplary way they make fun of drug misuse, racism, assault, and other real vices fools might accuse them of."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> He later gave it an "A" grade and asserted, "give it three plays and half a j's worth of concentration, and its high-speed volubility and riffs from nowhere will amaze and delight you ... an absolutely unpretentious and unsententious affirmation of cultural diversity, of where they came from and where they went from there.<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul%27s_Boutique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In retrospect, the album has also gone on to receive much critical acclaim and has been recognized as a landmark album in hip-hop.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> found the album to "still [be] an electrifying blast of cool" in a 1994 review, viewing it as a "younger incarnation" of <i><a href="/wiki/Ill_Communication" title="Ill Communication">Ill Communication</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-NME_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NME-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Mojo_(magazine)" title="Mojo (magazine)">Mojo</a></i> asserted that the album "shredded the rulebook" and called it "one of the most inventive rap albums ever made".<sup id="cite_ref-Mojo_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mojo-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> In a 2003 review, <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> gave it five stars and called it "a celebration of American junk culture that is still blowing minds today - even fourteen years of obsessive listening can't exhaust all the musical and lyrical jokes crammed into <i>Paul's Boutique</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-RS03_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RS03-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mark_Kemp" title="Mark Kemp">Mark Kemp</a> of <i>Rolling Stone</i> also gave the album five stars in a 2009 review, calling it a "hip-hop masterpiece".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Nate Patrin of <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> dubbed it "a landmark in the art of sampling, a reinvention of a group that looked like it was heading for a gimmicky early dead-end, and a harbinger of the pop-culture obsessions and referential touchstones that would come to define the ensuing decades' postmodern identity".<sup id="cite_ref-Patrin_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Patrin-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> In a review of the album for <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a>, contributor <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> summed the initial reaction to <i>Paul's Boutique</i> and praised the density that the album contains:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Musically, few hip-hop records have ever been so rich; it's not just the recontextulations of familiar music via samples, it's the flow of each song and the album as a whole, culminating in the widescreen suite that closes the record. Lyrically, the Beasties have never been better — not just because their jokes are razor-sharp, but because they construct full-bodied narratives and evocative portraits of characters and places. Few pop records offer this much to savor, and if <i>Paul's Boutique</i> only made a modest impact upon its initial release, over time its influence could be heard through pop and rap, yet no matter how its influence was felt, it stands alone as a record of stunning vision, maturity, and accomplishment.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Miles_Davis" title="Miles Davis">Miles Davis</a> said that he never got tired of listening to <i>Paul's Boutique</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Whalen_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whalen-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Later, in a <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">VIBE</a></i> interview of all three Beastie Boys, <a href="/wiki/Chuck_D" title="Chuck D">Chuck D</a> of <a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(band)" title="Public Enemy (band)" class="mw-redirect">Public Enemy</a> was quoted as saying that the "dirty secret" among the black hip-hop community at the time of release was that "Paul's Boutique had the best beats."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> During the same <i>VIBE</i> interview, <a href="/wiki/Mike_D" title="Mike D">Mike D</a> was asked about any possible hesitation he or the band might have had regarding their overt "sampling" of several minutes of well-known <a href="/wiki/Beatles" title="Beatles" class="mw-redirect">Beatles</a> background tracks, including the song "<a href="/wiki/The_End_(The_Beatles_song)" title="The End (The Beatles song)" class="mw-redirect">The End</a>" on "<a href="/wiki/The_Sounds_of_Science_(song)" title="The Sounds of Science (song)" class="mw-redirect">The Sounds of Science</a>". He claimed that the Beatles filed preliminary legal papers, and that his response was "What's cooler than getting sued by the Beatles?"<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
