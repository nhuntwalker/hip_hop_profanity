<p><i><b>The Black Album</b></i> is the eighth studio album by American rapper <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, released November 14, 2003, on <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a>. It was promoted as his final studio album, which serves as a recurring theme,<sup id="cite_ref-Tour.C3.A9_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tour.C3.A9-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> although Jay-Z returned to solo recording with <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Come_(Jay-Z_album)" title="Kingdom Come (Jay-Z album)">Kingdom Come</a></i> in 2006.</p>
<p>The album debuted at number 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 463,000 copies in its first week.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Black Album</i> has sold 3,516,000 original copies in the United States as of July 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It produced three singles that attained <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_charts" title="Billboard charts">Billboard</a></i> chart success, including <a href="/wiki/Hot_100" title="Hot 100" class="mw-redirect">Hot 100</a> top-ten hits "<a href="/wiki/Change_Clothes" title="Change Clothes">Change Clothes</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Dirt_Off_Your_Shoulder" title="Dirt Off Your Shoulder" class="mw-redirect">Dirt Off Your Shoulder</a>".</p>
<p>Upon its release, <i>The Black Album</i> received acclaim from music critics. It was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Grammy Award for Best Rap Album</a> at the <a href="/wiki/47th_Grammy_Awards" title="47th Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">47th Grammy Awards</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> ultimately losing to <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_College_Dropout" title="The College Dropout">The College Dropout</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was ranked #349 in <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone_Magazine" title="Rolling Stone Magazine" class="mw-redirect">Rolling Stone's</a> <a href="/wiki/500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">list of the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_promotion">Release and promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Black_Album_(Jay-Z_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Release and promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jay-Z said the album would have a different producer for each track, and early magazine advertisements listed a series of numbers (representing tracks) and a producer for each number. <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a> were originally supposed to be among these producers however did not make the final cut.<sup id="cite_ref-complex.com_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-complex.com-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The final album did feature a variety of producers, although <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella</a> producers <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> and <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a> produced two tracks each, in addition to the two produced by frequent Jay-Z collaborators <a href="/wiki/The_Neptunes" title="The Neptunes">The Neptunes</a>.</p>

<p><i>The Black Album</i> received acclaim from contemporary music critics; it holds an aggregate score of 84 out 100 at <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stone_Album_Guide" title="The Rolling Stone Album Guide">The Rolling Stone Album Guide</a></i> (2004), <i>The Black Album</i> is "old-school and utterly modern", as it showed Jay-Z "at the top of his game, able to reinvent himself as a rap classicist at the right time, as if to cement his place in hip-hop's legacy for generations to come".<sup id="cite_ref-Hoard_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoard-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Steve Jones of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> stated, "He enlisted beats from an all-star cast of producers, who come through with top-shelf work. But it's Jigga's trademark lyrical dexterity and diversified deliveries that put him on a level all his own."<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Dave Simpson of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> was less enthusiastic and dismissed the music as "an aural equivalent of that old American favourite, the schmaltzy biopic."<sup id="cite_ref-Simpson_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In his consumer guide for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave the album a three-star honorable mention,<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau1-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> indicating "an enjoyable effort consumers attuned to its overriding aesthetic or individual vision may well treasure."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> He cited "99 Problems" and "My 1st Song" as highlights and quipped, "Raps like a legend in his own time—namely, <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis</a> in Vegas".<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau1_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau1-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In a retrospective review, Christgau gave the album an "A", noted how each track benefits from different producers, and wrote of its legacy:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>History has vindicated this album. On a meticulously hyped valedictory no one believed would be his actual farewell, the fanfares, ovations, maternal reminiscences, and <a href="/wiki/Vamp_(music)" title="Vamp (music)" class="mw-redirect">vamp</a>-till-ready <a href="/wiki/Shout_out" title="Shout out" class="mw-redirect">shout-outs</a> were overblown at best. But on an album where the biggest rapper of all time announces that he's the biggest rapper of all time, they're prophetic. Bitch about <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Come_(Jay-Z_album)" title="Kingdom Come (Jay-Z album)">Kingdom Come</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/American_Gangster_(album)" title="American Gangster (album)">American Gangster</a></i> if you must, but not <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blueprint_3" title="The Blueprint 3">The Blueprint 3</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Watch_the_Throne" title="Watch the Throne">Watch the Throne</a></i>, and not his label presidency, amassed fortune, or close personal relationship with <a href="/wiki/Warren_Buffett" title="Warren Buffett">Warren Buffett</a>. He's got a right to celebrate his autobiography in rhyme because he's on track to become a personage who dwarfs any mere rapper, and not only can he hire the best help dark green can buy, he can make it sing.<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau2-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Black_Album_(Jay-Z_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> ranked <i>The Black Album</i> at number 90 on its list of the top 200 albums of the 2000s,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a> ranked it number 7 on its list of the Top 100 Albums of the 2000s.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i>, the album is Jay-Z's top selling album of the 2000s and the 136th highest selling album of the decade in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012 <a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a> named the album one of the classic albums of the last decade.<sup id="cite_ref-complex.com_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-complex.com-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
