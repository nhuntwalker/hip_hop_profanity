<p><i><b>The Slim Shady LP</b></i> is the second <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> and first major release from the American rapper <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>. It was released on February 23, 1999, under <a href="/wiki/Interscope_Records" title="Interscope Records">Interscope Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Aftermath_Entertainment" title="Aftermath Entertainment">Aftermath Entertainment</a>. Recorded in <a href="/wiki/Ferndale,_Michigan" title="Ferndale, Michigan">Ferndale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> following Eminem's recruitment by Dr. Dre and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Iovine" title="Jimmy Iovine">Jimmy Iovine</a>, the album features production from Dr. Dre, the <a href="/wiki/Bass_Brothers" title="Bass Brothers">Bass Brothers</a>, and Eminem himself. The majority of the record's lyrical content is written from the perspective of the rapper's <a href="/wiki/Alter_ego" title="Alter ego">alter ego</a> Slim Shady, whom the rapper created on <i><a href="/wiki/Slim_Shady_EP" title="Slim Shady EP">Slim Shady EP</a></i> (1997). The lyrics are noted for their over-the-top depictions of violence and heavy use of profanity.</p>
<p>The album was met with both critical and commercial success; critics praised Eminem for his unique lyrical style, and the record debuted at number two on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart behind <a href="/wiki/TLC_(band)" title="TLC (band)" class="mw-redirect">TLC</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/FanMail" title="FanMail">FanMail</a></i> with 283,000 copies sold in its opening week. The first official single, "<a href="/wiki/My_Name_Is" title="My Name Is">My Name Is</a>", peaked at number 36 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100. <i>The Slim Shady LP</i> went on to be certified quadruple-platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA). The record won a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards" title="Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">Grammy Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Album" title="Best Rap Album" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Album</a>, and in 2003, the album was ranked number 273 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of <a href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>.</p>
<p><i>The Slim Shady LP</i> turned Eminem from an unknown rapper into a high-profile celebrity. <a href="/wiki/Interscope_Records" title="Interscope Records">Interscope Records</a> awarded him with his own record label, <a href="/wiki/Shady_Records" title="Shady Records">Shady Records</a>, and he embarked on an extensive touring schedule to promote the album. In the summer of 1999, the rapper frequently performed on the <a href="/wiki/Vans_Warped_Tour" title="Vans Warped Tour" class="mw-redirect">Vans Warped Tour</a> and in hip-hop clubs. He also became a highly controversial figure due to his lyrical content, which some perceived to be <a href="/wiki/Misogyny" title="Misogyny">misogynistic</a> and a negative influence on American youth. The rapper was also sued multiple times following the release of the album for reasons including slander and unauthorized <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampling</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Slim_Shady_LP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eminem, born Marshall Bruce Mathers III, began rapping at age fourteen. In 1996, his debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Infinite_(Eminem_album)" title="Infinite (Eminem album)">Infinite</a></i>, which was recorded at the Bassmint, a recording studio owned by the <a href="/wiki/Bass_Brothers" title="Bass Brothers">Bass Brothers</a>, was released under their independent label <a href="/wiki/Web_Entertainment" title="Web Entertainment">Web Entertainment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Infinite</i> achieved little commercial success and was largely ignored by Detroit radio stations. The disappointment from this experience greatly influenced his lyrical style: "After that record, every rhyme I wrote got angrier and angrier. A lot of it was because of the feedback I got. Motherfuckers was like, 'You're a white boy, what the fuck are you rapping for? Why don't you go into rock &amp; roll?' All that type of shit started pissing me off."<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> After the release of <i>Infinite</i>, Eminem's personal struggles and abuse of drugs and alcohol culminated in an unsuccessful suicide attempt.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusicbio_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicbio-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The disappointment of <i>Infinite</i> inspired Eminem to create the <a href="/wiki/Alter_ego" title="Alter ego">alter ego</a> Slim Shady: "Boom, the name hit me, and right away I thought of all these words to rhyme with it".<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Slim Shady served as Eminem's vent for his frustrations, and in the spring of 1997, he recorded the eight-song extended play <i><a href="/wiki/Slim_Shady_EP" title="Slim Shady EP">Slim Shady EP</a></i>. During this time, Eminem and his girlfriend Kim Scott lived in a high-crime neighborhood with their newborn daughter Hailie, where their house was burglarized numerous times.<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> After being evicted from his home, Eminem traveled to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> to participate in the Rap Olympics, an annual nationwide <a href="/wiki/Rap_battle" title="Rap battle" class="mw-redirect">rap battle</a> competition. He placed second, and the staff at <a href="/wiki/Interscope_Records" title="Interscope Records">Interscope Records</a> who attended the Rap Olympics sent a copy of the <i>Slim Shady EP</i> to company CEO <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Iovine" title="Jimmy Iovine">Jimmy Iovine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Iovine played the tape for record producer <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/Aftermath_Entertainment" title="Aftermath Entertainment">Aftermath Entertainment</a>. Dr. Dre recalled, "In my entire career in the music industry, I have never found anything from a demo tape or a CD. When Jimmy played this, I said, 'Find him. Now.'"<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Eminem and Dr. Dre subsequently began work on his major label debut album.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Slim_Shady_LP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dr._Dre.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Dr._Dre.jpg/190px-Dr._Dre.jpg" width="190" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Dr._Dre.jpg/285px-Dr._Dre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Dr._Dre.jpg/380px-Dr._Dre.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="594" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dr._Dre.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.24wmf6/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Eminem had idolized <i>The Slim Shady LP</i> co-producer Dr. Dre (pictured in 2008) since he was a child.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>The Slim Shady LP</i> was recorded at Studio 8 at 430 <a href="/wiki/M-102_(Michigan_highway)" title="M-102 (Michigan highway)">8 Mile Road</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ferndale,_Michigan" title="Ferndale, Michigan">Ferndale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Studiowhere_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Studiowhere-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Eminem, who had idolized Dr. Dre since listening to his group <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a> as a teenager, was nervous to work with him on the album: "I didn't want to be starstruck or kiss his ass too much&#160;... I'm just a little white boy from Detroit. I had never seen stars, let alone Dr. Dre."<sup id="cite_ref-Bozza24_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bozza24-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> However, Eminem became more comfortable working with Dr. Dre after a series of highly productive recording sessions.<sup id="cite_ref-Stubbs58_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stubbs58-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The recording process generally began with Dr. Dre creating a beat and Eminem using the tracks as a template for his <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_rap" title="Freestyle rap">freestyle raps</a>; "Every beat he would make, I had a rhyme for", Eminem recalled.<sup id="cite_ref-Stubbs58_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stubbs58-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> He later said: "Every time I sat down with a pen, everything was just like: fuck you, fuck this, fuck them, fuck that, fuck the world, fuck what everybody thinks. Fuck them." Much of the album was recorded when Eminem was under the influence of <a href="/wiki/MDMA" title="MDMA">ecstasy</a>; Dr. Dre described the experience by saying "We get in there, get bugged out, stay in the studio for fuckin' two days. Then you're dead for three days. Then you wake up, pop the tape in, like, 'Let me see what I've done.'"<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> On the first day of recording, Eminem and Dr. Dre finished "<a href="/wiki/My_Name_Is" title="My Name Is">My Name Is</a>" in an hour.<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Three other songs, including "Role Model", were also recorded that day.<sup id="cite_ref-Bozza24_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bozza24-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/%2797_Bonnie_%26_Clyde" title="'97 Bonnie &amp; Clyde">'97 Bonnie &amp; Clyde</a>", which was formerly featured on the <i>Slim Shady EP</i> as "Just the Two of Us", was rerecorded for <i>The Slim Shady LP</i> to feature his daughter Hailie's vocals. Because the song focuses on disposing of his girlfriend's corpse, Eminem was uncomfortable with explaining the situation to Kim, and instead told her that he would be taking Hailie to <a href="/wiki/Chuck_E._Cheese%27s" title="Chuck E. Cheese's">Chuck E. Cheese's</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> He explained, "When she found out I used our daughter to write a song about killing her, she fucking blew. We had just got back together for a couple of weeks. Then I played her the song, and she bugged the fuck out."<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The song "<a href="/wiki/Guilty_Conscience" title="Guilty Conscience">Guilty Conscience</a>" contains a humorous reference to an occasion in which Dr. Dre assaulted rapper <a href="/wiki/Dee_Barnes" title="Dee Barnes">Dee Barnes</a>. Having only known Dr. Dre for a few days, Eminem was anxious about how he would react to such a line, and to his relief, Dr. Dre "fell out of his chair laughing" upon hearing the lyric.<sup id="cite_ref-Stubbs68_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stubbs68-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> "Ken Kaniff", a skit involving a prank call to Eminem, featured fellow Detroit rapper Aristotle. After a falling out between the two in the wake of Eminem's breakthrough success, Eminem instead played Ken Kaniff on skits on future albums.<sup id="cite_ref-Bozza43_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bozza43-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Slim_Shady_LP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">Lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Slim_Shady_LP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many of the songs from <i>The Slim Shady LP</i> are written from the perspective of Eminem's alter ego, Slim Shady, and contain cartoonish and over-the-top depictions of violence, which he refers to as "made-up tales of trailer-park stuff".<sup id="cite_ref-Bite_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bite-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The rapper explained that this subject matter is intended for entertainment value, likening his music to the <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">horror film</a> genre: "Why can't people see that records can be like movies? The only difference between some of my raps and movies is that they aren't on a screen."<sup id="cite_ref-Noshame.3F_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noshame.3F-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Some of the lyrics have also been considered to be <a href="/wiki/Misogyny" title="Misogyny">misogynistic</a> by critics and commentators.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardiancover_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardiancover-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Eminem acknowledged the accusations, and clarified, "I have a fairly salty relationship with women&#160;... But most of the time, when I'm saying shit about women, when I'm saying 'bitches' and 'hoes', its so ridiculous that I'm taking the stereotypical rapper to the extreme. I don't hate women in general. They just make me mad sometimes.'"<sup id="cite_ref-Guardiancover_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardiancover-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite the record's explicit nature, Eminem refused to say the word "<a href="/wiki/Nigga" title="Nigga">nigga</a>" on the album, with the rapper noting, "It's not in my vocabulary."<sup id="cite_ref-Guardiancover_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardiancover-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Slim Shady LP</i> begins with a "Public Service Announcement" introduction performed by producer Jeff Bass of the Bass Brothers, and serves as a sarcastic <a href="/wiki/Disclaimer" title="Disclaimer">disclaimer</a> discussing the album's explicit lyrical content.<sup id="cite_ref-Bestskit_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bestskit-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Later in the album, a skit entitled "Paul" features a phone call from <a href="/wiki/Paul_Rosenberg_(music_manager)" title="Paul Rosenberg (music manager)">Paul Rosenberg</a> to Eminem telling him to "tone down" his lyrics.<sup id="cite_ref-Hasted111_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted111-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"Guilty Conscience" is a concept song featuring Dr. Dre. The song focuses on a series of characters who are faced with various situations, while Dr. Dre and Eminem serve as the "angel" and "devil" sides of the characters' conscience, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Newsroom_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsroom-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The song draws inspiration from a scene in the 1978 comedy film <i><a href="/wiki/National_Lampoon%27s_Animal_House" title="National Lampoon's Animal House" class="mw-redirect">National Lampoon's Animal House</a></i>, in which a man takes advice from an angel and devil on his shoulder while considering raping an unconscious girl at a party.<sup id="cite_ref-Noshame.3F_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noshame.3F-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> In the film, he ends up deciding not to go through with the rape, but in "Guilty Conscience", the outcome is unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-Noshame.3F_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noshame.3F-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> On "My Fault", Eminem tells the story of a girl who overdoses on <a href="/wiki/Psilocybin_mushrooms" title="Psilocybin mushrooms" class="mw-redirect">psychedelic mushrooms</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Rave" title="Rave">rave</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bozza49_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bozza49-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/%2797_Bonnie_%26_Clyde" title="'97 Bonnie &amp; Clyde">'97 Bonnie &amp; Clyde</a>" features Eminem convincing his infant daughter to assist him in disposing of his wife's corpse. It is an <a href="/wiki/Epilogue" title="Epilogue">epilogue</a> to the song "<a href="/wiki/Kim_(Eminem_song)" title="Kim (Eminem song)" class="mw-redirect">Kim</a>", although "'97 Bonnie &amp; Clyde" was released first. Eminem wrote the song at a time in which he felt that his girlfriend was keeping him from seeing his daughter.<sup id="cite_ref-Noshame.3F_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noshame.3F-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Stephen Thomas Erlewine of Allmusic explained that "There have been more violent songs in rap, but few more disturbing, and it's not because of what it describes, it's how he describes it -- how the perfectly modulated phrasing enhances the horror and black humor of his words."<sup id="cite_ref-AllmusicReview_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllmusicReview-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> On the song "Brain Damage", Eminem discusses his childhood experiences with bullies at school.<sup id="cite_ref-AllegedBully_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllegedBully-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although many of the lyrics on the album are intended to be humorous, several songs depict Eminem's frustrations with living in poverty. When discussing <i>The Slim Shady LP</i>, Anthony Bozza of <i>Rolling Stone</i> described Eminem as "probably the only MC in 1999 who boasts low self-esteem. His rhymes are jaw-droppingly perverse, bespeaking a minimum-wage life devoid of hope, flushed with rage and weaned on sci-fi and slasher flicks."<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Eminem was inspired to write "Rock Bottom" after being fired from his cooking job at a restaurant days before his daughter's birthday.<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The song bemoans human dependency on money, discussing its ability to brainwash an individual.<sup id="cite_ref-Stubbs81_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stubbs81-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> He illustrates his struggles to provide for his daughter, describing himself as "discouraged, hungry, and malnourished."<sup id="cite_ref-Stubbs81_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stubbs81-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> "If I Had" follows a similar theme, as he describes living on minimum wage and remarks that he is "tired of jobs starting off at $5.50 an hour".<sup id="cite_ref-Hartigan162_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hartigan162-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In the song, he expresses his irritation with fitting the "white trash" stereotype.<sup id="cite_ref-Hartigan161_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hartigan161-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The album's production was handled primarily by the <a href="/wiki/Bass_Brothers" title="Bass Brothers">Bass Brothers</a>, Dr. Dre, and Eminem.<sup id="cite_ref-Newsroom_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsroom-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AllmusicReview_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllmusicReview-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The beats have been compared to <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast hip hop</a> and <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a> musical styles.<sup id="cite_ref-RSReview_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSReview-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Kyle Anderson of <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> wrote that "The beats are full of bass-heavy hallucinations and create huge, scary sandboxes that allow Em to play."<sup id="cite_ref-Newsroom_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsroom-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> According to the staff at <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a>, "'97 Bonnie &amp; Clyde" is backed by the "lulling serenity of a super silky groove".<sup id="cite_ref-IGNReview_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGNReview-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> "Cum On Everybody" contains an upbeat dance rhythm, while "My Name Is", which is built around a piano sample from British musician <a href="/wiki/Labi_Siffre" title="Labi Siffre">Labi Siffre</a>'s "I Got The", features a prominent <a href="/wiki/Bassline" title="Bassline">bassline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_music" title="Psychedelic music">psychedelic</a>-style keyboards.<sup id="cite_ref-Newsroom_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsroom-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IGNReview_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGNReview-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bozza25_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bozza25-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> "I'm Shady" was originally written over a <a href="/wiki/Sade_(band)" title="Sade (band)">Sade</a> track, but after hearing a sample of <a href="/wiki/Curtis_Mayfield" title="Curtis Mayfield">Curtis Mayfield</a>'s "Pusherman" in <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/I%27m_Your_Pusher" title="I'm Your Pusher">I'm Your Pusher</a>", Eminem decided it would be more fitting to use "Pusherman".<sup id="cite_ref-Stubbs84_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stubbs84-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Eminem's vocal inflection on the record has been described as a "nasal whine"; Jon Pareles of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> likened his "calmly sarcastic delivery" to "the early <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">Beastie Boys</a> turned cynical".<sup id="cite_ref-NYtimesrev_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYtimesrev-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Writing for the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i>, columnist Greg Kot compared the rapper's vocals to "<a href="/wiki/Pee-wee_Herman" title="Pee-wee Herman">Pee-wee Herman</a> with a nasal Midwestern accent".<sup id="cite_ref-ctrev_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctrev-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> A skit entitled "Lounge" appears before "My Fault" featuring Eminem and the Bass Brothers imitating <a href="/wiki/Rat_pack" title="Rat pack" class="mw-redirect">rat pack</a> crooners. Jeff Bass came up with the line "I never meant to give you mushrooms" for the skit, which in turn inspired Eminem to write "My Fault".<sup id="cite_ref-Stubbs75_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stubbs75-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Slim_Shady_LP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_response">Commercial response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Slim_Shady_LP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the album's first week of release, <i>The Slim Shady LP</i> sold 283,000 copies, debuting at number two on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart behind <a href="/wiki/TLC_(band)" title="TLC (band)" class="mw-redirect">TLC</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/FanMail" title="FanMail">FanMail</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Charts.3F_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charts.3F-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The record remained on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 for 100 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-BillboardChartHistory_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BillboardChartHistory-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> It also reached number one on the R&amp;B/Hip Hop Albums chart, staying on the chart for 92 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-BillboardChartHistory_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BillboardChartHistory-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> On April 5, 1999, ten days after the album's release, <i>The Slim Shady LP</i> was certified platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> for sales of over one million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_cert_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA_cert-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> On November 15, 2000, the record was certified quadruple platinum by the RIAA.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_cert_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA_cert-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> "My Name Is", the album's lead single, peaked at number 36 on the <i>Billboard</i> <a href="/wiki/Hot_100" title="Hot 100" class="mw-redirect">Hot 100</a>, remaining on the chart for ten weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-Mynameisbb_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mynameisbb-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> The single additionally peaked at number 18 on the magazine's R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs chart, 29 on the Pop Songs chart, and 37 on the Alternative Songs chart.<sup id="cite_ref-Mynameisbb_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mynameisbb-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> "Guilty Conscience" reached number 56 on the <i>Billboard</i> R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs chart, while "Just Don't Give a Fuck" peaked at number 62 on the chart.<sup id="cite_ref-GCbb_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GCbb-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-justdontbb_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-justdontbb-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> By November, 2013, the album sold 5,437,000 copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Outside of the United States, <i>The Slim Shady LP</i> reached number nine on the weekly <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Albums_Chart" title="Canadian Albums Chart">Canadian Albums Chart</a> and remained on the chart for twelve weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-BillboardChartHistory_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BillboardChartHistory-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, the album was certified double platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Recording_Industry_Association" title="Canadian Recording Industry Association" class="mw-redirect">Canadian Recording Industry Association</a> for shipments of over 200,000 units.<sup id="cite_ref-CanadianCert_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CanadianCert-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The record was also certified double platinum in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, where it peaked at number ten on the UK Albums chart and remained on the chart for a total of 114 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-UKcert_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UKcert-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> In Australia, the album peaked at number 49 on the <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Charts" title="ARIA Charts">ARIA Chart</a>, and was eventually certified platinum in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-aus_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aussiecert_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aussiecert-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was also certified gold in the Netherlands and New Zealand, where it peaked at the number 20 and 23 chart positions, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-dutchpeak_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dutchpeak-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NZpeak_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NZpeak-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dutchgold_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dutchgold-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The album was met with generally positive reviews, with many critics focusing on Eminem's lyrical style. Stephen Thomas Erlewine of <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> gave the album five stars out of five, praising the rapper's "expansive vocabulary and vivid imagination", adding that "Years later, as the shock has faded, it's those lyrical skills and the subtle mastery of the music that still resonate, and they're what make <i>The Slim Shady LP</i> one of the great debuts in both hip-hop and modern pop music."<sup id="cite_ref-AllmusicReview_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllmusicReview-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> David Browne of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> described the album's "unapologetic outrageousness" as a reaction to the "soul positivity" of <a href="/wiki/Conscious_hip_hop" title="Conscious hip hop" class="mw-redirect">conscious hip hop</a>, noting that "<i>The Slim Shady LP</i> marks the return of irreverent, wiseass attitude to the genre, heard throughout the album in its nonstop barrage of crudely funny rhymes&#160;... Even pop fans deadened to graphic lyrics are likely to flinch."<sup id="cite_ref-EWeekly_Review_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EWeekly_Review-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Soren Baker of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> gave the album three and a half stars out of four and stated that "He isn't afraid to say anything; his lyrics are so clever that he makes murder sound as if it's a funny act he may indulge in simply to pass the time" but lamented the "sometimes flat production that takes away from the power of Eminem's verbal mayhem."<sup id="cite_ref-LATimes_Review_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes_Review-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Many reviewers commented on the album's lyrical content. Gilbert Rodman of <i><a href="/wiki/Popular_Communications" title="Popular Communications">Popular Communications</a></i> states, "Eminem's music contains more than its fair share of misogynistic and homo-phobic lyrics, but simply to reduce it to these (as many critics do) doesn't help to explain Eminem. It merely invokes a platitude or a sound bite to explain him away."<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Rob Sheffield of <i>Rolling Stone</i> enjoyed the record's comedic nature, writing "Simply put: Eminem will crack you up", but also felt that the misogynistic lyrics grow tiresome, noting that "the wife-killing jokes of "97 Bonnie and Clyde' aren't any funnier than <a href="/wiki/Garth_Brooks" title="Garth Brooks">Garth Brooks</a>', and 'My Fault' belongs on some sorry-ass <a href="/wiki/Bloodhound_Gang" title="Bloodhound Gang">Bloodhound Gang</a> record."<sup id="cite_ref-RSReview_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSReview-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Nathan Rabin of <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i> felt that although the album is "sophomoric and uninspired" at times, Eminem's "surreal, ultraviolent, trailer-trash/post-gangsta-rap extremism is at least a breath of fresh air in a rap world that's despairingly low on new ideas."<sup id="cite_ref-Rabin_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rabin-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> Mike Rubin of <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> noted that "his scenarios are so far-fetched the songs almost never sound as ugly as they actually are."<sup id="cite_ref-Spinreview_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spinreview-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> Chris Dafoe of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Globe_and_Mail" title="The Globe and Mail">The Globe and Mail</a></i> opined that "Abused by fellow students and teachers, cheated on by his girlfriend, despised by society, Shady goes over the top now and then - or rather way over the top - but Dre's lean production, full of strange voice and comic interjections, hold things together."<sup id="cite_ref-Theglobereview_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Theglobereview-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Slim_Shady_LP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Rolling Stone</i> ranked <i>The Slim Shady LP</i> number 273 on the magazine's list of "The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time" and 33 on its list of the "100 Best Albums of the '90s".<sup id="cite_ref-273rd_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-273rd-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33rd_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33rd-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/42nd_Grammy_Awards" title="42nd Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">42nd Grammy Awards</a> in 1999, the record won <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Best Rap Album</a>, while "My Name Is" won <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance">Best Rap Solo Performance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSbio_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSbio-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> "Ken Kaniff" was listed as number 15 on <i><a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a>'</i>s "50 Greatest Hip-Hop Skits" list, while the "Public Service Announcement" introduction to the album, along with the "Public Service Announcement 2000" introduction from <i>The Marshall Mathers LP</i>, was listed as number 50 on the list.<sup id="cite_ref-Bestskit_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bestskit-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BestskitKK_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BestskitKK-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> It also won Outstanding National Album at the 2000 Detroit Music Awards.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>After the success of <i>The Slim Shady LP</i>, Eminem went from an unknown rapper to a high-profile celebrity. Interscope Records awarded him with his own record label, <a href="/wiki/Shady_Records" title="Shady Records">Shady Records</a>; the first artist Eminem signed was rapper <a href="/wiki/Proof_(rapper)" title="Proof (rapper)">Proof</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hasted_123_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted_123-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> Eminem, who had previously struggled to provide for his daughter, noted a drastic change in his lifestyle: "This last Christmas, there were so many fucking presents under the tree&#160;... My daughter wasn't born with a silver spoon in her mouth. But she's got one now. I can't stop myself from spoiling her."<sup id="cite_ref-Hasted_123_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted_123-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>To promote <i>The Slim Shady LP</i>, Eminem embarked on an extensive tour schedule. He joined the <a href="/wiki/Vans_Warped_Tour" title="Vans Warped Tour" class="mw-redirect">Vans Warped Tour</a> as a last-minute replacement for <a href="/wiki/Cypress_Hill" title="Cypress Hill">Cypress Hill</a>, a schedule that included 31 North American dates from June 25 to July 31, beginning in San Antonio and ending in Miami.<sup id="cite_ref-Huxley79_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huxley79-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> He often played a show in the afternoon on the Warped Tour, and then drove to another location to perform at a hip hop club at night.<sup id="cite_ref-Hasted_123_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted_123-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> During a performance in Hartford, Connecticut near the end of the Warped Tour, Eminem slipped on a puddle of liquid and fell ten feet down off the stage, cracking several ribs.<sup id="cite_ref-Huxley79_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huxley79-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hasted_125_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted_125-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> He recalled that the stress of his newfound fame led him to drink excessively, and reflected, "I knew I had to slow it down. The fall was like a reminder."<sup id="cite_ref-Hasted_125_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted_125-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> However, after receiving medical attention, he was well enough to travel to New York the following day for a performance on <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Request_Live" title="Total Request Live">Total Request Live</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Huxley79_60-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huxley79-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Eminem also became a highly controversial figure due to his lyrical content. He was labeled as "misogynist, a nihilist and an advocate of domestic violence", and in an editorial by <i>Billboard</i> editor in chief <a href="/wiki/Timothy_White_(editor)" title="Timothy White (editor)">Timothy White</a>, the writer accused Eminem of "making money by exploiting the world's misery."<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> During a radio appearance in San Francisco, Eminem reportedly angered local DJ Sista Tamu due to a <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_rap" title="Freestyle rap">freestyle</a> about "slapping a pregnant bitch" to the extent that she broke a copy of <i>The Slim Shady LP</i> on-air.<sup id="cite_ref-Hasted_125_61-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted_125-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> The rapper defended himself by saying, "My album isn't for younger kids to hear. It has an advisory sticker, and you must be eighteen to get it. That doesn't mean younger kids won't get it, but I'm not responsible for every kid out there. I'm not a role model, and I don't claim to be."<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits">Lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Slim_Shady_LP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lawsuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On September 17, 1999, Eminem's mother, Deborah Nelson, filed a $10 million lawsuit against him for <a href="/wiki/Slander" title="Slander" class="mw-redirect">slander</a> based on his claim that she uses drugs in the line "I just found out my mom does more dope than I do" from "My Name Is".<sup id="cite_ref-Bozza69_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bozza69-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Thereal_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thereal-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> After a two-year long trial, she was awarded $25,000, of which she received $1,600 after legal fees.<sup id="cite_ref-Bozza69_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bozza69-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> Eminem was not surprised that his mother had filed the lawsuit against him, referring to her as a "lawsuit queen", and alleging that "That's how she makes money. When I was five, she had a job on the cash register at a store that sold chips and soda. Other than that, I don't remember her working a day in her life."<sup id="cite_ref-Thereal_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thereal-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> She later filed another lawsuit against him for emotional damages suffered during the first trial, which was later dismissed.<sup id="cite_ref-Bozza69_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bozza69-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2001, DeAngelo Bailey, a janitor living in <a href="/wiki/Roseville,_Michigan" title="Roseville, Michigan">Roseville, Michigan</a> who was made the subject of the song "Brain Damage" in which he is portrayed as a school bully, filed a $1 million lawsuit against Eminem for slander and <a href="/wiki/Expectation_of_privacy" title="Expectation of privacy">invasion of privacy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AllegedBully_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllegedBully-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Bailey's attorney stated "Eminem is a Caucasian male who faced criticism within the music industry that he had not suffered through difficult circumstances growing up and he was therefore a 'pretender' in the industry&#160;... Eminem used Bailey, his African-American childhood schoolmate, as a pawn in his effort to stem the tide of criticism."<sup id="cite_ref-AllegedBully_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllegedBully-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1982, Eminem's mother unsuccessfully sued the Roseville school district for not protecting her son, as she claimed that attacks from bullies caused him headaches, nausea, and antisocial behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-AllegedBully_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllegedBully-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, Bailey had previously admitted to bullying Eminem in the April 1999 issue of <i>Rolling Stone</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BlowsUp_2-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlowsUp-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The lawsuit was later dismissed by a judge, who, in her explanation, wrote a humorous rap verse: "Bailey thinks he's entitled to some monetary gain/Because Eminem used his name in vain."<sup id="cite_ref-victim_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-victim-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2003, 70-year-old widow Harlene Stein filed suit against Eminem and Dr. Dre on the grounds that "Guilty Conscience" contains an unauthorized sample of "Pigs Go Home" composed for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Getting_Straight" title="Getting Straight">Getting Straight</a></i> by her husband, <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Stein" title="Ronald Stein">Ronald Stein</a>, who died in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-OldLady_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OldLady-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> Although the album's liner notes state that the song contains an "interpolation" of "Pigs Go Home", Stein is not credited as a composer and his wife was not paid royalties for use of the song.<sup id="cite_ref-OldLady_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OldLady-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> The lawsuit requested for 5 percent of the retail list price of 90 percent of the all copies of the record sold in America, and 2.5 percent of the retail price of 90 percent of the copies of the album sold internationally.<sup id="cite_ref-OldLady_65-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OldLady-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<li>"<a href="/wiki/Bad_Meets_Evil_(song)" title="Bad Meets Evil (song)">Bad Meets Evil</a>" contains a sample of "Me &amp; My Girlfriend" by <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">2Pac</a> and "Modaji" by <a href="/wiki/Dave_Grusin" title="Dave Grusin">Dave Grusin</a></li>
<li>"Rock Bottom" contains a sample of "Summertime" by Big Brother &amp; The Holding Company (with <a href="/wiki/Janis_Joplin" title="Janis Joplin">Janis Joplin</a>)</li>
<li>"I'm Shady" is based on "<a href="/wiki/Super_Fly_(soundtrack)" title="Super Fly (soundtrack)">Pusherman</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Curtis_Mayfield" title="Curtis Mayfield">Curtis Mayfield</a> and "I'm Your Pusher" by <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Notes</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>"Bitch", "Cum On Everybody", "Just Don't Give a Fuck" and "Still Don't Give a Fuck" are retitled "Zoe", "Come on Everybody", "Just Don't Give" and "Still Don't Give" on the clean version of the album.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>"If I Had" loosely references the melody to the chorus of "If I Had $1,000,000" by <a href="/wiki/Barenaked_Ladies" title="Barenaked Ladies">Barenaked Ladies</a></li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/%2797_Bonnie_%26_Clyde" title="'97 Bonnie &amp; Clyde">'97 Bonnie &amp; Clyde</a>" is a merged version of <i>Slim Shady EP</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s "Mommy (skit)" and "Just the Two of Us".</li>
</ul>
