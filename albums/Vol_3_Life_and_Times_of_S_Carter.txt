<p><i><b>Vol. 3… Life and Times of S. Carter</b></i> is the fourth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American rapper <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, released on December 28, 1999, on <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a>. It exhibits a return to the street-oriented sound of his first album, <i><a href="/wiki/Reasonable_Doubt" title="Reasonable Doubt" class="mw-redirect">Reasonable Doubt</a></i> (1996).<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Production for the album was handled by several hip hop producers, including <a href="/wiki/Swizz_Beatz" title="Swizz Beatz">Swizz Beatz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timbaland" title="Timbaland">Timbaland</a>, <a href="/wiki/K-Rob" title="K-Rob">K-Rob</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rockwilder" title="Rockwilder">Rockwilder</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Clue" title="DJ Clue" class="mw-redirect">DJ Clue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irv_Gotti" title="Irv Gotti">Irv Gotti</a>.</p>
<p>The album debuted at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 462,000 copies in its first week. It was a significant commercial success, shipping two million copies within its first month of release. Upon its release, <i>Vol. 3… Life and Times of S. Carter</i> received positive reviews from most music critics. On February 14, 2001, the album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">triple platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>, for shipments of three million copies in the United States.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vol._3..._Life_and_Times_of_S._Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It debuted at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, selling 462,000 copies in its first week,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> which is thirty-percent more than the first-week sales of Jay-Z previous album <i><a href="/wiki/Vol._2..._Hard_Knock_Life" title="Vol. 2... Hard Knock Life">Vol. 2... Hard Knock Life</a></i> (1998).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Vol. 3… Life and Times of S. Carter</i> peaked at number one on <i>Billboard</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-charts_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charts-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The album shipped two million copies in its first month of release.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> On February 14, 2001, it was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">triple platinum</a> in sales by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), for shipments of three million copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vol._3..._Life_and_Times_of_S._Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_reaction">Initial reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vol._3..._Life_and_Times_of_S._Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Initial reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon its release, <i>Vol. 3... Life and Times of S. Carter</i> received positive reviews from <a href="/wiki/Music_criticism" title="Music criticism">music critics</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> writer <a href="/wiki/Greg_Kot" title="Greg Kot">Greg Kot</a> gave it three out of four stars and stated, "Jay-Z's level of self-awareness is utterly in tune with his generation's; he's not an artist so much as a multimedia-savvy product manager, in tune with how the game is played".<sup id="cite_ref-Kot_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kot-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Richard Harrington of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> commented that the album "is full of reputation-building swagger, cataloguing of lyrical skills and autobiographical perspective".<sup id="cite_ref-Harrington_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrington-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Anthony_DeCurtis" title="Anthony DeCurtis">Anthony DeCurtis</a> viewed that the album "reconnect[s]" with Jay-Z's urban demographic, "with flair".<sup id="cite_ref-DeCurtis_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DeCurtis-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Steve Jones of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> wrote that Jay-Z's "lyrics and sense of timing are as razor-sharp as ever".<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Miles_Marshall_Lewis" title="Miles Marshall Lewis">Miles Marshall Lewis</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i> praised its production quality and lyrics, stating "Jay-Z is the best MC in hip hop. <i>Vol. 3… Life and Times of S. Carter</i> [...] is the quintessential 2000-model hip hop album, and Shawn Carter is the finest MC the form currently has to offer".<sup id="cite_ref-Lewis_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In his consumer guide for <i>The Village Voice</i>, critic <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave it an A rating.<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Christgau stated, "he goes for a rugged, expansive vigor, nailing both come-fly-with-me cosmopolitanism and the hunger for excitement that's turned gangster hangouts into musical hotbeds from Buenos Aires to Kansas City".<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> writer Kris Ex found that Jay-Z "has become a better architect of songs" and dubbed <i>Vol. 3...</i> "his strongest album to date, with music that's filled with catchy hooks, rump-shaking beats and lyrics fueled by Jay's hustler's vigilance".<sup id="cite_ref-Ex_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ex-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In a mixed review, <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> writer Soren Baker gave it two-and-a-half out of four stars and viewed that it lacks the "biting humor and spectacular wordplay" of his previous albums.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Dele Fadele of <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> found it less reflective than his previous work, but commended Jay-Z as "an intelligent man, with a gifted turn of slang, [...] spits venom one minute and cools out the next".<sup id="cite_ref-Fadele_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fadele-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retrospect">Retrospect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vol._3..._Life_and_Times_of_S._Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Retrospect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a retrospective review, Steve Juon of RapReviews gave it an eight-and-a-half out of 10 rating and viewed it as an improvement over Jay-Z's previous two albums.<sup id="cite_ref-Juon_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Juon-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> editor John Bush complimented his lyricism and called the album "a set of hard-hitting tracks with some of the best rhymes of Jay-Z's career … much more invigorating than its predecessor, and almost as consistently entertaining as his best album, <i><a href="/wiki/In_My_Lifetime,_Vol._1" title="In My Lifetime, Vol. 1">In My Lifetime, Vol. 1</a></i>".<sup id="cite_ref-Bush_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> columnist Spence D. wrote that <i>Vol. 3… Life and Times of S. Carter</i> presents Jay-Z "in battle with his two personas: the street soldier and the pop star".<sup id="cite_ref-Spence_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spence-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Rolling_Stone_Album_Guide" title="The New Rolling Stone Album Guide" class="mw-redirect">The New Rolling Stone Album Guide</a></i> (2004) gives the album four out of five stars and states, "<i>Life &amp; Times of S. Carter</i> took [<i>Vol. 2</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s] combination of style and substance to its apotheosis. In addition to maintaining a strong lyrical presence, Jay also showcased his talents as a master of flow, changing cadences and rhyme patterns with impressive regularity and flexibility. [...] Nearly every track on this album was sonically unique, and Jay rode each one with aplomb and skill".<sup id="cite_ref-RSguide_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSguide-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><sup>*</sup> indicates co-producer</p>
<p>The UK/Europe edition of the album (which was released several weeks before the US edition) features an alternate track listing. "Hova Interlude" replaced the track "Watch Me" and "There's Been a Murder" was replaced with "<a href="/wiki/Is_That_Your_Chick_(The_Lost_Verses)" title="Is That Your Chick (The Lost Verses)">Is That Yo Bitch</a>" (featuring <a href="/wiki/Missy_Elliott" title="Missy Elliott">Missy Elliott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twista" title="Twista">Twista</a>). "Is That Yo Bitch" is an alternate version of Memphis Bleek's "Is That Your Chick". It also has "<a href="/wiki/Anything_(Jay-Z_song)" title="Anything (Jay-Z song)">Anything</a>" as the closing song (track 16) before the two hidden bonus tracks. This version is how the album was really intended to sound but due to heavy bootlegging of the promotional copy Jay-Z went back and changed the US track listing on the eleventh hour.</p>
