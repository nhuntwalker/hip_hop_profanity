<p><i><b>The College Dropout</b></i> is the <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">debut album</a> by American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, released February 10, 2004, on <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a>. It was recorded over a period of four years, beginning in 1999. Prior to the album's release, West had worked on rapper <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blueprint" title="The Blueprint">The Blueprint</a></i> (2001), which showcased his melodic and <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soulful</a> style of <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_production" title="Hip hop production">hip hop production</a>. The production of the album was often halted by the lack of confidence that producers had in West's rapping ability, eventually being convinced based off the success of his early musical ventures.</p>
<p>The album had production entirely handled by West, and features musical contributions from Jay-Z, <a href="/wiki/John_Legend" title="John Legend">John Legend</a>, Ervin "EP" Pope, <a href="/wiki/Miri_Ben-Ari" title="Miri Ben-Ari">Miri Ben-Ari</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Syleena_Johnson" title="Syleena Johnson">Syleena Johnson</a>. Diverging from the then-dominant <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangster persona</a> in hip hop, West's lyrics on the album concern themes of family, religion, self-consciousness, materialism, and personal struggles. The album was promoted by singles including "<a href="/wiki/Through_the_Wire" title="Through the Wire">Through the Wire</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Jesus_Walks" title="Jesus Walks">Jesus Walks</a>", both of which received critical acclaim. "<a href="/wiki/All_Falls_Down" title="All Falls Down">All Falls Down</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Slow_Jamz" title="Slow Jamz">Slow Jamz</a>" both charted within the top 10 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, with the latter charting at number one.</p>
<p>The album debuted at number two on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 441,000 copies in its first week. It was a massive commercial success, producing five singles that achieved chart success. Upon its release, <i>The College Dropout</i> received general acclaim from music critics and earned West several accolades, including a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Grammy Award for Best Rap Album</a> at the <a href="/wiki/47th_Grammy_Awards" title="47th Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">47th Grammy Awards</a>. It is West's best-selling album in the United States, with domestic sales of over 3.1 million copies and worldwide sales of over four million copies. It has been named by <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> as one of the greatest albums of all time and is often listed amongst the greatest debut albums ever released by a hip hop artist.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_College_Dropout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although he had attained success as a producer, <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> aspired to be a rapper, but had struggled to attain a record deal.<sup id="cite_ref-Road_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Road-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Multiple record companies ignored him because he did not portray the gangsta image prominent in mainstream hip hop at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-Hess-p556_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess-p556-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> After a series of meetings with <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a>, West was ultimately denied an artist deal.<sup id="cite_ref-KanplicatedPg2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KanplicatedPg2-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> According to Capitol Record's A&amp;R, Joe Weinberger, he was approached by West and almost signed a deal with him, but another person in the company convinced Capitol's president not to.<sup id="cite_ref-KanplicatedPg2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KanplicatedPg2-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Desperate to keep West from defecting to another label, then-label head <a href="/wiki/Damon_Dash" title="Damon Dash">Damon Dash</a> reluctantly signed West to <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, West's colleague, later admitted that Roc-A-Fella was initially reluctant to support West as a rapper, claiming that many saw him as a producer first and foremost, and that his background contrasted with that of his labelmates.<sup id="cite_ref-Hess-p556_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess-p556-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>West's breakthrough came a year later on October 23, 2002, when, while driving home from a <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> recording studio after working late, he fell asleep at the wheel and was involved in a near-fatal car crash.<sup id="cite_ref-wsws_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsws-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The crash left him with a shattered jaw, which had to be wired shut in reconstructive surgery. The accident inspired West; two weeks after being admitted to hospital, he recorded a song at the <a href="/wiki/Record_Plant_Studios" title="Record Plant Studios" class="mw-redirect">Record Plant Studios</a> with his jaw still wired shut.<sup id="cite_ref-wsws_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsws-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The composition, "<a href="/wiki/Through_The_Wire" title="Through The Wire" class="mw-redirect">Through The Wire</a>", expressed West's experience after the accident, and helped lay the foundation for his debut album, as according to West "all the better artists have expressed what they were going through".<sup id="cite_ref-amg_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amg-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ebony_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebony-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> West added that "the album was my medicine", as working on the record distracted him from the pain.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> "Through The Wire" was first available on West's <i>Get Well Soon...</i> <a href="/wiki/Mixtape" title="Mixtape">mixtape</a>, released December 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> At the same time, West announced that he was working on an album called <i>The College Dropout</i>, whose overall theme was to "make your own decisions. Don't let society tell you, 'This is what you have to do.'"<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_College_Dropout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>The College Dropout</i> was recorded at <a href="/wiki/The_Record_Plant" title="The Record Plant" class="mw-redirect">The Record Plant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, California, but the production featured on the record were formed elsewhere over the course of several years.<sup id="cite_ref-Road_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Road-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> According to John Monopoly, West's friend, manager and business partner, the album "...[didn't have] a particular start date. He's been gathering beats for years. He was always producing with the intention of being a rapper. There's beats on the album he's been literally saving for himself for years." At one point, West hovered between making a portion of the production in the studio and the majority within his own apartment in <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark, New Jersey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Road_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Road-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Because it was a two-bedroom apartment, West was able to set up a home studio in one of the rooms and his bedroom in the other.<sup id="cite_ref-Road_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Road-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Carrying a <a href="/wiki/Louis_Vuitton" title="Louis Vuitton">Louis Vuitton</a> backpack filled with old disks and demos to the studio and back, West crafted his production in less than fifteen minutes at a time. He recorded the remainder of the album in Los Angeles while recovering from the car accident. Once he had completed the album, it was leaked months before its release date.<sup id="cite_ref-Road_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Road-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> However, West decided to use the opportunity to review the album, and <i>The College Dropout</i> was significantly remixed, remastered, and revised before being released. As a result, certain tracks originally destined for the album were subsequently retracted, among them "Keep the Receipt" with <a href="/wiki/Ol%27_Dirty_Bastard" title="Ol' Dirty Bastard">Ol' Dirty Bastard</a> and "The Good, the Bad, and the Ugly" with Consequence.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> West meticulously refined the production, adding <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">string arrangements</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gospel_choir" title="Gospel choir" class="mw-redirect">gospel choirs</a>, improved drum programming and new verses.<sup id="cite_ref-Road_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Road-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> West's perfectionism led <i>The College Dropout</i> to have its release postponed three times from its initial date in August 2003. First it got delayed to October, then to January 2004, and finally it hit stores on February.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_College_Dropout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The College Dropout</i> diverged from the then-dominant <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangster persona</a> in hip hop in favor of more diverse, topical subjects for the lyrics.<sup id="cite_ref-ebony_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebony-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Throughout the album, West touches on a number of different issues drawn from his own experiences and observations, including organized religion, family, sexuality, excessive materialism, self-consciousness, <a href="/wiki/Minimum-wage" title="Minimum-wage" class="mw-redirect">minimum-wage</a> labor, <a href="/wiki/Institutional_racism" title="Institutional racism">institutional prejudice</a>, and personal struggles.<sup id="cite_ref-Love_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Love-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cinquemani_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cinquemani-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Music journalist <a href="/wiki/Kelefa_Sanneh" title="Kelefa Sanneh">Kelefa Sanneh</a> wrote, "Throughout the album Mr. West taunts everyone who didn't believe in him: teachers, record executives, police officers, even his former boss at the Gap".<sup id="cite_ref-Sanneh_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanneh-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> West explained, "My persona is that I'm the regular person. Just think about whatever you've been through in the past week, and I have a song about that on my album."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The album begins with a <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_skit" title="Hip hop skit">skit</a> featuring a high school teacher asking West to deliver a graduation speech. The skit is followed by "We Don't Care" featuring West comically celebrating drug life with lines like "We wasn't supposed to make it past 25, joke's on you, we still alive" and then criticizing its influence amongst children.<sup id="cite_ref-Sanneh_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanneh-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> His lyrics contain various popular culture references such as allusions to rappers <a href="/wiki/Really_Doe" title="Really Doe">Really Doe</a> and Tupac Shakur and song "<a href="/wiki/21_Questions" title="21 Questions">21 Questions</a>" by <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The next track, "Graduation Day", features <a href="/wiki/Miri_Ben-Ari" title="Miri Ben-Ari">Miri Ben-Ari</a> on violin,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and vocals by <a href="/wiki/John_Legend" title="John Legend">John Legend</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-linernotes_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linernotes-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On "All Falls Down", West wages an attack on consumerism.<sup id="cite_ref-Kellman_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kellman-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Heaton_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heaton-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The song features singer Syleena Johnson and contains an <a href="/wiki/Interpolation_(music)" title="Interpolation (music)">interpolation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lauryn_Hill" title="Lauryn Hill">Lauryn Hill</a>'s "Mystery of Iniquity".<sup id="cite_ref-linernotes_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linernotes-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> West called upon Johnson to re-sing a vocal portion of "Mystery of Iniquity", which ended up in the final mix.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Gospel hymn with <a href="/wiki/Doo-wop" title="Doo-wop">doo-wop</a> elements "<a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Fly_Away" title="I'll Fly Away">I'll Fly Away</a>" precedes "Spaceship", a track with a relaxed beat containing a soulful <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Gaye" title="Marvin Gaye">Marvin Gaye</a> sample. The lyrics are mostly critical of the working world, where West muses about flying away in a spaceship to leave his boring job, and guest rappers <a href="/wiki/GLC_(rapper)" title="GLC (rapper)">GLC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Consequence_(rapper)" title="Consequence (rapper)">Consequence</a> add comparisons to modern day retail environment with slavery.<sup id="cite_ref-Heaton_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heaton-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On "Jesus Walks" West professes his belief in <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>, while also discussing how religion is used by various people and how the <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">media</a> seems to avoid songs that address matters of faith while embracing compositions on violence, sex, and drugs.<sup id="cite_ref-Heaton_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heaton-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USAToday_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> "Jesus Walks" is built around a sample of "Walk With Me" as performed by the ARC Choir.<sup id="cite_ref-linernotes_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linernotes-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Garry Mulholland of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> described it as a "towering inferno of martial beats, fathoms-deep <a href="/wiki/Chain_gang" title="Chain gang">chain gang</a> backing chants, a defiant children's <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a> wails, and sizzling orchestral breaks."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The first verse of the song is told through the eyes of a <a href="/wiki/Drug_dealer" title="Drug dealer" class="mw-redirect">drug dealer</a> seeking help from God, and it reportedly took over six months for West to draw inspiration for the second verse.<sup id="cite_ref-KanplicatedPg3_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KanplicatedPg3-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"Never Let Me Down" is influenced by West's near-death car crash. The song features Jay-Z who rhymes about maintaining status and power given his chart success, with West commenting about racism and poverty.<sup id="cite_ref-Heaton_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heaton-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The song features verses by spoken word performer <a href="/wiki/J._Ivy" title="J. Ivy">J. Ivy</a> who offers comments of upliftment. "Never Let Me Down" reuses a Jay-Z verse first heard in the remix of his song "<a href="/wiki/Hovi_Baby" title="Hovi Baby">Hovi Baby</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Heaton_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heaton-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Batey_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Batey-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> "Get Em High" is a collaboration by West with two socially conscious rappers, <a href="/wiki/Talib_Kweli" title="Talib Kweli">Talib Kweli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-common_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-common-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/The_New_Workout_Plan" title="The New Workout Plan">The New Workout Plan</a>" is a mostly comical song and is centered around the premise of an aerobics routine.<sup id="cite_ref-Heaton_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heaton-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> "Slow Jamz" features <a href="/wiki/Twista" title="Twista">Twista</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Foxx" title="Jamie Foxx">Jamie Foxx</a> and serves as a tribute to classic <a href="/wiki/Smooth_soul" title="Smooth soul">smooth soul</a> artists and <a href="/wiki/Slow_jam" title="Slow jam">slow jam</a> songs.<sup id="cite_ref-Kellman_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kellman-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The song also appeared on Twista's album <i><a href="/wiki/Kamikaze_(album)" title="Kamikaze (album)">Kamikaze</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kellman_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kellman-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> On the song "School Spirit", West relates the experience of dropping out of school and contains references to well-known <a href="/wiki/Fraternities" title="Fraternities" class="mw-redirect">fraternities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sororities" title="Sororities" class="mw-redirect">sororities</a>, singer <a href="/wiki/Norah_Jones" title="Norah Jones">Norah Jones</a>, and record label Roc-A-Fella Records. "Two Words" features commentary on social issues and features <a href="/wiki/Mos_Def" title="Mos Def">Mos Def</a>, Freeway, and the Harlem Boys Choir.<sup id="cite_ref-Ryan_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"Through the Wire" features a high-pitched vocal sample of <a href="/wiki/Chaka_Khan" title="Chaka Khan">Chaka Khan</a> and relates West's real life experience with being in a car accident.<sup id="cite_ref-wsws_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsws-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The song provides a mostly comedic account of his difficult recovery, and features West rapping with his jaw still wired shut from the accident.<sup id="cite_ref-wsws_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsws-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-linernotes_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linernotes-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Refrain" title="Refrain">chorus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Instrumental" title="Instrumental">instrumentals</a> sample a "pitched up" version of Chaka Khan's 1985 single "<a href="/wiki/Through_the_Fire_(song)" title="Through the Fire (song)">Through the Fire</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Kellman_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kellman-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> "Family Business" is a soulful tribute to one of West's incarcerated family members. The album ends with a nearly nine-minute autobiographical monologue that follows the song "Last Call".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Packaging">Packaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_College_Dropout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Packaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eric Duvauchelle, who was then part of Roc-A-Fella's in-house brand design team and now is now a Design Director at <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike, Inc.</a>, was assigned for the artwork of <i>The College Dropout</i>. West had already taken pictures dressed as the Dropout Bear - which would reappear in his later work - and Duvauchelle picked the image of him sitting on a set of <a href="/wiki/Bleacher" title="Bleacher">bleachers</a>, as he got attracted to the loneliness of what was supposed to be "the most popular representation of a school". The image is framed inside gold ornaments, which Duvauchelle found in a book of illustrations from the 16th-century and West wanted to use to "bring a sense of elegance and style to what was typically a <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangster-led image of rap artists</a>". The inside cover follows a college <a href="/wiki/Yearbook" title="Yearbook">yearbook</a>, with photos of the featured artists of the albums from their youth.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_College_Dropout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg/220px-Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg" width="220" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg/330px-Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg/440px-Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2551" data-file-height="2631" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.24wmf6/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>West's debut single, "Through the Wire", entered the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart at number ninety-four and peaked at number fifteen on February 3, 2004, for five weeks. It remained on the chart for twenty-one weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-acharts_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acharts-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> It performed better on the <a href="/wiki/Urban_contemporary" title="Urban contemporary">urban contemporary</a> charts, reaching number eight on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Singles_%26_Tracks" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks</a> and number four on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Tracks" title="Hot Rap Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot Rap Tracks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> In the United Kingdom, it debuted at number nine on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>, where it peaked for two weeks, and exited the chart after nine weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-acharts_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acharts-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> The track charted lower in other European countries, reaching the top thirty in Ireland, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>; the top fifty in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>; and number sixty-one in Germany. Its maximum peak time in those countries lasted one week.<sup id="cite_ref-acharts_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acharts-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> The single entered the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Singles_Chart" title="New Zealand Singles Chart" class="mw-redirect">New Zealand Singles Chart</a> at number twenty-four and peaked at number sixteen.<sup id="cite_ref-acharts_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acharts-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The second single, "Slow Jamz", peaked at number one in the United States, becoming Twista's, West's, and Foxx's first number one hit.<sup id="cite_ref-acharts_34-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acharts-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> "All Falls Down" was released as the third single, and it entered the UK Singles Chart at number ten and peaked at number seven on the U.S. Hot 100.<sup id="cite_ref-Road_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Road-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Fourth single, "Jesus Walks" earned widespread commercial success, peaking at number eleven in the United States and becoming West's fourth top twenty hit, while peaking at number sixteen in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-USAToday_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The fifth and final single, "The New Workout Plan", peaked at number fifty-nine on the U.S. Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Singles &amp; Tracks chart. The song's official remix was produced by <a href="/wiki/Lil_Jon" title="Lil Jon">Lil Jon</a> and features guest appearances from Twista, <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" title="Luther Campbell">Luke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fonzworth_Bentley" title="Fonzworth Bentley">Fonzworth Bentley</a>. The remix was later included on <i><a href="/wiki/The_College_Dropout_Video_Anthology" title="The College Dropout Video Anthology">The College Dropout Video Anthology</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The planned sixth single was "Spaceship", featuring rappers GLC and Consequence. This was confirmed in 2009 when GLC reported that a <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> had been made for the song, but never released; the label decided to move on from the album to begin promoting West's second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Registration" title="Late Registration">Late Registration</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> On June 3, 2009, West uploaded the video on his official blog.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> At one time, "Two Words" (featuring Mos Def and Freeway) was also intended to be released as a single, and a video for the song was filmed, and later uploaded by West online in May 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-common_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-common-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_College_Dropout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_College_Dropout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album debuted at number two on the U.S. <i>Billboard</i> 200 chart, behind <a href="/wiki/Norah_Jones" title="Norah Jones">Norah Jones</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Feels_Like_Home_(Norah_Jones_album)" title="Feels Like Home (Norah Jones album)">Feels Like Home</a></i>, selling 441,000 copies in its first week.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> It remained on the second spot under <i>Feels Like Home</i> for two more weeks, with 196,000 units sold in the second week and 132,000 in the third.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> By April 2004, it was certified by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> for having shipped in excess of 1 million copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> On June 30, 2004, the album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">double platinum</a> in sales by the RIAA, following sales of 2 million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> As of June 14, 2013, <i>The College Dropout</i> is West's best-selling album in the United States, with domestic sales of over 3.3 million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> It has also sold over 4 million copies worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-SMH_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SMH-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>The College Dropout</i> received universal acclaim from <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">music critics</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album received an <a href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean" class="mw-redirect">average</a> score of 87, based on 25 reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Kelefa Sanneh of <i>The New York Times</i> dubbed it "2004's first great hip-hop album".<sup id="cite_ref-Sanneh_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanneh-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Rabin" title="Nathan Rabin">Nathan Rabin</a> praised its "substance, social commentary, righteous anger, ornery humanism, dark humor, and even Christianity", calling it "one of those wonderful crossover albums that appeal to a huge audience without sacrificing a shred of integrity".<sup id="cite_ref-Rabin_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rabin-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Dave Heaton of <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a> called the album "musically engaging", writing that "every single one of these songs comes off like a genuine extension of Kanye's personality and experiences".<sup id="cite_ref-Heaton_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heaton-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/URB_(magazine)" title="URB (magazine)">URB</a></i> wrote that the album "manages to be both visceral and emotive, sprinkling the dancefloors with tears and sweat."<sup id="cite_ref-URB_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-URB-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Mojo_(magazine)" title="Mojo (magazine)">Mojo</a></i> dubbed the album "<a href="/wiki/Manna" title="Manna">manna</a> for hip hop fans starved of basic but ballistic beats-and-breaks fare in an increasingly litigious age."<sup id="cite_ref-Mojo_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mojo-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Josh Love of <a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a> wrote that West "subverts cliches from both sides of the hip-hop divide" and praised him for "trying to reflect the entire spectrum of hip-hop and black experience, looking for solace and salvation in the traditional safehouses of church and family".<sup id="cite_ref-Love_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Love-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Ethan Brown of <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_(magazine)" title="New York (magazine)">New York</a></i> commended West's "emotional brand of hip-hop" and stated "he makes autobiography universal in a way that hasn't really been heard in hip-hop since the mid-nineties".<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> Hua Hsu of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i> felt that his sped-up samples "carry a humble, human air. You can still hear tiny traces of actual people inside".<sup id="cite_ref-Hsu_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hsu-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> The newspaper's <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> wrote that "not only does [West] create a unique role model, that role model is dangerousâ€”his arguments against education are as market-targeted as other rappers' arguments for thug life".<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Michael Endelman elaborated on West's avoidance of the then-dominant "gangsta" persona of hip hop:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>West delivers the goods with a disarming mix of confessional honesty and sarcastic humor, earnest idealism and big-pimping materialism. In a scene still dominated by authenticity battles and gangsta posturing, he's a middle-class, politically conscious, post-thug, bourgeois rapper â€” and that's nothing to be ashamed of.<sup id="cite_ref-Endelman_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Endelman-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Although he commended West's vulnerability and signature sound, <i>Rolling Stone</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Jon Caramanica felt that "West isn't quite MC enough to hold down the entire disc".<sup id="cite_ref-Caramanica_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caramanica-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a>'s Sal Cinquemani observed "too many guest artists, too many interludes, and just too many songs period", but stated, "As chest-beatingly self-congratulatory as it may be, <i>The College Dropout</i> is at once laugh-out-loud funny ('The New Workout Plan'), genuinely touching ('Family Business'), and brutally honest ('All Falls Down')".<sup id="cite_ref-Cinquemani_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cinquemani-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a>'s Rob Mitchum called it a "flawed, overlong, hypocritical, egotistical, and altogether terrific album".<sup id="cite_ref-Mitchum_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchum-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> editor Andy Kellman felt that it shows West as a "remarkably versatile lyricist and a valuable MC".<sup id="cite_ref-Kellman_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kellman-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Renee Graham of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> asserted that "West has certainly raised the bar on what mainstream hip-hop can and should be".<sup id="cite_ref-Graham_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graham-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> In a retrospective review, <i>Rolling Stone</i> gave the album four-and-a-half out of five stars and called it "a demonstration that hip-hopâ€”real, banging, commercial hip-hopâ€”could be a vehicle for nuanced self-examination and musical subtlety."<sup id="cite_ref-RSguide_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSguide-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_College_Dropout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>West received 10 <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy</a> nominations at the <a href="/wiki/2005_Grammy_Awards" title="2005 Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">2005 Grammy Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-grammy_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grammy-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The College Dropout</i> was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Album_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Album of the Year">Album of the Year</a>, and won <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Best Rap Album</a>. "Jesus Walks" won <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Song" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Song">Best Rap Song</a>, and was nominated for both <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Song_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Song of the Year">Song of the Year</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap/Sung_Collaboration" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap/Sung Collaboration">Grammy Award for Best Rap/Sung Collaboration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-grammy_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grammy-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>The College Dropout</i> was voted as the best album of the year by <i>Rolling Stone</i> and in <i>The Village Voice</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Pazz_%26_Jop" title="Pazz &amp; Jop">Pazz &amp; Jop</a> critics poll.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Spin</i> ranked it number one on its list of 40 Best Albums of the Year.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> Comedian <a href="/wiki/Chris_Rock" title="Chris Rock">Chris Rock</a> has attested to listening to <i>The College Dropout</i> while writing his material.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2005, Pitchfork Media named it #50 in their best albums of 2000â€“2004.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2006, the album was named by <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> as one of the 100 best albums of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-time.com_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time.com-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> In its retrospective 2007 issue, <i><a href="/wiki/XXL_(magazine)" title="XXL (magazine)">XXL</a></i> awarded it a perfect "XXL" rating, which had previously been given to only sixteen other albums.<sup id="cite_ref-XXL_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> In its July 4, 2008 issue, <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> listed <i>College Dropout</i> as the fourth best album of the past 25 years.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> The magazine later listed it as the best album of the decade.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> placed <i>The College Dropout</i> among its Best Albums of the Decade list at number 3.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rhapsody_(online_music_service)" title="Rhapsody (online music service)">Rhapsody</a> named it the seventh best album of the decade and the fourth best hip hop album of the decade.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Rolling Stone</i> ranked it number 10 on its list of the 100 Best Albums of the Decade and stated, "Kanye expanded the musical and emotional language of hip-hop ... he challenged all the rules, dancing across boundaries others were too afraid to even acknowledge".<sup id="cite_ref-decade_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-decade-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012 <i><a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a></i> named the album one of the classic albums of the last decade,<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> and the 20th best hip hop debut album ever.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> The same year <i>Rolling Stone</i> ranked <i>The College Dropout</i> number 298 on its list of <a href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">the 500 Greatest Albums of All Time</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> and 19th on their list of debut records.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
