<p><i><b>Tha Carter IV</b></i> is the ninth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American rapper <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne" title="Lil Wayne">Lil Wayne</a>, released on August 29, 2011 through <a href="/wiki/Young_Money_Entertainment" title="Young Money Entertainment">Young Money Entertainment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cash_Money_Records" title="Cash Money Records">Cash Money Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/Universal_Republic_Records" title="Universal Republic Records">Universal Republic Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Recording sessions for the album began in late 2008, shortly after Lil Wayne's sixth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Tha_Carter_III" title="Tha Carter III">Tha Carter III</a></i> (2008) was released to strong sales and critical acclaim: however, the sessions were put on hold, as Lil Wayne claimed he did not want to follow an album he held in high regard so quickly with another, potentially inferior release. In the interim, Lil Wayne released two other solo albums: the largely rock-themed <i><a href="/wiki/Rebirth_(Lil_Wayne_album)" title="Rebirth (Lil Wayne album)">Rebirth</a></i> (2010) and <i><a href="/wiki/I_Am_Not_a_Human_Being" title="I Am Not a Human Being">I Am Not a Human Being</a></i> (2010). The latter was reportedly composed from unreleased material from the original <i>Tha Carter IV</i> sessions, as the album was released whilst Wayne served a <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne#Legal_issues" title="Lil Wayne">prison sentence</a> at <a href="/wiki/Rikers_Island" title="Rikers Island">Rikers Island</a> prison for illegal possession of a weapon, and was thus unable to record any new material: this also meant <i>Tha Carter IV'</i>s recording sessions were once more put on hold.</p>
<p>Following Wayne's release from prison, the album was re-recorded from scratch. The recording sessions resumed at various locations, involving several record producers including <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh_(producer)" title="Bangladesh (producer)" class="mw-redirect">Bangladesh</a>, Detail, <a href="/wiki/T-Minus_(producer)" title="T-Minus (producer)">T-Minus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noah_%2240%22_Shebib" title="Noah &quot;40&quot; Shebib">Noah "40" Shebib</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polow_da_Don" title="Polow da Don">Polow da Don</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jonsin" title="Jim Jonsin">Jim Jonsin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kane_Beatz" title="Kane Beatz">Kane Beatz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boi-1da" title="Boi-1da">Boi-1da</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willy_Will" title="Willy Will">Willy Will</a>, <a href="/wiki/StreetRunner" title="StreetRunner">StreetRunner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cool_%26_Dre" title="Cool &amp; Dre">Cool &amp; Dre</a>, Young Ladd, <a href="/wiki/The_Smeezingtons" title="The Smeezingtons">The Smeezingtons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>. The album's largely concerns the themes of sex, violence, drugs and crime, but also love, hurt and emotional conflict. Appearances on the album include <a href="/wiki/Cory_Gunz" title="Cory Gunz">Cory Gunz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drake_(entertainer)" title="Drake (entertainer)" class="mw-redirect">Drake</a>, <a href="/wiki/T-Pain" title="T-Pain">T-Pain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tech_N9ne" title="Tech N9ne">Tech N9ne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andre_3000" title="Andre 3000" class="mw-redirect">Andre 3000</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Ross" title="Rick Ross">Rick Ross</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Legend" title="John Legend">John Legend</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Mars" title="Bruno Mars">Bruno Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birdman_(rapper)" title="Birdman (rapper)">Birdman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Rudolf" title="Kevin Rudolf">Kevin Rudolf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bun_B" title="Bun B">Bun B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shyne" title="Shyne">Shyne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>.</p>
<p>Following a heavily delayed release, <i>Tha Carter IV</i> was released to digital retailers at midnight on August 28, 2011, following Wayne's scheduled performance at the <a href="/wiki/MTV_Video_Music_Award" title="MTV Video Music Award">MTV Video Music Awards</a>, and physical retailers received the album the following day. <i>Tha Carter IV</i> achieved first week sales of 964,000 copies in the United States and became Lil Wayne's second album to top the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> in its first week. The album received generally mixed reviews from music critics, who were divided in their responses towards the album's production and Wayne's own performances on the album: they felt it a disappointment compared to his previous work.<sup id="cite_ref-Retirement_hints_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Retirement_hints-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_development">Background and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tha_Carter_IV&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In June 2008, after a similarly delayed release, Lil Wayne released his previous album in the <i>Tha Carter</i> series, <i><a href="/wiki/Tha_Carter_III" title="Tha Carter III">Tha Carter III</a></i> (2008). The album sold 1,005,545 copies in its first week of sales in the United States, and produced three top ten singles in the US, including the number one-hit "<a href="/wiki/Lollipop_(Lil_Wayne_song)" title="Lollipop (Lil Wayne song)">Lollipop</a>". The album became the highest selling of the year in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In a September 2008 interview with Shaheem Reid of <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> Mixtape Monday, Lil Wayne revealed that he had begun work on his next official mixtape, <i><a href="/wiki/Dedication_3" title="Dedication 3">Dedication 3</a></i> (2008) and also confirmed a sequel to <i>Tha Carter III</i>, titled <i>Tha Carter IV</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Initial recording sessions for the album began in early October 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> but these were put on hold, as later that month Wayne claimed that he did not want the album to follow <i>Tha Carter III</i> immediately.</p>
<p>No more information emerged on the album until October 2009, when <a href="/wiki/Cash_Money_Records" title="Cash Money Records">Cash Money Records</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Birdman_(rapper)" title="Birdman (rapper)">Birdman</a> reported that Wayne would release three studio albums on December 15, 2009: <i>Tha Carter IV</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rebirth_(Lil_Wayne_album)" title="Rebirth (Lil Wayne album)">Rebirth</a></i>, an album currently being promoted as Wayne's debut rock music album, and <i><a href="/wiki/We_Are_Young_Money" title="We Are Young Money">We Are Young Money</a></i>, a collaborative recording with members of Wayne's record label, <a href="/wiki/Young_Money_Entertainment" title="Young Money Entertainment">Young Money Entertainment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> However, it was later confirmed that <i>Rebirth</i> and <i>We Are Young Money</i> would be released separately<sup id="cite_ref-Rebirth_.26_We_Are_Young_Money_sold_seperately_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebirth_.26_We_Are_Young_Money_sold_seperately-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and that <i>Tha Carter IV</i> would be released in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-is_going_to_be_released_on_May_17.2C_2011._8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-is_going_to_be_released_on_May_17.2C_2011.-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Tha Carter IV</i> was going to be released in late May,<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> but was pushed back to June. <a href="/wiki/Mack_Maine" title="Mack Maine">Mack Maine</a> confirmed that the album's release was postponed because they still needed time to make it perfect.<sup id="cite_ref-mtv.com_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv.com-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> On June 2, 2011, the album was pushed back further, and the album was due for release on August 29, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-new_date_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_date-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album's cover was released to the internet on April 19, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> A deluxe edition has been confirmed for <i>Tha Carter IV</i>, with the album's cover being released to the internet as well.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Also the track "Dear Anne (Stan Part 2)" (originally "Anne") was originally supposed to be on <i>Tha Carter IV</i> but has since then been removed from the album. Lil Wayne recently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (October 2011)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> said in an interview with XXL that he was not a fan of "Dear Anne" and that it was originally planned to appear on <i>Tha Carter III</i>. On July 8, 2011, producer <a href="/wiki/Swizz_Beatz" title="Swizz Beatz">Swizz Beatz</a> hinted at Wayne possibly re-recording a new version of Anne, after he had said the verses were too "old". Prior to <i>Tha Carter IV'</i>s release, Swizz Beatz released the song on his Monster Mondays free music program through his official website.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On June 13, 2011, a track called "Nightmares of the Bottom" from <i>Tha Carter IV</i> was confirmed on MTV's Unplugged by Lil Wayne performing live.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> On July 11, 2011, Lil Wayne confirmed in an interview with MTV that <i>Tha Carter IV</i> is finished and will be releasing on August 29, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-new_date_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_date-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> On August 7–8, 2011 videos of Lil Wayne recording a song called "<a href="/wiki/She_Will" title="She Will">She Will</a>" and featuring <a href="/wiki/Drake_(entertainer)" title="Drake (entertainer)" class="mw-redirect">Drake</a> was posted online and would be on the album. The song was released on the Internet on August 12, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> HipHollywood released a <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> video about <a href="/wiki/T-Pain" title="T-Pain">T-Pain</a> giving a song to Lil Wayne for his album called, "How to Hate," confirming that it will be on the album.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tha_Carter_IV&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album's lead single, "<a href="/wiki/6_Foot_7_Foot" title="6 Foot 7 Foot">6 Foot 7 Foot</a>", which features <a href="/wiki/Cory_Gunz" title="Cory Gunz">Cory Gunz</a>, was released on December 16, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-6_Foot_7_Foot_iTunes_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6_Foot_7_Foot_iTunes-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> It peaked at nine on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and at two on both the US <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> chart and US <a href="/wiki/Rap_Songs" title="Rap Songs" class="mw-redirect">Rap Songs</a> chart, in addition to reaching the top fifty in Canada. The video made premieres on <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> on March 3, 2011 and on <a href="/wiki/Black_Entertainment_Television" title="Black Entertainment Television" class="mw-redirect">BET's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/106_%26_Park" title="106 &amp; Park">106 &amp; Park</a></i> on March 4, 2011. The video (directed by <a href="/wiki/Hype_Williams" title="Hype Williams">Hype Williams</a>) was inspired by the film <i><a href="/wiki/Inception" title="Inception">Inception</a></i>, and consists of numerous scenes which visualize many of the metaphors and similes Wayne says in the song.<sup id="cite_ref-explicit_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-explicit-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/John_(Lil_Wayne_song)" title="John (Lil Wayne song)">John</a>", which features <a href="/wiki/Rick_Ross" title="Rick Ross">Rick Ross</a> was released as the second single on March 24, 2011<sup id="cite_ref-John_iTunes_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John_iTunes-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and debuted at twenty-two on the US Hot 100. It also reached nineteen on US R&amp;B charts and twelve on US Rap charts. The official music video was released on VEVO on May 12, 2011. The video also featured a cameo by <a href="/wiki/Birdman_(rapper)" title="Birdman (rapper)">Birdman</a> and was directed by <a href="/wiki/Colin_Tilley" title="Colin Tilley">Colin Tilley</a>, director of "<a href="/wiki/Look_at_Me_Now_(Chris_Brown_song)" title="Look at Me Now (Chris Brown song)">Look at Me Now</a>" by Chris Brown, and No Sleep by Wiz Khalifa.</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/How_to_Love" title="How to Love">How to Love</a>" was released as the third single on May 31, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-How_To_Love_iTunes_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_To_Love_iTunes-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> It had peaked at number five on the US Hot 100, becoming Wayne's fourteenth top ten hit and the best performing single from the album. It also peaked at number two on US R&amp;B charts and number two on US Rap charts, in addition to reaching the top forty in Canada and top fifty in the UK. Detail, the song's producer, claimed Lil Wayne used no <a href="/wiki/Auto-Tune" title="Auto-Tune">Auto-Tune</a> in the song;<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The music video (directed by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Robinson_(director)" title="Chris Robinson (director)">Chris Robinson</a>) premiered August 23, 2011 on <a href="/wiki/MTV_Jams" title="MTV Jams">MTV Jams</a> as "Jam of the Week".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The fourth single, "<a href="/wiki/She_Will" title="She Will">She Will</a>", which features <a href="/wiki/Drake_(entertainer)" title="Drake (entertainer)" class="mw-redirect">Drake</a>, was released on the internet on August 12, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The song previously was to be titled "Maybe She Will", and originally featured a verse from <a href="/wiki/Rick_Ross" title="Rick Ross">Rick Ross</a>, however it did not make the final cut.<sup id="cite_ref-Complex_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Complex-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-She_Will_original_version_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-She_Will_original_version-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The single released on digital download format in the United States on August 16, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-She_Will_iTunes_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-She_Will_iTunes-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/It%27s_Good" title="It's Good">It's Good</a>", which features Drake and <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</a>, was solicited to urban radio as the album's fifth single on September 13, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The sixth single from the album is "<a href="/wiki/Mirror_(Lil_Wayne_song)" title="Mirror (Lil Wayne song)">Mirror</a>" featuring <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Mars" title="Bruno Mars">Bruno Mars</a>, which is a bonus track on the deluxe edition. It was released to urban radio on September 13, 2011. It was sent to Rhythmic radio and re-released to urban radio on November 1, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-webcitation.org_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webcitation.org-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Upon the release of <i>Tha Carter IV</i> it debuted at number sixteen on the US Hot 100 based on digital downloads alone.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tha_Carter_IV&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Tha Carter IV</i> had 300,000&#160;downloads in its first four days online, which broke an iTunes record previously set by <i><a href="/wiki/Watch_the_Throne" title="Watch the Throne">Watch the Throne</a></i>. In the United States, <i>Tha Carter IV</i> topped the <i>Billboard</i> 200 on the issue of September 17, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-bill_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bill-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Selling 964,000 copies in its first week, it achieved the highest first-week album sales since <a href="/wiki/Lady_Gaga" title="Lady Gaga">Lady Gaga</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Born_This_Way" title="Born This Way">Born This Way</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-bill_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bill-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> It retained the top spot in its second week, despite a 77% decrease in sales, selling 219,000 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> On November 16, 2011, the album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">double platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>, for shipments of two million copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-riaa.com_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-riaa.com-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> By February 2012, the album had sold 3.5 million copies worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> By July 2013, it had sold 2,296,000 copies in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> In Canada, the album debuted on the top spot on the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Albums_Chart" title="Canadian Albums Chart">Canadian Albums Chart</a>, selling 31,000 copies in its first week.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Tha Carter IV</i> received generally mixed reviews from <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">music critics</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean" class="mw-redirect">weighted mean</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album received an <a href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean" class="mw-redirect">average</a> score of 60, based on 29 reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> The consensus among critics is that the album is a disappointment.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> writer <a href="/wiki/Greg_Kot" title="Greg Kot">Greg Kot</a> viewed that Wayne "sounds slower, more methodical, less unhinged" and felt that he is held back by "repetitive subject matter — even Wayne sounds bored by trying to flip yet one more clever couplet about blunts and 'hos."<sup id="cite_ref-Kot_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kot-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Sean Fennessey of <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> wrote that "it's not a terribly ambitious mess, nor is it much fun, which for Wayne is a sin," and criticized his lyrics, stating "He rarely divulges specific moments [...] usually keeping the gritty details unexplained."<sup id="cite_ref-Fennessey_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fennessey-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a>'s Matthew Cole commented that the album's production "chases trends far more often than it attempts to set them" and found Wayne "not in exhilarating top form".<sup id="cite_ref-Cole_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cole-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> writer Jeff Weiss viewed his lyrics as "predictable" and called the album "more pedestrian than embarrassing."<sup id="cite_ref-Weiss_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiss-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Andy Hutchins of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i> called it "a bad rap album" and criticized its music as "a composition of a lot of rapping styles Wayne's dabbled in and production styles that have been bubbling in rap for some time, except little of it clicks."<sup id="cite_ref-Hutchins_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchins-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Jo Fuertes-Knight of <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> said that it "flops not because it’s straight-up bad, but because it’s boring."<sup id="cite_ref-Fuertes_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fuertes-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In a positive review, <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> writer <a href="/wiki/Rob_Sheffield" title="Rob Sheffield">Rob Sheffield</a> stated, "it's thrilling how unhinged Weezy sounds", adding that "even the failed moments sound like nobody else".<sup id="cite_ref-Sheffield_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheffield-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> editor David Jeffries stated, "If <i><a href="/wiki/Tha_Carter_II" title="Tha Carter II">II</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tha_Carter_III" title="Tha Carter III">III</a></i> were the arguable masterpieces, this one is less convincing, but it is a solid, above average hip-hop album".<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffries_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffries-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Jon Caramanica of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> felt that the guest rappers bring "their A game" and stated, "even on this album’s weak tracks, and there are several, [Wayne] remains a commanding presence, deploying just enough of his insistent croak to tether the song together."<sup id="cite_ref-Caramanica_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caramanica-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
