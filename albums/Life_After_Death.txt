<p><i><b>Life After Death</b></i> is the second and final <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American rapper <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>, released March 25, 1997 on <a href="/wiki/Bad_Boy_Records" title="Bad Boy Records">Bad Boy Records</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Double_album" title="Double album">double album</a>, it was released posthumously following his death on March 9, 1997, a fact reflected in the album's title. It features collaborations with guest artists such as <a href="/wiki/112_(band)" title="112 (band)" class="mw-redirect">112</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lil%27_Kim" title="Lil' Kim">Lil' Kim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mase" title="Mase">Mase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bone_Thugs-n-Harmony" title="Bone Thugs-n-Harmony">Bone Thugs-n-Harmony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too $hort</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angela_Winbush" title="Angela Winbush">Angela Winbush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Darryl_McDaniels" title="Darryl McDaniels">D.M.C.</a> of <a href="/wiki/Run-D.M.C." title="Run-D.M.C." class="mw-redirect">Run-D.M.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/R._Kelly" title="R. Kelly">R. Kelly</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_LOX" title="The LOX" class="mw-redirect">The LOX</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Puff_Daddy" title="Puff Daddy" class="mw-redirect">Puff Daddy</a>. <i>Life After Death</i> exhibits The Notorious B.I.G. further delving into the <a href="/wiki/Mafioso_rap" title="Mafioso rap" class="mw-redirect">mafioso rap</a> subgenre.</p>
<p><i>Life After Death</i> was released to a significant amount of critical praise and commercial success. In 2000, the album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">diamond</a> in sales by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), and it has been credited as one of the best-selling rap albums of all time. It also made the largest jump to number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart in history, jumping from number 176 to number one in one week. Also, it spent four weeks at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart and topped the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Year-End" title="Billboard Year-End">Billboard Year-End</a> chart as an R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Album for 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album was nominated for three <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards" title="Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">Grammy Awards</a> in 1998, including <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Best Rap Album</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance">Best Rap Solo Performance</a> for its first single "<a href="/wiki/Hypnotize_(The_Notorious_B.I.G._song)" title="Hypnotize (The Notorious B.I.G. song)">Hypnotize</a>", and <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Performance_by_a_Duo_or_Group" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group">Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group</a> for its second single "<a href="/wiki/Mo_Money_Mo_Problems" title="Mo Money Mo Problems">Mo Money Mo Problems</a>". Along with <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx%E2%80%A6" title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx…" class="mw-redirect">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx…</a></i> (1995), <a href="/wiki/AZ_(rapper)" title="AZ (rapper)">AZ</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Doe_Or_Die" title="Doe Or Die" class="mw-redirect">Doe Or Die</a></i> (1995), <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Reasonable_Doubt_(album)" title="Reasonable Doubt (album)">Reasonable Doubt</a></i> (1996), and <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/It_Was_Written" title="It Was Written">It Was Written</a></i> (1996) <i>Life After Death</i> has been considered by music writers as one of the seminal mafioso rap albums, as well as a <a href="/wiki/Landmark" title="Landmark">landmark</a> in hip hop music. In 2003, the album was ranked number 483 on Rolling Stone magazine's list of the 500 greatest albums of all time.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conception">Conception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life_After_Death&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Conception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life_After_Death&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two and a half years prior to the album's release, The Notorious B.I.G. who had married <a href="/wiki/Faith_Evans" title="Faith Evans">Faith Evans</a>, became East Coast's icon in the East Coast vs. West Coast rivalry and made guest appearances on albums by <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" title="Luther Campbell">Luke</a> amongst others. The album was originally supposed to be released on <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a> of 1996, but was pushed back to 1997. Two weeks prior to the album's release, The Notorious B.I.G. was shot four times in a drive-by shooting and pronounced dead at Cedars Sinai Hospital in Los Angeles. The album sold 690,000 copies its first week and was certified diamond three years later.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feud_references_and_subliminal_disses">Feud references and subliminal disses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life_After_Death&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Feud references and subliminal disses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Conflict with other rappers is a major theme shown throughout the album. Many songs on both discs contain subtle references to B.I.G.'s rivals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Kick_in_the_Door.22">"Kick in the Door"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life_After_Death&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: &quot;Kick in the Door&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The track "Kick in the Door" is directed at <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeru_the_Damaja" title="Jeru the Damaja">Jeru the Damaja</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghostface_Killah" title="Ghostface Killah">Ghostface Killah</a> and even the track's producer <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>. The subliminal messages had been speculated by listeners and confirmed by artists on several occasions, including <a href="/wiki/XXL_Magazine" title="XXL Magazine" class="mw-redirect">XXL Magazine</a>'s April 2003 edition "The Making of: Life After Death".</p>
<p>In "The Making of: Life After Death" Nashiem Myrick reveals that the second verse has lines directed at <a href="/wiki/Jeru_the_Damaja" title="Jeru the Damaja">Jeru the Damaja</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-xxlmag.com_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xxlmag.com-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> "Nas said that record was for him, but when Big said, “Son, I’m surprised you run with them/I think they got cum in them, ’cause they nothin’ but dicks,” he was talking about Jeru the Damaja to Premo ’cause Jeru was going at Big and Puff and all them [with the Premier-produced “One Day” in Jeru's album <i><a href="/wiki/Wrath_of_the_Math" title="Wrath of the Math">Wrath of the Math</a></i>]."</p>
<p>The line "Fuck that, why try, throw bleach in your eye" is a reference to Raekwon's jab on the track "Ice Water" from <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx..." title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...</a></i> where Raekwon raps</p>
<blockquote>
<p>That's life, to top it all off, beef for white<br />
pullin bleach out tryin to throw it in my eyesight<br /></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The lines on the final verse are directed at Nas as a reference to Nas challenging The Notorious B.I.G. for the title of King of N.Y.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> in the song "<a href="/wiki/The_Message_(Nas_song)" title="The Message (Nas song)">The Message</a>" from the album <i><a href="/wiki/It_Was_Written" title="It Was Written">It Was Written</a></i> in which Nas raps:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Yo let me let y'all niggaz know one thing<br />
There's one life, one love, so there can only be one King<br /></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Biggie struck back with the lines:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Ain't no other kings in this rap thing<br />
They siblings, nothing but my chil'ren<br />
One shot, they disappearin'<br />
It's ill when MC's used to be on cruddy shit<br />
Took home Ready to Die, listened, studied shit<br />
Now they on some money shit, successful out the blue<br /></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In "The Making of: Life After Death" article Lil’ Cease explains: "Big talked about Nas a little bit in that shit. It was the King of New York part, the last verse: “This goes out for those that chose to use disrespectful views on the King of NY.” That’s when Nas had that freestyle out, where he was like, “I’ll take the crown off the so-called King and lock it down.” That’s when Big had the cover of The Source, and it said, “The King of New York.”<sup id="cite_ref-xxlmag.com_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xxlmag.com-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Nas has touched on the track on his album <i><a href="/wiki/God%27s_Son" title="God's Son">God's Son</a></i>. Here Nas states:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Y'all don't know about my Biggie wars<br />
Who you thought "Kick in the Door" was for?<br /></p>
</blockquote>
<p>And finally commenting on the whole situation with Ghostface and Raekwon:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>BIG was ahead of his time, him and Raekwon my niggaz<br />
But dig it, they couldn't get along<br />
That's when Ghostface said it on the Purple Tape<br />
Bad Boy biting Nas album cover, wait??<br />
BIG told me Rae was stealing my slang<br />
And Rae told me out in Shaolin BIG would do the same thing<br /></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Long_Kiss_Goodnight.22">"Long Kiss Goodnight"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life_After_Death&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: &quot;Long Kiss Goodnight&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was also speculated by many listeners that the song "Long Kiss Goodnight" contains cryptic insults towards <a href="/wiki/2Pac" title="2Pac" class="mw-redirect">2Pac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Suge_Knight" title="Suge Knight">Suge Knight</a>, although at the time it was never confirmed due to the sensitive nature of both rappers' recent deaths.</p>
<p>However, Lil' Cease, Biggie's cousin and a member of Junior M.A.F.I.A. claimed the following in <a href="/wiki/XXL_Magazine" title="XXL Magazine" class="mw-redirect">XXL Magazine</a>'s April 2003 issue about Long Kiss Goodnight: "That was about ’Pac. He had some shit at the beginning of that though, nobody heard it, on the reel. We had to change it. It was a little too much. I can’t remember what Big said about him, but it was terrible. It couldn’t make it. He didn’t want to do it. He had some fire. But he didn’t want to make it too much. He just wanted to address it and to let nigga know, “I know what’s going on, and I could get wreck if I want to.” Like, “If I really wanted to get on ya niggas, I could.”</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/P_Diddy" title="P Diddy" class="mw-redirect">Sean Combs</a>, however, denies these claims stating: "Naw, it was just some emcee lyrics. I know people wanna have their imagination, but it was just lyrics. You're hearing it from the horse's mouth. I would tell the truth."<sup id="cite_ref-xxlmag.com_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xxlmag.com-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the first verse, the lyric "Laugh Now, Cry Later" is allegedly a reference to two tattoos on 2Pac’s back.<sup id="cite_ref-rapgenius.com_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rapgenius.com-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> A line in the first verse is supposedly aimed at Shakur:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>When my men bust you just move with such stamina<br />
Slugs missed ya, <a href="/wiki/I_Ain%27t_Mad_at_Cha" title="I Ain't Mad at Cha">I Ain't Mad at Cha</a> (We Ain't Mad at Cha)<br /></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The last 2 verses in particular seem to be directed towards Tupac:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I'm flaming gats, aimin' at these fuckin' maniacs<br />
Put my name in raps, what part the game is that?<br />
Like they hustle backwards<br />
I smoke Backwoods and Dutchies, ya can't touch me<br />
Try to rush me, slugs go touchy-touchy<br />
You're bleeding lovely with your spirit above me or beneath me<br />
Your whole life you live sneaky<br />
Now you rest eternally, sleepy, you burn when you creep me<br />
Rest where the worms and the weak be<br /></p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Slugs hit your chest, tap your spine, flat line<br />
Heard through the grapevine, <a href="/wiki/Prison_rape" title="Prison rape">you got fucked fo' times</a><br />
Damn that three to nine, fucked you up for real though<br />
Slugs still slow, as for remorse, we feel no<br /></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The lines seem to be making reference to Tupac frequently mentioning Biggie <i>by name</i> in his raps, and allegations spread by <a href="/wiki/Wendy_Williams" title="Wendy Williams">Wendy Williams</a> that he had been raped during his prison term at <a href="/wiki/Rikers_Island" title="Rikers Island">Rikers Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rapgenius.com_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rapgenius.com-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Although some fans have interpreted these lines as references to Shakur's murder, XXL Magazine wrote that the song was most likely recorded before 2Pac's death.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_references">Other references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life_After_Death&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In "Going Back to Cali" the second verse opens up with Biggie's thoughts on the inter-coastal war and his relationship with the West Coast:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>If I got to choose a coast I got to choose the East<br />
I live out there, so don't go there<br />
But that don't mean a nigga can't rest in the West<br />
See some nice breasts in the West<br />
Smoke some nice sess in the West, y'all niggas is a mess<br />
Thinkin' I'm gon' stop, givin' L.A. props<br />
All I got is beef with those that violate me<br />
I shall annihilate thee<br />
Case closed</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the song "<a href="/wiki/Notorious_Thugs" title="Notorious Thugs" class="mw-redirect">Notorious Thugs</a>", B.I.G. clearly refers to long time nemesis <a href="/wiki/2Pac" title="2Pac" class="mw-redirect">2Pac</a> in the line "so called beef with you-know-who", calling the feud between him and Shakur 'bullshit', while <a href="/wiki/Bone_Thugs-N-Harmony" title="Bone Thugs-N-Harmony" class="mw-redirect">Bone Thugs-N-Harmony</a> (who featured 2Pac on one of their songs the same year) throw jabs at <a href="/wiki/Three_6_Mafia" title="Three 6 Mafia">Three 6 Mafia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twista" title="Twista">Twista</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crucial_Conflict" title="Crucial Conflict">Crucial Conflict</a> and <a href="/wiki/Do_or_Die_(group)" title="Do or Die (group)">Do or Die</a>.</p>
<p>The tracks "What's Beef" and "My Downfall", both of which deal with the subject of feuding, as well as "Last Day" and "You're Nobody (Till Somebody Kills You)" are also said to contain apparent jabs aimed at his rivals (including Shakur), even though Biggie stated in a <a href="/wiki/Spin_Magazine" title="Spin Magazine" class="mw-redirect">Spin Magazine</a> interview that the song "You're Nobody (Till Somebody Kills You)" was not directed at Shakur, who at the time had recently been shot.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Life After Death</i> received positive reviews from contemporary <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">music critics</a>. Jon Pareles of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> described the album as "flaunting affluence with a leisurely swagger, midtempo grooves and calmly arrogant raps".<sup id="cite_ref-Pareles_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pareles-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Anthony DeCurtis of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine called it a "conscious continuation of <i><a href="/wiki/Ready_to_Die" title="Ready to Die">Ready to Die</a></i>", and stated "<i>Life After Death</i> captures crime's undeniable glamour but doesn't stint on the fear, desperation and irretrievable loss that the streets inevitably exact".<sup id="cite_ref-DeCurtis_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DeCurtis-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Cheo Hodari Coker from <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> wrote that "<i>Life After Death</i> reflects both the dark and the heartfelt sides of the rapper's <a href="/wiki/Gemini_(astrology)" title="Gemini (astrology)">Gemini</a> personality. It's not only a complex testament to who he was in his private life, but also a demonstration of his amazing rhyming ability. In key moments, B.I.G. does a marvelous job of surfing between accessible music fare tailored for the radio, and more challenging material that will be savored by hard-core rap fans who have long admired B.I.G.'s microphone skills. Rarely has a rapper attempted to please so many different audiences and done it so brilliantly".<sup id="cite_ref-Coker_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coker-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Although David Browne of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> was un-favorable of the album’s long length, and some of its violent and materialistic content, he commended Notorious B.I.G.’s "bicoastal respect" by working with other hip-hop styles and artists from other regions of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Browne_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retrospect">Retrospect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life_After_Death&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Retrospect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since its release, <i>Life After Death</i> has received retrospective acclaim from critics. <a href="/wiki/Rob_Sheffield" title="Rob Sheffield">Rob Sheffield</a>, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stone_Album_Guide" title="The Rolling Stone Album Guide">The Rolling Stone Album Guide</a></i> (2004), called it "a <a href="/wiki/Filler_(media)" title="Filler (media)">filler</a>-free two-disc rush of musical bravado" and commented that the Notorious B.I.G.'s voice and lyrics were "deeper" than before.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoard_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoard-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a>’s Jason Birchmeier stated "It may have taken the Notorious B.I.G. a few years to follow up his milestone debut, <i>Ready to Die</i>, with another album, but when he did return with <i>Life After Death</i>, he did so in a huge way. The ambitious album, intended as somewhat of a sequel to <i>Ready to Die</i>, picked up where its predecessor left off".<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Birchmeier further stated "Over the course of only two albums, he achieved every success imaginable, perhaps none greater than this unabashedly over-reaching success".<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Evan McGarvey of <a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a> wrote in his review "<i>Life After Death</i> is a grand exercise in personal mythology, narrative sweep, and truly diverse, universal pop excellence. As a double album it is the very definition of cinematic; it essentially perfected the concept and standard in hip-hop ... Sequenced as an unpacking of sorts, the album’s progression from song to song is an essay itself".<sup id="cite_ref-McGarvey_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGarvey-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2013, VIBE named Life After Death the greatest Hip-Hop/R&amp;B album since 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_influence">Legacy and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life_After_Death&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though released in the wake of B.I.G.'s fatal shooting, <i>Life After Death</i> signaled a stylistic change in <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a> as it crossed over to the commercial mainstream. After the release of <i>Life After Death</i>, <a href="/wiki/Puff_Daddy" title="Puff Daddy" class="mw-redirect">Puff Daddy</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Bad_Boy_Records" title="Bad Boy Records">Bad Boy Records</a> continued to bring pop and gangsta rap closer together: the references to violence and drug dealing remained, as did the entire "gangsta" rhetoric, but the overall production style changed from the previously darker sound to a cleaner, <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sample</a>-heavy, more upbeat sound that was directly fashioned for the mainstream pop charts, as seen in the single "Mo Money Mo Problems". The Notorious B.I.G. is often credited with initiating this transition, as he was among the first mainstream rappers to produce albums with a calculated attempt to include both gritty and realistic gangsta narratives as well as more radio-friendly productions. Much of the album is produced by Deric "D-Dot" Angelettie, Carlos "July Six" Broady, Ron "Amen-Ra" Lawrence, and Nashiem Myrick. However, other notable hip-hop producers such as <a href="/wiki/Easy_Mo_Bee" title="Easy Mo Bee">Easy Mo Bee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Havoc_(Mobb_Deep)" title="Havoc (Mobb Deep)" class="mw-redirect">Havoc</a> from <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a> and <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA" class="mw-redirect">RZA</a> from <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a> contributed beats to the album as well.</p>
<p>Various artists were specifically influenced by several songs on <i>Life After Death</i>. <a href="/wiki/Evidence_(artist)" title="Evidence (artist)" class="mw-redirect">Evidence</a>'s "Down in New York City" is essentially "Going Back to Cali" from the perspective of a <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast</a> artist. <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> borrows four bars from "The World Is Filled..." in his song "<a href="/wiki/I_Just_Wanna_Love_U_(Give_It_2_Me)" title="I Just Wanna Love U (Give It 2 Me)">I Just Wanna Love U (Give It 2 Me)</a>", as well as the chorus from his song "Squeeze 1st" from "<a href="/wiki/Hypnotize_(The_Notorious_B.I.G._song)" title="Hypnotize (The Notorious B.I.G. song)">Hypnotize</a>", a line in "The Ruler's Back" from "Kick in the Door" and "You're Nobody ('Til Somebody Kills You)" on "<a href="/wiki/D.O.A._(Death_of_Auto-Tune)" title="D.O.A. (Death of Auto-Tune)">D.O.A. (Death of Auto-Tune)</a>". <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a> borrows the chorus from "Kick in the Door" for his song "Child Support". <a href="/wiki/Monica_(entertainer)" title="Monica (entertainer)" class="mw-redirect">Monica</a> uses the "I Love the Dough" beat and a verse from B.I.G. as featured on 2010's "<a href="/wiki/Everything_to_Me_(Monica_song)" title="Everything to Me (Monica song)">Everything to Me</a>" official remix. On their <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA</a> album, <i><a href="/wiki/Release_Some_Tension" title="Release Some Tension">Release Some Tension</a></i>, R&amp;B trio <a href="/wiki/SWV" title="SWV">SWV</a> sampled the song on as the opening track, "Someone" which features B.I.G's friend Combs which was released five months later. French rapper <a href="/wiki/Rohff" title="Rohff">Rohff</a> named his album "La Vie Avant La Mort" (Life Before Death) (2001) as a tribute to BIG, the album has sold 350,000 copies.</p>
