<p><i><b>Country Grammar</b></i> is the debut <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> rapper <a href="/wiki/Nelly" title="Nelly">Nelly</a>. It was released on June 27, 2000 through <a href="/wiki/Universal_Records" title="Universal Records">Universal Records</a>, who released the album after listening to demos by Nelly and signing a record deal with the rapper in 1999. The majority of the album's production was done so by <a href="/wiki/Jason_%22Jay_E%22_Epperson" title="Jason &quot;Jay E&quot; Epperson">Jason "Jay E" Epperson</a>, with additional production by C-Love, Kevin Law, <a href="/wiki/City_Spud" title="City Spud">City Spud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_%22Blast%22_Wills" title="Steve &quot;Blast&quot; Wills" class="mw-redirect">Steve "Blast" Wills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basement_Beats" title="Basement Beats">Basement Beats</a>. Nelly contributed to all lyrics on the album, with Epperson and City Spud also contributing. The album is predominantly <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">Southern hip hop</a> based, and introduces Nelly's vocal style of <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>-<a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">rap</a> singalongs and Midwestern, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> twang.</p>
<p>The album produced four successful singles: "<a href="/wiki/Country_Grammar_(Hot_Shit)" title="Country Grammar (Hot Shit)">Country Grammar (Hot Shit)</a>", "<a href="/wiki/E.I._(song)" title="E.I. (song)">E.I.</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Ride_wit_Me" title="Ride wit Me">Ride wit Me</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Batter_Up" title="Batter Up">Batter Up</a>". Its lead single, "Country Grammar (Hot Shit)", peaked at number seven on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>. "E.I." charted at number sixteen, twelve and eleven on the Hot 100, UK Singles Chart and <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Singles_Chart" title="ARIA Singles Chart" class="mw-redirect">ARIA Singles Chart</a>, respectively. "Ride wit Me" peaked within the top five on the Hot 100, ARIA Singles Chart, <a href="/wiki/Irish_Singles_Chart" title="Irish Singles Chart">Irish Singles Chart</a> and UK Singles Chart. The album's final single, "Batter Up" featuring <a href="/wiki/Murphy_Lee" title="Murphy Lee">Murphy Lee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ali_(rapper)" title="Ali (rapper)">Ali</a>, achieved moderate chart success.</p>
<p><i>Country Grammar</i> received positive reviews, with critics praising Nelly's vocal style and the album's production. It topped the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart for five consecutive weeks, and the US <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart for six consecutive weeks. It peaked in the top five on the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_New_Zealand" title="Recording Industry Association of New Zealand" class="mw-redirect">New Zealand Albums Chart</a> and <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Charts" title="ARIA Charts">Australian Albums Chart</a>, as well as the top ten on the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Albums_Chart" title="Canadian Albums Chart">Canadian Albums Chart</a> and <a href="/wiki/MegaCharts" title="MegaCharts">Dutch Albums Chart</a>. The album was certified three times platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_New_Zealand" title="Recording Industry Association of New Zealand" class="mw-redirect">Recording Industry Association of New Zealand</a> (RIANZ) and <a href="/wiki/Music_Canada" title="Music Canada">Music Canada</a> (MC), denoting shipments of 45,000 and 300,000 copies, respectively.</p>
<p>By May 18, 2013, <i>Country Grammar</i> had sold 8.5 million copies, according to <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_SoundScan" title="Nielsen SoundScan">Nielsen SoundScan</a>, ranking it as the ninth best selling rap album of all time in the US. It was certified nine times platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), making it one of the highest certified albums in the US history. Its commercial success secured Nelly's status as one of the most successful hip hop acts of the 2000s decade. On <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s decade-end chart, Nelly ranked as the third most successful act of the 2000–2009 decade, largely in part to the success of <i>Country Grammar</i> and his follow-up album <i><a href="/wiki/Nellyville" title="Nellyville">Nellyville</a></i> (2002).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_production">Background and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Country_Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Inside_the_H3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Inside_the_H3.jpg/250px-Inside_the_H3.jpg" width="250" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Inside_the_H3.jpg/375px-Inside_the_H3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Inside_the_H3.jpg/500px-Inside_the_H3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2464" data-file-height="1632" /></a>

Production trio <a href="/wiki/Basement_Beats" title="Basement Beats">Basement Beats</a> contributed to <i>Country Grammar</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s production.</div>

<p>In his early years, <a href="/wiki/Nelly" title="Nelly">Nelly</a> frequently moved locations within the US, before residing in the <a href="/wiki/Independent_city_(United_States)" title="Independent city (United States)">city</a> of <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-background_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-background-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It was there where he discovered rap artists synonymous within the city; in his teenage years, Nelly was moved to a University City, where he shifted his aspirations of becoming a Baseball player, to producing stories and rhymes.<sup id="cite_ref-background_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-background-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Along with some <a href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high school</a> friends—<a href="/wiki/Ali_(rapper)" title="Ali (rapper)">Ali Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murphy_Lee" title="Murphy Lee">Torhi Harper</a>, Kyjuan and <a href="/wiki/City_Spud" title="City Spud">Lavell Webb</a>—Nelly formed the group <a href="/wiki/St._Lunatics" title="St. Lunatics">St. Lunatics</a> in 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-background_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-background-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Frustrated by the lack of attention from record companies, the group formed the consensus to allow Nelly to pursue a solo career, with the rest possibly releasing their own solo albums.<sup id="cite_ref-background_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-background-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Nelly produced demos, which were sent to national labels, eventually gaining attention from <a href="/wiki/Universal_Records" title="Universal Records">Universal Records</a> who signed a record deal with Nelly and the St. Lunatics in 1999, with Universal releasing the former's debut album, <i>Country Grammar</i>, in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-vibe_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vibe-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-background_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-background-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> All songs on <i>Country Grammar</i> were <a href="/wiki/Recording_engineering" title="Recording engineering" class="mw-redirect">recorded</a> by Steve Eigner, and <a href="/wiki/Mixing_engineer" title="Mixing engineer">mixed</a> by Rich Travali. Kenny Dystra provided recording assistance and Jason Standard assisted in mixing.<sup id="cite_ref-liner_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liner-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Audio_mastering" title="Audio mastering">Mastering</a> was performed by Herb Powers, with <a href="/wiki/Artists_and_repertoire" title="Artists and repertoire">A&amp;R</a> directed by Kevin Law and Coordinated by Craig Yoskowitz.<sup id="cite_ref-liner_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liner-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Management was provided by Tony Davis and Courtney Benson, with legality handled by Todd Rubenstein. Creative direction was handled by Sandra Brummels, with design and photography done so by BENTO Design and Jonathen Mannion, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-liner_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liner-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Country_Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Much of Nelly's rap style draws from his origins, as it contains <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">Southern drawl</a> with Midwestern,<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier1-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> twang,<sup id="cite_ref-Shapiro.2C_Peter_2005.2C_p._274_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shapiro.2C_Peter_2005.2C_p._274-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> that incorporates both <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a> and urban styles.<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier1-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> In conjunction, Nelly approaches a <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>-<a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">rap</a> singalong vocal style, which <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a>'s Jason Birchmeier notes present within <i>Country Grammar</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s tracks including "<a href="/wiki/Ride_wit_Me" title="Ride wit Me">Ride wit Me</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/E.I._(song)" title="E.I. (song)">E.I.</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Shapiro_(journalist)" title="Peter Shapiro (journalist)">Peter Shapiro</a> described Nelly's vocals as using "unforgettable <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hooks</a> based on schoolyard songs, double-dutch chants, and nonsense rhymes".<sup id="cite_ref-Shapiro.2C_Peter_2005.2C_p._274_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shapiro.2C_Peter_2005.2C_p._274-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Much of <i>Country Grammar</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s tracks are bass-heavy,<sup id="cite_ref-Browne_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ex_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ex-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> that are primarily Southern hip-hop based and minimalistic.<sup id="cite_ref-People_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-People-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In the album's self-titled track, Nelly's vocals are slurred and slow,<sup id="cite_ref-nme_review_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nme_review-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and are a "smooth, slippery-fast instrument" with "<a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a> inflections".<sup id="cite_ref-Browne_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> found the song's lyrics to depict Nelly "riding around town in an expensive SUV with an assault weapon".<sup id="cite_ref-Ex_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ex-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Ride_wit_Me" title="Ride wit Me">Ride wit Me</a>" is rap, pop crossover,<sup id="cite_ref-nme_review_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nme_review-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> that samples composition from <a href="/wiki/DeBarge" title="DeBarge">DeBarge</a>'s 1982 "<a href="/wiki/I_Like_It_(DeBarge_song)" title="I Like It (DeBarge song)">I Like It</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-liner_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liner-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and its lyrics feature Nelly introspecting.<sup id="cite_ref-Browne_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Rolling Stone</i> found the chorus of "E.I." to contain the vocal style of rapper <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ex_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ex-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> "Luven Me" samples "Don't Stop (Ever Loving Me)" and "<a href="/wiki/Whatever_You_Want_(Tony!_Toni!_Ton%C3%A9!_song)" title="Whatever You Want (Tony! Toni! Toné! song)">Whatever You Want</a>" from bands <a href="/wiki/One_Way_(American_band)" title="One Way (American band)">One Way</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony!_Toni!_Ton%C3%A9!" title="Tony! Toni! Toné!">Tony! Toni! Toné!</a>, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-liner_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liner-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> interpreted "Luven Me" as a "virtual rewrite" of rapper <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a>'s 1995 "<a href="/wiki/Dear_Mama" title="Dear Mama">Dear Mama</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-nme_review_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nme_review-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Country_Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Country_Grammar_(Hot_Shit)" title="Country Grammar (Hot Shit)">Country Grammar (Hot Shit)</a>" was released as the album's first single on February 29, 2000 in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The song was written by Nelly and <a href="/wiki/Jason_%22Jay_E%22_Epperson" title="Jason &quot;Jay E&quot; Epperson">Jason "Jay E" Epperson</a>, and produced by the latter.<sup id="cite_ref-liner_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liner-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Its lyrics are based off the children <a href="/wiki/Clapping_game" title="Clapping game">clapping game</a> <a href="/wiki/Down_Down_Baby" title="Down Down Baby">Down Down Baby</a>. "Country Grammar (Hot Shit)" peaked at number seven on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-uksingle_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uksingle-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Billboard100_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard100-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> It also reached number ten and twenty on the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Hot_100" title="Canadian Hot 100">Canadian Hot 100</a> and <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Singles_Chart" title="ARIA Singles Chart" class="mw-redirect">ARIA Singles Chart</a>, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Canada100_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canada100-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aus_singles_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus_singles-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The song was certified gold by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA) and <a href="/wiki/Australian_Recording_Industry_Association" title="Australian Recording Industry Association">Australian Recording Industry Association</a> (ARIA).<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aus_cert_2001_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus_cert_2001-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The second single, "<a href="/wiki/E.I._(song)" title="E.I. (song)">E.I.</a>", was written by Nelly and Epperson and produced by the latter.<sup id="cite_ref-liner_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liner-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It reached number sixteen on the Hot 100,<sup id="cite_ref-Billboard100_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard100-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> number eleven on the UK Singles Chart and number twelve on the ARIA Singles Chart.<sup id="cite_ref-uksingle_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uksingle-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ride_wit_me_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ride_wit_me-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> It was certified gold by the ARIA.<sup id="cite_ref-aus_cert_2001_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus_cert_2001-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Written by Nelly and Epperson and produced by the latter, "<a href="/wiki/Ride_wit_Me" title="Ride wit Me">Ride wit Me</a>" was released as <i>Country Grammar</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s third single. The track features <a href="/wiki/City_Spud" title="City Spud">City Spud</a>, and reached number three on the Hot 100, the highest charting song from <i>Country Grammar</i> in the US. It also peaked at number three on the UK Singles chart,<sup id="cite_ref-uksingle_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uksingle-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> number four on the ARIA Singles Chart,<sup id="cite_ref-ride_wit_me_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ride_wit_me-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Irish_Recorded_Music_Association" title="Irish Recorded Music Association">Irish Singles Chart</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ride_wit_me_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ride_wit_me-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> number five on the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Top_40" title="Dutch Top 40">Dutch Singles Chart</a> and number seven on the <a href="/wiki/VG-lista" title="VG-lista">Norwegian Singles Chart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ride_wit_me_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ride_wit_me-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> It was certified gold by the RIAA and platinum by the ARIA.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aus_cert_2001_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus_cert_2001-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Batter_Up" title="Batter Up">Batter Up</a>" was released as the fourth and final single from the album. It features <a href="/wiki/Murphy_Lee" title="Murphy Lee">Murphy Lee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ali_(rapper)" title="Ali (rapper)">Ali</a>, and was written by Nelly, Epperson and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Wills" title="Steve Wills">Steve "Blast" Wills</a>, while being produced by Wills. "Batter Up" peaked at number eight on the <a href="/wiki/Ultratop_40" title="Ultratop 40" class="mw-redirect">Belgian Singles Chart</a> (Flanders) and number nineteen on the ARIA Singles Chart.<sup id="cite_ref-ride_wit_me_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ride_wit_me-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Country Grammar</i> was well received by <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">music critics</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> lauded the album, praising Nelly's utilization of vocal characteristics from other <a href="/wiki/Rap" title="Rap" class="mw-redirect">rap</a> artists, including <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cypress_Hill" title="Cypress Hill">Cypress Hill</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nme_review_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nme_review-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The magazine described the album as a "rarity", noting stand-out tracks such as the "seductive <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rap</a>/<a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a> crossover" "<a href="/wiki/Ride_wit_Me" title="Ride wit Me">Ride wit Me</a>", while likening "<a href="/wiki/Batter_Up" title="Batter Up">Batter Up</a>" to "<a href="/wiki/DMX_(rapper)" title="DMX (rapper)">DMX</a> with a humour infusion".<sup id="cite_ref-nme_review_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nme_review-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>NME</i> closed their review declaring <i>Country Grammar</i> as "album of the year".<sup id="cite_ref-nme_review_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nme_review-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Giving the album a B–, <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s David Browne wrote the album demonstrates that "tiresome rap topics" are not restricted to "the coasts".<sup id="cite_ref-Browne_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Though what salvages the album is Nelly's "smooth, slippery-fast" voice, which contains "reggae inflections".<sup id="cite_ref-Browne_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Browne describes the album's content as minimalistic, with "introspective moments" such as "Ride wit Me".<sup id="cite_ref-Browne_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a>'s <i>Consumer Guide</i> review, he wrote that Nelly presents an "easy mix of N.O. Bounce, Cleveland thug harmony, and L.A. tweedle-twaddle", noting Nelly's heavy accent which makes his <a href="/wiki/Hedonism" title="Hedonism">hedonism</a> more accessible.<sup id="cite_ref-Robert_Christgau_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Christgau-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/People_(magazine)" title="People (magazine)">People</a></i> noted that Nelly implements his own "laid-back charm" to <i>Country Grammar</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Southern_hip_hop" title="Southern hip hop">southern hip hop</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-People_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-People-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite Nelly showing "limited thematic vocabulary" he articulates <a href="/wiki/Escapism" title="Escapism">escapism</a> to compensate for this.<sup id="cite_ref-People_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-People-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a>'s Jason Birchmeier praised the album's "tongue-twisting" self-titled track as "infectious", noting other tracks to contain the same elements, including "Ride wit Me" and "<a href="/wiki/E.I._(song)" title="E.I. (song)">E.I.</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Birchmeier wrote that the album transcends regional styles such as southern hip hop to appeal to rap-pop audiences, while praising producer <a href="/wiki/Jason_%22Jay_E%22_Epperson" title="Jason &quot;Jay E&quot; Epperson">Jason "Jay E" Epperson</a>'s contribution to the album.<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> explained that the album's "liquid bass bumps" interlopes well with Nelly's "wordplay-heavy sing-song rhyme-flow", while declaring <i>Country Grammar</i> to be the best thing to come out of <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a> since <a href="/wiki/Comedian" title="Comedian">comedian</a> <a href="/wiki/Redd_Foxx" title="Redd Foxx">Redd Foxx</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ex_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ex-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/2001_Soul_Train_Music_Awards" title="2001 Soul Train Music Awards">2001 Soul Train Music Awards</a>, <i>Country Grammar</i> earned Nelly the award for Best R&amp;B/Soul or Rap New Artist.<sup id="cite_ref-soul_train_award_2001_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soul_train_award_2001-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Nelly was nominated for four awards at the Online Hip-Hop Awards, for Favorite Music Video, Song of the Year (both for "E.I."), Album of the Year (for <i>Country Grammar</i>) and Outstanding Graphic Design for his official website.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> He won the awards for Song of the Year and Artist of the Year.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Country_Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Country Grammar</i> entered the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> on the week of July 15, 2000, at number three, selling 235,000 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The album remained within the top ten for several weeks before topping it on the week of August 17, 2000, selling 235,000 copies and passing 1.5 million in sales that week.<sup id="cite_ref-ew_second-week_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ew_second-week-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> put the album's sales down to its support by its lead single, "<a href="/wiki/Country_Grammar_(Hot_Shit)" title="Country Grammar (Hot Shit)">Country Grammar (Hot Shit)</a>", as well as the lack of releases from other music artists during that period.<sup id="cite_ref-ew_second-week_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ew_second-week-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The album sold 235,000 copies again the following week, continuing to stay atop the <i>Billboard</i> 200.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Country Grammar</i> topped US Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums chart for six consecutive weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-R.26BUSalbum_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R.26BUSalbum-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> By October 30, 2000, the album had sold three million copies, and was certified three-times platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), denoting shipments of three million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-jet_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jet-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> By June 29, 2002, <i>Country Grammar</i> had sold 7.7 million copies, according to <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_SoundScan" title="Nielsen SoundScan">Nielsen SoundScan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard_mag_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard_mag-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was certified nine-times platinum by the RIAA on April 27, 2004, denoting shipments of nine million copies. By May 18, 2013, <i>Country Grammar</i> had sold 8.5 million copies in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-CGsales_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CGsales-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Country Grammar</i> entered the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_New_Zealand" title="Recording Industry Association of New Zealand" class="mw-redirect">New Zealand Albums Chart</a> on the week of September 24, 2000, at number five.<sup id="cite_ref-nz_chart_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nz_chart-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> It reached its peak at number three, twenty-eight weeks after its debut on the chart, where it remained there for two non-consecutive weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-nz_chart_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nz_chart-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> It stayed on the chart for forty-two weeks before dropping out.<sup id="cite_ref-nz_chart_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nz_chart-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was certified three times platinum by the Recording Industry Association of New Zealand (RIANZ) for shipments of 45,000 units. On the <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Charts" title="ARIA Charts">Australian Albums Chart</a>, <i>Country Grammar</i> debuted at number forty-five.<sup id="cite_ref-aus_chart_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus_chart-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> It re-entered the chart five times, and reached its peak at number four on the week of October 7, 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-aus_chart_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus_chart-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The album remained on the chart for thirty-three weeks, and was certified three times platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Recording_Industry_Association" title="Australian Recording Industry Association">Australian Recording Industry Association</a> (ARIA).<sup id="cite_ref-aus_chart_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus_chart-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Country Grammar</i> reached number seven on the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Albums_Chart" title="Canadian Albums Chart">Canadian Albums Chart</a>, and stayed on the chart for twenty-five weeks before dropping out.<sup id="cite_ref-Canada_Charts_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canada_Charts-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> It was certified three times platinum by <a href="/wiki/Music_Canada" title="Music Canada">Music Canada</a> (MC), denoting shipments of 300,000 copies. On the <a href="/wiki/MegaCharts" title="MegaCharts">Dutch Albums Chart</a>, the album peaked at number eight.<sup id="cite_ref-ultratop50_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ultratop50-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a>, <i>Country Grammar</i> reached number fourteen, and stayed on it for thirty-one weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-ukchart_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ukchart-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> It was certified gold by the <a href="/wiki/British_Phonographic_Industry" title="British Phonographic Industry">British Phonographic Industry</a> (BPI), for shipments of 100,000 units. The album peaked within the top thirty on the <a href="/wiki/Ultratop" title="Ultratop">Belgian Albums Chart</a> (Flanders), <a href="/wiki/The_Official_Finnish_Charts" title="The Official Finnish Charts">Finnish Albums Chart</a> and <a href="/wiki/IFPI_Denmark" title="IFPI Denmark">Danish Albums Chart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ultratop50_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ultratop50-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> It reached number 45 on the <a href="/wiki/Media_Control_Charts" title="Media Control Charts">German Albums Chart</a>, remaining on it for forty-three weeks. It peaked on the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Music_Charts" title="Swiss Music Charts" class="mw-redirect">Swiss Albums Chart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syndicat_National_de_l%27%C3%89dition_Phonographique" title="Syndicat National de l'Édition Phonographique">French Albums Chart</a> at number ninety and 109, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-ultratop50_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ultratop50-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Country Grammar</i> topped the US Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums year-end chart in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-R.26BUSalbum_Year_end_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R.26BUSalbum_Year_end-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Country_Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Country Grammar</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s success cemented Nelly's position as one of the most successful hip hop acts of the emerging decade.<sup id="cite_ref-jet_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jet-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-billboard_mag_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard_mag-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vibe_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vibe-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> emphasized Nelly's expeditious fame, writing that the rapper debuted without the benefit of "guest spots or <a href="/wiki/Pen_%26_Pixel" title="Pen &amp; Pixel">Pen and Pixel</a> produced teasers on his CD cover".<sup id="cite_ref-vibe_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vibe-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The magazine continued to note the rapper's absence of being associated with a notable group, "he just came out and sold two million records in less than a month".<sup id="cite_ref-vibe_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vibe-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Nelly's success helped in making <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a> more notable for emerging hip hop acts, increasing the city's general reputation.<sup id="cite_ref-vibe_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vibe-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jet_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jet-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Country Grammar</i> experienced commercial success, topping the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart for five weeks in 2000, eventually going on to sell 8.5 million copies in the US, making it the ninth best-selling rap album of all time in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-CGsales_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CGsales-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> It is one of the highest certified albums in the US history, being certified nine times platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Nelly ranked third on the <i>Billboard</i> 2000–2009 decade-end chart, due to the success of <i>Country Grammar</i> as well as his follow-up album <i><a href="/wiki/Nellyville" title="Nellyville">Nellyville</a></i> (2002).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> The latter album went on to sell 6,488,000 copies in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
