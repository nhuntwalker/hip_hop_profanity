<p><i><b>Get Rich or Die Tryin<span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span></b></i> is the debut <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> of American rapper <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>, released February 6, 2003, on <a href="/wiki/Aftermath_Entertainment" title="Aftermath Entertainment">Aftermath Entertainment</a> under a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Shady_Records" title="Shady Records">Shady Records</a> and distribution by <a href="/wiki/Interscope_Records" title="Interscope Records">Interscope Records</a>. Its initially planned release was pushed seven days ahead due to heavy bootlegging and <a href="/wiki/Internet_leak" title="Internet leak">Internet leakage</a>. The album featured production from <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>, among others and featured guest appearances from Eminem and then-newcomers <a href="/wiki/Young_Buck" title="Young Buck">Young Buck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Banks" title="Lloyd Banks">Lloyd Banks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Yayo" title="Tony Yayo">Tony Yayo</a>.</p>
<p>The album debuted at number one on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 872,000 copies in its first week.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It spawned four singles, including the number 1-hits "<a href="/wiki/In_da_Club" title="In da Club">In da Club</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/21_Questions" title="21 Questions">21 Questions</a>", and the international single "<a href="/wiki/P.I.M.P." title="P.I.M.P.">P.I.M.P.</a>". By December 2003, <i>Get Rich or Die Tryin<span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span></i> had sold six million copies in the United States and was certified eight times platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>. As of 2013, it has sold nine million copies in the US, making it the fourth highest selling hip hop album of all time in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-Yahoo_Music_01.2F30.2F11_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yahoo_Music_01.2F30.2F11-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Upon its release, the album received positive reviews from most music critics and was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Grammy Award for Best Rap Album</a>, losing to <a href="/wiki/OutKast" title="OutKast" class="mw-redirect">OutKast</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Speakerboxxx/The_Love_Below" title="Speakerboxxx/The Love Below">Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</a></i> at the <a href="/wiki/46th_Grammy_Awards" title="46th Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">46th Grammy Awards</a>. It ranked Number 37 on <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a> magazine's list of best albums of the 2000's.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Get_Rich_or_Die_Tryin%27_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2002, <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a> listened to a copy of 50 Cent's <i><a href="/wiki/Guess_Who%27s_Back%3F" title="Guess Who's Back?">Guess Who's Back?</a></i> mixtape album through Jackson's attorney, who was working with Eminem's manager <a href="/wiki/Paul_Rosenberg_(Music_Manager)" title="Paul Rosenberg (Music Manager)" class="mw-redirect">Paul Rosenberg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dubcnn_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dubcnn-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> After being impressed with the mixtape, Eminem invited 50 Cent to Los Angeles where he was introduced to producer <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> 50 Cent signed a one million dollar record deal with Dr. Dre and released his next mixtape, <i><a href="/wiki/No_Mercy,_No_Fear" title="No Mercy, No Fear">No Mercy, No Fear</a></i>. It featured the <i><a href="/wiki/8_Mile_(soundtrack)" title="8 Mile (soundtrack)" class="mw-redirect">8 Mile</a></i> single, "<a href="/wiki/Wanksta" title="Wanksta">Wanksta</a>", which was later put on <i>Get Rich or Die Tryin<span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span></i>. Both Eminem and Dr. Dre had started working-productions on his debut album with additional help from producers <a href="/wiki/Mike_Elizondo" title="Mike Elizondo">Mike Elizondo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sha_Money_XL" title="Sha Money XL">Sha Money XL</a> among others. The first single "<a href="/wiki/In_da_Club" title="In da Club">In da Club</a>" was the first of seven tracks he recorded in five days with Dr. Dre. The next single "<a href="/wiki/21_Questions" title="21 Questions">21 Questions</a>" was not in line to be on the album to Dr. Dre, he stated that he did not want the song on the album. According to 50 Cent, "Dre was, like, 'How you goin' to be gangsta this and that and then put this sappy love song on?'"<sup id="cite_ref-MSNBC_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> 50 Cent responded saying, "I'm two people. I've always had to be two people since I was a kid, to get by. To me that's not diversity, it's necessity."<sup id="cite_ref-MSNBC_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> "Back Down" and "Heat" were instrumentals originally composed by <a href="/wiki/Rakim" title="Rakim">Rakim</a>, Tommy Coster and <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>. They were both originally intended to be used on Rakim's debut <a href="/wiki/Aftermath_Entertainment" title="Aftermath Entertainment">Aftermath</a> album, <i>Oh My God,</i> but due to creative differences was not released. Early pressings of <i>Get Rich or Die Tryin'</i> included a limited edition bonus DVD.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Singles">Singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Get_Rich_or_Die_Tryin%27_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album's lead single, "<a href="/wiki/In_da_Club" title="In da Club">In da Club</a>", was certified platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), becoming 50 Cents' first song to top the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> for nine weeks and remained on the charts for twenty-two weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aCharts_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aCharts-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The track also reached number one on the <a href="/wiki/Top_40_Tracks" title="Top 40 Tracks">Top 40 Tracks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Tracks" title="Hot Rap Tracks" class="mw-redirect">Hot Rap Tracks</a> charts.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The song reached number one in Denmark, Germany, Ireland, and Switzerland and the top five in Austria, Belgium, Finland, Greece, Norway, Sweden, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom. It received two <a href="/wiki/Grammy" title="Grammy" class="mw-redirect">Grammy</a> nominations for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Male_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Male Rap Solo Performance">Best Male Rap Solo Performance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Song" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Song">Best Rap Song</a>. This is considered to be one of the best rap songs of all time, introducing a new sound and rhythm to rap. It was listed at number 18 on VH1's "100 Greatest Hip-Hop Songs of All Time".</p>
<p>Its second single, "<a href="/wiki/21_Questions" title="21 Questions">21 Questions</a>", became 50 Cent's second chart topper on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, where it remained for four non-consecutive weeks. It spent seven weeks on top of the <i>Billboard</i> <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> charts. Outside the States, "21 Questions" reached number six in the United Kingdom. It was certified gold by the RIAA. The third single "<a href="/wiki/P.I.M.P." title="P.I.M.P.">P.I.M.P.</a>" was shipped with a remix featuring rapper <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a> and trio-group <a href="/wiki/G-Unit" title="G-Unit">G-Unit</a>. It was the third single that peaked at number three on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100 and number one on "Hot Rap Tracks", becoming the third single from the album to peak in the top then on the "Hot 100" chart. It also reached number one in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. It was certified Gold by RIAA. The album's final single, "<a href="/wiki/If_I_Can%27t" title="If I Can't">If I Can't</a>", peaked at number seventy-six on the Billboard Hot 100 and thirty-four on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> charts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Get_Rich_or_Die_Tryin%27_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Get Rich or Die Tryin'</i> debuted at number one on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, selling 872,000 copies in its first week.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> On December 9, 2003, the album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">sextuple platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>, for shipments of six million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> It was among the best selling albums of the decade, selling 12 million copies by the end of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-TheCurrent_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheCurrent-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> It remains as 50 Cent's best-selling album to date, with sales of over 9,000,000 copies in the United States making it the fourth highest selling rap album of all-time in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> In Russia, the album was certified 5x platinum, selling 120,000 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Viewed as a classic upon its release,<sup id="cite_ref-Ex_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ex-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Get Rich or Die Tryin<span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span></i> has been called the most hyped rap debut in over a decade.<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, it holds an aggregate score of 73 out of 100, based on 19 reviews, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a> writer Jason Birchmeier called the album impressive and incredibly calculated in his positive four star review.<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Christian Hoard commended 50 Cent's undeniable showcase of skill, radio-ready tracks, and marketable thug persona in addition to the album's production for its dark synth grooves, buzzy keyboards, and persistently funky bounce.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoard_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoard-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Brett Berliner of <a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a> gave <i>Get Rich or Die Tryin'</i> a B rating and commended 50 Cent for his variety as a rapper.<sup id="cite_ref-Berliner_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berliner-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> It is one of only 19 rap albums to receive a perfect rating from <i><a href="/wiki/XXL_(magazine)" title="XXL (magazine)">XXL</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-XXL_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> In his consumer guide for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave the album a two-star honorable mention (<span style="white-space:nowrap" title="(2-star Honorable Mention)"><img alt="(2-star Honorable Mention)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Black_star.svg/11px-Black_star.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Black_star.svg/17px-Black_star.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Black_star.svg/22px-Black_star.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="110" data-file-height="110" /><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Black_star.svg/11px-Black_star.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Black_star.svg/17px-Black_star.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Black_star.svg/22px-Black_star.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="110" data-file-height="110" /></span>),<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> indicating a "likable effort consumers attuned to its overriding aesthetic or individual vision may well enjoy."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> He cited "What Up Gangsta" and "Patiently Waiting" as highlights and quipped, "Gets no cuter as his character unfolds."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Get_Rich_or_Die_Tryin%27_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/In_da_Club" title="In da Club">In da Club</a>" earned the number-one spot on <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> 2003's single and album of the year, the first since <a href="/wiki/Ace_of_Base" title="Ace of Base">Ace of Base</a> had both in the same year. Album cut "Back Down" was listed on <i><a href="/wiki/XXL_(magazine)" title="XXL (magazine)">XXL</a></i>'s list of the greatest diss tracks of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> In December 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> magazine ranked <i>Get Rich or Die Tryin'</i> at number 12 on the magazine's Top 200 Albums of the Decade.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012 <a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a> named <i>Get Rich or Die Tryin</i> one of the classic albums of the last decade.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
