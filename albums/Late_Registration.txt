<p><i><b>Late Registration</b></i> is the second studio album by American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, released on August 30, 2005, by <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a>. Recording sessions for the album took place over the course of a year at <a href="/wiki/Record_Plant_Studios" title="Record Plant Studios" class="mw-redirect">Record Plant Studios</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chalice_Recording_Studios" title="Chalice Recording Studios">Chalice Recording Studios</a>, and Grandmaster Recording Studios in Hollywood, and at <a href="/wiki/Sony_Music_Studios" title="Sony Music Studios">Sony Music Studios</a> in New York City. West collaborated with American record producer and composer <a href="/wiki/Jon_Brion" title="Jon Brion">Jon Brion</a> to produce <i>Late Registration</i>, and the album features guest contributions from artists such as <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lupe_Fiasco" title="Lupe Fiasco">Lupe Fiasco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Foxx" title="Jamie Foxx">Jamie Foxx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brandy_(entertainer)" title="Brandy (entertainer)" class="mw-redirect">Brandy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adam_Levine" title="Adam Levine">Adam Levine</a>, among others.</p>
<p>The production is notably more lush and elaborate than West's previous album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_College_Dropout" title="The College Dropout">The College Dropout</a></i>. By collaborating with <a href="/wiki/Jon_Brion" title="Jon Brion">Jon Brion</a>, West was able to utilize production styles and instruments not commonly associated with hip-hop music. More intricate sampling methods and the inclusion of <a href="/wiki/String_arrangement" title="String arrangement" class="mw-redirect">string arrangements</a> meant that the production featured on the album was unique to hip-hop music. West sought to deliver lyrics that allowed him to continue his style of storytelling during songs, and the album covers topics of social issues, such as poverty, drug trafficking, health care, and <a href="/wiki/Blood_diamond" title="Blood diamond">blood diamond</a> trades.</p>
<p>The album produced five singles, including the international hits "<a href="/wiki/Touch_the_Sky" title="Touch the Sky">Touch the Sky</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Heard_%27Em_Say" title="Heard 'Em Say">Heard 'Em Say</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Gold_Digger_(Kanye_West_song)" title="Gold Digger (Kanye West song)">Gold Digger</a>", the last of which topped the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> at number one. Music videos for all five singles were produced. West supported the album with a promotional concert tour, and the live album <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Orchestration" title="Late Orchestration">Late Orchestration</a></i>. <i>Late Registration</i> debuted at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, selling 860,000 copies in its first week. The album has sold over 3.1 million units in the United States and has been certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">triple platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>.</p>
<p>Upon its release, <i>Late Registration</i> received rave reviews from music critics and earned West several accolades, including a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Grammy Award for Best Rap Album</a> at the <a href="/wiki/2006_Grammy_Awards" title="2006 Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">2006 Grammy Awards</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Album_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Album of the Year">Album of the Year</a> nomination. It appeared at the top of several publications year-end lists of top albums. <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> named it the best album of 2005, and included it at number 118 on its list of <a href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time" class="mw-redirect">the 500 Greatest Albums of All Time</a> in 2012.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Registration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Late Registration</i> is the second of Kanye West's planned four education-themed studio albums.<sup id="cite_ref-Pg121_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pg121-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Following the major success of <i>The College Dropout</i>, the album reveals his progression in writing lyrics and an incorporation of a wider range of musical styles.<sup id="cite_ref-Sheffield_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheffield-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> At the time, the focal point of West's production style was the use of sped-up vocal samples from <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a> records.<sup id="cite_ref-Sheffield_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheffield-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> However, due in part to the acclaim of <i><a href="/wiki/The_College_Dropout" title="The College Dropout">The College Dropout</a></i>, such sampling had been much copied by others; with that overuse, and also because West felt he had become too dependent on the technique, he decided to find a new sound.<sup id="cite_ref-Pg121_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pg121-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A longtime fan of the English <a href="/wiki/Trip_hop" title="Trip hop">trip hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Portishead_(band)" title="Portishead (band)">Portishead</a>, West had been significantly influenced by <i><a href="/wiki/Roseland_NYC_Live" title="Roseland NYC Live">Roseland NYC Live</a></i>, the band's 1998 live album with the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="New York Philharmonic Orchestra" class="mw-redirect">New York Philharmonic Orchestra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Praise_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Praise-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Early in his career, the live album had inspired him to incorporate <a href="/wiki/String_arrangement" title="String arrangement" class="mw-redirect">string arrangements</a> into his <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_production" title="Hip hop production">hip hop production</a>. Though West had not been able to afford many live instruments around the time of his debut album, the money from his commercial success enabled him to hire a <a href="/wiki/String_orchestra" title="String orchestra">string orchestra</a> for his second album.<sup id="cite_ref-Praise_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Praise-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> West juxtaposed the lush, intricate <a href="/wiki/Melodies" title="Melodies" class="mw-redirect">melodies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">string section</a> with the hard, pounding drum rhythms of hip-hop, and used the sound for the foundation of his rapping.<sup id="cite_ref-Praise_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Praise-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>West collaborated with American <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">film score</a> composer Jon Brion, who served as the album's co-executive producer for several tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-Pilot_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> West had been exposed to Brion's work while watching the film <i><a href="/wiki/Eternal_Sunshine_of_the_Spotless_Mind" title="Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind">Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind</a></i>, for which Brion had composed music. West was also listening to songs Brion had produced for <i><a href="/wiki/When_the_Pawn..." title="When the Pawn...">When the Pawn...</a></i>, the second studio album of <a href="/wiki/Alternative_music" title="Alternative music" class="mw-redirect">alternative</a> singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Fiona_Apple" title="Fiona Apple">Fiona Apple</a>, another one of West's favorite musicians and sources of musical inspiration for the album.<sup id="cite_ref-Pilot_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Although Brion had no prior experience in creating hip-hop records, he and West found that they could productively work together after their first afternoon in the studio where they discovered that neither confined his musical knowledge and vision to one specific genre.<sup id="cite_ref-Pg124_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pg124-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> When questioned if his presence made <i>Late Registration</i> any less hip-hop, Brion replied, "There are colors and ideas that make [the album] different from average hip-hop, but Kanye is already different from the average hip-hop guy. He's got this sense of pop record-making which is really solid, and he likes tracks with a lot of things going on in them — which is not necessarily common for hip-hop. He was already barking up that tree. This is definitely not just a hip-hop album. But it is also by no means overtly arty, or non-hip-hop. I don't think it's a weird record by any means."<sup id="cite_ref-Pilot_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Registration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

Film composer <a href="/wiki/Jon_Brion" title="Jon Brion">Jon Brion</a> assisted with the album's production.</div>

<p>West took over a year and invested two million dollars towards the construction of <i>Late Registration</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The majority of the recording sessions for the album took place at <a href="/wiki/Sony_Music_Studios" title="Sony Music Studios">Sony Music Studios</a> in New York City and at <a href="/wiki/The_Record_Plant" title="The Record Plant" class="mw-redirect">The Record Plant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_California" title="Hollywood, California" class="mw-redirect">Hollywood, California</a>; other sessions took place at <a href="/wiki/Chalice_Recording_Studios" title="Chalice Recording Studios">Chalice Recording Studios</a> and Grandmaster Recording Studios in Hollywood.<sup id="cite_ref-linernotes_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linernotes-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> He began working in the studio after he finished touring with <a href="/wiki/Usher_(entertainer)" title="Usher (entertainer)">Usher</a> on the <a href="/wiki/R%26B" title="R&amp;B" class="mw-redirect">R&amp;B</a> singer's <i>The Truth Tour</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> By November 2004, West had completed nearly seventy-five percent of the album.<sup id="cite_ref-Elaborate_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elaborate-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> However he felt unsatisfied with its outcome and in March of the following year, he brought in Jon Brion, which drastically altered the project's direction.<sup id="cite_ref-Pilot_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album's recording sessions between West and Brion were largely exploratory, with the two experimenting with a broad spectrum of sounds. West would construct a song's basic structure, bringing in samples, drum beat programming and occasionally unfinished rap verses.<sup id="cite_ref-Pg121_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pg121-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> After brainstorming over the musical direction the album could go, he would then select from a variety of unique instruments that Brion provided (and played) and attempt to incorporate their distinctive sound into the song's texture.<sup id="cite_ref-Pilot_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pg123_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pg123-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> West envisioned the album as like the creation of a film: visualizing the songs as scenes, outlining each in such a way that they efficiently conveyed their respective social or introspective context, and ensuring that all synchronized within the fabric of the complete set.<sup id="cite_ref-Pg121_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pg121-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> This sentiment was shared by Jon Brion who said, "He thinks in frequency ranges. I can recognize when someone sees music architecturally, which is how I work. I see it as a spatial thing: left to right, front to back, up and down. It's animated and it's moving in real time. Kanye has that. He tries things out until it fits, until it sits where it is supposed to sit and everything has the correct emotional function. He has real instincts like any great record-maker."<sup id="cite_ref-Pg123_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pg123-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Late Registration</i> has a diverse number of collaborations for its individual tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-Praise_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Praise-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> West chose his guest artists based on the effect each of their voices had on him when he heard them, citing the serene vocals of <a href="/wiki/Adam_Levine" title="Adam Levine">Adam Levine</a>, the trademark sound of Brandy, and the lyricism of <a href="/wiki/Lupe_Fiasco" title="Lupe Fiasco">Lupe Fiasco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Wall" title="Paul Wall">Paul Wall</a> as primary examples.<sup id="cite_ref-Vh1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vh1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Adam Levine, lead vocalist of <a href="/wiki/Pop_rock" title="Pop rock">pop rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Maroon_5" title="Maroon 5">Maroon 5</a> is featured on the album's opening track, "Heard 'Em Say." The two had previously collaborated when Maroon 5 commissioned West to produce a <a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remix</a> for "<a href="/wiki/This_Love_(Maroon_5_song)" title="This Love (Maroon 5 song)">This Love</a>" and later developed a friendship while sitting together on a flight to Rome for the 2004 <a href="/wiki/MTV_Europe_Music_Awards" title="MTV Europe Music Awards">MTV Europe Music Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WestLevine_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WestLevine-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> While playing songs from his second album on his <a href="/wiki/IPod" title="IPod">iPod</a> for him during the flight, West came across the <a href="/wiki/Demo_(music)" title="Demo (music)">demo</a> for "Heard 'Em Say" to which Levine added a <a href="/wiki/R%26B" title="R&amp;B" class="mw-redirect">R&amp;B</a> <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hook</a> he had recently written and thought was perfect for it.<sup id="cite_ref-WestLevine_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WestLevine-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The track was recorded quickly after the <a href="/wiki/2005_Grammy_Awards" title="2005 Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">2005 Grammy Awards</a> ceremony, as Levine only had a couple of free hours available for time in the studio, and Brion was able to translate the two compositions in a matter of hours.<sup id="cite_ref-Pilot_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>West originally produced and recorded "Gold Digger" in <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>'s home in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia" class="mw-redirect">Atlanta, Georgia</a> for <a href="/wiki/Shawnna" title="Shawnna">Shawnna</a>'s 2004 debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Worth_Tha_Weight" title="Worth Tha Weight" class="mw-redirect">Worth Tha Weight</a></i> and had written the chorus from a female first-person viewpoint. However, Shawnna passed on the song. West rewrote the two verses from a male's point-of-view for himself; about a year later, just before "Gold Digger" was set to be released, adding a third verse, recording and mastering it at <a href="/wiki/Sony_Music_Studios" title="Sony Music Studios">Sony Music Studios</a> in New York in a week.<sup id="cite_ref-Gold_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gold-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> After he went with friend <a href="/wiki/John_Mayer" title="John Mayer">John Mayer</a> to see Foxx's portrayal of <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a> in the 2004 film <i><a href="/wiki/Ray_(film)" title="Ray (film)">Ray</a></i>, West decided to have Foxx sing an <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">interpolation</a> of Charles' song "<a href="/wiki/I_Got_a_Woman" title="I Got a Woman">I Got a Woman</a>" in place of the original sample.<sup id="cite_ref-Gold_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gold-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Once the track was in place, it was layered with additional instruments provided by Brion and individually selected by West.<sup id="cite_ref-Gold_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gold-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>-based rapper Paul Wall appears alongside West and his <a href="/wiki/G.O.O.D._Music" title="G.O.O.D. Music" class="mw-redirect">G.O.O.D. Music</a> label-mate <a href="/wiki/GLC_(rapper)" title="GLC (rapper)">GLC</a> on "<a href="/wiki/Drive_Slow" title="Drive Slow">Drive Slow</a>", which was recorded in Los Angeles after the two had met while posing for a photo shoot in an August issue of <i><a href="/wiki/King_(magazine)" title="King (magazine)">King magazine</a></i> in a spread titled "Coming Kings".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> West had originally wanted <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lankan</a> rapper <a href="/wiki/M.I.A._(artist)" title="M.I.A. (artist)" class="mw-redirect">M.I.A.</a> to appear on the track, but she opted out of the appearance due to a busy schedule.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> "My Way Home" is performed by West's close friend and G.O.O.D. Music associate <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a>, whose sixth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Be_(Common_album)" title="Be (Common album)">Be</a></i> was being produced and recorded by West simultaneously with <i>Late Registration</i>. Certain tracks West originally crafted for <i>Be</i> that Common passed on subsequently ended up on his second album.<sup id="cite_ref-Diamonds_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diamonds-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While the original version of "<a href="/wiki/Diamonds_from_Sierra_Leone" title="Diamonds from Sierra Leone">Diamonds from Sierra Leone</a>" featured West as the sole performer, he decided to record a remix to the song which included guest verse provided by Jay-Z—who had come out of retirement from rapping—after learning of the civil war in <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a> financed by <a href="/wiki/Conflict_diamonds" title="Conflict diamonds" class="mw-redirect">conflict diamonds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Aswad_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aswad-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Both the original and remix versions of "Diamond from Sierra Leone" appear on the album, with the former included as a bonus track. The song contains live drums played by <a href="/wiki/Michel_Gondry" title="Michel Gondry">Michel Gondry</a>, the director of <i><a href="/wiki/Eternal_Sunshine" title="Eternal Sunshine" class="mw-redirect">Eternal Sunshine</a></i> and later the first music video for "Heard 'Em Say", who had visited the studio on a day Brion set up a drum kit.<sup id="cite_ref-Pilot_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WestLevine_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WestLevine-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> According to Jay-Z, West mixed "Diamonds from Sierra Leone" about fourteen times before he felt comfortable to release it as the album's <a href="/wiki/Lead_single" title="Lead single">lead single</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Peek_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peek-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The recording also experienced delay when West and Brion were required to wait two weeks to rent the <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a> that they used for <a href="/wiki/Percussion" title="Percussion" class="mw-redirect">percussion</a> on the song.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>West recorded a verse by rap artist Nas—one of his idol rappers—for the track "We Major" without informing Jay-Z, who at the time was engaged in a <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z_vs._Nas_feud" title="Jay-Z vs. Nas feud" class="mw-redirect">feud with Nas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-beef_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beef-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> G.O.O.D, music label-mate <a href="/wiki/Really_Doe" title="Really Doe">Really Doe</a> also appears on the track, delivering its elongated chorus. West later revealed that part of the reason he created the song was to dismantle the feud between the MCs, which they did later that year.<sup id="cite_ref-beef_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beef-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Hey_Mama_(Kanye_West_song)" title="Hey Mama (Kanye West song)">Hey Mama</a>", dedicated to his mother Donda West,<sup id="cite_ref-Sheffield_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheffield-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> was originally recorded by West as early as 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-ODB_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODB-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Brion ran into some obstruction while conducting a twenty-piece orchestra for "Celebration", as its musicians found themselves giggling at West's humorous lyrics which hampered their playing.<sup id="cite_ref-Pilot_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> On "Roses," West and Brion had some minor discord; Brion initially layered it with keyboard arrangements, only for West to remove his keys along with the beat and completely reconfigure the entire song in such a way that its verses are built around the rhythm formed by his vocals and Brion's arrangements arrive during the choruses. Brion later lightheartedly compared the indecision surrounding the construction of the track to that of <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>'s famous last-minute removal of the <a href="/wiki/Bass_line" title="Bass line" class="mw-redirect">bass line</a> from "<a href="/wiki/When_Doves_Cry" title="When Doves Cry">When Doves Cry</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Pilot_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_style">Music and style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Registration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music and style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width: 24%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 89%; background-color: #FFFFF0;">
<div style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div style="margin:0px 2em;">
<p>West's prize catch, audibly enriching at least half [the] songs, is co-producer Jon Brion ... adding an unprecedented third element to West's proven meld of hitbound <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a> <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hooks</a> and rhythm tracks made or played. There's never been hip-hop so complex and subtle musically.</p>
</div>
<div style="position: absolute; left: 0px; top: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">“</div>
<div style="position: absolute; right: 0px; bottom: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">”</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: right;">— <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a><sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
<p>On <i>Late Registration</i>, West drew inspiration from English <a href="/wiki/Trip_hop" title="Trip hop">trip hop</a> band <a href="/wiki/Portishead_(band)" title="Portishead (band)">Portishead</a> and collaborated with film score composer Jon Brion. The album's music blends West's primary soulful <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> production with Brion's elaborate <a href="/wiki/Chamber_pop" title="Chamber pop" class="mw-redirect">chamber pop</a> orchestration, and experimentally delves into a wide array of different genres, including <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">alto jazz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, R&amp;B, <a href="/wiki/Spoken_word" title="Spoken word">spoken word</a>, <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turntablism" title="Turntablism">turntablism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">western classical</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_soul" title="Psychedelic soul">psychedelic soul</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sheffield_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheffield-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> With the presence of Brion, who conducts a twenty-piece <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestra</a> and plays instruments individually selected by West, the album is largely orchestral in nature, brandishing a euphony of <a href="/wiki/Strings_(music)" title="Strings (music)" class="mw-redirect">string arrangements</a>, piano chords, brass flecks, and horn riffs among other symphonic instrumentation.<sup id="cite_ref-Pilot_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> They also incorporated a myriad of foreign and vintage instruments not typical in popular music, let alone hip hop, such as a <a href="/wiki/Celesta" title="Celesta">celesta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chamberlin" title="Chamberlin">Chamberlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_CS-80" title="Yamaha CS-80">CS-80</a> <a href="/wiki/Analog_synthesizer" title="Analog synthesizer">analog synthesizer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_bell" title="Chinese bell" class="mw-redirect">Chinese bells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berimbau" title="Berimbau">berimbau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vibraphones" title="Vibraphones" class="mw-redirect">vibraphones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marimba" title="Marimba">marimba</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Serena Kim of <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> finds the album's unconventional styles and sudden musical <a href="/wiki/Shift_(music)" title="Shift (music)" class="mw-redirect">shifts</a> of the song structures to be similar to <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> during their experimental era.<sup id="cite_ref-Vibe_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibe-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> described <i>Late Registration</i> as West claiming "the whole world of music as hip-hop turf" chronicling the album as "his mad quest to explode every cliché about hip-hop identity."<sup id="cite_ref-Sheffield_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheffield-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Kim concurred with this sentiment, stating, "West ambitiously attempts to depart from the street sensibilities of <i>Dropout</i> by giving <i>Late Registration</i> a shiny, quasi-<a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alt-pop</a> finish."<sup id="cite_ref-Vibe_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibe-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The album's opening track "Heard 'Em Say" exhibits a cascading piano melody provided by excerpts of "Someone That I Used To Love" as performed by <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Cole" title="Natalie Cole">Natalie Cole</a> embellished over tumbling beats and warped bass as well as acoustic guitar.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The song's intricately composed outro, which adopts new sonic elements such as synthesizer and foreign bells, exemplifies the musical complexity of the album as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> "Touch the Sky" stands as the sole song on the entire album not to feature production by West. The song was produced by fellow <a href="/wiki/Roc-a-Fella" title="Roc-a-Fella" class="mw-redirect">Roc-a-Fella</a> producer <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a>, who uses a slowed-down sample of <a href="/wiki/Curtis_Mayfield" title="Curtis Mayfield">Curtis Mayfield</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Move_on_Up" title="Move on Up" class="mw-redirect">Move on Up</a>" filled with jubilant Latin horn blares and dynamic drum patterns.<sup id="cite_ref-Caramanica_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caramanica-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> "Gold Digger" contains an <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">interpolation</a> of "<a href="/wiki/I_Got_a_Woman" title="I Got a Woman">I Got a Woman</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a> and a bouncy beat formed from handclaps as well as <a href="/wiki/Scratching" title="Scratching">scratches</a> by West's touring DJ <a href="/wiki/A-Trak" title="A-Trak">A-Trak</a>. Towards the end, the song employs vintage 1970s synthesizers which emit a honking sound in <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cadence" class="extiw" title="wikt:cadence">cadence</a> to Kanye's voice.<sup id="cite_ref-Observer_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observer-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> West's production approach comes full circle within "Drive Slow", a song that samples <a href="/wiki/Hank_Crawford" title="Hank Crawford">Hank Crawford</a>'s "Wildflower" and distinctively retains a sluggish but smooth <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">alto jazz</a>-infused drum <a href="/wiki/Music_loop" title="Music loop" class="mw-redirect">loop</a>, antithetical to his once essential sped-up soul style.<sup id="cite_ref-Kellman_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kellman-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fennessey_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fennessey-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The interlude "My Way Home" contains a sample of "Home is Where the Hatred Is" by <a href="/wiki/Gil_Scott-Heron" title="Gil Scott-Heron">Gil Scott-Heron</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-linernotes_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linernotes-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> This facet is accentuated by Common's performance, which pays homage to the poet by the delivering its single verse in a distinctive <a href="/wiki/Proto-rap" title="Proto-rap" class="mw-redirect">proto-rap</a> manner reminiscent of Scott-Heron's influential vocal style. "Crack Music" is sparsely built on incessant <a href="/wiki/Snare_drum" title="Snare drum">snare drum</a> hits and clipped horn blares. The track sees an ephemeral return of West's old production attributes, as it possesses a syncopated martial beat, <a href="/wiki/Gospel_choir" title="Gospel choir" class="mw-redirect">gospel choir</a> symphony, and a <a href="/wiki/Spoken_word" title="Spoken word">spoken word</a> passage within its coda. The poetic "Roses" is partially <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a> in structure, displaying verses rapped over sparse keyboards and a slowed rhythm with the music arriving at the chorus, which features additional vocals, trumpet riffs, electric guitar phrasings, and finally a line sampled from <a href="/wiki/Bill_Withers" title="Bill Withers">Bill Withers</a>' "Rosie". "Bring Me Down" carries a bombast dramatic air, as it holds more orchestration than any other track on <i>Late Registration</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-linernotes_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linernotes-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, it features an <a href="/wiki/Overdubbing" title="Overdubbing">overdubbing</a> of Brandy's vocals to create a <a href="/wiki/Chorus_effect" title="Chorus effect">chorus effect</a>, a recording technique in which her lone voice produces the illusion of a <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choir</a> singing <a href="/wiki/Harmony" title="Harmony">harmonies</a> during the choruses.<sup id="cite_ref-Pareles_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pareles-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> The up-tempo arrangement of "Addiction" contains ambient, rhythmic guitar <a href="/wiki/Lick_(music)" title="Lick (music)">licks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Congas" title="Congas" class="mw-redirect">congas</a>, filtered <a href="/wiki/Hi-hat" title="Hi-hat">hi-hats</a> and a sampled line from "My Funny Valentine" as performed by <a href="/wiki/Etta_James" title="Etta James">Etta James</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> All the while, West's <a href="/wiki/Overdubbed" title="Overdubbed" class="mw-redirect">overdubbed</a> vocals reverbs in and out of the track.<sup id="cite_ref-Pareles_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pareles-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> For "Diamonds from Sierra Leone", West used a looped vocal sample of the theme song for the 1971 <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> film, <i><a href="/wiki/Diamonds_Are_Forever_(film)" title="Diamonds Are Forever (film)">Diamonds Are Forever</a></i> as performed by <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Bassey" title="Shirley Bassey">Shirley Bassey</a> and layered it with live drums, piano keys, horn stabs, harpsichord arpeggios, and string arrangements that all build in intensity with his voice.<sup id="cite_ref-Observer_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observer-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Late Registration</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s longest track, the seven minute-long "We Major", implements exuberant, amplified backing vocals and a "splashy <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">disco</a> groove" embellished by horn blares, <a href="/wiki/Drone_(music)" title="Drone (music)">droning</a> bassline, and electric piano <a href="/wiki/Glissandos" title="Glissandos" class="mw-redirect">glissandos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sheffield_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheffield-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Henderson_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henderson-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The melody of "Hey Mama" is laced with a <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folksy</a> looped "La-la-la" vocal sample from "Today Won't Come Again" by Donal Leace while its beat is produced by a tin drum. Additionally, it contains <a href="/wiki/Vocoder" title="Vocoder">vocoder</a>-processed background vocals, a <a href="/wiki/Xylophone" title="Xylophone">xylophone</a> solo and a cascading <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synth</a> outro.<sup id="cite_ref-Fennessey_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fennessey-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Opening with an electronic twinkling sound, "Celebration" contains samples of "Heavenly Dream" by The KayGees.<sup id="cite_ref-Pilot_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pilot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> A columnist for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> described it as evoking "the lavish 1970s <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_soul" title="Psychedelic soul">psychedelic soul</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rotary_Connection" title="Rotary Connection">Rotary Connection</a>." Some of the most elaborate orchestral arrangement expressed on the entire album is contained within its closing track "Gone." The composition begins with a vocal sample of "It's Too Late" by <a href="/wiki/Otis_Redding" title="Otis Redding">Otis Redding</a> and a two-chord piano ostinato, followed by a simplistic <a href="/wiki/Funk_music" title="Funk music" class="mw-redirect">funk</a> beat. As the song progresses, its structure gradually morphs and develops more and more musicality. Overtime, the composition assumes ten <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violins</a>, four <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">violas</a> and four <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cellos</a> in the midst of verses, all of which initially come in brief staccato bursts that simply punctuate the rhythm but eventually expand and consolidate into a fully formed <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">string section</a> by the arrival of the harmonic choruses. After its third verse, the song enters an instrumental passage before returning with a fourth verse from West, where the rise and fall of his voice is intricately emulated by the fluctuation of the string orchestra.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrical_content">Lyrical content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Registration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lyrical content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg/220px-Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg" width="220" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg/330px-Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg/440px-Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2551" data-file-height="2631" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jamie_Foxx_with_Kanye_West.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.24wmf6/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
West and Foxx collaborated on track "Gold Digger".</div>

<p>West stated that his goal for the album was to touch on topics that people from all walks of life could find relatable, while remaining true to himself: "[I wanted to have] raps that were just as ill as <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</a> and just as understandable as <a href="/wiki/Will_Smith" title="Will Smith">Will Smith</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Vh1_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vh1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The opening track "<a href="/wiki/Heard_%27Em_Say" title="Heard 'Em Say">Heard 'Em Say</a>" is a "mournfully contemplative" song that "talks about being honest with yourself in a world that is not." The song is written from the perspective of an afflicted, impoverished American quietly lamenting the fallacies of society and questioning the ways of the world around him.<sup id="cite_ref-Pg573_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pg573-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> West delivers a tongue-in-cheek lyrical narrative within "<a href="/wiki/Gold_Digger_(Kanye_West_song)" title="Gold Digger (Kanye West song)">Gold Digger</a>" in which he critically depicts the disastrous life of a man married to a woman who manipulates him for financial gain. However, another story arises within the third verse, which illustrates a once destitute black male who earns a fortune and decides to leave a loyal, unselfish girlfriend for a white girl.<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"Crack Music" continues the avenue of socio-political commentary initiated within "Heard 'Em Say." However, two songs express polar opposite tones; where "Heard 'Em Say" was self-effacing and passive-aggressive, "Crack Music" is audacious and straightforward.<sup id="cite_ref-Vh1_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vh1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> In the song, West dually discusses the spread and devastating impact of <a href="/wiki/Crack_cocaine" title="Crack cocaine">crack cocaine</a> in black communities and champions the sovereignty of music pioneered by black musicians, metaphorically equating their contrarily addictive power and influence on American society.<sup id="cite_ref-Vh1_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vh1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> On the sentimental "Roses", Kanye gives a firsthand account of the hospitalization of his ailing grandmother within a melancholic <a href="/wiki/Poem" title="Poem" class="mw-redirect">poem</a> to produce a critique of the healthcare system.<sup id="cite_ref-Sheffield_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheffield-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The original version of "Diamonds from Sierra Leone" is largely free-associative and is filled with a litany of lyrical punchlines which serve to loosely chronicle his past experiences being a part of the <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella" title="Roc-A-Fella" class="mw-redirect">Roc-A-Fella</a> family, from touring with <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> on his <i>Blueprint Lounge Tour</i> to the label's subsequent fall out and revival.<sup id="cite_ref-Peek_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peek-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> However, West uses the <a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remix</a> to "Diamonds from Sierra Leone" to directly address the issue of "<a href="/wiki/Blood_diamonds" title="Blood diamonds" class="mw-redirect">blood diamonds</a>" that people unknowingly wear every day are used to fund horrific civil wars in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Aswad_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aswad-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Lyrically, the extensive, uplifting "We Major" is a spiritual exhultation of generational and personal success.<sup id="cite_ref-Pg573_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pg573-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> "Hey Mama" is West's dedication to his mother, Donda West. In the ballad, West recounts past hardships he and his mother suffered through together and expresses his love and devotion for her and appreciation for her tireless support, even when he was going directly against her expectations for him.<sup id="cite_ref-Vh1_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vh1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, the album includes a series of humorous <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_skit" title="Hip hop skit">skits</a> that involve West joining a fictional black <a href="/wiki/Fraternities_and_sororities" title="Fraternities and sororities" class="mw-redirect">fraternity</a>, "Broke Phi Broke," whose members pride themselves in living a life without money or worldly possessions, despite the glaring disadvantages such a lifestyle brings.<sup id="cite_ref-Pg574_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pg574-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> His character is eventually expelled from the fraternity after their leader discovers that not only has West been making beats for cash on the side but has also been breaking some of its rules, such as eating meals everyday, buying new clothes, and taking showers. According to music writer Mickey Hess, the skits serve to encapsulate, "a contradiction at the core of contemporary American life: the need to belong, to fit in, with your fellow humans versus the <a href="/wiki/Darwinistic" title="Darwinistic" class="mw-redirect">Darwinistic</a> mad grab at material things, success in the latter being the very definition of success in our culture."<sup id="cite_ref-Pg574_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pg574-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_promotion">Release and promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Registration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Release and promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dropout_Bear_Late.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e0/Dropout_Bear_Late.png/250px-Dropout_Bear_Late.png" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e0/Dropout_Bear_Late.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e0/Dropout_Bear_Late.png 2x" data-file-width="332" data-file-height="249" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dropout_Bear_Late.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.24wmf6/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Dropout Bear as he appears within the album artwork of <i>Late Registration</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>West presented music audiences with the first taste of <i>Late Registration</i> on April 20, 2005 while appearing on New York radio station <a href="/wiki/WQHT" title="WQHT">Hot 97</a>, where he played his lead single "Diamonds from Sierra Leone."<sup id="cite_ref-Diamonds_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diamonds-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The album was originally set to be released on July 12, 2005, but was shifted to August 16 by Roc-A-Fella/Def Jam.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> It was pushed back once more to August 30 by West himself as he needed more time to complete the album. <i>Late Registration</i> was anticipated to become the biggest-selling record of the year and over 1.6 million copies were distributed to stores in preparation of its first week of release.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">iTunes Store</a>, the album become one of the most pre-ordered titles in the <a href="/wiki/Online_shopping" title="Online shopping">online</a> <a href="/wiki/Digital_media" title="Digital media">digital media</a> store's history.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> West filmed a live album featuring tracks from <i>Late Registration</i> and <i>College Dropout</i>, titled <i>Late Orchestration</i> which was released April 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A television advertisement for <i>Late Registration</i> was directed and animated by Maggie Rogers, Abby Johnson and Paul Tuersley of Mr &amp; Mrs Smith Design Ltd. It featured a gigantic version of West's teddy bear mascot Dropout Bear roaming through the streets of London. The advert received an award from British music magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Music_Week" title="Music Week">Music Week</a></i> for Best Music TV Commercial.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> On the day of the album's release, West made an in-store appearance at New York's <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Center" title="Lincoln Center" class="mw-redirect">Lincoln Center</a> <a href="/wiki/Tower_Records" title="Tower Records">Tower Records</a> to autograph copies for fans.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> That same day, <i>Late Registration</i> was released in its entirety for online streaming on <a href="/wiki/AOL_Music" title="AOL Music" class="mw-redirect">AOL Music</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The art direction and music packaging for <i>Late Registration</i> was done by <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> graphic design studio Morning Breath, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-linernotes_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linernotes-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Similar to its predecessor, the album artwork of the second album carries an educational motif. Where <i>The College Dropout</i> was designed in a manner reminiscent of a high school yearbook, the images contained within the <a href="/wiki/Liner_notes" title="Liner notes">liner notes</a> of <i>Late Registration</i> were taken at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>. West's vision for the style of the pictures was inspired by the works of American satirical <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painter</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Currin" title="John Currin">John Currin</a>, one of his favorite artists.<sup id="cite_ref-linernotes_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linernotes-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The liner notes also contain a banner that reads <i>Tardus Subcriptio</i>, which is <a href="/wiki/Latin_(language)" title="Latin (language)" class="mw-redirect">Latin</a> for <i>Late Registration</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-linernotes_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linernotes-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The album artwork centers around "Dropout Bear", West's <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphic" title="Anthropomorphic" class="mw-redirect">anthropomorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Teddy_bear" title="Teddy bear">teddy bear</a> mascot, who is dressed in a collegian outfit.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Entering Princeton on the front cover, Dropout wanders its hallways, sits in empty <a href="/wiki/Lecture_hall" title="Lecture hall">lecture halls</a>, and reads multiple library books before departing from the institution the same way he came in on the back cover.<sup id="cite_ref-linernotes_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linernotes-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Registration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Late Registration</i> debuted at number one on the US <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_charts" title="Billboard charts">Billboard</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">200</a> chart, grossing 860,000 copies in the United States in its first week alone, nearly double that of <i>The College Dropout</i> first week sales.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> The album also debuted at number one on the charts in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-b1_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-b1-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> In the United Kingdom, <i>Late Registration</i> debuted and peaked at number two on the charts for the issue date of September 5.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> The second effort maintained its chart-topping position on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 for two consecutive weeks, and by its second week, had already gone platinum, selling an additional 283,000 units to reach a cumulative total of 1.14 million.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Selling 165,000 copies, the album dropped to number two the following week with the debut of Paul Wall's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Peoples_Champ" title="The Peoples Champ">The Peoples Champ</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> The very next week, it yielded another slot to the position of three, selling 124,000 units, bringing its cumulative total to 1.4 million.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> The album managed to remain in the top five the following week, despite the multiple new releases from various other recording artists. It sold over 110,000 copies and slid down two places to the fifth slot on the charts.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Late Registration</i> finally fell out of the top five in its sixth week on the chart, where it sold 94,000 copies and fell one spot to number six.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> However, on October 19, the album climbed up one space and re-entered the top five, selling 83,000 units.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> On the issue date of October 26, the album fell five places to number ten, selling 78,000 copies for its eighth week on the charts.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> The album rebounded once again on November 2 when it sold 56,000 units rise two places to reach the eighth position.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> As of June 14, 2013, <i>Late Registration</i> has sold 3.1 million copies in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Late Registration</i> received rave reviews from contemporary <a href="/wiki/Music_criticism" title="Music criticism">music critics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album received an <a href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean" class="mw-redirect">average</a> score of 85, which indicates "universal acclaim", based on 31 reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's <a href="/wiki/Rob_Sheffield" title="Rob Sheffield">Rob Sheffield</a> deemed the album "an undeniable triumph, packed front to back, so expansive it makes the debut sound like a rough draft", while calling West "a real <a href="/wiki/MC" title="MC" class="mw-redirect">MC</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Sheffield_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheffield-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In his review for <i><a href="/wiki/Uncut_(magazine)" title="Uncut (magazine)">Uncut</a></i> magazine, <a href="/wiki/Simon_Reynolds" title="Simon Reynolds">Simon Reynolds</a> found most of the album brilliant and highlighted by West's unparalleled use of vocal samples.<sup id="cite_ref-Reynolds_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Josh_Tyrangiel" title="Josh Tyrangiel">Josh Tyrangiel</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> praised West's storytelling ability and musicality, particularly the sample and string arrangements on "Gone", which he felt may persuade listeners to believe his own hype.<sup id="cite_ref-TimeBest05_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TimeBest05-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Petridis" title="Alexis Petridis">Alexis Petridis</a>, writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, commended West's topicality and subversive studio production: "<i>Late Registration</i> suggests an artist effortlessly outstripping his peers: more ideas, better lyrics, bigger hooks, greater depth."<sup id="cite_ref-Petridis_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Petridis-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> called the album a significant milestone in hip hop and West "the <a href="/wiki/Brian_Wilson" title="Brian Wilson">Brian Wilson</a> of hip-hop" who "plays up the struggle between conscience and covetousness, the pop mainstream and what can be achieved within the notional boundaries of hip hop".<sup id="cite_ref-Observer_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observer-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> In his review for the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hilburn" title="Robert Hilburn">Robert Hilburn</a> compared West's dignified execution of pop crossover to that of The Beatles, <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Cash" title="Johnny Cash">Johnny Cash</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley" title="Bob Marley">Bob Marley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hilburn_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hilburn-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> Sean Fennessey of <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> felt West avoids the <a href="/wiki/Sophomore_slump" title="Sophomore slump">sophomore slump</a> with an "expansive, imperfect masterpiece" that draws on his enthusiastic, ambitious, and scattered personality.<sup id="cite_ref-Fennessey_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fennessey-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Robert Christgau of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i> wrote that every song has "exquisite details", many of which are lyrical as well as musical, and concluded that West is "as good as he thinks he is—a backpacker at heart who, like many brilliant nerds before him, has accrued precious metal by following his dream. He wants everybody to buy this record. So do I".<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In a less enthusiastic review, Jon Pareles of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> surmised that West's elevated status undermines the underdog quality that accentuated his debut album: "for much of <i>Late Registration</i>, the striver has turned into a hip-hop V.I.P., and a cool arrogance has crept into the songs".<sup id="cite_ref-Pareles_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pareles-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> magazine's Jon Caramanica viewed that the augmented versatility and eccentricity of West's <a href="/wiki/Flow_(music)" title="Flow (music)" class="mw-redirect">flow</a> still "pales in comparison to his sonic ambition."<sup id="cite_ref-Caramanica_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caramanica-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> wrote that it lacks the "rubbish lyrics" and clumsy charm of his debut, but commended West for "freshening up his style without entirely abandoning it" and stated, "what makes this album so brilliant is the absurdly ace drums; this is pure cranium-crushing boom bap at its best".<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic" class="mw-redirect">Allmusic</a>'s Andy Kellman wrote that "[Jon] Brion string arrangements and brass flecks add a new dimension to West's beats without overshadowing them, and the results are neither too adventurous nor too conservative".<sup id="cite_ref-Kellman_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kellman-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Registration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Late Registration</i> topped numerous music critic polls and was called the best album of the year by numerous publications, including <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-TimeBest05_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TimeBest05-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> awarded the second effort the highest position on their year-end top albums list and hailed it as a "sweepingly generous, absurdly virtuosic hip-hop classic."<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> In <i>The Village Voice</i><span style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s 2005 <a href="/wiki/Pazz_%26_Jop" title="Pazz &amp; Jop">Pazz &amp; Jop</a> nationwide poll of 795 popular music critics, <i>Late Registration</i> finished at number one by a wide margin over any of the other album nominees. This was the second year in a row that West topped the poll, a feat that had occurred only one other time over twenty years ago by <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">The Clash</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> It was also named the year's second best album by <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> and eighteenth best by <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Late Registration</i> became West's second consecutive album to be rated "XXL" by <i><a href="/wiki/XXL_Magazine" title="XXL Magazine" class="mw-redirect">XXL</a></i>, the magazine's highest rank, which has been awarded to only sixteen other hip-hop albums.<sup id="cite_ref-XXL_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/2006_Grammy_Awards" title="2006 Grammy Awards" class="mw-redirect">2006 Grammy Awards</a>, <i>Late Registration</i> received a nomination for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Album_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Album of the Year">Album of the Year</a> and won <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Best Rap Album</a>. West's single "Gold Digger" was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Record_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Record of the Year">Record of the Year</a> and received the award for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance">Best Rap Solo Performance</a>. He also won <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Song" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Song">Best Rap Song</a> for "Diamonds from Sierra Leone."<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/37th_NAACP_Image_Awards" title="37th NAACP Image Awards">37th NAACP Image Awards</a>, the second album received a nomination for <a href="/wiki/NAACP_Image_Award_for_Outstanding_Album" title="NAACP Image Award for Outstanding Album">Outstanding Album</a>. <i>Late Registration</i> was nominated for Album of the Year at the third annual <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Vibe_Awards" title="Vibe Awards" class="mw-redirect">Awards</a>, but lost to <a href="/wiki/Mariah_Carey" title="Mariah Carey">Mariah Carey</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Emancipation_of_Mimi" title="The Emancipation of Mimi">The Emancipation of Mimi</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> West received the awards for Top Rap Album for <i>Late Registration</i> as well as Hot Rap Track for "Gold Digger" at the 2006 Billboard R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Awards, where he also received nominations for Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Album and Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Song.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Late Registration</i> was nominated for best International Album at the <a href="/wiki/2006_BRIT_Awards" title="2006 BRIT Awards">2006 BRIT Awards</a>. At the end of the 2000s decade, <i>Rolling Stone</i> ranked the album number 40 on its list of the 100 Best Albums of the Decade.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, Rolling Stone named the album number 118 on its revised 500 Greatest Albums of All Time list in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-Sheffield_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheffield-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
