<p><i><b>The Predator</b></i> is the third <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>. Released within months of the <a href="/wiki/1992_Los_Angeles_riots" title="1992 Los Angeles riots">1992 Los Angeles riots</a>, many songs comment on the racial tensions. The title is in part reference to the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Predator_2" title="Predator 2">Predator 2</a></i>, and the album itself includes samples from the film.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Though not his most critically successful album, commercially <i>The Predator</i> is Ice Cube's most successful album, reaching double <a href="/wiki/RIAA_Certification" title="RIAA Certification" class="mw-redirect">platinum</a> status in the United States, and it contains his most well-known single, "<a href="/wiki/It_Was_a_Good_Day" title="It Was a Good Day">It Was a Good Day</a>." <i>The Predator</i> is his only number one album on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> to date, selling 193,000 copies in its first week.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It has sold 2,210,283 to date, according to <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_Soundscan" title="Nielsen Soundscan" class="mw-redirect">Nielsen Soundscan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Predator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the opening song, "When Will They Shoot," Ice Cube addressed criticisms of <a href="/wiki/Anti-Semitism" title="Anti-Semitism" class="mw-redirect">anti-Semitism</a> he received for his last effort, <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Certificate_(album)" title="Death Certificate (album)">Death Certificate</a></i>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>White man is something I tried to study,</dd>
<dd>But I got my hands bloody, yeah.</dd>
<dd>They say I can sing like a <a href="/wiki/Jaybird" title="Jaybird" class="mw-redirect">jaybird</a></dd>
<dd>But, nigga, don’t say the <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">j-word</a></dd>
</dl>
<p>Elsewhere "We Had to Tear This Mothafucka Up" is directed at the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Police" title="Los Angeles Police" class="mw-redirect">LA Police officers</a> acquitted in the <a href="/wiki/Rodney_King" title="Rodney King">Rodney King</a> trial; an event that ignited the <a href="/wiki/1992_Los_Angeles_riots" title="1992 Los Angeles riots">1992 LA Riots</a>. The similarly themed "Who Got the Camera?" imagines a scenario in which a Black man is subjected to <a href="/wiki/Police_brutality" title="Police brutality">police brutality</a>. The songs are broken up by interludes involving interviews with Ice Cube and what appears to be a debate between members of a congregation or talk-show audience.</p>

<p>Although not as lauded as his previous efforts, <i>The Predator</i> was well-received. <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> called it "Ice Cube's strongest, most cohesive work yet" (11/20/92, p.&#160;88).<sup id="cite_ref-towerrecords.com_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-towerrecords.com-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Q_Magazine" title="Q Magazine" class="mw-redirect">Q Magazine</a></i> included it in its "90 Best Albums Of The 1990s" (12/99, p.&#160;74).<sup id="cite_ref-towerrecords.com_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-towerrecords.com-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_Magazine" title="Spin Magazine" class="mw-redirect">Spin Magazine</a></i> called it a record that "demands to be heard" (1/93, p.&#160;61).<sup id="cite_ref-towerrecords.com_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-towerrecords.com-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Retrospectively, however, the album has been praised and is now considered a classic by many for its groundbreaking production, subject matter and controversial themes, and lyricism.</p>
<p>It spawned three hit singles: "It Was A Good Day," which was a hit in March 1993; "Check Yo Self"; and "Wicked" (which was later covered by the band <a href="/wiki/Korn" title="Korn">Korn</a>). Both the album and single version of "Check Yo Self" include an appearance from <a href="/wiki/Das_EFX" title="Das EFX">Das EFX</a>, with the latter's single featuring a remix utilizing a sample of <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_Flash" title="Grandmaster Flash">Grandmaster Flash</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/The_Message_(Grandmaster_Flash_and_the_Furious_Five_song)" title="The Message (Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five song)">The Message</a>". The song also received continuous radio and <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> play.</p>
