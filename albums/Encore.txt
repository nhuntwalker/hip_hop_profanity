<p><i><b>Encore</b></i> (stylized as <i><b>ƎNCORE</b></i>) is the fifth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album" class="mw-redirect">studio album</a> by American rapper <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a> by <a href="/wiki/Shady_Records" title="Shady Records">Shady Records</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aftermath_Entertainment" title="Aftermath Entertainment">Aftermath Entertainment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interscope_Records" title="Interscope Records">Interscope Records</a>. Its release was set for November 16, 2004, but was moved up to November 12 (coincidentally, exactly eight years to the day his debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Infinite_(Eminem_album)" title="Infinite (Eminem album)">Infinite</a></i>, was released) after the album was leaked to the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>.</p>
<p><i>Encore</i> sold 710,000 copies in its first three days,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and went on to sell over 1.5 million copies in its first two weeks of release in the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> certified quadruple-platinum that mid-December.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Nine months after its release, worldwide sales of the album stood at 11 million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Critical reception was generally mixed. Most critics and fans alike did note the subpar quality of the lyrics, which were more simplistic when compared to his previous albums. The album was nominated for three <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammys</a> at the <a href="/wiki/48th_Annual_Grammy_Awards" title="48th Annual Grammy Awards">48th Annual Grammy Awards</a> including for <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Album" title="Best Rap Album" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Album</a> but lost to <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Registration" title="Late Registration">Late Registration</a></i>. The album made digital history in becoming the first album to sell 10,000 digital copies in one week.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> As of November 2013, the album has sold 5,343,000 copies in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Encore_(Eminem_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album contains several lyrical themes, including Eminem's relationship with his ex-wife, Kim, ("Puke", and "Love You More"), their daughter Hailie Jade Mathers ("<a href="/wiki/Mockingbird_(Eminem_song)" title="Mockingbird (Eminem song)">Mockingbird</a>"), his childhood ("Yellow Brick Road"), his relationships with his parents ("Evil Deeds"), and opposition to then-American President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> ("Mosh" and "We As Americans") "<a href="/wiki/Just_Lose_It" title="Just Lose It">Just Lose It</a>" is a parody of Michael Jackson's "<a href="/wiki/Beat_It" title="Beat It">Beat It</a>", as well as a <a href="/wiki/Pepsi" title="Pepsi">Pepsi</a> commercial in 1984. Similar to Eminem's previous album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Eminem_Show" title="The Eminem Show">The Eminem Show</a></i>, <i>Encore</i> opens with a skit called "Curtains Up", indicative of the start of the show.</p>
<p>Eminem has stated on numerous occasions (including on his 2010 album <i><a href="/wiki/Recovery_(Eminem_album)" title="Recovery (Eminem album)">Recovery</a></i>) that it was during the recording of <i>Encore</i> that he began to form an addiction to prescription drugs, and that he was not pleased with the album.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Censorship">Censorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Encore_(Eminem_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Censorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In "We As Americans", the line "F**k money, I don't rap for dead presidents, I'd rather see the president dead" has "dead" reversed in "See the president dead" on both clean and explicit versions of the album. Simultaneously with the original, a <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">censored</a> version was released, from which the profanities, violent and sexual content, as well the <a href="/wiki/Recreational_drug" title="Recreational drug" class="mw-redirect">drug</a> references had been edited.</p>
<p>"My 1st Single" has a bleep instead of a muted part in the verse "This was supposed to be my first single, but I just fucked that up so... Fuck it let's all have fun lets mingle" like the clean version of "<a href="/wiki/The_Real_Slim_Shady" title="The Real Slim Shady">The Real Slim Shady</a>". The word "ass" is left uncensored in "Yellow Brick Road", "One Shot 2 Shot", "Encore" and "We As Americans", but is censored out in "<a href="/wiki/Ass_Like_That" title="Ass Like That">Ass Like That</a>", "Mosh", "Spend Some Time", "My 1st Single", and "Just Lose It", and also in the song "Rain Man", the word "ass" was used twice, but only censored once. The word "goddamn" was left uncensored in "Spend Some Time." In the "clean" version's album booklet, the written lyrics have been removed, however on the songs "Puke", "My 1st Single" and "<a href="/wiki/Just_Lose_It" title="Just Lose It">Just Lose It</a>", lyrics were changed to avoid long censorship. Other profanities on all other songs are blanked out; and the song "Ass Like That" is listed as "A** Like That". The song "<a href="/wiki/Encore_(Eminem_song)" title="Encore (Eminem song)">Encore/Curtains Down</a>" has the shooting sequence at the end of the track removed on the censored album. Also, on the track "One Shot 2 Shot", the intro to the song is removed and the song starts at the first chorus, with more lines blanked out during the remainder of the track. Also, in "One Shot 2 Shot", during Kon Artis' verse, the word "fuck" was left uncensored. "Yellow Brick Road" leaves the word "goddamn" uncensored once. "One Shot 2 Shot" has the violent content edited, and the word "shot" is blanked throughout the song. However, the original title remains written on the back cover. The bonus disc was also censored for the album's clean version.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artwork">Artwork</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Encore_(Eminem_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Artwork">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album featured two covers, the first cover features Eminem standing in front of an audience, bowing to the crowd. The tray insert features Eminem holding a <a href="/wiki/Gun" title="Gun">gun</a> behind his back. The inlay shows Eminem holding the pistol in his mouth without the jacket of his shirt and tie. The CD itself shows a note written by Eminem saying "To my family &amp; All my friends, thank you for everything, I will Always love you. To my fans, I'm Sorry, Marshall" with a <a href="/wiki/Bullet" title="Bullet">bullet</a> underneath the note. The note is also seen in the album's booklet, where Eminem is writing the note. Some pictures show Eminem shooting everybody, which makes a reference to the ending of the album's <a href="/wiki/Encore_(Eminem_song)" title="Encore (Eminem song)">title track</a>. The second cover features the same audience from the inlay on a black background with a <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> splat on the top right. This cover is used for the Shady Collecter's Edition.</p>

<p>Upon its release, <i>Encore</i> received generally positive reviews. At Metacritic, which assigns a normalized rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album received an average score of 64, based on 26 reviews, which indicates "generally favorable reviews".<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> opined that <i>Encore</i> shares similar subject themes as its predecessor <i><a href="/wiki/The_Eminem_Show" title="The Eminem Show">The Eminem Show</a></i>. He called the music "spartan", built on "simple unadorned beats and keyboard loops" and described the lyrics as "plain-spoken and literal".<sup id="cite_ref-AllMusic_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusic-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a>, in a review for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, gave the album an A grade and characterized it as "funny, catchy and clever, and irreverent past his allotted time", noting that even the bonus tracks "keep on pushing".<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i>, the same author said that <i>Encore</i> wasn't as "astonishing" as <a href="/wiki/The_Marshall_Mathers_LP" title="The Marshall Mathers LP">The Marshall Mathers LP</a>, but praised Eminem for maturing his lyrical abilities while retaining his sense of humour.<sup id="cite_ref-RS_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RS-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Steve Jones from <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> also spoke positively about the album, calling Eminem's producing and lyrical skills as "top-flight" and noting that the record explores "the many sides of Marshall Mathers".<sup id="cite_ref-USA_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USA-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>However, not all reviews were so enthusiastic. Josh Love, writing in <a href="/wiki/Stylus_Magazine" title="Stylus Magazine">Stylus Magazine</a>, felt that Eminem was "dying" with this album. He further said that the concept was "end-to-end mea culpa", full of "clarifications, rectifications and excuses", revising the history of "a man who knows he doesn't have much time left".<sup id="cite_ref-Stylus_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stylus-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Scott Plangenhoef from <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> called <i>Encore</i> a "transitional record", further saying that the LP is "the sound of a man who seems bored of re-branding and playing celebrity games".<sup id="cite_ref-Pitchfork_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Pete Cashmore, writing in <a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a>, stated that this was Marshall's "retirement speech" because he seemed "intent on bringing ongoing disputes to a close".<sup id="cite_ref-NME_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NME-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> By contrast, Sal Cinquemani of <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a> wrote that <i>Encore</i> was "part of Eminem's evolution as an artist". He defended Eminem's repetitive lyrics by saying that the essence of an encore is "to sate the demand of an audience", not to "satisfy the artist's compulsion to create something new".<sup id="cite_ref-Slant_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slant-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/BBC_Music" title="BBC Music" class="mw-redirect">BBC Music</a>'s Adam Webb observed that <i>Encore</i> starts "fantastically" but ends "abominably". He criticized the album for having too many "lowpoints" and for failing to be Eminem's <i><a href="/wiki/In_Utero_(album)" title="In Utero (album)">In Utero</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> David Browne from <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> said that Eminem "sacrifices the rich, multi-textured productions" of his two previous albums for "thug-life monotony, cultural zingers for petty music-biz score-settling, and probing self-analysis for juvenile humor". He concluded his review by saying that Eminem has become "predictable" on <i>Encore</i>, something that he wasn't before.<sup id="cite_ref-EW_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EW-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album did provoke some controversy over anti-<a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">Bush</a> lyrics and lyrics that parodied and targeted <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>, who was upset about <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>'s depiction of him in the video for "<a href="/wiki/Just_Lose_It" title="Just Lose It">Just Lose It</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> On December 8, 2003, the United States Secret Service admitted it was "looking into" allegations that Eminem had threatened the President of the United States, George Bush,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> after the song "We as Americans", as an unreleased bootleg, circulated with the lyrics "Fuck money, I don't rap for dead presidents. I'd rather see the president dead." This line was eventually used as a sample in Immortal Technique's single "Bin Laden", which featured Mos Def and Chuck D. The incident was later referenced in the video for his song <a href="/wiki/Mosh_(song)" title="Mosh (song)">"Mosh"</a> as one several news clips on a wall, along with other newspaper articles about other unfortunate incidents in Bush's career. The song eventually appeared on the album's bonus disc, where the lyrics were extensively censored.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Encore_(Eminem_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album earned Eminem three Grammy Award nominations at the 48th Annual Grammy Awards: these included <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Album" title="Best Rap Album" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Album</a>, <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Performance_by_a_Duo_or_Group" title="Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group</a> for the song "<a href="/wiki/Encore_(Eminem_song)" title="Encore (Eminem song)">Encore</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Best Rap Solo Performance" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Solo Performance</a> for the song "<a href="/wiki/Mockingbird" title="Mockingbird">Mockingbird</a>". However, it did not win any of them, making it the only Eminem major studio album not to win a <a href="/wiki/Best_Rap_Album" title="Best Rap Album" class="mw-redirect">Best Rap Album</a> award.</p>
